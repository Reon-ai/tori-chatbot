# Tiletoria RAG Calculation Reference Guide

This RAG-ready document gives the chatbot factual, self-contained calculation guidance for common tile, LVT, grout, spacer, levelling system, trim, adhesive, self-levelling, waterproofing, sanitaryware and tapware quantity questions.

Source basis: TCNA and ANSI tile installation principles for mortar contact and movement risk, Ceramic Tile Education Foundation installer guidance, RUBI and other levelling-system manufacturer logic, MAPEI and LATICRETE calculator principles, ARDEX self-levelling product data-sheet logic, and resilient flooring installation principles for glue-down Luxury Vinyl Tile. Exact product coverage must always be confirmed against the specific product data sheet, bag, bucket, box or supplier calculator.

Important chatbot rule: This document provides estimating guidance only. Final quantities depend on the actual site, layout, installer method, product coverage, wastage, substrate condition and Tiletoria stock packaging. The chatbot must never guarantee exact quantities without product-specific data and installer confirmation.

## Measurement Inputs the Chatbot Should Request First

Summary: Accurate calculations start with the correct room dimensions, product size, pack size and installation method.

Customer inputs required: room length, room width, wall height where relevant, door/window openings where relevant, tile or plank size, grout joint width, tile thickness, area type, layout pattern, pack coverage, and installer preference.

Ask for length and width in metres when calculating floor area.

Ask for wall length and wall height in metres when calculating wall tile area.

Ask whether the measurement is the actual surface to be tiled, not the full room size if only part of the room is tiled.

Ask whether there are openings such as doors, windows, niches, cabinets or built-in baths that should be deducted.

Ask whether the tile layout is straight, staggered, diagonal, herringbone, chevron, border, feature panel or mixed format.

Ask whether the job is internal dry, internal wet, external, commercial, shower, ramp, pool surround or large format.

Ask whether the product is tile, glue-down Luxury Vinyl Tile, mosaic, large-format tile, trim, grout, adhesive, levelling compound, silicone, sanitaryware or tapware.

Ask whether the customer already knows the pack coverage, bag size, cartridge size or manufacturer coverage rate.

Escalation rule: If the customer cannot provide measurements or the area has many angles, curves, steps, niches or falls, recommend a site measurement by the installer or Tiletoria consultant.

## Core Area Calculation for Floors

Summary: Floor area is normally length multiplied by width, then adjusted for waste and packaging.

Formula: floor area in square metres = length in metres × width in metres.

Example: A room that is 4.2 m long and 3.6 m wide has a floor area of 15.12 m².

Formula: order area = measured floor area × waste factor.

Waste factor examples:
Straight simple layout: measured area × 1.10.
Complex room or many cuts: measured area × 1.12 to 1.15.
Diagonal, herringbone or pattern layout: measured area × 1.15 to 1.20.
Repairs or future spares: add extra boxes where practical.

Important rule: Do not subtract small fixed items unless they genuinely reduce the tiled area and the installer confirms it. Small deductions can create stock shortages.

Chatbot wording: “As a practical estimate, calculate the floor area and then add waste. For many straight tile layouts, 10% is commonly used, but complex layouts may need more.”

Escalation rule: Ask the installer to confirm waste where tiles are large format, imported, batch-sensitive, special order, patterned or installed diagonally.

## Core Area Calculation for Walls

Summary: Wall tile quantity is calculated from wall length multiplied by tiled height, then adjusted for openings and wastage.

Formula: wall area = wall length in metres × tiled height in metres.

Formula: total wall tile area = sum of all wall areas − large openings not being tiled.

Example: A wall that is 2.4 m wide and tiled to 2.1 m high has an area of 5.04 m².

Opening deduction rule: Deduct large openings such as windows or doors only if the installer confirms they should be deducted.

Niche rule: Shower niches often increase cuts and edge work. Do not assume they reduce waste.

Waste factor examples:
Simple wall tiling: add about 10%.
Walls with many cuts, niches or windows: add about 12% to 15%.
Feature or patterned walls: add about 15% to 20%.

Chatbot wording: “Wall areas often need more waste than customers expect because of cuts around corners, windows, niches and plumbing points.”

Escalation rule: Wet walls, shower walls and waterproofed walls should be checked by the installer before final quantity confirmation.

## Tile Quantity from Square Metres

Summary: Tile pieces are calculated from tile area, but orders should normally be rounded to full boxes.

Formula: tile area in square metres = tile length in metres × tile width in metres.

Formula: tile pieces required before waste = tiled area ÷ tile area.

Formula: tile pieces required after waste = order area ÷ tile area.

Formula: boxes required = order area ÷ box coverage, rounded up to the next full box.

Example: A 600 mm × 600 mm tile is 0.6 m × 0.6 m = 0.36 m² per tile.

Example: If the order area is 33 m² and the box covers 1.44 m², boxes required = 33 ÷ 1.44 = 22.92, so order 23 boxes.

Important rule: Always round up to full boxes.

Important rule: Keep spare tiles from the same batch because later batches may not match shade, calibre or design.

Escalation rule: For discontinued, imported, sale, batch-sensitive or high-variation tiles, recommend extra spares.

## Tile Waste Allowance Rules

Summary: Waste is not optional; it covers cuts, breakages, pattern matching, layout losses and future repairs.

Simple square room: add about 10%.

Room with many walls, doors, columns or cabinets: add about 12% to 15%.

Diagonal layout: add about 15%.

Herringbone or chevron layout: add about 15% to 20%.

Large-format tile: add at least 10%, and more if many cuts are required.

Outdoor steps, ramps or complex falls: add 15% or installer-confirmed allowance.

Mosaics: add 10% to 15%, depending on sheet size and cutting complexity.

Repair stock: keep at least one spare box where practical, more for exclusive or batch-sensitive products.

Chatbot warning: “Running short later may mean the same shade, calibre or batch is no longer available.”

Escalation rule: Let the installer confirm waste for all patterned, diagonal, large-format, imported or special-order jobs.

## Tile Adhesive Quantity

Summary: Adhesive quantity depends on area, trowel size, tile size, substrate condition, adhesive type and required mortar contact.

Formula: adhesive bags required = tiled area ÷ coverage per bag, rounded up.

Coverage input required: product coverage from the adhesive bag or technical data sheet.

Important rule: Do not use one universal adhesive coverage rate because coverage changes with notch size, substrate flatness, tile back profile and installation method.

Dry internal areas: tile industry standards commonly require a minimum of about 80% mortar contact.

Wet, shower or exterior areas: tile industry standards commonly require about 95% mortar contact.

Large-format tile: usually needs more careful trowel selection, directional trowelling, back-buttering and coverage checking.

Chatbot wording: “Adhesive coverage must be checked against the product data sheet and trowel size. Large-format, wet and exterior areas normally use more adhesive than small dry internal tiles.”

Escalation rule: Escalate adhesive estimates for showers, exterior areas, balconies, ramps, pools, large-format tiles, heated floors or unusual substrates.

## Adhesive Coverage Adjustment Factors

Summary: Real adhesive usage can be higher than theoretical bag coverage because jobsite conditions affect transfer and bed thickness.

Factors that increase adhesive use:
Large-format tiles.
Uneven substrates.
Textured or ribbed tile backs.
Large notch trowels.
Back-buttering.
Wet or exterior installations requiring higher coverage.
Natural stone.
Installer correcting minor variations with adhesive.
Porous or rough screeds.
Walls or floors outside flatness tolerance.

Never calculate adhesive from square metres alone unless the product coverage rate and trowel size are known.

Chatbot safe wording: “The calculation can estimate bag count, but the installer must confirm trowel size and coverage on site.”

Escalation rule: Where adhesive is being used to compensate for a poor substrate, recommend substrate correction instead of over-relying on adhesive.

## Grout Quantity Calculation

Summary: Grout usage depends on tile size, joint width, joint depth, tiled area and grout density.

Inputs required: tile length in millimetres, tile width in millimetres, grout joint width in millimetres, grout depth in millimetres, tiled area in square metres, grout product coverage or density factor.

Practical formula for grout joint volume:
Grout volume in litres = area m² × ((1000 ÷ tile length mm) + (1000 ÷ tile width mm)) × joint width mm × joint depth mm ÷ 1000.

Formula for grout mass:
Grout kg = grout volume litres × product density factor.

Important rule: The density factor must come from the grout manufacturer or calculator. Cement grout, epoxy grout and ready-mixed grout can differ.

Example: 600 mm × 600 mm tile, 3 mm joint, 8 mm joint depth, 20 m² area.
Joint length factor: (1000 ÷ 600) + (1000 ÷ 600) = 3.33.
Volume: 20 × 3.33 × 3 × 8 ÷ 1000 = 1.60 litres before waste and density conversion.

Waste allowance: Add 5% to 10% for grout overage unless manufacturer guidance says otherwise.

Important warning: Smaller tiles and mosaics use much more grout per square metre than large-format tiles because there are more joints.

Chatbot wording: “For an accurate grout quantity, use the manufacturer calculator or data sheet after confirming tile size, joint width, joint depth and area.”

## Grout Quantity by Common Situation

Summary: Grout need increases when joints are wider, tiles are smaller or joints are deeper.

Large-format tile with narrow joints: lower grout use per m².

Small floor tile with wider joints: medium grout use per m².

Mosaic sheets: high grout use per m².

Textured tiles: allow extra because grout residue and cleaning loss can be higher.

Deep joints: higher grout use.

Rectified tiles with narrow joints: lower grout use, but still require proper grout joints.

Outdoor tiles: use manufacturer-approved grout and allow for full joint filling.

Chatbot warning: “Do not estimate grout from area only. Tile size and joint width make a major difference.”

Escalation rule: Epoxy grout, pools, commercial kitchens, showers, exterior areas and natural stone should be escalated to a Tiletoria consultant.

## Silicone and Sealant Quantity

Summary: Silicone quantity is calculated from total joint length, joint width, joint depth and cartridge volume.

Inputs required: total joint length in metres, joint width in millimetres, joint depth in millimetres, cartridge volume in millilitres.

Formula for approximate rectangular bead coverage:
Coverage in metres per cartridge = cartridge volume ml ÷ (joint width mm × joint depth mm).

Example: 300 ml cartridge, 5 mm wide joint, 5 mm deep joint.
Coverage = 300 ÷ (5 × 5) = 12 linear metres before waste.

Triangular bead note: Some sealant beads are triangular or tooled, so real coverage can differ.

Waste allowance: Add 10% to 20% for starts, stops, tooling and uneven joints.

Use cases: bath edges, basin backs, shower screens, vanity junctions, internal corners, movement joints and some perimeters.

Important rule: Silicone is not structural fixing and does not replace waterproofing.

Escalation rule: Escalate shower leaks, bath movement, failed silicone, mould, waterproofing and structural movement questions.

## Tile Trim Quantity

Summary: Trim quantity is calculated in linear metres and rounded up to full trim lengths.

Formula: trim length required = sum of exposed tile edges in metres.

Formula: number of trim pieces = total trim length ÷ trim piece length, rounded up.

Waste factor: Add 5% to 10% for mitres, corners, mistakes and offcuts.

Common trim lengths: Confirm the actual Tiletoria stock length because trim lengths differ by supplier and product.

Applications: exposed wall tile edges, external corners, niches, step edges, transitions, splashbacks and tile terminations.

Important rule: Trim depth must suit tile thickness plus adhesive bed and installation method.

Chatbot wording: “Measure every exposed tiled edge and add a small waste allowance. Then round up to full trim lengths.”

Escalation rule: Escalate trims for stairs, ramps, external corners, wet areas, heavy-duty use or special finishes.

## Tile Skirting Quantity

Summary: Tile skirting is calculated from room perimeter, less openings, then adjusted for cuts and tile strip height.

Formula: room perimeter = 2 × length + 2 × width.

Formula: skirting length = room perimeter − openings where skirting is not installed.

Formula: skirting area = skirting length × skirting height in metres.

Example: Room 5 m × 4 m has perimeter = 18 m. If the doorway is 0.9 m, skirting length = 17.1 m.

Tile-strip rule: If skirting strips are cut from floor tiles, calculate how many strips can be cut from one tile and allow for waste.

Waste factor: Add 10% to 15% because skirting involves many cuts.

Trim rule: Top edges may need trim or neat finishing depending on the tile and design.

Escalation rule: Confirm skirting height, door openings, corners and whether skirting is cut from tile or supplied as a dedicated skirting.

## Spacer Quantity

Summary: Spacer quantity depends on tile count, layout, whether spacers are placed at intersections or along joints, and whether levelling clips also act as spacers.

Inputs required: area length, area width, tile length, tile width, layout pattern, spacer type and installer method.

Grid calculation:
Columns = ceiling(area width ÷ tile width).
Rows = ceiling(area length ÷ tile length).

Cross-spacer approximation for a simple grid:
Internal intersections = (columns − 1) × (rows − 1).

Perimeter T-spacer approximation:
Perimeter edge spacers depend on installer method and whether spacers are removed before grouting.

Important rule: Many installers use additional spacers along long edges, around perimeters, at cuts and near walls.

If levelling clips set the joint width, separate spacers may still be needed for perimeters, cuts, wall lines and layout control.

Chatbot wording: “Spacer quantity is an estimate because installers place spacers differently. Confirm whether the levelling clips also set the joint width.”

Escalation rule: Escalate for mosaics, herringbone, very large-format tiles, walls, patterned layouts and jobs where the installer has a preferred spacer method.

## Wedge and Clip Levelling System Calculation

Summary: Clips are normally single-use consumables, while wedges are reusable and should be calculated from the installer’s active daily working area.

Key rule: Buy clips for the whole job. Buy wedges for the installer’s active working section or daily production, not necessarily for the whole job.

Clip inputs required: tile size, tiled area, layout, clip spacing, clip type, tile thickness, joint width and installer method.

Wedge inputs required: installer daily square metres, adhesive setting cycle, number of installers, reuse timing and buffer.

Simple clip estimate method:
Use manufacturer clips-per-square-metre calculator where available.

Geometric clip estimate method for a rectangular grid:
Columns = ceiling(area width ÷ tile width).
Rows = ceiling(area length ÷ tile length).
Vertical shared edges = (columns − 1) × rows.
Horizontal shared edges = columns × (rows − 1).
Clips on long shared edge = installer-selected clips per tile long edge.
Clips on short shared edge = installer-selected clips per tile short edge.
Total clips = vertical shared edges × clips per vertical shared edge + horizontal shared edges × clips per horizontal shared edge.
Add 5% to 10% extra clips.

Clip placement rule: Clips are placed between tiles at shared joints, not randomly in the middle of a tile and not only at open outside edges.

Wedge calculation:
Daily clips used = total estimated clips ÷ total project area m² × installer daily m².
Wedges needed = daily clips used × reuse-cycle factor.
Common reuse-cycle factor: 1.1 to 1.25 for one active working day plus buffer.
Use 2.0 only if wedges cannot be removed and reused before the next working section.

Example: If clip rate is 8 clips per m² and installer lays 20 m² per day, daily clips = 160. Wedges may be about 175 to 200 with a buffer, not 800 for a 100 m² project.

Important rule: Wedges are reusable; clips are normally broken off and consumed.

Important rule: Levelling systems help reduce lippage between adjacent tiles, but they do not fix an uneven substrate.

Escalation rule: Escalate large-format, outdoor, wet-area, very uneven substrate or high-value installations to confirm clip spacing and wedge quantity with the installer.

## Clip Selection by Tile Thickness and Joint Width

Summary: Levelling clips must suit tile thickness and desired grout joint width.

Inputs required: tile thickness, grout joint width, tile format, clip brand and levelling system type.

Clip thickness rule: Use clips rated for the tile thickness range.

Joint width rule: Some clips create a fixed grout joint width; others need additional spacers.

Large-format rule: Larger tiles usually need more clips along each shared edge.

Outdoor or wet-area rule: Do not let levelling clips replace proper adhesive coverage, movement joints, falls or drainage.

Chatbot wording: “Please confirm the tile thickness and desired grout joint width so the correct clip size can be selected.”

Escalation rule: Escalate if the tile thickness is unknown, the tile is extra thick, the area is external, or the installer uses a different levelling system.

## Wedge Reuse and Rotation

Summary: Wedges should be bought according to how many clips are active before the wedges can be removed and reused.

Reusable item: wedge.

Consumable item: clip.

Possible reusable item: pliers, depending on system.

Removal rule: Wedges are normally removed after adhesive has set enough for the clips to be broken off according to system instructions.

Daily-production logic:
If one installer lays 10 m² per day, wedges should cover roughly that active area plus buffer.
If two installers lay 20 m² per day together, wedges should cover the combined active area plus buffer.
If work continues before yesterday’s wedges are removed, the wedge requirement increases.

Chatbot wording: “Wedge quantity is not based on total project area alone. It is based on how much fresh tiling the installer has active before wedges are removed and reused.”

Escalation rule: Ask the installer how many square metres they lay per day and when they remove wedges before finalising wedge packs.

## Self-Levelling Compound Quantity

Summary: Self-levelling quantity depends on area, average thickness, product consumption rate and bag size.

Inputs required: area in square metres, average thickness in millimetres, product consumption in kg per m² per mm, bag size in kg.

Formula: kg required = area m² × average thickness mm × product consumption kg/m²/mm.

Formula: bags required = kg required ÷ bag size kg, rounded up.

Example: Area 20 m², average thickness 3 mm, product consumption 1.5 kg/m²/mm, bag size 25 kg.
Kg required = 20 × 3 × 1.5 = 90 kg.
Bags = 90 ÷ 25 = 3.6, so order 4 bags before any additional waste.

Important rule: Product consumption differs by brand and product. Typical data sheets often state about 1.4 to 1.6 kg per m² per mm, but the exact product data sheet must be used.

Uneven substrate rule: Rough or uneven floors use more material than the theoretical calculation.

Waste factor: Add 5% to 10%, or more if the floor is very uneven.

Escalation rule: Escalate when thickness exceeds the product limit, the floor is damp, timber, cracked, painted, contaminated, external or load-sensitive.

## Primer Quantity

Summary: Primer quantity depends on surface porosity, substrate type, dilution and product coverage.

Formula: primer required = area ÷ product coverage rate, rounded up to full containers.

Coverage input required: manufacturer coverage rate for the specific primer and dilution.

High-porosity substrates: may use more primer.

Non-porous substrates: may need a different primer.

Moisture-control primers: must not be substituted with ordinary primer.

Important rule: Primer is system-specific. The primer must match the substrate and the self-leveller, adhesive or waterproofing system.

Chatbot wording: “Primer quantity must be based on the specific primer data sheet because porous screeds and non-porous surfaces need different products and coverage.”

Escalation rule: Escalate painted floors, old adhesive, existing tiles, damp screeds, timber, gypsum screeds and moisture-control applications.

## Waterproofing Quantity

Summary: Waterproofing quantity is calculated from the surface area to be waterproofed plus overlaps, corners, upturns and reinforcement.

Inputs required: floor area, wall heights, shower area, upturn heights, corners, pipe penetrations, membrane coverage, number of coats and reinforcement requirements.

Formula: waterproofing area = floor area + wall waterproofing area + upturns + niches + benches + returns.

Formula: product required = waterproofing area × number of coats ÷ product coverage, rounded up.

Important rule: Waterproofing coverage and thickness must follow the product data sheet.

Reinforcement items: corners, joints, wall-floor junctions, pipe penetrations, shower niches and movement-prone areas may need membrane band, tape, fabric or collars.

Chatbot wording: “Waterproofing is a system, not just a liquid. Include primer, membrane, reinforcing tape and pipe collars where required.”

Escalation rule: Escalate all shower, balcony, wet-room, external, leaking or commercial waterproofing questions.

## Glue-Down LVT Area and Waste Calculation

Summary: Glue-down Luxury Vinyl Tile is calculated from floor area, waste factor, pack coverage and layout.

Formula: floor area = length × width.

Formula: order area = floor area × waste factor.

Formula: boxes = order area ÷ box coverage, rounded up.

Waste factor examples:
Straight plank layout: 5% to 10%.
Multiple rooms or many cuts: 10%.
Herringbone, diagonal or feature layouts: 10% to 15% or installer-confirmed.

Important rule: Glue-down LVT should not be calculated without considering subfloor preparation, adhesive, primer, self-leveller, trims and floor protection.

Batch rule: Use one batch in continuous areas where possible.

Chatbot wording: “For glue-down LVT, calculate the area and waste, then also confirm adhesive, primer, levelling products, trims and vinyl-safe cleaner.”

Escalation rule: Escalate damp floors, bathrooms, underfloor heating, direct sun, old floor coverings, commercial areas and uneven subfloors.

## Glue-Down LVT Adhesive Quantity

Summary: LVT adhesive quantity depends on product coverage, trowel notch, substrate porosity and adhesive type.

Formula: adhesive required = area ÷ adhesive coverage rate, rounded up.

Coverage input required: adhesive technical data sheet or bucket label.

Important rule: Tile adhesive is not LVT adhesive. Glue-down LVT requires an adhesive approved for vinyl flooring.

Factors that affect coverage:
Trowel notch.
Substrate porosity.
Primer use.
Adhesive type.
Temperature.
Open time.
Floor smoothness.

Chatbot wording: “The correct adhesive must be matched to the LVT, substrate and room conditions. Please confirm the approved adhesive and coverage before installation.”

Escalation rule: Escalate underfloor heating, wet areas, direct sun, commercial use, damp concrete and unknown substrates.

## LVT Trims and Transition Quantity

Summary: LVT trims are measured in linear metres and rounded to full trim lengths.

Formula: trim length = sum of doorway widths, transitions, exposed edges and level changes.

Formula: trim pieces = total trim length ÷ trim piece length, rounded up.

Applications: doorways, tile-to-vinyl transitions, vinyl-to-carpet transitions, exposed edges, stair nosings and level changes.

Important rule: Trim profile must suit height difference, floor type, traffic and finish preference.

Chatbot wording: “Measure every doorway and transition where the LVT meets another floor finish, then round up to full trim lengths.”

Escalation rule: Stairs, commercial thresholds, wet areas, large height differences and wheelchair-accessible transitions should be escalated.

## Shower Channel and Waste Quantity

Summary: Shower waste quantity is not based only on floor area; it depends on outlet flow, drainage position, floor falls and waterproofing design.

Inputs required: shower size, shower outlet type, expected flow, waste position, channel length, tile size, fall direction and waterproofing system.

One shower usually needs one correctly sized waste or channel.

Linear channel length must suit shower width, tile layout and fall design.

High-flow showers may need higher drainage capacity.

Large-format shower floors may need careful fall planning.

Important rule: Select the shower waste or channel before screeding and waterproofing.

Chatbot wording: “The shower waste or channel must be planned before tiling because it affects falls, waterproofing and tile cuts.”

Escalation rule: Wet rooms, linear channels, walk-in showers, large-format shower floors and high-flow shower heads must be escalated.

## Movement Joint and Perimeter Joint Estimating

Summary: Movement joints are not optional in many tiled installations and should be planned by the installer or specifier.

Inputs required: area size, internal or external location, sun exposure, substrate type, joint layout, tile type and project specification.

Typical locations:
Perimeters.
Changes in plane.
Internal corners.
Door thresholds.
Large floor areas.
Over structural movement joints.
External and sun-exposed areas.
Around columns.
Between different substrates.

Quantity method:
Measure linear metres of planned movement joints.
Select correct movement joint material or profile.
Add waste for cuts and intersections.
Round up to full lengths or cartridges.

Important rule: Do not fill movement joints with rigid grout.

Chatbot wording: “Movement joint layout must be confirmed by the installer or specifier. The chatbot can help list the materials but should not design the joint system.”

Escalation rule: Large floors, exterior areas, balconies, heated floors, commercial spaces and structural joints must be escalated.

## Step Tile and Stair Nosing Quantity

Summary: Steps require separate linear measurement for treads, risers, nosings and exposed edges.

Inputs required: number of steps, tread depth, step width, riser height, tile size, nosing type and edge finish.

Formula: tread area = step width × tread depth × number of steps.

Formula: riser area = step width × riser height × number of steps.

Formula: nosing length = step width × number of steps.

Add waste: 10% to 15%, or more for mitres and patterned tiles.

Important rule: Steps and stair edges have higher safety and impact risk; correct trim or nosing selection matters.

Escalation rule: Escalate exterior steps, public steps, commercial staircases, ramps and slip-resistance questions.

## Mosaic Sheet Quantity

Summary: Mosaic quantity is based on sheet coverage, but grout, adhesive and waste are higher than for large-format tiles.

Formula: sheets required = order area ÷ sheet coverage, rounded up.

Waste factor: Add 10% to 15%, or more for niches, curves, borders and detailed cuts.

Grout rule: Mosaics use more grout because there are more joints per square metre.

Adhesive rule: Use suitable adhesive and trowel size to avoid adhesive squeezing through joints.

Trim rule: Exposed mosaic edges may need trim or careful finishing.

Chatbot wording: “Mosaics often need more grout and more careful cleaning than large tiles.”

Escalation rule: Shower floors, pools, curved surfaces, epoxy grout and natural stone mosaics should be escalated.

Step 2: Collect measurements.

Step 3: Calculate base area or length.

Step 4: Add waste.

Step 5: Convert to sale units.

Step 6: Add required accessories.

Step 7: Warn what must be confirmed by installer or Tiletoria.

Example chatbot response:

Escalation rule: If the customer needs legally sensitive or safety-critical advice, stop calculating and escalate.

## Universal Escalation Triggers for Calculations

Summary: The chatbot should escalate when calculations depend on site conditions, product data or professional design.

Escalate when:
The customer does not know the measurements.
