# Tiletoria RAG Utility File 05: Can I Use This Here? Product Suitability Decision Guide

This RAG-ready file helps the chatbot answer consumer questions such as “Can I use this tile outside?”, “Can I use LVT in a bathroom?”, “Can this tap work with my water pressure?”, “Can I tile over existing tiles?” and “Is this product suitable for my home?” It is written for South African retail customers and Tiletoria’s product categories.

Source basis: TCNA guidance that grout joints are not waterproof in wet areas, Ceramic Tile Education Foundation guidance on substrate flatness for large-format tile, Polyflor Luxury Vinyl Tile installation guidance on acclimatisation and subfloor preparation, SANS 10400 Part P drainage and SANS 10252 water supply/drainage awareness, and manufacturer technical data-sheet practice. Final suitability must always be confirmed against the product data sheet, site conditions and installer or plumber assessment.

Important chatbot rule: The chatbot must not answer suitability with a simple “yes” unless the product data, site conditions and installation system are known. It should use conditional language: “It may be suitable if...” and “Please confirm...”

## Master Suitability Rule

Summary: Product suitability depends on the product, location, substrate, water exposure, installation system, maintenance and installer approval.

Core decision questions:
Where will the product be used?
Is it floor, wall, wet area, dry area, indoor, outdoor, residential or commercial?
Will it be exposed to water, sun, oil, cleaning chemicals or heavy traffic?
What is the substrate?
Is the surface flat, dry, clean, stable and prepared?
What adhesive, grout, waterproofing or connector system will be used?
Is the product rated or recommended for that use?
Has the installer or plumber confirmed suitability?

Safe answer structure:
“It may be suitable if the product is rated for that application, the substrate is correctly prepared, and the correct installation products are used.”

Avoid:
“Yes, definitely.”
“No risk.”
“Slip-proof.”
“Waterproof tile installation.”
“Any installer can do it.”
“You can use it anywhere.”

Escalation rule: Escalate all wet areas, exterior areas, ramps, balconies, commercial spaces, glue-down LVT moisture concerns, concealed plumbing and water-pressure questions.

Summary: Tile suitability should be checked by area, finish, slip risk, substrate and installation system.

Ask first:

Is it indoor or outdoor?
Is it wet or dry?

Is the tile floor-rated or wall-only?

General rule:
Floor tiles can sometimes be used on walls if weight and wall structure allow.
Wall tiles must not be assumed suitable for floors.
Indoor tiles must not be assumed suitable for outdoor use.
Polished tiles must not be assumed suitable for wet floors or outdoors.
Textured tiles must not be described as slip-proof.

Chatbot answer: “Tile suitability depends on where it will be used. Please confirm whether it is floor or wall, indoor or outdoor, wet or dry, and whether the product is rated for that application.”

Escalation rule: Escalate showers, ramps, balconies, pool surrounds, commercial floors and outdoor wet areas.

## Can I Use Wall Tiles on the Floor?

Summary: Wall tiles are usually not suitable for floors unless specifically rated for floor use.

Answer: Usually no. A wall tile should not be used on a floor unless the manufacturer or product information confirms it is suitable for floor traffic.

Why:
Wall tiles may not be designed for foot traffic.
Wall tiles may not have suitable breaking strength or surface wear resistance.
Wall tiles may become unsafe or fail when used incorrectly.
Wall tiles may be too glossy or slippery for floor use.

Safe wording: “Please do not use wall tiles on floors unless the product is clearly rated for floor application.”

Escalation rule: Escalate if the customer wants to use wall tiles on bathroom floors, shower floors, outdoor floors, commercial floors or stairs.

## Can I Use Floor Tiles on the Wall?

Summary: Floor tiles can often be used on walls, but wall strength, tile weight, adhesive and installer method must be checked.

Answer: Sometimes. A floor tile may be suitable for a wall if the wall can support it and the correct adhesive and installation method are used.

Check:
Tile weight.
Tile size.
Wall substrate.
Wall flatness.
Adhesive suitability.
Mechanical support where needed.
Installer skill.

Large-format warning:
Large-format floor tiles on walls need extra planning because weight, flatness and adhesive coverage become more important.

Chatbot answer: “Floor tiles may be usable on walls, but the installer must confirm the wall substrate, tile weight, adhesive and fixing method.”

Escalation rule: Escalate large-format wall tiles, shower walls, plasterboard walls and commercial walls.
