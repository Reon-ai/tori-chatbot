# Category 4: Slip Resistance, Coefficient of Friction, R-Ratings and Wet Areas — 300 Q&As

This RAG-ready knowledge base gives conservative, factual customer-support answers for South African tile customers asking about slip resistance, Coefficient of Friction (COF), Dynamic Coefficient of Friction (DCOF), R-ratings, wet areas, ramps, bathrooms, showers, pool surrounds and outdoor floors. It avoids unsafe guarantees and treats final suitability as product-specific and site-specific.

Source basis: South African accessibility guidance in SANS 10400-S refers to floor and ground surfaces on accessible routes as stable, firm and slip-resistant under wet and dry conditions. Internationally recognised slip-resistance methods include DIN 51130 / EN 16165 Annex B for shod ramp testing, DIN 51097 / EN 16165 Annex A for wet barefoot ramp testing, and ANSI A326.3 for Dynamic Coefficient of Friction (DCOF). These tests are useful for comparison but do not guarantee that a slip cannot occur in real use.

## Slip Resistance Basics

Summary: This section explains slip resistance in plain customer language and confirms that no tile should be described as slip-proof or non-slip.

### Q001: What does slip resistance mean for tiles?

Answer: Slip resistance describes how well a tile surface helps reduce the chance of slipping under specific tested conditions. It is affected by the tile surface, water, dirt, cleaning residue, footwear, slope, drainage and maintenance.

### Q002: Is any tile completely non-slip?

Answer: No tile should be described as completely non-slip. A tile may be slip-resistant, but slip performance depends on the product, installation, contamination, water, slope, footwear and cleaning conditions.

### Q003: Why should the chatbot avoid saying a tile is non-slip?

Answer: The phrase non-slip can create a false guarantee. The safer wording is that a tile has a slip-resistant surface or rating, subject to correct product selection, installation, drainage, use and maintenance.

### Q004: Can a slip-resistant tile still be slippery?

Answer: Yes. A slip-resistant tile can still be slippery if it is wet, greasy, dusty, soapy, poorly cleaned, installed on a slope, or used with unsuitable footwear.

### Q005: Does a rougher tile always mean safer?

Answer: Not always. A rougher surface may improve grip in some conditions, but safety also depends on drainage, slope, contaminants, footwear and cleaning. Very rough tiles can also trap dirt if not maintained correctly.

### Q006: Does a smooth tile always mean unsafe?

Answer: No. A smooth tile may be suitable for dry internal areas, but it is usually not the safest choice for wet floors, ramps, outdoor areas or pool surrounds unless the product is specifically suitable.

### Q007: What is the main customer question when choosing a slip-resistant tile?

Answer: The main question is where the tile will be installed and what conditions it will face, such as dry indoor use, wet barefoot use, outdoor rain exposure, ramps, commercial traffic or grease contamination.

### Q008: Why does water make some tiles slippery?

Answer: Water can reduce friction between the foot or shoe and the tile surface. Soap, oil, dust, algae or cleaning residue can make this worse.

### Q009: Why can a tile feel different when wet compared with dry?

Answer: A surface may offer acceptable grip when dry but lose friction when wet or contaminated. Wet performance must be considered separately from dry appearance or dry feel.

### Q010: Is slip resistance only about the tile?

Answer: No. Slip resistance is part of a system that includes the tile, installation slope, drainage, cleaning regime, footwear, lighting, user behaviour and maintenance.

### Q011: Can the same tile perform differently in different locations?

Answer: Yes. A tile may perform well indoors but poorly outdoors if it is exposed to rain, dust, algae, slope or poor drainage. Product suitability must be matched to the actual area.

### Q012: Why does Tiletoria need to ask where the tile will be used?

Answer: Tiletoria needs the application because slip resistance requirements differ between living rooms, bathrooms, showers, kitchens, patios, pool surrounds, ramps and commercial areas.

### Q013: What is the safest general answer for slip resistance questions?

Answer: The safest answer is that slip resistance is product-specific and site-specific. The customer should confirm the exact tile, area, wet/dry exposure, slope, drainage and traffic level before purchase.

### Q014: Can a customer choose a tile only by looking at it?

Answer: No. Appearance alone does not confirm slip performance. The product finish, rating, intended use and site conditions must be checked.

### Q015: Does a matt tile automatically mean slip-resistant?

Answer: No. Matt tiles often feel more practical than polished tiles, but matt finish alone does not confirm slip resistance. The product specification and intended application must be checked.

### Q016: Does a textured tile automatically mean outdoor-safe?

Answer: No. Texture can help, but outdoor suitability also depends on the tile specification, water exposure, drainage, slope, frost or weather exposure where applicable, and correct installation.

### Q017: Why should slip resistance be treated as a safety issue?

Answer: Falls can cause injury, especially in wet areas, ramps, public walkways, pool surrounds and commercial spaces. Conservative wording protects the customer and the business.

### Q018: What should a chatbot say if a customer asks for the safest tile?

Answer: The chatbot should ask for the area of use and avoid naming a universal safest tile. It should recommend confirming the correct slip-resistant product for the specific site conditions.

### Q019: What should a chatbot say if a customer asks whether a tile will prevent slipping?

Answer: The chatbot should say that no tile can guarantee prevention of slipping. It can help reduce risk if correctly specified, installed, cleaned and used in the right application.

### Q020: When must slip resistance questions be escalated?

Answer: Escalate slip resistance questions for ramps, public areas, commercial projects, showers, pool surrounds, external walkways, old-age facilities, schools, healthcare areas and any claim involving injury or liability.

## Coefficient of Friction (COF) and Dynamic Coefficient of Friction (DCOF)

Summary: This section explains Coefficient of Friction and Dynamic Coefficient of Friction as laboratory comparison measures, not real-world safety guarantees.

### Q021: What does Coefficient of Friction mean?

Answer: Coefficient of Friction (COF) is a measurement of friction between two surfaces. For tiles, it is used to compare how much resistance a surface may offer under a specific test method.

### Q022: What does Dynamic Coefficient of Friction mean?

Answer: Dynamic Coefficient of Friction (DCOF) measures friction while a surface is already in motion under a defined test method. It is commonly used in North American tile testing.

### Q023: What is the difference between COF and DCOF?

Answer: Coefficient of Friction (COF) is a general term for friction measurement. Dynamic Coefficient of Friction (DCOF) specifically measures friction during movement, which is more relevant to walking than static friction alone.

### Q024: Does a COF number guarantee that a tile is safe?

Answer: No. A COF number is a test result under controlled conditions. It does not guarantee that a slip cannot occur in real-world use.

### Q025: Does a DCOF number guarantee that a tile is safe?

Answer: No. Dynamic Coefficient of Friction is useful for comparison, but real-world slip risk also depends on water, grease, dust, footwear, slope, drainage, cleaning and user behaviour.

### Q026: Why can COF values be misleading?

Answer: COF values can be misleading if the test method, wet or dry condition, surface condition and intended use are not understood. Different test methods can produce different results.

### Q027: Can two tiles with the same COF perform differently in real use?

Answer: Yes. Two tiles with similar test values may perform differently because of texture, wear, cleaning residue, contaminants, installation slope, drainage and footwear.

### Q028: What is ANSI A326.3?

Answer: ANSI A326.3 is an American National Standard test method used to measure Dynamic Coefficient of Friction for hard surface flooring under defined conditions.

### Q029: What does a wet DCOF result mean?

Answer: A wet Dynamic Coefficient of Friction result shows measured friction under a specific wet laboratory test. It should be used as guidance, not as a guarantee of safety.

### Q030: Is the American DCOF test the same as an R-rating?

Answer: No. Dynamic Coefficient of Friction and R-ratings come from different test methods and should not be treated as directly interchangeable.

### Q031: Can Tiletoria rely only on COF for product suitability?

Answer: No. COF can assist product comparison, but suitability must also consider application, wet exposure, slope, traffic, cleaning, installation and manufacturer guidance.

### Q032: Should a customer ask for COF when buying tiles?

Answer: A customer can ask for available technical information, including COF or Dynamic Coefficient of Friction where supplied. The result should be interpreted with the intended application.

### Q033: Why might some tiles not have a COF value shown on a price card?

Answer: Not all supplier documents list Coefficient of Friction values. Some products use R-ratings, barefoot ratings or manufacturer application guidance instead.

### Q034: Can a chatbot invent a COF value if the customer asks?

Answer: No. The chatbot must never invent a Coefficient of Friction value. It should say that technical data must be confirmed from the product specification or supplier.

### Q035: What if the customer asks whether a COF of 0.42 is safe?

Answer: The chatbot should not confirm safety based only on the number. It should explain that 0.42 is a test threshold in some American standards for level interior wet areas, but site suitability still requires professional assessment.

### Q036: Is COF more important than R-rating?

Answer: Neither is automatically more important. The relevant test depends on the market, product data and application. The test result must match the real use condition.

### Q037: Can COF change over time?

Answer: Yes. Wear, polishing, poor cleaning, residue, sealers, coatings, contamination and damage can change the effective slip resistance of a floor in use.

### Q038: Can cleaning chemicals affect COF?

Answer: Yes. Incorrect cleaners, oily residues, waxes, soaps and build-up can reduce friction and make a floor more slippery.

### Q039: What should the chatbot say about COF and wet areas?

Answer: The chatbot should say that wet areas require careful product selection and that Coefficient of Friction or Dynamic Coefficient of Friction is only one factor in suitability.

### Q040: When should COF questions be escalated?

Answer: Escalate COF questions when they involve commercial compliance, public safety, ramps, pools, old-age facilities, schools, healthcare, injury claims or formal specifications.

## R-Ratings for Shod Footwear Areas

Summary: This section explains R9 to R13 ratings as ramp-test classifications for areas walked on with footwear, not as universal safety labels.

### Q041: What is an R-rating on tiles?

Answer: An R-rating is a slip-resistance classification from a ramp test for floors walked on with footwear. Common classes are R9, R10, R11, R12 and R13, with R13 being the highest rating in that system.

### Q042: What does R9 mean?

Answer: R9 is the lowest common R-rating category in the shod ramp test system. It may be suitable for some dry internal areas, but it should not be assumed suitable for wet or risky areas.

### Q043: What does R10 mean?

Answer: R10 indicates a higher ramp-test classification than R9. It may suit some internal or transitional areas, but actual suitability depends on the product, wet exposure, slope, traffic and maintenance.

### Q044: What does R11 mean?

Answer: R11 indicates a higher shod ramp-test classification than R10. It is often considered for outdoor or wetter areas, but it is not a guarantee of safety and must match the site conditions.

### Q045: What does R12 mean?

Answer: R12 is a high shod ramp-test classification often associated with more demanding or slippery environments. It may be harder to clean than smoother surfaces, so the application must be considered.

### Q046: What does R13 mean?

Answer: R13 is the highest common shod R-rating classification and is usually associated with very demanding slip-risk environments. It may be very textured and may require more intensive cleaning.

### Q047: Are there R1 to R8 tile ratings?

Answer: In the common DIN shod ramp-test classification used for flooring, the practical classes are R9 to R13. R1 to R8 are not used in that classification.

### Q048: Does a higher R-rating always mean a better tile?

Answer: No. A higher R-rating may improve grip in certain conditions, but it can also make the tile harder to clean. The best rating is the one suitable for the actual application.

### Q049: Does R11 mean the tile is non-slip?

Answer: No. R11 means the tile achieved a specific classification under a shod ramp test. It does not mean the tile is non-slip or safe in every wet, sloped or contaminated condition.

### Q050: Can an R10 tile be used outdoors?

Answer: Possibly, but not automatically. Outdoor suitability depends on product specification, surface texture, water exposure, drainage, slope, climate, maintenance and project requirements.

### Q051: Can an R11 tile be used on a ramp?

Answer: An R11 rating may be relevant, but ramps need extra caution. The correct tile depends on the ramp gradient, exposure to rain, drainage, traffic, footwear, cleaning and applicable project requirements.

### Q052: Can an R-rating be compared directly with COF?

Answer: No. R-ratings and Coefficient of Friction results come from different test methods and should not be converted directly without expert interpretation.

### Q053: Is R-rating tested wet or dry?

Answer: The traditional shod DIN 51130 ramp test uses footwear and oil as the contaminant. It is not the same as a wet barefoot shower or pool test.

### Q054: Does R-rating apply to barefoot showers?

Answer: Not directly. Wet barefoot areas are commonly assessed using barefoot classification methods such as A, B or C under DIN 51097 or newer EN 16165 approaches.

### Q055: Why can R-ratings be misunderstood?

Answer: R-ratings are often treated as simple safety labels, but they are laboratory classifications under a specific test method. Real-world slip risk depends on many additional factors.

### Q056: Can Tiletoria advertise a tile only as R11?

Answer: Tiletoria can state an R-rating if supported by supplier data, but should also explain the intended use and avoid implying that the tile is guaranteed non-slip.

### Q057: Should all bathroom floors be R11?

Answer: Not necessarily. Bathroom suitability depends on whether the area is dry, splash-prone, a shower floor, public bathroom or commercial wet area. Product-specific advice is required.

### Q058: Should all outdoor tiles be R11 or higher?

Answer: Not automatically. R11 is commonly considered for external wet-prone areas, but final suitability depends on the site, slope, drainage, traffic, cleaning and formal specification.

### Q059: Can an R13 tile be used in a home?

Answer: It can be used if suitable, but it may be unnecessarily rough and harder to clean for normal residential use. The application should guide the choice.

### Q060: When should R-rating questions be escalated?

Answer: Escalate R-rating questions for ramps, pool surrounds, public walkways, commercial floors, legal compliance, injury claims, healthcare, schools and any formal architectural specification.

## Wet Barefoot Ratings: A, B and C

Summary: This section explains wet barefoot classifications for showers, pools and barefoot wet areas separately from shod R-ratings.

### Q061: What are A, B and C barefoot slip ratings?

Answer: A, B and C are wet barefoot classification groups from a ramp-test method used for areas walked on barefoot, such as showers, changing rooms and pool surrounds.

### Q062: What does Class A mean in barefoot slip testing?

Answer: Class A is the lower wet barefoot classification and is generally associated with mainly dry barefoot walkways. It is not the highest level for wet barefoot risk.

### Q063: What does Class B mean in barefoot slip testing?

Answer: Class B indicates a higher wet barefoot classification than Class A and is commonly associated with showers, pool surrounds and wetter barefoot areas, subject to product and site suitability.

### Q064: What does Class C mean in barefoot slip testing?

Answer: Class C is the highest common wet barefoot classification and is associated with more demanding wet barefoot areas, such as steps into water or walk-through pool zones.

### Q065: Are barefoot ratings the same as R-ratings?

Answer: No. Barefoot ratings and R-ratings come from different tests. R-ratings are for footwear conditions, while A, B and C relate to wet barefoot areas.

### Q066: Should a shower floor use an R-rating or barefoot rating?

Answer: A shower floor is a wet barefoot area, so barefoot performance is especially relevant. Supplier data may not always list barefoot ratings, so product suitability should be confirmed.

### Q067: Can an R11 tile automatically be used in a shower?

Answer: No. R11 is a shod ramp-test classification and does not automatically confirm suitability for a wet barefoot shower floor.

### Q068: Can a Class B tile still be slippery?

Answer: Yes. Class B is a test classification, not a guarantee. Soap, shampoo, body oils, water pooling, incorrect slope and poor cleaning can increase slip risk.

### Q069: Why are pool surrounds different from patios?

Answer: Pool surrounds are often wet and walked on barefoot, while patios are often walked on with footwear. The relevant slip-performance requirement can therefore differ.

### Q070: Do barefoot ratings apply to commercial change rooms?

Answer: Yes, wet barefoot performance can be relevant in change rooms, showers and leisure facilities. Commercial projects should be assessed against the project specification.

### Q071: Why is soap important in barefoot slip testing?

Answer: Soap and body products can reduce friction significantly. Wet barefoot areas such as showers should be selected with this contamination risk in mind.

### Q072: Should the chatbot recommend mosaics for shower floors?

Answer: The chatbot may say mosaics or smaller tiles are often used because they follow falls and create more grout joints, but the product, grout, waterproofing and installation must be suitable.

### Q073: Are more grout joints always safer in showers?

Answer: More grout joints can add texture and help accommodate falls, but grout can also stain or deteriorate if poorly installed or maintained. It is not a substitute for correct tile suitability.

### Q074: Can polished tile be used in a shower floor?

Answer: Polished tile is generally not recommended for shower floors because it can become slippery when wet and soapy. Confirm any wet barefoot use with Tiletoria before purchase.

### Q075: Can textured outdoor tiles be used in showers?

Answer: Possibly, but not automatically. The texture must be comfortable barefoot, cleanable, suitable for the shower environment and compatible with correct falls and drainage.

### Q076: What should the chatbot say about wet barefoot safety?

Answer: The chatbot should say that shower, pool and barefoot wet areas need product-specific confirmation and that no tile can be guaranteed slip-proof.

### Q077: Should a bathroom wall tile need a barefoot rating?

Answer: No. Barefoot slip ratings apply to walking surfaces, not wall tiles. Bathroom wall tiles still need to be suitable for wet wall use and cleaning.

### Q078: Does a pool coping need slip consideration?

Answer: Yes. Pool coping and pool surrounds should be selected carefully because they are often wet, exposed outdoors and used barefoot.

### Q079: Can a customer rely on a barefoot rating from another country?

Answer: A barefoot rating can be useful technical information, but local project requirements, South African conditions, installation and maintenance must still be considered.

### Q080: When should barefoot-rating questions be escalated?

Answer: Escalate barefoot-rating questions for showers, pool surrounds, spas, changing rooms, public bathrooms, hotels, gyms, healthcare facilities and any injury-risk application.

## Bathrooms and General Wet Floors

Summary: This section covers normal bathroom floors and splash-prone wet areas using cautious product-selection language.

### Q101: What tile finish is generally better for bathroom floors?

Answer: A matt or slip-resistant floor-rated tile is generally more practical for bathroom floors than a polished or high-gloss tile, especially where water may be present.

### Q102: Can polished tiles be used on bathroom floors?

Answer: Polished tiles can be used only if suitable for the specific bathroom area, but they may become slippery when wet. They are not normally the safest choice for wet bathroom floors.

### Q103: Are bathroom floors considered wet areas?

Answer: Yes. Bathroom floors may be exposed to splashes, steam, wet feet, soap and cleaning products, so finish and slip resistance must be considered.

### Q104: Do bathroom wall tiles need slip resistance?

Answer: No. Slip resistance applies to walking surfaces. Bathroom wall tiles should instead be suitable for wet wall use and cleaning.

### Q105: What should a customer consider for a family bathroom floor?

Answer: Consider a practical floor-rated tile with a matt or slip-resistant finish, easy cleaning, correct waterproofing where required, and proper installation around baths, showers and basins.

### Q106: What should a customer consider for an en-suite bathroom floor?

Answer: Consider wet-foot traffic, ventilation, cleaning, lighting, tile size, grout colour and a suitable surface finish that is practical for daily use.

### Q107: Can a glossy ceramic wall tile be used on a bathroom floor?

Answer: No, unless the tile is specifically rated for floor use. Wall tiles may not be strong enough or slip-appropriate for bathroom floors.

### Q108: Can a floor tile be used on a bathroom wall?

Answer: Often yes, if the wall can carry the tile and the adhesive system is suitable. Large or heavy tiles should be confirmed by a qualified installer.

### Q109: Does waterproofing make a bathroom floor slip-resistant?

Answer: No. Waterproofing protects against water movement below the tile surface; it does not make the tile surface slip-resistant.

### Q110: Does grout make a bathroom floor safer?

Answer: Grout joints can add some texture, especially with smaller tiles, but grout is not a safety guarantee. The tile surface, falls, cleaning and drainage remain important.

### Q111: Should small tiles be used in bathrooms?

Answer: Small tiles can help with falls and design, especially in showers, but larger tiles can also work in general bathroom areas if the floor is suitable and properly installed.

### Q112: Can large-format tiles be used on bathroom floors?

Answer: Yes, if the floor is flat enough, falls are planned correctly, and the tile finish is suitable. Shower floors may require smaller pieces to follow drainage falls.

### Q113: What makes a bathroom tile dangerous?

Answer: A bathroom tile can be risky if it is too smooth for wet use, incorrectly installed, poorly drained, contaminated with soap, or maintained with slippery residues.

### Q114: Can bath mats solve slip risk?

Answer: Bath mats can help in some areas but should not be used to justify choosing an unsuitable tile. Mats can also move or create trip hazards if not managed properly.

### Q115: What should the chatbot say about elderly users in bathrooms?

Answer: It should recommend extra caution, a suitable slip-resistant surface, good drainage, grab rails where needed, and professional advice for accessible or assisted-living bathrooms.

### Q116: Are hotel bathrooms different from home bathrooms?

Answer: Yes. Hotel bathrooms have higher and more varied use, so product suitability, cleaning, slip risk and formal specification should be checked carefully.

### Q117: Can a customer choose a bathroom tile from a sample only?

Answer: A sample helps show colour and texture, but product suitability, finish, rating and installation area must still be confirmed.

### Q118: What if a customer says their bathroom tile is slippery?

Answer: The chatbot should ask whether the floor is wet, soapy or recently cleaned, and escalate to Tiletoria for assessment rather than diagnosing from a message.

### Q119: Should bathroom floors have falls to drains?

Answer: Where a floor drain or shower drain is present, correct falls are important to reduce water pooling. The installer or contractor must set the falls correctly.

### Q120: When should bathroom slip questions be escalated?

Answer: Escalate questions involving shower floors, elderly users, commercial bathrooms, public bathrooms, injuries, complaints, polished floors or uncertainty about product suitability.

## Showers and Wet Rooms

Summary: This section treats showers and wet rooms as high-risk wet barefoot areas that require product-specific confirmation and correct waterproofing, falls and drainage.

### Q121: What tile is best for a shower floor?

Answer: A shower floor needs a floor-rated tile suitable for wet barefoot use, with correct falls, drainage, waterproofing, adhesive, grout and installation. Product-specific confirmation is required.

### Q122: Can any bathroom tile be used in a shower floor?

Answer: No. Shower floors are more demanding because they are wet, soapy and used barefoot. The tile must be suitable for that specific application.

### Q123: Why are shower floors high-risk?

Answer: Shower floors combine water, soap, shampoo, body oils, bare feet and sloped surfaces. This combination increases slip risk.

### Q124: Can polished porcelain be used in a shower floor?

Answer: Polished porcelain is generally not recommended for shower floors because it can become slippery when wet and soapy. Confirm with Tiletoria before selecting any polished tile for wet walking surfaces.

### Q125: Are mosaics good for shower floors?

Answer: Mosaics are often used because they can follow falls and provide more grout joints, but the mosaic must be suitable for wet floors and installed correctly.

### Q126: Can large tiles be used on a shower floor?

Answer: Large tiles can be difficult on shower floors because they may not follow falls to the drain properly. The installer must confirm the layout and drainage design.

### Q127: What is a wet room?

Answer: A wet room is a bathroom area where the shower is open or integrated into the floor. It requires careful waterproofing, falls, drainage and slip-resistant product selection.

### Q128: Does a wet room need more slip resistance than a standard bathroom?

Answer: Often yes, because more of the floor can become wet during use. Product suitability and drainage design are very important.

### Q129: Can a textured outdoor tile be used inside a shower?

Answer: Possibly, but it must be comfortable barefoot, cleanable, suitable for wet internal use and compatible with the shower installation system.

### Q130: What grout is best for shower floors?

Answer: The grout must be suitable for wet areas and installed correctly. Epoxy or high-performance grouts may be specified in some demanding areas, but the final choice depends on the project.

### Q131: Does epoxy grout make a shower floor slip-resistant?

Answer: No. Epoxy grout can help with stain and water resistance in some applications, but it does not make the tile surface slip-resistant.

### Q132: Can sealing make a shower floor safer?

Answer: Sealing does not automatically improve slip resistance and some sealers can affect surface friction. Only use products recommended for the tile and wet area.

### Q133: Why must shower floors drain properly?

Answer: Standing water increases slip risk, staining, residue build-up and maintenance problems. Correct falls and drainage are part of the safety system.

### Q134: Who is responsible for shower falls?

Answer: The installer or contractor is responsible for setting correct falls and drainage. Tiletoria can advise on product options but does not control site workmanship.

### Q135: Can a shower tile be guaranteed safe if it has a rating?

Answer: No. Ratings help with selection, but wet barefoot safety also depends on soap, water, falls, drainage, cleaning, user behaviour and maintenance.

### Q136: Should shower tiles be tested on site?

Answer: For high-risk or commercial showers, on-site slip testing may be appropriate. This should be handled by a suitably qualified professional.

### Q137: What should the chatbot say if a customer asks for a non-slip shower tile?

Answer: It should say that Tiletoria can help with slip-resistant options, but no tile can be guaranteed non-slip. The exact product must be matched to the shower design and conditions.

### Q138: Are shower walls a slip concern?

Answer: No. Shower walls need wet-wall suitability, waterproofing behind the tile and cleanability. Slip resistance is relevant to the shower floor.

### Q139: Can soap residue make a shower tile slippery?

Answer: Yes. Soap, shampoo, conditioners and body oils can reduce friction and must be cleaned properly.

### Q140: When should shower questions be escalated?

Answer: Escalate all shower-floor, wet-room, commercial shower, pool shower, injury, complaint, polished-tile and compliance questions to a Tiletoria consultant or technical contact.
