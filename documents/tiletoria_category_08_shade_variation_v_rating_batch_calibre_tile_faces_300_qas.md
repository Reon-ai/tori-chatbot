# Category 8: Shade Variation, V-Rating, Batch, Calibre and Tile Faces

This RAG-ready knowledge-base document explains tile shade variation, V-rating, batch numbers, calibre, tile faces, blending and customer expectation management for Tiletoria customer support.

Research basis: ANSI A137.1 aesthetic classes describe variation ratings from V0 to V4; Ceramic Tile Education Foundation guidance highlights preparing for tile shade variation; manufacturer and industry guidance consistently recommends checking shade, batch, calibre and blending tiles from multiple boxes before installation. This document uses conservative customer-support wording and does not treat variation ratings as quality, slip-resistance or durability ratings.

## Understanding Shade Variation in Tiles

Summary: Shade variation is the visible difference in colour, tone, texture or pattern between tiles in the same range, and it must be understood before purchase and installation.

**Q001: What does shade variation mean in tiles?**
A: Shade variation means that individual tiles in the same range may differ in colour, tone, texture, veining or pattern. This can be intentional, especially on stone-look, marble-look, cement-look, wood-look and handmade-look tiles. Customers should view the variation before purchase and ensure the installer blends tiles properly during installation.

**Q002: Is shade variation a defect?**
A: Shade variation is not automatically a defect. Many ceramic and porcelain tiles are intentionally designed with variation to create a more natural or realistic appearance. A defect concern should be checked against the product specification, shade rating, batch details and manufacturer tolerance before installation continues.

**Q003: Why do tiles in the same range sometimes look different?**
A: Tiles in the same range can look different because of digital printing, glaze effects, firing conditions and intentional design variation. Natural-look tiles often use multiple faces to avoid a flat or repetitive appearance. The installer should open several boxes and blend the tiles across the area.

**Q004: Can shade variation happen within the same box?**
A: Yes. Shade, tone or pattern variation can occur within the same box, especially with tiles rated as moderate or high variation. This is usually part of the design and should be assessed by laying out multiple pieces before fixing them permanently.

**Q005: Can shade variation happen between different boxes?**
A: Yes. Tiles from different boxes can vary slightly, especially if the boxes come from different shade batches or production lots. Before installation, boxes should be checked for product code, shade, calibre, batch and finish, and tiles should be mixed from several boxes.

**Q006: Why does a tile sample sometimes look different from the delivered stock?**
A: A sample may come from a different production batch or may show only one face from a range with several design variations. The delivered stock should be checked before installation so that the customer can confirm the shade, calibre, finish and overall appearance.

**Q007: Should customers expect every tile to look identical?**
A: No. Customers should not expect every tile to look identical unless the product is specifically designed as a very uniform tile. Many modern ranges are made with intentional variation in tone, veining, print or texture.

**Q008: Why is shade variation important before buying tiles?**
A: Shade variation affects the final visual result of the installation. A customer who wants a calm, uniform floor may prefer a low-variation tile, while a customer who wants a natural stone or handmade look may prefer a higher-variation tile.

**Q009: How should Tiletoria explain shade variation to customers?**
A: Tiletoria should explain that shade variation is the expected difference between individual tiles in a range. The consultant should encourage the customer to view more than one tile, check the variation rating where available, and confirm that the selected look suits the room.

**Q010: What should a customer do if they dislike variation?**
A: If a customer dislikes visible variation, they should choose a low-variation tile and view multiple tiles before purchasing. The consultant should avoid recommending high-variation stone-look, marble-look, terrazzo-look or handmade-look products to customers who want a perfectly uniform result.

**Q011: Why do natural-look tiles usually have more variation?**
A: Natural-look tiles often copy stone, marble, cement, timber or handmade materials, which naturally have differences in colour, tone and texture. Manufacturers use variation to make the installation look more realistic and less repetitive.

**Q012: Can lighting make shade variation look stronger?**
A: Yes. Natural light, downlights, reflective surfaces and shadows can make shade, texture and pattern variation appear stronger. Customers should view tile samples in lighting similar to the installation area where possible.

**Q013: Can grout colour affect perceived shade variation?**
A: Yes. A contrasting grout colour can make tile edges and tone differences more visible, while a closer grout colour can create a softer and more blended look. Grout colour should be selected with the tile and the desired final effect in mind.

**Q014: Can surface texture affect perceived shade variation?**
A: Yes. Textured tiles can reflect light unevenly, which may make colour or tone variation appear stronger. This is usually part of the surface character and should be considered before purchase.

**Q015: Can shade variation be seen more clearly on large areas?**
A: Yes. Large open floors and long walls make shade variation easier to see because many tiles are viewed together. For large areas, customers should view multiple pieces and installers should blend tiles carefully from different boxes.

**Q016: Can shade variation be seen more clearly on large-format tiles?**
A: Yes. Large-format tiles show more of the design on each tile and can make veins, tones or faces more noticeable. Layout planning and blending are important for a balanced large-format installation.

**Q017: Is shade variation more noticeable on polished tiles?**
A: It can be. Polished or glossy tiles reflect light strongly, so tone, veining, smudges or surface differences may appear more visible. Customers should view polished tiles in realistic lighting before purchase.

**Q018: Is shade variation more noticeable on matt tiles?**
A: Matt tiles usually reflect less light, but variation can still be visible, especially in cement-look, stone-look, terrazzo-look or handmade-look ranges. The product design and variation rating are more important than the finish alone.

**Q019: Does shade variation affect tile performance?**
A: Shade variation is mainly an aesthetic issue and does not normally affect technical performance. Performance depends on factors such as tile classification, water absorption, slip resistance, substrate preparation, adhesive coverage and correct installation.

**Q020: When should shade variation be escalated to a consultant?**
A: Escalate shade variation queries when the customer is mixing batches, matching old tiles, working on a large project, disputing installed tiles, or asking whether the variation is a defect. These cases need product-specific checking before a firm answer is given.

## V-Rating and Aesthetic Variation Scale

Summary: The V-rating is an aesthetic variation guide used to describe how much visible variation a tile range may show from tile to tile.

**Q021: What is a V-rating on tiles?**
A: A V-rating is an aesthetic variation rating that indicates how much visible variation in colour, tone, texture or pattern can be expected within a tile range. It helps customers understand whether a tile will look uniform, slightly varied, moderately varied or highly varied once installed.

**Q022: What does the V in V-rating stand for?**
A: The V in V-rating stands for variation. The number after the V indicates the general degree of expected visual variation within the tile series.

**Q023: Is a V-rating a slip rating?**
A: No. A V-rating is not a slip rating. It relates to visual variation only, such as colour, tone, texture or pattern differences. Slip resistance must be assessed separately through the relevant slip-resistance information for the product and application.

**Q024: Is a V-rating a quality rating?**
A: No. A V-rating is not a quality grade. A high V-rating does not mean poor quality, and a low V-rating does not mean better quality. It only describes the intended visual variation of the tile.

**Q025: What does V1 mean on tiles?**
A: V1 means uniform appearance. Tiles in a V1 range should have minimal visible difference from tile to tile. This is best for customers who want a calm, consistent and uniform look.

**Q026: What does V2 mean on tiles?**
A: V2 means slight variation. Tiles in a V2 range may show small differences in tone, texture or pattern while still keeping a generally consistent overall appearance.

**Q027: What does V3 mean on tiles?**
A: V3 means moderate variation. Tiles in a V3 range may show clearly visible differences in colour, tone, texture or pattern. The installer should blend tiles from several boxes to create a balanced effect.

**Q028: What does V4 mean on tiles?**
A: V4 means substantial or high variation. Tiles in a V4 range can show strong differences from tile to tile and may create a dramatic, natural or random look. Customers should view multiple pieces before purchasing.

**Q029: Can a V4 tile look very different from the sample?**
A: Yes. A single sample may show only one part of a highly varied V4 design. Customers should view several tiles, product images or display panels before buying a V4 tile, especially for large visible areas.

**Q030: Is V4 suitable for customers who want a clean uniform floor?**
A: Usually not. V4 tiles are designed for visible variation and may not suit customers who want a very even or uniform floor. For a uniform look, V1 or V2 is usually more appropriate.

**Q031: Is V3 suitable for modern interiors?**
A: Yes. V3 can work very well in modern interiors, especially where a natural stone, cement, marble or handmade look is desired. The customer must be comfortable with visible variation across the installation.

**Q032: Can a V1 tile still show slight differences?**
A: Yes. Even a V1 tile may show minor manufacturing or lighting differences, but it should appear generally uniform. Customers still need to check product, shade, batch and calibre before installation.

**Q033: Can a V2 tile have visible pattern differences?**
A: Yes. V2 tiles can have noticeable but controlled differences in pattern, texture or tone. The final effect should remain relatively balanced when installed correctly.

**Q034: Why should V3 and V4 tiles be dry-laid before installation?**
A: V3 and V4 tiles should be laid out before fixing so the customer and installer can check the balance of colour, veining and pattern. Dry-laying helps avoid clusters of similar or strongly contrasting pieces in one area.

**Q035: Does every tile supplier show a V-rating?**
A: Not always. Some product labels or specifications show a V-rating, while others may describe the variation in words or images. If the variation is unclear, customers should ask Tiletoria to help check the product information before purchase.

**Q036: Can V-ratings differ between ranges from the same factory?**
A: Yes. Each tile range can have its own design intent and variation rating. A factory can produce very uniform products and highly varied products, so the rating must be checked per range, not assumed by brand.

**Q037: Can V-ratings differ between colours in the same collection?**
A: Yes. Different colours within a collection can sometimes show different levels of visible variation. Always check the specific colour, size and finish being purchased.

**Q038: Should the chatbot recommend a V-rating based on taste?**
A: The chatbot can guide customers generally, but it should not make a final design decision for them. It can say that V1 or V2 suits a uniform look, while V3 or V4 suits a natural or dramatic look, and then recommend viewing multiple pieces before purchase.

**Q039: What is the safest way to explain V-ratings to customers?**
A: Explain that V-ratings describe expected visual variation and are not quality, slip or durability ratings. Encourage the customer to view multiple tiles and confirm they are comfortable with the appearance before installation.

**Q040: When should V-rating questions be escalated?**
A: Escalate V-rating questions when the customer is buying for a large area, matching existing tiles, using a V3 or V4 product, or raising a complaint after installation. These cases need product-specific review.

## Batch, Lot and Shade Numbers

Summary: Batch, lot and shade numbers identify production groupings and help reduce the risk of visible mismatch in one installation area.

**Q041: What is a tile batch number?**
A: A tile batch number identifies a production grouping of tiles. Tiles from the same batch are more likely to match in shade, tone, calibre and finish than tiles from different batches.

**Q042: What is a tile lot number?**
A: A tile lot number is another way of identifying a production group or manufacturing run. Lot details help trace tiles and reduce the risk of mixing noticeably different production runs.

**Q043: What is a shade number on a tile box?**
A: A shade number identifies the colour or tone grouping of the tiles. Boxes with different shade numbers may not match visually when installed together.

**Q044: Why should batch numbers be checked before installation?**
A: Batch numbers should be checked before installation to confirm that the correct product and matching production group are being used. This helps prevent visible mismatch across the floor or wall.

**Q045: Can two boxes with the same product code have different shade numbers?**
A: Yes. The same product code can be produced in different shade batches over time. Customers and installers must check the shade number, not only the product name or code.

**Q046: Can two boxes with the same product name look slightly different?**
A: Yes. The same product name can have different production batches or shade numbers. This is why boxes must be checked before installation and extra tiles should be bought upfront where possible.

**Q047: Is it safe to mix different shade numbers in one room?**
A: Mixing different shade numbers in one room is risky because the tiles may look visibly different once installed. It should only be done after a careful dry layout and customer approval.

**Q048: Can different batches be blended successfully?**
A: Sometimes different batches can be blended, but it depends on how close the shades and calibres are. This should be checked visually before installation and approved by the customer before tiles are fixed.

**Q049: Why does batch matching matter more on plain tiles?**
A: Plain or low-variation tiles often show shade differences more clearly because there is less pattern to disguise the mismatch. Batch consistency is especially important for uniform designs.

**Q050: Why does batch matching matter on large open floors?**
A: Large open floors make batch differences easier to see because many tiles are viewed together under the same light. Matching batch, shade and calibre is important for a clean result.

**Q051: Can batch variation affect replacements later?**
A: Yes. Replacement tiles bought later may come from a different batch and may not match the original installation exactly. Keeping spare tiles from the original batch is strongly recommended.

**Q052: Should customers buy all tiles for a project at once?**
A: Yes. Customers should buy enough tiles for the full project, including waste and future spares, at the start. This improves the chance of receiving matching shade and calibre for the whole installation.

**Q053: Can Tiletoria guarantee future batch availability?**
A: No. Future batch, shade or calibre availability cannot be guaranteed. Stock changes over time, and later production runs may differ from the original batch.

**Q054: What should the customer check on each box label?**
A: The customer or installer should check product name, product code, size, finish, shade, batch, lot, calibre and quantity. Any mismatch must be reported before installation begins.

**Q055: Who should check batch numbers before tiling starts?**
A: The installer should check box labels before installation, and the customer should also confirm the correct product has been supplied. Any concerns must be raised before tiles are fixed.

**Q056: What if the installer opens boxes and finds different batches?**
A: The installer should stop and check with the customer and Tiletoria before installing. Different batches may need to be separated, blended carefully or replaced depending on the situation.

**Q057: What if different batches were already installed?**
A: If different batches were already installed, the area should be assessed before any further action is taken. Resolution may be difficult after installation, so these issues must be prevented before fixing tiles.

**Q058: Why is batch control a customer-service issue?**
A: Batch control helps prevent avoidable complaints about colour mismatch, uneven appearance and failed expectations. Clear advice before installation protects the customer, installer and supplier.

**Q059: Can batch variation be seen only after grouting?**
A: Sometimes variation becomes more noticeable after grouting because grout frames the tiles and the area is viewed as a finished surface. This is why dry-laying and checking before fixing are important.

**Q060: When should batch questions be escalated?**
A: Escalate batch questions when boxes show different shade, calibre or lot numbers, when the customer is buying extra stock later, or when a mismatch complaint arises after installation.

## Calibre and Tile Size Grouping

Summary: Calibre describes the actual size grouping of tiles after production and helps installers manage grout joints and alignment.

**Q061: What does calibre mean on a tile box?**
A: Calibre means the actual size grouping of the tile after manufacturing. Ceramic and porcelain tiles can have small size variations, so tiles are grouped into calibres to help installations align correctly.

**Q062: Is calibre the same as tile size?**
A: No. Tile size is the nominal size, such as 600x600 millimetres. Calibre refers to the actual size grouping after production, which may be slightly different from the nominal size.

**Q063: Why do tiles have calibres?**
A: Tiles have calibres because firing and manufacturing can create small dimensional differences. Grouping tiles by calibre helps installers keep joints and alignment consistent.

**Q064: Can two boxes of the same tile have different calibres?**
A: Yes. The same tile range can sometimes be supplied in different calibres. Different calibres may not align well if mixed in the same installation area.

**Q065: Is it safe to mix different calibres?**
A: Mixing different calibres is not recommended unless the installer has checked the size difference and adjusted the layout and grout joints accordingly. It should be approved before installation starts.

**Q066: How does calibre affect grout joints?**
A: Calibre affects grout joints because tiles with slightly different actual sizes need enough joint width to absorb size variation. Very narrow joints may make size differences more visible.

**Q067: Does rectified tile still have calibre?**
A: Yes. Rectified tiles are mechanically finished for more precise edges, but they can still have production groupings and tolerances. Rectified does not mean there is no size variation at all.

**Q068: Does non-rectified tile need wider grout joints because of calibre?**
A: Usually yes. Non-rectified pressed-edge tiles often need wider grout joints to accommodate normal size variation and edge shape. The installer should follow product guidance and industry practice.

**Q069: Can calibre differences cause uneven grout lines?**
A: Yes. If different calibres are mixed, grout lines may look inconsistent because the tiles are not exactly the same actual size. This is why box labels should be checked before installation.

**Q070: Can calibre differences cause lippage?**
A: Calibre differences mainly affect sizing and joint alignment, while lippage is height difference between tile edges. However, poor sorting, incorrect layout and inadequate joints can make the installation look uneven.

**Q071: Should calibre be checked on all boxes?**
A: Yes. Calibre should be checked on every box before installation, especially for large areas, open-plan floors and rectified tiles with narrow grout joints.

**Q072: What should an installer do if calibre numbers differ?**
A: The installer should stop and check the size difference before fixing the tiles. The customer and Tiletoria should be consulted to decide whether to use, separate, exchange or re-plan the material.

**Q073: Can calibre matter less on small feature areas?**
A: It may matter less on small isolated areas, but it still needs to be checked. Different calibres can still create joint alignment problems, especially if the tiles meet at corners or transitions.

**Q074: Does calibre matter for wall tiles?**
A: Yes. Calibre can matter for wall tiles because size differences can affect joint alignment, corners, niches and feature layouts. The installer should check calibre before starting.

**Q075: Does calibre matter for mosaics?**
A: Mosaics are usually supplied on sheets, but sheet size and alignment still need to be checked. If mosaics are from different lots or sheets, the installer should inspect alignment and shade before fixing.

**Q076: Can calibre be fixed after installation?**
A: No practical correction is usually possible after tiles are fixed, except removal and replacement in severe cases. Calibre concerns must be identified before installation.

**Q077: How should Tiletoria explain calibre simply?**
A: Tiletoria can explain calibre as the real-size grouping of the tile. The product may be called 600x600 millimetres, but the factory still groups tiles by actual size to help installers achieve a consistent result.

**Q078: Is calibre a sign of poor manufacturing?**
A: No. Calibre is a normal part of ceramic and porcelain tile manufacturing. Good batch and calibre control helps manage normal production tolerances.

**Q079: Should customers keep a note of the calibre used?**
A: Yes. Customers should keep photos of the box labels or paperwork showing product code, shade, batch and calibre. This helps if spare tiles or future repairs are needed.

**Q080: When should calibre questions be escalated?**
A: Escalate calibre questions when different calibre numbers are found, when narrow joints are requested, when tiles are rectified, or when replacement stock is being matched to an existing installation.

## Tile Faces and Digital Print Variation

Summary: Tile faces are the different printed design versions within a range and strongly influence how natural or repetitive an installation looks.

**Q081: What is a tile face?**
A: A tile face is one printed design variation within a tile range. A range with multiple faces has several different surface patterns that repeat across boxes and pallets.

**Q082: Why do modern tiles have different faces?**
A: Modern tiles use different faces to imitate natural materials such as stone, marble, cement, timber and terrazzo. Multiple faces make the installation look more realistic and less repetitive.

**Q083: Does each box contain all tile faces?**
A: Not always. A box may contain only some of the faces in the range. Customers should not assume one box shows the full design variation of the product.

**Q084: Can two tiles from the same range have different veining?**
A: Yes. If the range has multiple faces, two tiles can have different veining, markings or pattern movement. This is normal for many marble-look, stone-look and cement-look products.

**Q085: Can tile faces repeat in an installation?**
A: Yes. Tile faces can repeat because a range has a limited number of printed designs. Good installers rotate and blend tiles to reduce obvious repetition.

**Q086: How can installers avoid obvious repeated faces?**
A: Installers can avoid obvious repetition by opening several boxes, identifying strong faces, rotating tiles and distributing similar patterns across the area instead of placing them next to each other.

**Q087: Why should tile faces be dry-laid first?**
A: Dry-laying allows the customer and installer to see how faces, veins and tones work together before fixing. This is especially important for marble-look, V3 and V4 ranges.

**Q088: What is bookmatching in tiles?**
A: Bookmatching is a planned layout where veined tiles are placed to mirror or continue a pattern. Only ranges designed for bookmatching should be used this way, and the layout must be planned before installation.

**Q089: Can any marble-look tile be bookmatched?**
A: No. Not every marble-look tile is designed for bookmatching. The product must have suitable faces and the installer must follow a planned layout.

**Q090: What is a random-face tile design?**
A: A random-face tile design uses multiple surface patterns that are intended to look natural and varied. The installer should still blend them carefully to avoid unwanted clusters or repetition.

**Q091: Can tile faces affect customer expectations?**
A: Yes. A customer who sees one calm sample may be surprised by stronger faces in the delivered stock. Consultants should show multiple faces where possible or explain the variation clearly.

**Q092: How should Tiletoria sell tiles with many faces?**
A: Tiletoria should position many faces as a design benefit for customers who want a natural, premium or less repetitive look. The consultant should also explain that variation must be accepted and blended correctly.

**Q093: How should Tiletoria sell tiles with few faces?**
A: Tiletoria should explain that fewer faces can create a more consistent look but may show pattern repetition more clearly. The right choice depends on the customer’s design preference and room size.

**Q094: Do large-format tiles usually show faces more clearly?**
A: Yes. Large-format tiles show more printed design on each piece, so the faces, veins and pattern movement are more visible. Layout planning is important.

**Q095: Can tile faces be selected one by one?**
A: Usually no. Tiles are supplied by boxes or pallets, not by individual printed face selection. Customers should expect a mix of faces according to the range and stock supplied.

**Q096: Can a customer reject a tile because one face is bold?**
A: A bold face is not automatically a defect if it forms part of the approved range design. The customer should view the range variation before purchase and installation.

**Q097: What if a box contains too many similar faces?**
A: The installer should open more boxes and blend tiles across the area. If the face mix appears unusual or incorrect, the issue should be checked before installation continues.

**Q098: Can tile faces affect cutting waste?**
A: Yes. If a customer wants a specific vein or face position, more planning and waste may be required. This is common with feature walls, large-format marble looks and bookmatch-style layouts.

**Q099: Should tile faces be considered for feature walls?**
A: Yes. Feature walls make tile faces highly visible. The customer and installer should plan the face distribution and vein direction before installation.

**Q100: When should tile-face questions be escalated?**
A: Escalate tile-face questions when the customer wants bookmatching, a vein-continuation layout, a feature wall, a large-format marble look, or a complaint about repeated or bold faces.

## Samples, Displays and Customer Expectations

Summary: Samples and displays help customers understand a tile, but they may not show every face, batch or variation in the delivered stock.

**Q101: Does a sample show the full tile variation?**
A: No. A sample may show only one tile face, one shade or one part of the design. Customers should view multiple pieces, display panels or product images where possible before making a final decision.

**Q102: Can the delivered tile look different from the showroom display?**
A: Yes. The delivered tile may come from a different batch, shade or production run, or the display may show only part of the design variation. This should be explained before purchase.

**Q103: Why should customers look at more than one tile?**
A: Customers should look at more than one tile because one piece cannot always show the full colour, pattern, texture and face variation of the range.

**Q104: Should customers take samples home?**
A: Where possible, customers should view samples in the room where the tile will be used. Lighting, wall colours, cabinets and natural light can change how the tile appears.

**Q105: Can showroom lighting change tile appearance?**
A: Yes. Showroom lighting may differ from home lighting, and this can affect how colour, gloss, texture and variation appear. Customers should view the tile in their own lighting where practical.

**Q106: Can photos accurately show shade variation?**
A: Photos can help, but they may not show exact colour, texture, gloss or batch tone. Customers should not rely only on photos for final colour approval.

**Q107: Can online images accurately show tile variation?**
A: Online images are useful for inspiration, but screen settings, lighting and photography can change colour appearance. Final selection should be based on the physical product where possible.

**Q108: Should customers approve tiles before installation?**
A: Yes. Customers should inspect the tiles before installation and confirm that the product, colour, shade, calibre, finish and variation are acceptable. Concerns should be raised before tiles are fixed.

**Q109: What should a consultant say when a customer chooses from one sample?**
A: The consultant should explain that one sample may not show the full variation and should encourage the customer to view multiple pieces or product images before placing the order.

**Q110: How should a consultant manage high-variation tile expectations?**
A: The consultant should clearly explain that high-variation tiles may show strong differences between pieces and that this is part of the design. The customer should approve the look before installation.

**Q111: Should customers compare the sample with delivered stock?**
A: Yes. Customers should compare delivered stock with the sample or showroom display before installation, allowing for normal batch and variation differences. Any concern must be raised before fixing.

**Q112: What if a customer says the tile is darker than expected?**
A: The consultant should ask whether the customer has checked multiple boxes, lighting conditions, shade numbers and finish. If there is a concern, installation should stop and the product should be reviewed before further work continues.

**Q113: What if a customer says the tile is lighter than expected?**
A: The consultant should advise checking product code, shade, batch, lighting and several tiles from different boxes. Installation should not continue until the customer is satisfied or the issue has been assessed.

**Q114: What if the sample is old?**
A: An older sample may not represent the current production batch exactly. Customers should verify current stock before purchase, especially for large projects or matching work.

**Q115: Can display boards contain discontinued batches?**
A: Yes, a display board may have been made from an earlier production batch. Current stock should always be checked for shade, calibre and overall appearance before installation.

**Q116: How can Tiletoria reduce misunderstandings from samples?**
A: Tiletoria can reduce misunderstandings by explaining variation ratings, showing several pieces where possible, photographing box labels, and reminding customers to inspect tiles before installation.

**Q117: Should customers sign off high-variation tiles?**
A: For high-variation tiles, a customer sign-off or clear acknowledgement is useful because it confirms the customer understands the intended appearance before installation.

**Q118: Should customers approve feature-wall layouts?**
A: Yes. Feature-wall layouts should be approved before fixing because face distribution, veins and shade movement are highly visible in a focal area.

**Q119: Can a small sample make a tile look calmer than a full floor?**
A: Yes. A small sample can make variation look less noticeable than it will across a full floor. Large areas show pattern, face and shade variation more clearly.

**Q120: When should sample-related questions be escalated?**
A: Escalate sample-related questions when the customer is matching an existing tile, questioning a delivered batch, buying a high-variation product, or disputing a difference between sample and stock.

## Ordering Quantity, Waste and Spare Tiles

Summary: Customers should order enough tiles from the same batch at the start because future matching cannot be guaranteed.

**Q121: Why should customers order extra tiles?**
A: Customers should order extra tiles for cuts, breakages, layout waste and future repairs. Extra tiles from the original batch help avoid future mismatch if a tile is damaged later.

**Q122: How much extra tile should be ordered for shade safety?**
A: The extra amount depends on the project, layout and tile size, but many standard installations allow around 10 percent extra. Complex layouts, diagonal patterns, large-format tiles and high-waste areas may need more.

**Q123: Why is ordering all tiles at once important?**
A: Ordering all tiles at once improves the chance of receiving the same shade, batch and calibre for the full project. Later orders may come from a different production run and may not match exactly.

**Q124: Can Tiletoria match extra tiles later?**
A: Tiletoria can try to help match extra tiles later, but an exact match cannot be guaranteed. Batch, shade, calibre and stock availability change over time.

**Q125: Should customers keep spare tiles after installation?**
A: Yes. Customers should keep spare tiles from the original installation. They are useful for plumbing repairs, electrical changes, impact damage or future maintenance.

**Q126: How many spare tiles should be kept?**
A: The number depends on the area and tile size, but customers should keep enough spares to replace several damaged pieces. Larger formats may require more spare square metres because each tile covers a larger area.

**Q127: Why are spare tiles more important for discontinued products?**
A: Discontinued products may not be available later. Spare tiles from the original batch may be the only realistic way to achieve a close repair match in future.

**Q128: Why are spare tiles important for V3 and V4 products?**
A: V3 and V4 products have stronger variation, so future matching can be more difficult. Keeping original spare tiles gives the best chance of a visually acceptable repair.

**Q129: Why are spare tiles important for large-format tiles?**
A: Large-format tiles are harder to replace if no matching stock is available because each piece is visually prominent and covers a large area. Spares should be planned upfront.

**Q130: Should customers store box labels with spare tiles?**
A: Yes. Customers should keep box labels or photos showing product code, shade, batch, calibre and finish. This helps if future matching or technical checking is needed.

**Q131: Can spare tiles age differently from installed tiles?**
A: Yes. Installed tiles may be exposed to wear, cleaning products, sunlight and traffic, while stored tiles remain protected. Even original spares should be checked against the installed area before repair.

**Q132: Should spare tiles be stored safely?**
A: Yes. Spare tiles should be stored flat or safely supported in a dry area where they will not chip, crack or become separated from their labels.

**Q133: Can a customer return all leftover tiles?**
A: Return rules depend on Tiletoria’s policy, product condition and whether the stock is a standard or special-order item. Customers should not assume that all surplus tiles can be returned.

**Q134: Should customers avoid ordering too little to save money?**
A: Ordering too little can create a costly problem if the same batch is unavailable later. It is usually safer to buy the correct quantity, including waste and spares, before installation begins.

**Q288: What if several tiles need replacing?**
A: If several tiles need replacing and no exact match is available, the customer may need to replace a larger section or create a planned feature or transition.

**Q289: Can grout colour help replacement tiles blend?**
A: Grout colour can help the repair look more integrated, but it cannot guarantee a match if the tile shade or calibre is different.

**Q290: Can a repair be done with a different batch?**
A: A repair can sometimes be done with a different batch, but the customer must accept possible visible differences. The tile should be compared before installation.

**Q291: Can a discontinued tile be sourced again?**
A: Sometimes discontinued stock may still be available, but it cannot be guaranteed. Tiletoria can check stock, but the customer should expect that exact matching may not be possible.

**Q292: Can a similar tile be used if the original is unavailable?**
A: Yes. A similar tile can be used, but it should be treated as a design decision rather than an exact match. The customer should approve the visual difference before installation.

**Q293: Should customers keep invoices for future matching?**
A: Yes. Invoices and box labels can help identify product code, size, batch and purchase date if future matching is needed.

**Q294: Can batch matching matter for insurance repairs?**
A: Yes. Insurance repairs often require matching existing materials, but exact tile matching may be impossible if the original batch or product is unavailable.

**Q295: What should a chatbot say about insurance tile matching?**
A: The chatbot should explain that Tiletoria can assist with identification and closest available options, but exact matching depends on stock, batch and product availability.

**Q296: Should matching be done from photos only?**
A: No. Photos can help identify a tile, but physical comparison is more reliable because colour, finish and texture may appear differently on screens.

**Q297: Can tile thickness affect replacement?**
A: Yes. A replacement tile with a different thickness can create height differences. The installer must check thickness and plan the adhesive bed and transitions.

**Q298: Can calibre affect replacement?**
A: Yes. A different calibre may affect grout joint alignment and tile size matching. The replacement should be checked before fixing.

**Q299: Can wear affect replacement matching?**
A: Yes. Existing tiles may have surface wear, cleaning residue or sunlight exposure, while new tiles look fresh. This can make even original spares appear slightly different.

**Q300: When should matching questions be escalated?**
A: Escalate matching questions when the customer has an old installation, insurance repair, discontinued product, no box label, or a request for an exact match.
