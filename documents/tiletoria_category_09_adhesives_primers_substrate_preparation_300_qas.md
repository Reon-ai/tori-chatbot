## Adhesive Basics for Tile Installations

Summary: Tile adhesive bonds the tile to a prepared substrate, but the correct adhesive depends on tile type, substrate, environment and manufacturer instructions.

Q001: What is tile adhesive?
A: Tile adhesive is the bonding material used to fix tiles to a suitable substrate. It must be chosen according to the tile type, tile size, substrate, indoor or outdoor use, wet or dry conditions, and the adhesive manufacturer’s instructions.

Q002: Is tile adhesive the same as cement?
A: No. Tile adhesive is not ordinary cement. Tile adhesive is a formulated product designed to bond tiles to specific substrates under defined conditions. Ordinary cement or sand-cement mixes should not be used as a substitute for a suitable tile adhesive.

Q003: Why does the correct tile adhesive matter?
A: The correct tile adhesive helps achieve proper bond strength, coverage and long-term performance. The wrong adhesive can contribute to loose tiles, hollow areas, cracked grout, tile movement or installation failure.

Q004: Can one adhesive be used for every tile job?
A: No. Adhesives differ by chemistry, flexibility, strength, setting time and application suitability. Porcelain tiles, large-format tiles, wet areas, external areas and difficult substrates may require different adhesive systems.

Q005: Who should choose the final adhesive for a project?
A: The final adhesive selection should be confirmed by the installer, adhesive manufacturer guidance and the specific site conditions. Tiletoria can assist with general product guidance, but the installer must confirm suitability before installation.

Q006: What information is needed to choose adhesive?
A: The key information is tile type, tile size, tile thickness, surface finish, floor or wall application, substrate type, indoor or outdoor location, wet or dry conditions, expected traffic and any underfloor heating or movement risk.

Q007: Does porcelain need a special adhesive?
A: Porcelain is dense and has very low water absorption, so it usually requires a suitable cement-based or polymer-modified adhesive designed for porcelain. The exact product must be confirmed from the adhesive manufacturer’s instructions.

Q008: Does ceramic tile need the same adhesive as porcelain?
A: Not always. Some ceramic tiles can be installed with standard cement-based adhesives on suitable substrates, while dense porcelain, large-format tiles or wet areas may need a higher-performance adhesive.

Q009: Can adhesive compensate for a bad substrate?
A: No. Adhesive is not a substitute for proper substrate preparation. The surface must be sound, stable, clean, dry where required, correctly primed where needed and flat enough for the selected tile.

Q010: Can adhesive fix an uneven floor?
A: Tile adhesive should not be used as a general floor-levelling product. Uneven floors should normally be corrected with appropriate screed repair, self-levelling compound or surface preparation before tiling.

Q011: What is a cement-based tile adhesive?
A: A cement-based tile adhesive is a powdered adhesive made with cement, fillers and performance additives. It is mixed with clean water or a specified liquid additive according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

Q012: What is a polymer-modified tile adhesive?
A: A polymer-modified tile adhesive contains additives that improve properties such as bond strength, flexibility, water resistance or workability. It is often used for porcelain, large-format tiles, wet areas or more demanding installations.

Q013: What is ready-mixed tile adhesive?
A: Ready-mixed tile adhesive is a premixed paste adhesive used only for certain light-duty wall applications where permitted by the manufacturer. It is generally not suitable for porcelain floors, wet shower floors, outdoor areas or large-format floor tiles.

Q014: Can ready-mixed adhesive be used in showers?
A: Ready-mixed adhesive should not be assumed suitable for showers. Wet areas require a system confirmed by the adhesive and waterproofing manufacturers, and many premixed adhesives are not suitable for continuously wet or high-moisture conditions.

Q015: Can ready-mixed adhesive be used for floors?
A: Ready-mixed adhesive is generally not recommended for floor tiling unless the manufacturer specifically approves that use. Cement-based or other suitable setting adhesives are normally used for floor tiles.

## Adhesive Standards and Product Classification

Summary: Adhesive standards and classifications help describe performance, but the classification does not replace project-specific suitability checks.

Q016: What does EN 12004 mean for tile adhesives?
A: EN 12004 is a European standard used to classify tile adhesives by type and performance. It helps describe adhesive properties, but the correct adhesive still depends on the tile, substrate, environment and manufacturer instructions.

Q017: What does a C adhesive classification mean?
A: In EN 12004 terminology, C usually refers to a cementitious adhesive. A cementitious adhesive is supplied as a powder and mixed according to the manufacturer’s instructions before use.

Q018: What does C1 mean on tile adhesive?
A: C1 indicates a normal-setting cementitious adhesive under EN 12004 classification. It may be suitable for basic applications, but it is not automatically suitable for porcelain, large-format tiles, wet areas or outdoor installations.

Q019: What does C2 mean on tile adhesive?
A: C2 indicates an improved cementitious adhesive with higher performance than C1 under EN 12004 classification. It is commonly considered for more demanding tiles or substrates, but the manufacturer’s instructions must still be followed.

Q020: What does S1 mean on tile adhesive?
A: S1 indicates deformability or flexibility under EN 12004-related classification. It is often relevant where limited movement is expected, but it does not remove the need for movement joints or correct substrate preparation.

Q021: What does S2 mean on tile adhesive?
A: S2 indicates higher deformability than S1. It may be used in more demanding movement conditions when specified, but the whole installation system must still be designed correctly.

Q022: What does T mean on adhesive packaging?
A: T generally indicates reduced slip or improved vertical hold. This is useful for wall tiling, but it does not mean the adhesive is suitable for every wall tile or substrate.

Q023: What does E mean on adhesive packaging?
A: E generally indicates extended open time. Extended open time may help in certain conditions, but installers must still work within the adhesive manufacturer’s stated limits.

Q024: What does F mean on adhesive packaging?
A: F generally indicates fast-setting performance. Fast-setting adhesives can be useful where faster return to service is needed, but they require careful planning because working time may be shorter.

Q025: What is ANSI A118?
A: ANSI A118 is a group of American National Standards Institute material standards covering tile installation products such as mortars, adhesives and grouts. It is commonly referenced in international technical guidance.

Q026: What is ANSI A108?
A: ANSI A108 is a group of American National Standards Institute installation standards for ceramic tile. It covers workmanship and installation practices, including substrate and mortar considerations.

Q027: Should South African customers rely only on EN or ANSI classifications?
A: No. EN and ANSI classifications are useful references, but South African customers must also follow local building requirements, adhesive manufacturer instructions, product data sheets and site-specific professional guidance.

Q028: Does a C2 adhesive guarantee a successful tile installation?
A: No. A C2 adhesive can provide improved performance, but the installation can still fail if the substrate is unsuitable, coverage is poor, water exposure is unmanaged, movement joints are missing or the product is used incorrectly.

Q029: Does flexible adhesive remove the need for expansion joints?
A: No. Flexible adhesive does not replace movement joints. Movement joints are still required where specified by standards, product guidance, site conditions and good tiling practice.

Q030: Does an adhesive standard prove it is suitable for external use?
A: Not by itself. External suitability depends on the full product data sheet, substrate, climate exposure, tile type, coverage, drainage, movement joints and correct installation.

## Substrate Preparation Principles

Summary: A suitable substrate must be sound, stable, clean, correctly cured and flat enough before tile adhesive is applied.

Q031: What is a substrate in tiling?
A: The substrate is the surface that the tile is fixed onto, such as a concrete slab, screed, wall plaster, cement board, waterproofing layer or existing tile surface.

Q032: Why is substrate preparation important?
A: Substrate preparation is critical because the adhesive can only perform properly when the surface is sound, stable, clean and suitable for the selected tile system.

Q033: What does a sound substrate mean?
A: A sound substrate is firm, stable and free from loose, crumbling, hollow, weak or delaminating material. Unsound areas must be repaired before tiling.

Q034: What does a clean substrate mean?
A: A clean substrate is free from dust, oil, grease, paint, curing compounds, laitance, old adhesive residue and anything else that may interfere with adhesive bond.

Q035: What does a stable substrate mean?
A: A stable substrate is not moving, flexing, shrinking excessively or cracking in a way that can transfer stress to the tile installation.

Q036: Can tiles be installed over dust?
A: No. Dust can prevent adhesive from bonding properly to the substrate. The surface must be cleaned and prepared before adhesive is applied.

Q037: Can tiles be installed over oil or grease?
A: No. Oil or grease can prevent proper bonding. Contaminated surfaces must be professionally cleaned or prepared before tiling.

Q038: Can tiles be installed over paint?
A: Tiling over paint is risky unless the paint is firmly bonded and the adhesive manufacturer permits it. Glossy, peeling, soft or water-sensitive paint should be removed or mechanically prepared before tiling.

Q039: Can tiles be installed over old adhesive?
A: Old adhesive must be assessed. Loose, soft, dusty or incompatible adhesive should be removed. Remaining residue must be stable, clean, flat and approved by the new adhesive manufacturer.

Q040: Can tiles be installed over a cracked screed?
A: A cracked screed must be assessed before tiling. Active movement, structural cracks or moisture problems can cause tile failure, and a suitable repair or movement-control system may be needed.

Q041: Can tiles be installed directly onto a new screed?
A: A new screed must cure and dry sufficiently before tiling. The required waiting period depends on screed type, thickness, moisture, conditions and adhesive manufacturer guidance.

Q042: Can tiles be installed over a weak screed?
A: No. A weak or friable screed can fail below the adhesive layer. The screed must be repaired, strengthened or replaced before tiling.

Q043: What is laitance?
A: Laitance is a weak, dusty or powdery layer that can form on the surface of concrete or screed. It must be removed because it can prevent proper adhesive bonding.

Q044: How is laitance removed?
A: Laitance is usually removed by mechanical preparation such as grinding, sanding or other approved methods. The correct method depends on the site and the substrate condition.

Q045: Can primer solve a poor substrate?
A: No. Primer can improve bonding or control absorption on suitable substrates, but it cannot make a weak, moving, contaminated or badly cracked substrate acceptable.

## Primer Selection and Use

Summary: Primers are used to prepare certain substrates, control absorption or improve bonding, but they must match the adhesive and substrate system.

Q046: What is tile primer?
A: Tile primer is a preparation product applied to certain substrates before adhesive, levelling compound or waterproofing. It can help control absorption, bind light surface dust or improve bond when used correctly.

Q047: Is primer always needed before tiling?
A: No. Primer is needed only when the substrate, adhesive or levelling product requires it. The product data sheet should be followed.

Q048: Can primer be skipped if the surface looks clean?
A: Primer should not be skipped where the adhesive or substrate preparation system requires it. A surface can look clean but still be too porous, dusty or unsuitable without the correct preparation.

Q049: Can too much primer cause problems?
A: Yes. Over-application, pooling or incorrect dilution can create a weak layer that affects bonding. Primer must be applied according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

Q050: Can primer make tiles stick to any surface?
A: No. Primer is not a universal bonding solution. The substrate must still be suitable, stable, clean and compatible with the adhesive system.

Q051: What is a porous substrate?
A: A porous substrate absorbs water quickly, such as some cement screeds, plaster or cement-based surfaces. Porous substrates may need primer to control suction before adhesive or levelling compound is applied.

Q052: What is a non-porous substrate?
A: A non-porous substrate absorbs little or no water, such as existing glazed tiles or dense concrete. Non-porous substrates may need a special bonding primer or mechanical preparation.

Q053: Can the same primer be used on porous and non-porous substrates?
A: Not always. Some primers are designed for porous surfaces and others for dense or non-porous surfaces. The manufacturer’s instructions must be followed.

Q054: Should primer be used before self-levelling compound?
A: Yes, primer is commonly required before self-levelling compound to control suction and improve bond. The exact primer, dilution and drying time must follow the levelling compound manufacturer’s instructions.

Q055: Should primer be used before waterproofing?
A: Primer may be required before waterproofing depending on the waterproofing system and substrate. The waterproofing manufacturer’s instructions must be followed.

Q056: Can primer be applied to a wet substrate?
A: Primer should not be applied to a wet substrate unless the specific product allows it. Most systems require a clean and suitably dry surface.

Q057: How long must primer dry before tiling?
A: Drying time depends on the primer, substrate, temperature, humidity and ventilation. The product data sheet must be followed before applying adhesive, waterproofing or levelling compound.

Q058: Can tiles be fixed directly onto fresh primer?
A: Tiles should not be fixed until the primer has dried or cured as required by the manufacturer. Installing too early can reduce bond performance.

Q059: Can primer replace waterproofing in a shower?
A: No. Primer is not waterproofing. Showers and wet areas require a proper waterproofing system where specified, not only a primer.

Q060: Can primer replace adhesive?
A: No. Primer prepares the substrate. Adhesive bonds the tile to the substrate. They are separate products with different functions.

## Moisture, Curing and Screed Readiness

Summary: Moisture and curing conditions must be assessed because excess moisture, shrinkage or uncured screeds can damage tile installations.

Q061: Why does screed moisture matter before tiling?
A: Excess moisture can affect adhesive curing, cause debonding, contribute to efflorescence or create moisture-related failures. The screed must be sufficiently cured and dry for the selected adhesive system.

Q062: How long should a screed cure before tiling?
A: Curing time depends on screed type, thickness, mix design, site conditions and adhesive manufacturer guidance. The installer must confirm readiness instead of relying only on a fixed number of days.

Q063: Can tiles be installed on a damp screed?
A: Tiles should not be installed on a damp screed unless the adhesive system is specifically approved for that condition. Moisture levels should be checked before tiling.

Q064: What is residual moisture?
A: Residual moisture is moisture remaining inside a slab or screed after placement. It can continue moving through the substrate and affect adhesives, grout, flooring or finishes.

Q065: Can moisture cause tiles to lift?
A: Moisture can contribute to lifting or debonding when combined with poor substrate preparation, unsuitable adhesive, lack of movement joints or trapped water. The cause must be assessed on site.

Q066: Can moisture cause efflorescence?
A: Yes. Moisture moving through cement-based materials can carry salts to the surface, causing white deposits called efflorescence. The source of moisture should be addressed.

Q067: What is efflorescence?
A: Efflorescence is a white or pale mineral deposit that can appear when moisture carries soluble salts to the surface of cement-based materials. It is often linked to moisture movement.

Q068: Can tile adhesive stop rising damp?
A: No. Tile adhesive is not a damp-proofing system. Rising damp or moisture ingress must be treated with the correct building or waterproofing solution before tiling.

Q069: Can tiles be installed over a waterproofing membrane that is still wet?
A: No. Waterproofing membranes must dry or cure as required by the manufacturer before adhesive and tiles are applied.

Q070: Can adhesive cure properly if trapped under very dense porcelain?
A: Dense porcelain absorbs very little water, so adhesive selection, bed thickness, coverage, open time and curing conditions are important. The correct adhesive and curing time must follow the manufacturer’s instructions.

Q071: Can fast-setting adhesive solve a moisture problem?
A: No. Fast-setting adhesive does not solve substrate moisture, rising damp or waterproofing problems. The moisture source must be assessed and treated properly.

Q072: Should moisture be tested before tiling?
A: Moisture testing is recommended where there is any doubt, especially on new screeds, slabs, ground floors, wet areas, basements, balconies or installations sensitive to moisture.

Q073: Who should test substrate moisture?
A: A qualified installer, flooring specialist or relevant professional should test moisture using an appropriate method for the substrate and flooring system.

Q074: Can visual inspection confirm a screed is dry?
A: No. A screed can look dry on the surface while still containing moisture inside. Testing may be needed where moisture risk exists.

Q075: Why do new screeds shrink?
A: New cement-based screeds shrink as they cure and dry. If tiling is done too early or without movement control, shrinkage stress can contribute to cracking or debonding.

## Adhesive Coverage and Trowel Technique

Summary: Correct adhesive coverage and trowel technique are essential to support tiles and reduce hollow spots, cracks and debonding.

Q076: What is adhesive coverage?
A: Adhesive coverage refers to how much of the back of the tile is properly supported by adhesive after the tile is bedded into place.

Q077: Why is adhesive coverage important?
A: Good adhesive coverage supports the tile, improves bond and reduces voids. Poor coverage can lead to hollow sounds, cracked tiles, broken corners, water pockets or debonding.

Q078: What adhesive coverage is generally expected for dry internal areas?
A: Recognised tile installation guidance commonly references a minimum of about 80% mortar contact for dry internal areas, with full support at edges and corners. The installer must follow the relevant standard and manufacturer instructions.

Q079: What adhesive coverage is generally expected for wet or external areas?
A: Wet areas, exterior areas and demanding installations commonly require about 95% mortar contact, with full support at edges and corners. The exact requirement should follow the applicable standard and product guidance.

Q080: Does a tile need adhesive under the corners?
A: Yes. Tile corners and edges need support. Unsupported corners are more vulnerable to cracking or chipping under load or impact.

Q081: What is back-buttering?
A: Back-buttering means applying a thin layer of adhesive to the back of the tile before placing it into the combed adhesive bed. It is often used to improve contact, especially with large-format or textured-back tiles.

Q082: Is back-buttering always required?
A: Back-buttering is often recommended for large-format tiles, exterior installations and tiles with uneven backs, but the installer must confirm the method required for the specific tile and adhesive.

Q083: What is a notched trowel?
A: A notched trowel is a tool with shaped notches used to spread adhesive at a controlled thickness. The notch size and shape affect adhesive bed depth and coverage.

Q084: How is the correct trowel size chosen?
A: The correct trowel size depends on tile size, tile back profile, substrate flatness and required coverage. The installer should check coverage by lifting tiles during installation.

Q085: Can a small trowel be used for large tiles?
A: A small trowel may not provide enough adhesive for large-format tiles. Large tiles often need a larger notch and careful bedding to achieve proper coverage.

Q086: What does combing adhesive mean?
A: Combing adhesive means spreading adhesive with the notched side of the trowel to create straight, even ridges that allow the tile to be bedded into the adhesive.

Q087: Should adhesive ridges be swirled?
A: Straight-line trowelling is generally preferred because it helps air escape when the tile is bedded. Swirled ridges can trap voids and reduce coverage.

Q088: Should tiles be twisted into adhesive?
A: Tiles are usually moved perpendicular to the trowel ridges to collapse the ridges and improve contact. The installer should follow accepted installation practice and product guidance.

Q089: What is spot bonding?
A: Spot bonding is applying adhesive in isolated blobs instead of a continuous bed. It is generally not acceptable for proper tile installation because it creates voids and weak support.

Q090: Why is spot bonding risky?
A: Spot bonding leaves large unsupported voids behind the tile, which can cause hollow sounds, cracked tiles, water pockets, impact damage and poor long-term performance.

## Open Time, Pot Life and Curing Time

Summary: Adhesives have working-time limits that must be followed to avoid weak bonding and premature installation failure.

Q091: What is adhesive open time?
A: Open time is the period after adhesive is spread onto the substrate during which tiles can still be placed and achieve proper bond. It is affected by temperature, wind, humidity and substrate absorption.

Q092: What is adhesive pot life?
A: Pot life is the time the mixed adhesive remains usable in the bucket. Once pot life expires, the adhesive should not be used.

Q093: What is working time?
A: Working time is the practical time available to mix, spread, adjust and bed tiles before the adhesive becomes unsuitable for use.

Q094: Can water be added to old adhesive to make it usable again?
A: No. Re-tempering adhesive with extra water after it has started setting can weaken performance and should not be done unless the product instructions specifically allow remixing without added water.

Q095: Why does adhesive skin over?
A: Adhesive skins over when the exposed surface starts drying before the tile is placed. Heat, wind, low humidity, direct sun and absorbent substrates can shorten open time.

Q096: How can installers avoid skinning?
A: Installers should spread only as much adhesive as can be tiled within the open time, avoid harsh conditions where possible and check adhesive transfer regularly.

Q097: Can tiles be adjusted after the adhesive starts setting?
A: Adjustment is only possible within the adhesive’s stated adjustment time. Moving tiles too late can break the bond or disturb the adhesive bed.

Q098: What is setting time?
A: Setting time is the period required for adhesive to harden sufficiently. It varies by adhesive type, temperature, humidity, bed thickness and tile porosity.

Q099: When can a tiled floor be walked on?
A: The waiting time before foot traffic depends on the adhesive, tile, substrate and site conditions. The adhesive manufacturer’s instructions should be followed.

Q100: When can grout be applied after tiling?
A: Grouting time depends on adhesive type, tile size, tile porosity, bed thickness and conditions. The adhesive manufacturer’s instructions should be followed before grouting.

Q101: Can grout be applied too soon?
A: Yes. Grouting too soon can trap moisture, disturb tiles or affect curing. The installer should wait until the adhesive has set sufficiently.

Q102: Can a floor be loaded before adhesive cures?
A: No. Heavy loads should not be placed on tiled floors before the adhesive has cured sufficiently, because movement or pressure can weaken the bond.

Q103: Does cold weather slow adhesive curing?
A: Yes. Cold conditions can slow setting and curing. The installer should follow the product’s temperature limits and curing guidance.

Q104: Does hot weather affect tile adhesive?
A: Yes. Hot weather, wind and direct sun can shorten open time and increase the risk of skinning. Installation conditions must be managed carefully.

Q105: Does humidity affect adhesive drying?
A: Yes. Humidity can affect drying and curing, especially for certain adhesive types and dense tiles. Manufacturer guidance should be followed.

## Porcelain, Ceramic and Dense Tile Adhesive Selection

Summary: Dense porcelain and low-absorption tiles usually need higher-performance adhesive systems than some standard ceramic wall or floor tiles.

Q106: Why does porcelain need careful adhesive selection?
A: Porcelain has very low water absorption, so the adhesive must bond properly to a dense tile body. A suitable porcelain-rated or polymer-modified adhesive is often required.

Q107: Can standard ceramic adhesive be used for porcelain?
A: Standard ceramic adhesive should not be assumed suitable for porcelain. The adhesive must be approved for porcelain by the manufacturer.

Q108: What adhesive is used for ceramic wall tiles?
A: Ceramic wall tiles may be installed with an adhesive approved for the tile, substrate and location. Light-duty wall applications may allow different products than floors or wet areas.

Q109: What adhesive is used for porcelain floor tiles?
A: Porcelain floor tiles generally require a cement-based adhesive suitable for porcelain, the tile size and the substrate. Large formats or wet/external areas may require improved or flexible products.

Q110: Can porcelain be installed with ready-mixed adhesive?
A: Porcelain should not be installed with ready-mixed adhesive unless the manufacturer specifically approves that exact use. Dense porcelain can prevent some premixed adhesives from drying correctly.

Q111: Does glazed porcelain need different adhesive from unglazed porcelain?
A: The tile back, tile size, water absorption and application matter more than the decorative face. The adhesive must be suitable for the specific porcelain product and installation conditions.

Q112: Does tile thickness affect adhesive choice?
A: Tile thickness can affect weight, bed requirements and installation handling. The adhesive and method must be suitable for the tile weight, size and application.

Q113: Do heavy wall tiles need special adhesive?
A: Heavy wall tiles may need a high-grab or non-slip cement-based adhesive and a suitable wall substrate. The installer must confirm the wall can carry the tile load.

Q114: Can the same adhesive be used for wall and floor porcelain?
A: Sometimes, but not always. The adhesive must be approved for the tile, substrate and application. Wall installations may need non-slip properties, while floors need coverage and load support.

Q115: What adhesive is best for vitrified tiles?
A: Vitrified tiles are dense and generally require an adhesive suitable for low-absorption tiles. The exact adhesive should be confirmed against the tile and adhesive manufacturer’s guidance.

Q116: What adhesive is best for rectified porcelain?
A: Rectified porcelain usually needs a suitable porcelain adhesive and careful installation because grout joints may be narrower and lippage control is important. Substrate flatness remains critical.

Q117: What adhesive is best for polished porcelain?
A: Polished porcelain needs an adhesive suitable for dense porcelain. Light-coloured or stain-sensitive products may need additional caution, including correct adhesive colour and cleaning procedures.

Q118: Can dark adhesive stain light tiles?
A: Some light, translucent or porous materials can be affected by adhesive colour or moisture. For standard glazed porcelain this is less common, but the manufacturer’s guidance should be checked.

Q119: Does ceramic absorb water from adhesive?
A: Some ceramic tiles are more absorbent than porcelain, but adhesive selection still depends on the exact tile, substrate and location. Overly absorbent substrates or tiles may affect open time.

Q120: Can adhesive bond to a very smooth tile back?
A: Very smooth or dense tile backs need an adhesive approved for that tile type and proper installation technique. Back-buttering may be needed to improve contact.

## Large-Format Tile Adhesive and Substrate Requirements

Summary: Large-format tiles require flatter substrates, careful adhesive selection, strong coverage and skilled installation to reduce lippage and voids.

Q121: Why do large-format tiles need special preparation?
A: Large-format tiles cover more area with fewer joints, so substrate flatness, adhesive coverage and lippage control become more critical than with smaller tiles.

Q122: What is considered a large-format tile?
A: Large-format tile is commonly understood as tile with at least one side greater than about 15 inches or 380 millimetres, although local suppliers may use the term for formats such as 600x1200, 800x800 or 1200x1200 millimetres.

Q123: Can large-format tiles be installed on an uneven floor?
A: Large-format tiles should not be installed on an uneven floor without correction. The substrate must be flat enough for the tile size to avoid lippage, poor coverage and stress.

Q124: Does large-format tile need a flexible adhesive?
A: Large-format tiles often need an improved or deformable adhesive, especially where movement, dense porcelain, heated floors or demanding conditions are present. The exact adhesive must be confirmed from product guidance.

Q125: Does large-format tile require back-buttering?
A: Back-buttering is commonly recommended for large-format tiles to help improve adhesive transfer and coverage. The installer should confirm the required method for the tile and adhesive.

Q126: Can large-format tile be spot bonded?
A: No. Spot bonding is especially risky for large-format tiles because it leaves unsupported voids that can cause cracking, hollow sounds or failure.

Q127: What trowel is used for large-format tile?
A: Large-format tiles usually require a larger notched trowel than small tiles, but the correct trowel depends on tile size, tile back profile, substrate flatness and required coverage.

Q128: Can adhesive be built up thick under large-format tiles?
A: Adhesive should only be used within the manufacturer’s approved bed thickness. Significant height correction should be done by proper substrate preparation, not by excessive adhesive build-up.

Q129: Why do large-format tiles sound hollow when coverage is poor?
A: Large voids under large-format tiles can create hollow sounds and weak support. Proper adhesive coverage and bedding technique are essential.

Q130: Does a levelling clip system replace proper adhesive coverage?
A: No. Levelling clip systems can assist with edge alignment, but they do not replace substrate preparation, correct adhesive, full coverage or skilled installation.

Q131: Can large-format tiles be installed by a DIY customer?
A: Large-format tiles are less forgiving and are usually best installed by an experienced tile installer. Customers should consult Tiletoria and a qualified installer before starting.

Q132: Why is lippage more visible with large-format tiles?
A: Large tiles span high and low spots more easily, and narrow grout joints make edge differences more noticeable. Flatness and layout planning are very important.

Q133: Can large-format tiles be used on walls?
A: Yes, if the wall structure, substrate, adhesive and fixing method are suitable for the tile weight and size. The installer must confirm wall load and preparation requirements.

Q134: Do large-format wall tiles need temporary support?
A: Large or heavy wall tiles may need support while the adhesive sets. The installer should follow the adhesive manufacturer’s guidance and safe installation practice.

Q135: Can large-format tiles be used in showers?
A: Yes, but shower floors require falls, drainage, wet-area suitability, waterproofing and high adhesive coverage. Large tiles may be difficult on shower floors with multiple slopes.

## Wet Areas, Showers and Waterproofed Substrates

Summary: Wet areas require compatible adhesive, proper waterproofing, correct falls and high adhesive coverage; adhesive and grout are not waterproofing systems.

Q136: Is tile adhesive waterproof?
A: Tile adhesive should not be treated as waterproofing. Some adhesives are water-resistant when cured, but wet areas still require correct waterproofing where specified.

Q137: Is grout waterproof?
A: Standard grout is not a waterproofing system. Water can pass through grout joints, so wet areas require properly designed waterproofing behind or beneath the tile system.

Q138: Can tiles be installed directly in a shower without waterproofing?
A: No. Showers require a suitable waterproofing or tanking system according to local requirements and manufacturer instructions. Tiles and grout alone should not be relied on to stop water.

Q139: What adhesive is used over waterproofing?
A: The adhesive must be compatible with the specific waterproofing membrane and tile. The waterproofing and adhesive manufacturers’ instructions should both be followed.

Q140: Can any adhesive bond to waterproofing?
A: No. Waterproofing membranes can have different surfaces and curing requirements. Only compatible adhesives should be used.

Q141: How long must waterproofing dry before tiling?
A: Waterproofing must dry or cure for the time stated by the manufacturer before adhesive is applied. Tiling too early can compromise the system.

Q142: What adhesive coverage is needed in showers?
A: Wet areas commonly require very high adhesive contact, often about 95%, with full support at edges and corners. The installer must follow the applicable standard and product guidance.

Q143: Can ready-mixed adhesive be used on shower floors?
A: Ready-mixed adhesive should not be used on shower floors unless the manufacturer specifically approves it for that exact use. Cement-based systems are generally more common for wet floors.

Q144: Can large-format tiles be used on shower walls?
A: Yes, if the waterproofing, wall substrate, adhesive and installation method are suitable for the tile weight and size. The installer must confirm compatibility.

Q145: Can large-format tiles be used on shower floors?
A: Large-format tiles can be difficult on shower floors because the floor must fall toward the drain. Smaller tiles or mosaics are often easier to fit to multiple slopes.

Q146: Does adhesive choice affect shower durability?
A: Yes. The adhesive must handle wet-area conditions, tile type and substrate. Incorrect adhesive can contribute to debonding, moisture problems or failure.

Q147: Can bathroom walls use ordinary adhesive?

Q148: Can adhesive be used to create shower falls?

Q149: Can water trapped under tiles cause problems?
A: Yes. Voids under tiles in wet areas can trap water and contribute to staining, efflorescence, odours, freeze-thaw risk in some climates or bond issues.

Q150: Can tile adhesive repair a leaking shower?

Summary: Exterior tile installations require exterior-rated materials, correct drainage, movement accommodation and high adhesive coverage.

Q151: Can indoor adhesive be used outside?
A: Indoor-only adhesive should not be used outside. Exterior installations require adhesive approved for outdoor exposure, the selected tile, the substrate and the site conditions.

Q152: Why are exterior tile installations more demanding?

Q153: What adhesive coverage is needed outside?

Q154: Can porcelain tiles be installed outside?

Q155: Can adhesive be used to correct balcony falls?

Q156: Why does drainage matter for exterior adhesive?
A: Poor drainage allows water to pond or penetrate joints, increasing the risk of moisture-related failure, staining, efflorescence and slip hazards.

Q157: Can tiles be installed over an exterior waterproofing membrane?
A: Yes, only when the waterproofing membrane is designed to receive tiles and the adhesive is compatible with it. The full system must be confirmed by the manufacturers.

Q158: Can ordinary grout protect an exterior installation from water?
A: No. Grout is not a waterproofing system. Exterior installations need proper falls, waterproofing where required, compatible adhesive and correctly designed movement joints.

Q159: Why are movement joints important outside?
A: Exterior tiles expand and contract with temperature changes. Movement joints help manage stress and reduce the risk of cracking, tenting or debonding.

Q160: Can flexible adhesive replace movement joints outside?

Q161: Can tiles be installed outside in direct sun?
A: Direct sun can shorten adhesive open time and affect curing. Installers must manage site conditions and follow temperature limits in the product instructions.

Q162: Can tiles be installed outside in rain?
A: Tiling in rain or onto wet substrates is generally not acceptable unless the product system specifically permits it. Adhesive and substrate conditions must be controlled.

Q163: Can exterior tile adhesive fail from water behind tiles?
A: Yes. Water trapped behind tiles can contribute to debonding, efflorescence and freeze-thaw stress in climates where freezing occurs. Proper coverage, falls and waterproofing are important.

Q164: Are ramps high-risk tile areas?

Q165: Can the chatbot recommend adhesive for a public ramp?

Q168: How do you check if existing tiles are firmly bonded?

Q169: Can tiles be installed over glossy old tiles?

Q170: Can primer make tiling over old tiles safe?

Q171: Can tiles be installed over vinyl flooring?

Q172: Can tiles be installed over laminate flooring?

Q174: Can tiles be installed over plywood?

Q175: Can tiles be installed over gypsum plaster?

Q176: Can tiles be installed over drywall?

Q177: Can tiles be installed over fibre-cement board?
A: Fibre-cement board can be a suitable substrate in certain systems if installed correctly, stable, clean and compatible with the adhesive and waterproofing system.

Q178: Can tiles be installed over metal?

Q179: Can tiles be installed over glass?

Q180: Can tiles be installed over painted walls?
A: Only if the paint is firmly bonded and the adhesive manufacturer permits it. Loose, glossy, soft, peeling or water-sensitive paint must be removed or prepared properly.

## Floor Levelling, Flatness and Surface Correction

Summary: Surface correction should be done before tiling because adhesive is not intended to replace proper levelling or flattening systems.

Q181: What is self-levelling compound?
A: Self-levelling compound is a floor preparation product used to create a smoother, flatter surface before flooring or tiling. It must be selected and installed according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

Q182: Is self-levelling compound the same as tile adhesive?
A: No. Self-levelling compound prepares the floor. Tile adhesive bonds the tile. They are different products with different functions.

Q183: Can tile adhesive be used instead of self-levelling compound?
A: Tile adhesive should not be used as a substitute for self-levelling compound except within the manufacturer’s permitted bed thickness and use. Major correction should be done before tiling.

Q184: Why must floors be flat before tiling?
A: Flat floors help achieve proper adhesive coverage, reduce lippage and improve the finished appearance. Flatness becomes more critical as tile size increases.

Q185: Can a floor be flat but not level?
A: Yes. A floor can be flat but sloped, such as a shower floor or balcony. Flatness relates to evenness, while level relates to horizontal position.

Q186: Should patios and balconies be level?
A: Patios and balconies often need falls for drainage and should not be perfectly level if water must drain away. The surface must be correctly sloped and flat enough for tiling.

Q187: What causes lippage from substrate problems?
A: High and low spots in the substrate can force tiles to sit at different heights. This is especially visible with large-format or rectified tiles.

Q188: Can levelling clips fix an uneven floor?
A: No. Levelling clips can help align tile edges during installation, but they cannot correct a substrate that is too uneven or unsuitable.

Q189: When should a floor be levelled before tiling?
A: A floor should be levelled or flattened when it has high spots, low spots, waves or irregularities that would prevent proper adhesive coverage or acceptable tile alignment.

Q190: Who determines whether a floor needs levelling?
A: The installer should assess the floor using appropriate tools such as a straightedge and decide whether surface correction is required before tiling.

Q191: Can a wall need flattening before tiling?
A: Yes. Walls may need flattening before large-format or rectified tiles are installed because uneven walls can cause lippage and poor contact.

Q192: Can plaster be used to flatten shower walls?
A: Wet areas require suitable boards, renders, waterproofing and adhesive systems. Ordinary plaster may not be suitable in wet zones unless the full system permits it.

Q193: Can self-levelling compound be used outside?
A: Only if the product is approved for exterior use. Many self-levelling compounds are for internal use only.

Q194: Can self-levelling compound be used in showers?
A: Only if the product and waterproofing system are approved for wet-area use. Shower falls must be correctly formed to the drain.

Q195: Does self-levelling compound need primer?
A: Most self-levelling compounds require primer. The correct primer, dilution and drying time must follow the manufacturer’s instructions.

## Movement, Cracks and Structural Considerations

Summary: Tile installations must accommodate movement because adhesive alone cannot prevent cracks caused by structural movement, shrinkage or thermal expansion.

Q196: Why do tile installations need movement joints?
A: Movement joints allow the tile installation to accommodate expansion, contraction and building movement. Without them, stress can cause cracking, tenting or debonding.

Q197: Can flexible adhesive replace movement joints?
A: No. Flexible adhesive can help in certain conditions but does not replace movement joints or proper installation design.

Q198: What is tile tenting?
A: Tenting is when tiles lift or buckle upward, often due to movement stress, lack of movement joints, poor bonding or substrate movement.

Q199: Can adhesive stop structural cracks?
A: No. Adhesive cannot stop structural movement or active cracks. Cracks must be assessed and treated with an appropriate repair or movement-control system.

Q200: Can tiles be installed over hairline cracks?
A: Hairline cracks must still be assessed. Some may be stable and treatable, while others may indicate active movement. The installer must decide the correct system.

Q201: What is a crack isolation membrane?
A: A crack isolation membrane is a system designed to reduce transfer of certain substrate cracks through to the tile surface. It must be used according to manufacturer instructions.

Q202: Does a crack isolation membrane fix structural movement?
A: No. Crack isolation membranes have limits and do not solve structural movement. Active or structural cracks need professional assessment.

Q203: Can tiles crack because of building movement?
A: Yes. Building movement, slab movement, screed shrinkage or thermal expansion can crack tiles or grout if not properly accommodated.

Q204: Can grout cracks indicate movement?
A: Yes. Cracked grout can indicate movement, poor adhesive coverage, weak substrate, incorrect grout, missing movement joints or installation issues.

Q205: Can adhesive cracks cause tile failure?
A: Adhesive cracking can occur when the substrate moves, coverage is poor, adhesive is misused or the product is unsuitable. The cause must be assessed.

Q206: Can expansion in sunny rooms affect tiles?
A: Yes. Areas with strong sun or temperature changes can experience expansion and contraction. Movement joints and suitable adhesive systems are important.

Q207: Are movement joints needed in doorways?
A: Movement joints may be needed at changes in plane, perimeters, transitions and other stress points. The installer must follow relevant standards and site requirements.

Q208: Are perimeter joints important?
A: Yes. Perimeter movement gaps help prevent tiles from being trapped against walls or fixed objects. These gaps are usually covered by skirting or trim.

Q209: Can tiles be hard grouted against walls?
A: Tiles should not usually be rigidly locked against perimeter walls. Movement accommodation is needed to reduce stress.

Q210: Can adhesive fill perimeter movement gaps?
A: No. Movement gaps should not be filled with tile adhesive or rigid grout. They require appropriate flexible joint treatment where specified.

Q212: What adhesive is used for wall tiles?

Q213: Can floor tiles be installed on walls?

Q214: Can large porcelain slabs be installed on walls?

Q215: What does non-slip adhesive mean for walls?

Q216: Can ready-mixed adhesive hold large wall tiles?

Q217: Can bathroom wall tiles be installed over plaster?

Q218: Can shower wall tiles be installed over ordinary drywall?

Q219: Can tiles be installed over painted bathroom walls?

Q220: Do wall tiles need full adhesive coverage?

Q221: Can adhesive dabs be used on wall tiles?

Q222: Can heavy tiles slide down the wall during installation?

Q223: Can adhesive be mixed thicker to stop wall tiles slipping?

Q224: Can tiles be installed on exterior walls?

Q225: Can tiles be installed over brick walls?

Q226: Can tiles be installed over underfloor heating?

Q227: Does underfloor heating require flexible adhesive?

Q228: Can heating be switched on immediately after tiling?

Q229: Why does underfloor heating affect tile installation?

Q230: Can any adhesive be used over heating mats?

Q231: Can self-levelling compound be used over heating wires?

Q232: Can underfloor heating cause tiles to crack?

Q233: Are movement joints needed with underfloor heating?
A: Yes. Movement joints are especially important with heated floors because temperature changes increase expansion and contraction.

Q234: Can tiles be installed over old heated screeds?

Q235: Can large-format tiles be used with underfloor heating?

Q236: Does grout need to be flexible with underfloor heating?

Q237: Can ready-mixed adhesive be used with underfloor heating?

Q238: Should heating be tested before tiling?

Q239: Can heating cables be damaged during tiling?

Q240: Can tile adhesive be used to bury heating cables?

## Grouting Readiness and Adhesive-Related Timing

Q241: When can tiles be grouted after adhesive?
A: Grouting time depends on the adhesive type, tile size, substrate, bed thickness and site conditions. The adhesive manufacturer’s instructions should be followed.

Q242: What happens if tiles are grouted too early?
A: Early grouting can trap moisture, disturb tiles, slow curing or contribute to colour and performance issues. The installer should wait until the adhesive is ready.

Q243: Can floor tiles move during grouting?
A: Yes. If the adhesive has not set enough, grouting can disturb the tiles and affect alignment or bond.

Q244: Does porcelain affect grouting time?
A: Dense porcelain absorbs very little moisture, so adhesive may need adequate time to cure before grouting. The product instructions should be followed.

Q245: Do large-format tiles need longer before grouting?
A: Large-format tiles may require careful curing time because of dense tile bodies and larger adhesive beds. The adhesive manufacturer’s instructions should be followed.

Q246: Can fast-setting adhesive be grouted sooner?
A: Fast-setting adhesive may allow earlier grouting if the manufacturer states so, but site conditions and tile format still matter.

Q247: Can grout hide poor adhesive coverage?
A: No. Grout cannot fix poor adhesive coverage or loose tiles. Coverage problems must be prevented during tile installation.

Q248: Can grout lock tiles in place if adhesive is weak?
A: No. Grout is not a replacement for adhesive bond. Tiles must be properly bonded before grouting.

Q249: Can a hollow tile be fixed by regrouting?
A: Regrouting may improve joint appearance, but it will not properly fix a tile that is loose or poorly bonded. The cause of the hollow sound should be assessed.

Q250: When can a tiled floor receive furniture?
A: Furniture should be placed only after adhesive and grout have cured sufficiently. Heavy loads too early can disturb the installation.

Q251: When can a bathroom be used after tiling?
A: Use of a bathroom depends on adhesive, grout, silicone and waterproofing cure times. The installer should follow all manufacturer instructions before exposing the area to water.

Q252: When can a shower be used after tiling?
A: A shower should not be used until adhesive, grout, silicone and waterproofing-related products have cured fully according to manufacturer instructions.

Q253: Does silicone timing matter after tiling?
A: Yes. Silicone should be applied at the correct stage and allowed to cure before water exposure. Manufacturer instructions must be followed.

Q254: Can tile adhesive affect grout colour?

Q255: Can adhesive squeeze into grout joints?

## Common Adhesive and Substrate Failures

Summary: Many tile failures are caused by unsuitable substrates, poor preparation, wrong adhesive, poor coverage, moisture or missing movement accommodation.

Q256: Why do tiles come loose?
A: Tiles can come loose because of poor substrate preparation, incorrect adhesive, poor adhesive coverage, movement, moisture, contamination, early traffic or installation errors.

Q257: Why do tiles sound hollow?
A: Hollow sounds may indicate voids, poor adhesive coverage, debonding or differences in substrate. The installation should be assessed if tiles are loose, cracked or grout is failing.

Q258: Why does grout crack after tiling?
A: Grout can crack because of movement, poor adhesive coverage, loose tiles, weak substrate, wrong grout, missing movement joints or early traffic.

Q259: Why do tiles crack after installation?
A: Tiles can crack due to substrate movement, insufficient adhesive coverage, impact, structural cracks, poor flatness, lack of movement joints or incorrect installation.

Q260: Why do tiles lift or tent?
A: Tile tenting can result from movement stress, lack of movement joints, poor bonding, thermal expansion, moisture or substrate movement.

Q261: Can adhesive failure be caused by dust?
A: Yes. Dust on the substrate can prevent adhesive from bonding properly and contribute to debonding.

Q262: Can adhesive failure be caused by too much water in the mix?
A: Yes. Adding too much water can weaken adhesive and affect setting, bond strength and performance.

Q263: Can adhesive failure be caused by too little water?
A: Yes. Too little water can reduce workability, coverage and proper hydration, resulting in poor bond.

Q264: Can expired adhesive cause failure?
A: Yes. Expired or moisture-damaged adhesive may not perform correctly and should not be used.

Q265: Can poor trowel technique cause failure?
A: Yes. Incorrect trowel size, swirled ridges, spot bonding or failure to collapse ridges can reduce coverage and bond.

Q266: Can installation in hot weather cause failure?
A: Hot, windy or sunny conditions can shorten open time and cause skinning, which can reduce bond if not managed properly.

Q267: Can wet substrates cause adhesive failure?
A: Wet or moisture-contaminated substrates can interfere with bonding or curing unless the adhesive system is specifically approved for that condition.

Q268: Can tiling over old tiles cause failure?
A: Yes, if the old tiles are loose, hollow, contaminated, glossy, uneven or incompatible with the new adhesive system.

Q269: Can hollow tiles always be blamed on the tile product?
A: No. Hollow sounds are usually related to installation, adhesive contact or substrate conditions rather than the tile itself, but the cause must be assessed.

Q270: Can adhesive shrinkage cause problems?
A: Incorrect bed thickness, wrong mix or unsuitable adhesive can contribute to shrinkage-related issues. Adhesive must be used within its product limits.
