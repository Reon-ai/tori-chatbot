# Master Waterproofing Rule

## Critical Statement

> Tiles and grout are not the waterproofing system.

## Safe Answer

> In wet areas, waterproofing must be treated as a system behind or below the tile. The tile, grout and silicone are part of the finish, but the waterproofing system protects the building.

## Never Say

Do not say:

- “Tiles are waterproofing.”
- “Grout waterproofs the shower.”
- “You do not need waterproofing.”
- “Large tiles remove the need for waterproofing.”
- “Epoxy grout means waterproofing is not needed.”

---

# Shower Waterproofing

## Check

- Waterproofing product/system.
- Correct substrate.
- Corners.
- Pipe penetrations.
- Floor/wall junctions.
- Drain integration.
- Cure time.
- Flood test where required by project professional.
- Tiling method.
- Silicone/change-of-plane details.

## Safe Answer

> Before tiling a shower, confirm the waterproofing is complete, cured and integrated with the drain and penetrations. Poor waterproofing can fail even if the tile looks perfect.

## Escalate

Always escalate active leaks, failed waterproofing, wet rooms and shower floors.

# Slip Resistance

## Critical Statement

No tile should be called slip-proof.

Slip performance depends on:

- Tile surface.
- Slope.
- Drainage.
- Wet/dry condition.
- Cleaning.
- Contaminants.
- Footwear.
- Traffic.
- Maintenance.
- User behaviour.

## Safe Answer

> No tile should be treated as slip-proof. Slip resistance depends on the tile surface, slope, drainage, cleaning, contaminants, footwear and site use.

## Never Say

Do not say:

- “Non-slip.”
- “Slip-proof.”
- “Guaranteed safe.”
- “Safe when wet.”
- “Approved for ramps.”
- “Safe for all users.”

---

# R-Rating and COF

## Simple Explanation

R-rating and COF are ways of describing slip-resistance performance, but they are not a universal guarantee of safety.

## Safe Answer

> R-ratings and COF values can be useful technical information, but final suitability depends on the project, application, slope, drainage, cleaning and applicable specification.

## Escalate

Escalate for:

- Ramps.
- Public walkways.
- Commercial spaces.
- Pool surrounds.
- Slip incidents.
- Injuries.
- Formal compliance questions.

---

# Ramps

## High-Risk Rule

Ramps are always safety-sensitive.

## Safe Answer

> Ramps need professional review. Slip performance depends on gradient, tile surface, wet/dry conditions, drainage, cleaning, footwear and traffic. Tiletoria can help with product data, but a professional should confirm final suitability.

---

# Pool Surrounds

## High-Risk Rule

Pool areas are often wet and barefoot.

## Safe Answer

> Pool surrounds need careful review because they are wet, barefoot areas. Product-specific data, drainage, maintenance and edge details must be confirmed before purchase.

---

# Balconies

## High-Risk Rule

Balconies combine waterproofing, drainage, movement and safety.

## Safe Answer

> Balconies are high-risk because failure can affect the structure and areas below. The waterproofing, falls, drainage, tile, adhesive, grout and movement joints must be reviewed as a system.

---

# Outdoor Wet Areas

## Check

- Exterior-rated tile.
- Suitable surface.
- Slope.
- Drainage.
- Exterior adhesive.
- Exterior grout.
- Movement joints.
- Maintenance plan.
- Algae cleaning.
- Sun and thermal movement.

## Safe Answer

> Outdoor wet areas need the correct surface, drainage and maintenance plan. A textured tile alone does not guarantee slip safety.

---

# Accessible Routes

## South African Context

For accessible routes, South African building guidance refers to floor and ground surfaces needing to be stable, firm and slip-resistant under wet and dry conditions. Do not translate this into a single universal tile rating.

## Safe Answer

> For accessible routes and public areas, the project professional should confirm the required performance based on the exact application and applicable South African requirements.
