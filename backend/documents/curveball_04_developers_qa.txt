# CURVEBALL Q&A — PERSONA 4: DEVELOPERS
## 200 Awkward, Strange & Unexpected But TRUE Questions South African Developers Actually Ask

---

### SECTION A: NHBRC WARRANTY & ENROLMENT NIGHTMARES

**Q: 1** We forgot to enrol 12 townhouses with the NHBRC before breaking ground. The foundations are already in. What's the actual penalty per unit, and is there any way to negotiate it down?
**A:** The penalty for late enrolment is a levy calculated on the total construction cost, typically around R2,000 to R4,000 per dwelling depending on value, plus administrative fees. There is no formal negotiation process — the NHBRC's system calculates the levy automatically based on the enrolled value. You cannot obtain an occupation certificate without proof of enrolment. If foundations are already laid, you must enrol immediately and pay the late penalty. The NHBRC may also conduct a "stage inspection" late assessment which can flag defects retrospectively.

**Q: 2** A buyer is claiming the porcelain tiles in their unit are "structurally defective" under the NHBRC 5-year warranty because they make a hollow sound when tapped. Is tile debonding a structural defect?
**A:** Tile debonding alone is not classified as a structural defect under the NHBRC warranty, which covers major structural elements (foundations, walls, roof structure). However, if the debonding is caused by structural movement — such as foundation settlement, slab deflection, or wall cracking — the NHBRC may accept it as evidence of a structural defect. The builder must prove the substrate was stable at the time of tiling. Get a structural engineer's report. Hollow-sounding tiles alone fall under finish quality (1-year maintenance period), not the 5-year structural warranty.

**Q: 3** We sold a unit, transferred it, and now the new owner (6 months later) wants to claim under the NHBRC warranty. But we never enrolled the home because it was a "custom build for a client on their own land." Does the NHBRC warranty still apply?
**A:** Yes. Section 10 of the Housing Consumers Protection Measures Act requires ALL new homes to be enrolled with the NHBRC, regardless of whether the consumer owns the land. If the home was built for a housing consumer (not for resale as an investment property by a registered builder), it must be enrolled. Non-enrolment is an offence. The housing consumer can report you to the NHBRC, which may impose penalties and require enrolment retroactively. You cannot contract out of this obligation.

**Q: 4** The NHBRC inspector failed our roof slab because of hairline cracks in the screed beneath the tiles. The screed contractor says the cracks are "shrinkage only" and don't affect structural integrity. Can we tile over cracked screed if the engineer certifies it structurally sound?
**A:** You can tile over cracked screed if a professional engineer certifies the cracks are non-structural (shrinkage or plastic settlement) and the screed's residual compressive strength meets design requirements (typically minimum 15 MPa for residential floors). However, the NHBRC inspector's decision can only be overturned through the formal appeals process. You must seal the cracks with an epoxy or polyurethane crack-sealing system, apply a bonded screed topping if necessary, and install a crack-isolation membrane before tiling. The engineer's sign-off and the membrane specification must be submitted to NHBRC as part of your re-inspection application.

**Q: 5** We have 40 completed units that the NHBRC passed, but now 15 owners report cracked tiles after the first winter. The cracks follow the line of the construction joints in the concrete slab. Is this the NHBRC's problem or ours?
**A:** If the NHBRC passed the units, the warranty responsibility still sits with you as the developer for the 5-year structural warranty period. Hairline cracks following construction joints typically indicate either inadequate load transfer across the joint, dowel misalignment, or failure to install a movement joint in the tile bed directly above the construction joint. SANS 10107 and SANS 10400-J require that movement joints in the tile assembly align with structural joints in the substrate. This is a design/installation error, not an NHBRC inspection failure. You must repair the affected units; consider claiming from your tiling subcontractor if they failed to install movement joints.

**Q: 6** Our development includes both free-standing houses and sectional title apartments. Do we need separate NHBRC enrolments for each, and does the 5-year warranty apply differently?
**A:** Each individual dwelling unit must be separately enrolled with the NHBRC. Free-standing houses are enrolled as individual homes. For sectional title schemes, each apartment unit is enrolled separately. The 5-year structural warranty applies identically to both. However, common property (lobbies, staircases, corridors) is NOT covered by the NHBRC warranty — it falls under the developer's contractual warranty to the body corporate and your professional indemnity insurance. Ensure your enrolment count matches the number of saleable units exactly.

**Q: 7** A buyer threatened to lay a criminal charge because we sold them a home that wasn't NHBRC enrolled. Can they actually do that, or is it a civil matter only?
**A:** Non-enrolment of a home with the NHBRC is a criminal offence in terms of Section 24 of the Housing Consumers Protection Measures Act. A private individual cannot lay a criminal charge directly in the sense of prosecuting it, but they can report the offence to the NHBRC and the SAPS. The NHBRC has investigative powers and can refer the matter to the National Prosecuting Authority. Fines can be substantial, and directors of the development company may face personal liability. This is both a criminal and civil matter — the buyer can also sue for damages.

**Q: 8** We're building 80 units in phases over 3 years. Can we enrol all 80 upfront and pay the levy in one go, or must we enrol as each phase breaks ground?
**A:** The NHBRC enrolment system ties to actual construction commencement. You cannot enrol units before breaking ground, and phased enrolment is the standard practice. However, you can apply for a "project registration" that covers the full development, with individual unit enrolments triggered as each phase commences. This allows you to lock in levy rates and avoid annual increases applying to later phases. Contact the NHBRC project liaison office to set up a master enrolment agreement.

**Q: 9** The NHBRC inspector passed our floor tiles during construction, but 18 months later, efflorescence is pushing through the grout joints in 30% of units. The warranty period is 5 years. Is efflorescence a defect?
**A:** Efflorescence is generally classified as a maintenance issue, not a structural defect, under the NHBRC warranty. However, if the efflorescence is caused by a failure in the waterproofing system beneath the tile bed (such as inadequate damp-proofing or a breached membrane in wet areas), it may constitute a defect under the general building warranty. For the NHBRC 5-year structural warranty specifically, efflorescence alone does not qualify. You should address it under your 1-year maintenance obligation or investigate whether the waterproofing subcontractor's 10-year guarantee applies.

**Q: 10** We subcontracted the entire build to a turnkey contractor. The NHBRC warranty certificate was issued in their name, not ours. Now buyers are suing us directly. Are we protected?
**A:** No. As the developer and seller of the units, you remain the responsible party to the housing consumer under the CPA and the sale agreement. The NHBRC warranty runs with the home, not the contractor. If your turnkey contractor holds the NHBRC certificate, you must claim an indemnity from them, but the buyer's primary claim is against you. Your sale agreement should include a hold-harmless clause from the contractor, and you should ensure the contractor is registered with the NHBRC as a "home builder." Always obtain your own NHBRC enrolment as the developer.

### SECTION B: CPA CLAIMS & CONSUMER RIGHTS

**Q: 11** A buyer is rejecting their unit because the floor tiles are "a slightly warmer shade of beige" than the show house tiles, which they say constitutes a false representation under CPA Section 41. Are tile shade variations a CPA issue?
**A:** Section 41 of the CPA prohibits false, misleading, or deceptive representations. Tile shade variation is a normal manufacturing characteristic — porcelain and ceramic tiles from the same batch can vary within ISO 10545 tolerances. However, if you displayed a specific tile in the show house and specified it in the sale agreement, you must deliver that exact tile or an equivalent within reasonable commercial tolerances. Your sales documentation should include a clause stating that "tile shades may vary from show house samples due to manufacturing batch differences." Without such a disclaimer, a motivated buyer could pursue a CPA claim. The test is whether a reasonable consumer would have been misled.

**Q: 12** We used rectified porcelain tiles in the show house but supplied non-rectified in the sold units to save costs. The difference is barely visible. A buyer discovered this and claims it's a material breach. Is it?
**A:** Yes, this is a material breach of contract. Rectified tiles (cut to precise dimensions after firing) have sharp, square edges and require minimal grout joints (1.5mm–2mm). Non-rectified tiles have slightly rounded edges and require wider grout joints (3mm–5mm). The finished appearance is visibly different. Under CPA Section 55, goods must be reasonably suitable for the purposes they are generally used for, and if you displayed a rectified tile, that becomes the expected standard. You must either replace the tiles or compensate the buyer for the cost difference. This is an expensive lesson — never substitute tile types without written buyer consent.

**Q: 13** A buyer wants us to replace their kitchen floor tiles because they're "too slippery" when wet, claiming this is unsafe and breaches CPA Section 55 (safe, good-quality goods). The tiles are standard glazed porcelain with no slip-rating specified. Do we have to replace them?
**A:** Under SANS 10107 and SANS 10400-S (facilities for persons with disabilities), dry interior floors in residential units must achieve a minimum R9 slip rating (pendulum test value ≥ 36). Standard glazed porcelain typically meets this for dry conditions. However, if the tiles are in a kitchen where wet conditions are foreseeable, the buyer's argument has merit. If you did not specify a slip rating and the tiles are genuinely slippery when wet (below R9 when tested pendulum wet), a court or tribunal may find a breach. For future developments, specify R9 or R10 rated tiles for all residential floors and document this in the sales pack.

**Q: 14** We advertised "imported Italian porcelain tiles" but switched to a Turkish supplier due to shipping delays. The Turkish tiles are equivalent spec and lower cost. A buyer claims false advertising under the CPA. Do they have a case?
**A:** Yes. "Imported Italian porcelain tiles" is a specific representation about place of origin. Switching to Turkish tiles without disclosure constitutes a misrepresentation under CPA Section 41(1)(c), regardless of whether the Turkish tiles are technically equivalent. The CPA provides for damages, refund, or replacement at the consumer's election. You must either reinstall Italian tiles, compensate the buyer for the perceived value difference (Italian tiles carry a premium), or obtain written consent for the substitution. Going forward, use generic language like "imported premium porcelain tiles" in marketing.

**Q: 15** A buyer is demanding we replace all tiles in their unit because three tiles have minor chips from the transportation process. The chips are less than 5mm on the tile edges. Does the CPA require full replacement?
**A:** No. The CPA requires goods to be free of defects, but minor edge chips that do not affect the structural integrity or general appearance of the floor when grouted are considered acceptable under SANS 1449 and industry norms. You are required to replace the three affected tiles, not the entire floor. If the chips are in a conspicuous location and visible after grouting, offer to replace those specific tiles and compensate for inconvenience. Document the damage with photos and obtain the buyer's sign-off on the repair. Full replacement of an entire tiled floor for three minor chips would be considered an unreasonable remedy.

**Q: 16** We built a show house with upgraded 600x600mm tiles, but the standard spec in the sales contract is 450x450mm. A buyer claims the show house constitutes a "sample" under CPA Section 42 and demands 600x600mm at no extra cost. Are they right?
**A:** Section 42 of the CPA states that if goods are exhibited as a sample, the bulk must correspond with the sample in quality. However, the test is whether the show house was presented as the standard product or as a display with upgrades. Your sales contract must clearly state: "The show house features optional upgrades not included in the standard specification. The standard specification is as set out in Annexure A." If this disclaimer is absent or unclear, the buyer has a strong CPA claim. Ensure show house viewing is accompanied by a specification sheet highlighting upgrades versus standard inclusions.

**Q: 17** A buyer claims our tiles "emit a chemical smell" that causes headaches and wants the entire installation removed under CPA health and safety provisions. They've produced a "mould air quality report" from a private company. What are our obligations?
**A:** Porcelain and ceramic tiles are inert, fired-clay products that do not emit VOCs or chemical fumes. The "smell" is likely from the tile adhesive, grout, or sealant used during installation — these do release minor VOCs during curing, which dissipate within days. Request the buyer's report and have it reviewed by an occupational hygienist registered with the South African Institute of Occupational Hygiene. If the report lacks scientific rigour (no control samples, no calibrated instruments), challenge it. If there is a legitimate odour from adhesives, ventilate the unit for 72 hours and document the resolution. Tile replacement is not a reasonable remedy unless hazardous materials are proven present.

**Q: 18** We sold a penthouse unit where the purchaser specified their own imported tiles, supplied by them. The tiles cracked during installation. They're demanding we pay for replacement under CPA warranty. Do we have any liability?
**A:** Under CPA Section 55, if the consumer supplied their own goods, the supplier (you) is only liable if the goods were damaged due to your negligence or improper installation. If the tiles cracked due to inherent defects (poor quality, inadequate thickness for the application, or unsuitability for floor use), the consumer must claim from the tile supplier or manufacturer. However, if your installer failed to prepare the substrate properly, used incorrect adhesive, or omitted expansion joints, you remain liable for installation defects. Obtain an independent assessment of the cracking cause before admitting liability.

**Q: 19** A buyer wants to know the exact country of origin, factory name, and batch numbers of every tile in their unit for "CPA transparency rights." Do we have to provide this?
**A:** The CPA does not require disclosure of factory names or batch numbers as a general right. However, Section 22 requires disclosure of material facts, and if the buyer specifically requests information about product origin, you must answer truthfully. You should maintain records of tile specifications, supplier invoices, and country of origin for warranty and traceability purposes. Provide the buyer with the tile specification sheet (including country of origin and SANS/ISO compliance), but factory names and batch numbers are commercial information you can withhold unless subpoenaed. Never falsify country of origin — this constitutes fraud.

**Q: 20** We offered a "10-year tile warranty" in our marketing brochure. Our attorney says this exceeds the NHBRC warranty and creates unnecessary liability. Can we withdraw it for units not yet sold?
**A:** For unsold units, you can amend your marketing materials and remove the 10-year tile warranty. However, for units already sold where the brochure formed part of the sale agreement or was relied upon by the purchaser, the warranty stands as an express contractual undertaking. Under CPA Section 56, any warranty you voluntarily offer becomes binding. A 10-year tile warranty is unusual — porcelain tiles typically last decades, but the installation (adhesion, grouting) does not. If you offer such a warranty, define it precisely: does it cover tile fracture only, or installation defects? Consider limiting it to manufacturer defects in the tile body itself.

### SECTION C: B-BBEE, COMPLIANCE & SUPPLIER ISSUES

**Q: 21** Our tiling supplier's B-BBEE certificate expired three months into our 8-month project. They're a Level 4 but now have no valid certificate. Does this affect our B-BBEE scorecard if we continue using them?
**A:** For your measured entity scorecard, only supplier B-BBEE certificates that are valid at the time of procurement count. Payments to suppliers after their certificate expiry cannot be claimed for procurement recognition unless they obtain a renewed certificate. You should immediately request an updated certificate from the supplier. If they fail to provide one within a reasonable period, you must either source from an alternative supplier with a valid certificate or accept that those procurement points will not contribute to your scorecard. For large developments, include a clause in your supply agreement requiring maintenance of a valid B-BBEE certificate throughout the contract period.

**Q: 22** We're a Level 2 B-BBEE contributor but our main tiling subcontractor is non-compliant (Level 8, no black ownership). For a government-subsidised housing project, can we still appoint them as a subcontractor?
**A:** For government-subsidised housing (RDP/gap housing), the Department of Human Settlements requires all contractors and subcontractors to meet minimum B-BBEE thresholds. A non-compliant subcontractor puts your entire project at risk of cancellation and may disqualify you from future tenders. You need a minimum of 51% black ownership in your subcontractor base for government housing projects. Either require your subcontractor to achieve compliance through a joint venture or consortium arrangement, or replace them with a compliant CIDB-registered contractor. Document all B-BBEE verification letters before appointing any subcontractor.

**Q: 23** We imported tiles directly from Italy for a luxury development. Can we claim B-BBEE procurement points for the import value, even though the foreign supplier has no B-BBEE certificate?
**A:** No. B-BBEE procurement recognition only applies to South African-registered suppliers with valid B-BBEE certificates. Imports from foreign entities receive zero procurement points on your scorecard. To maximise points, you could either purchase through the South African distributor or subsidiary of the Italian manufacturer (if they have a valid B-BBEE certificate) or ensure that other aspects of your scorecard (ownership, skills development, enterprise development) compensate for the lost procurement points. The DTIC B-BBEE Codes of Good Practice are clear on this — there is no import exemption.

**Q: 24** Our B-BBEE verification agency is no longer accredited by SANAS. The certificates they issued us for the past two years — are they still valid?
**A:** SANAS-accredited verification agencies must maintain their accreditation. If an agency loses SANAS accreditation, certificates issued AFTER the date of withdrawal are invalid. Certificates issued BEFORE the loss of accreditation remain valid for their 12-month validity period, provided the agency was accredited at the time of issue. However, a SANAS site audit of your measured entity may scrutinise these certificates more closely. If you suspect your agency's accreditation status, verify it on the SANAS and IRBA websites. For a large development, obtain a new certificate from an alternative accredited agency to be safe.

**Q: 25** We're a foreign-owned developer (100% offshore parent company) building in South Africa. Do B-BBEE requirements apply to us, and if so, how do we achieve any level of compliance with zero local ownership?
**A:** B-BBEE applies to all entities operating in South Africa, including foreign-owned subsidiaries. With zero local ownership, you score zero on the ownership element (25 points). However, you can still achieve a meaningful overall score through the other scorecard elements: management control (even foreign companies can appoint black South African directors), skills development (train black South African employees), enterprise and supplier development (support local black-owned businesses), and socio-economic development. Foreign-owned entities that achieve Level 4 or better can still trade successfully in SA. Consider a sale of shares to a B-BBEE partner if long-term competitiveness is a priority.

**Q: 26** A main contractor on our development has fronting concerns — their "black owner" is an employee with no real economic interest. We're the developer, not the contractor. Could we be implicated if fronting is proven?
**A:** Fronting is a criminal offence under the B-BBEE Amendment Act. While the primary liability rests with the measured entity (the contractor), you as the developer could face reputational damage, cancellation of contracts, and blacklisting from future government or SOE projects if you knowingly benefited from a fronting arrangement. The B-BBEE Commission has investigative powers and can fine any party involved in fronting up to 10% of annual turnover or imprison individuals for up to 10 years. Conduct due diligence on your contractors' ownership structures, and include an anti-fronting warranty in your subcontract agreements.

**Q: 27** For a Transnet-adjacent industrial park development, we need a Level 1 B-BBEE rating. Our spend on tiles is 15% of total project cost. How much does the tile supplier's rating actually affect our overall scorecard?
**A:** Procurement contributes up to 25 points on the generic B-BBEE scorecard (25% weighting). Your tile supplier's rating directly affects the procurement points you can claim. If your tile supplier is Level 1, you receive full recognition (multiply spend by 1.35 for preferential procurement). If they are Level 4, the multiplier drops to 1.0. Level 8 = 0.5. For a Level 1 target, every procurement point matters. A Level 1 tile supplier could contribute approximately 3–4 points to your overall scorecard from tile procurement alone. Given that Level 1 requires 100+ points out of 109, this is material. Source from a Level 1–2 supplier if possible.

**Q: 28** Our development company has a B-BBEE certificate, but the SPV (special purpose vehicle) created for this specific project is newly incorporated and has no certificate. Do we need a separate certificate for the SPV?
**A:** Yes. Each juristic person is a separate measured entity and requires its own B-BBEE certificate. If the SPV is newly incorporated with no trading history, it may qualify for an EME (Exempted Micro Enterprise) certificate (annual turnover below R10 million) issued by a CIPC-registered auditor, accounting officer, or accredited B-BBEE verification agency. An EME automatically achieves Level 4 (or Level 2 if 100% black-owned). This is often sufficient for project-level requirements. Obtain the EME certificate before tendering or applying for development finance.

### SECTION D: PERFORMANCE BONDS, PENALTIES & FINANCIAL INSTRUMENTS

**Q: 29** Our development finance agreement requires a performance bond for the tiling contractor (R12 million contract value). The bank wants 2.5% annual premium and a 15% cash margin. Is this market standard in South Africa?
**A:** For a performance bond on a R12 million tiling contract, 2.5% annual premium is at the upper end of market range (standard is 1.5%–2.5%). The 15% cash margin is steep — typically 10%–15% for lesser-known contractors, but 5%–10% for established contractors with strong balance sheets. Counter-offer: provide a parent company guarantee from a listed entity, offer a bank guarantee instead of an insurance-backed bond (often cheaper), or negotiate a reducing bond that decreases as milestones are reached. For a reputable CIDB-registered contractor with Grade 6 or higher, you should push for 10% margin maximum.

**Q: 30** The municipality imposed a penalty of R25,000 per day for late completion past the agreed practical completion date. Load shedding caused 45 days of delays. Can we claim force majeure to void the penalty?
**A:** Load shedding is increasingly being recognised as a force majeure event in South African construction contracts, but only if the contract specifically includes it in the force majeure clause. Standard JBCC agreements now include load shedding as a compensable delay event. You must: (1) prove the delays were solely attributable to load shedding and not concurrent delay, (2) provide load shedding schedules from Eskom showing Stage 3+ during the delay period, (3) demonstrate that you took reasonable measures (generator backup, shift scheduling) to mitigate, and (4) notify the principal agent within the contractual timeframes (typically 5 days of becoming aware). If all conditions are met, the penalty should be waived for the proven delay period.

**Q: 31** We signed a fixed-price contract with our tiling contractor in March. Since then, the Rand has depreciated 18% against the Euro, and the imported tiles specified have increased in price by 22%. The contractor wants a price adjustment. Do we have to pay?
**A:** Under a fixed-price contract, the contractor bears the currency risk unless the contract includes a currency fluctuation clause or material escalation provision. If no such clause exists, you are not contractually obligated to pay the increase. However, if the contractor becomes insolvent or walks off site, your project delays may cost more than the price increase. The pragmatic approach: negotiate a partial adjustment (e.g., 50% of the increase) in exchange for a written commitment to maintain the programme. For future contracts, include a currency fluctuation cap (e.g., adjustment only if Rand moves more than 10% against the basket currency).

**Q: 32** Our development has a R500,000/day liquidated damages clause for late sectional title registration. The tiling contractor's delay pushed us past the deadline by 3 weeks. Can we claim the full R10.5 million from the contractor?
**A:** Liquidated damages must be a genuine pre-estimate of loss to be enforceable. R500,000/day for delayed registration may be challengeable as a penalty if it vastly exceeds actual losses. However, if the amount is specified in your development finance agreement and you are paying interest on extended facilities, the losses may be real. You can claim from the tiling contractor if their delay was the direct cause, but you must prove causation and that you gave them notice of the consequences of delay. The contractor's liability is typically capped at the contract value or a specified percentage (e.g., 10%). Check your subcontract for the cap.

**Q: 33** We need a performance bond for a R80 million mixed-use development. The bank quoted 3.5% for the bond — which seems high. What's the actual market rate, and are there alternatives to bank bonds?
**A:** 3.5% is above market for a standard performance bond. Current South African market rates are 1.5%–2.5% for established developers with strong financials. Alternatives: (1) Insurance-backed performance bonds from short-term insurers (sometimes cheaper, but may include more exclusions), (2) Parent company guarantees if you have a listed or investment-grade parent, (3) cash deposits held in trust (tie up capital but cost-effective), (4) retention monies (typically 5%–10% of contract value held until practical completion). For a R80 million project, shop around — Santam, Hollard, and Guardrisk all offer construction bonds. Also consider splitting the bond between bank and insurance to reduce concentration risk.

**Q: 34** The main contractor has demanded we provide an advance payment guarantee for 20% of the tiling contract value. We've never done this before. Is it standard practice, and what are the risks?
**A:** Advance payment guarantees are standard when the contractor needs upfront capital for material procurement, especially for imported tiles with long lead times. The guarantee ensures that if the contractor defaults, you can recall the advance. The risk is that the guarantee is unconditional — the bank pays on demand without investigating the default. Structure it as a "conditional" advance payment guarantee if possible, or ensure the guarantee reduces pro-rata as materials are delivered to site and verified. The cost (0.5%–1.5% of guarantee value) is typically borne by the party receiving the advance.

### SECTION E: SPECIFICATION CHANGES, MUNICIPAL & REGULATORY DELAYS

**Q: 35** We want to change from 300x300mm ceramic floor tiles to 600x600mm porcelain tiles on a development where municipal plans are already approved. The floor slabs were designed for the lighter ceramic tiles. Do we need plan amendments?
**A:** Yes. The change from ceramic to porcelain tiles increases the dead load on the slab — porcelain is denser (typically 2,300–2,400 kg/m³) than ceramic (1,800–2,000 kg/m³), and the larger format may require a thicker adhesive bed. If the original slab was designed for the lighter load, the increase could affect structural compliance under SANS 10400-B. You must have a professional engineer assess whether the slab can accommodate the additional load. If the slab is adequate, a professional sign-off is required. If not, you need amended structural drawings, which trigger a plan amendment at the municipality (4–8 week delay). Do not proceed without engineer sign-off — it voids your NHBRC enrolment.

**Q: 36** Our municipal building inspector rejected the tiling in the common area corridors because we used a rectified edge tile with 1.5mm grout joints, and he claims SANS 10400 requires 3mm minimum. Is there a minimum grout joint width in the building regulations?
**A:** SANS 10400 does not specify a minimum grout joint width — it refers to SANS 10107 (the tile installation code) for workmanship standards. SANS 10107 allows grout joint widths down to 1.0mm for rectified tiles, provided the tiles are installed by a competent tiler and the substrate is sufficiently flat (maximum 3mm deviation over 2m). The inspector may be conflating grout width with general workmanship requirements. Provide the inspector with a copy of SANS 10107, the tile manufacturer's installation guidelines, and a flatness survey of the substrate. If they persist, escalate to the municipal building control manager — an incorrect interpretation by a junior inspector should not delay your project.

**Q: 37** The municipality's town planning department says our building plans with tile specifications are "incomplete" because we didn't specify the tile weight per m². Is this a valid objection?
**A:** No, this is not a standard plan submission requirement. Building plans must include floor finishes (tile type, slip rating for certain applications), but the exact weight per m² is not a submission requirement under SANS 10400-A. The municipality may be requesting this because the structural engineer's slab design must account for superimposed dead loads, and tile weight is part of that calculation. Provide the superimposed dead load specification from your structural engineer (typically 1.0–1.5 kPa for standard floor tiles), which should satisfy the town planning department. If they insist on tile weight per m², it indicates they are confusing town planning and building control functions — escalate.

**Q: 38** Our approved plans specify "ceramic floor tiles" but we've decided to use SPC vinyl flooring in the bedrooms instead. The walls and floors are already built. Do we need to amend plans for this change?
**A:** SANS vinyl flooring is lighter than ceramic tiles, so there are no structural load concerns. However, the National Building Regulations require that approved plans accurately reflect the completed building. A material change to specified finishes may require a plan amendment, depending on your municipality's interpretation. In practice, many municipalities do not require amendment for finish changes within the same building classification (e.g., Class 2 residential), provided the change does not affect fire ratings, structural loads, or disability access. Submit a letter of amendment to the building control officer and request written confirmation of whether an amended plan submission is required. Budget 2–4 weeks for a response.

**Q: 39** We've been waiting 14 weeks for municipal water connection to our 120-unit development. The units are complete but we can't issue occupation certificates. Can we claim damages from the municipality?
**A:** Claiming damages from a South African municipality for delayed service connections is technically possible but practically very difficult. Municipalities enjoy significant legal protection, and their service level agreements typically exclude liability for delays. However, you can: (1) escalate through the municipal manager and mayoral office, (2) apply for a "provisional connection" or tanker water supply with written municipality approval, (3) engage a private contractor for temporary bulk water supply if the municipality consents, (4) claim the delay as a force majeure event in your sale agreements to avoid purchaser penalties. Document all correspondence. For future projects, apply for water connection at the same time as plan submission, not after construction completion.

**Q: 40** The municipality's electrical inspector failed our installation because the tile layer covered the floor junction boxes in the screed. The tiling is complete in 40 units. Who pays for the rectification?
**A:** The tile layer is responsible for not locating and preserving junction boxes before tiling. However, as the developer, you bear the rectification cost initially and must recover from the subcontractor. The SANS 10142-1 wiring code requires junction boxes to remain accessible — they cannot be permanently covered by tiles or screed. Rectification involves cutting open the tiles, relocating the junction boxes to accessible positions (in the wall or a pull-box at floor level), and re-tiling. This is a significant cost. Deduct the rectification cost from the tiling contractor's final payment and, if necessary, claim against their performance guarantee. Always require the electrical contractor to mark junction box locations before screeding and tiling begin.

### SECTION F: FIRE RATINGS, ACOUSTICS & THERMAL PERFORMANCE

**Q: 41** Our 8-storey residential building requires fire-rated floors between units. The tiles are porcelain on a concrete slab. Does the tile layer contribute to the fire resistance level (FRL), and what FRL do we need?
**A:** Under SANS 10400-T, residential buildings above two storeys require fire-rated compartment floors. The concrete slab itself typically provides the FRL — a 150mm reinforced concrete slab achieves approximately FRL 120/120/120. The tile layer does not structurally contribute to the fire rating but must not compromise it. Tiles and adhesive must not contain combustible materials that could fuel a fire or produce toxic smoke. Standard porcelain tiles and cement-based adhesives are non-combustible and comply. However, if you use adhesive with polymer additives, check the fire rating of the additive — some organic compounds are combustible. For an 8-storey building, you typically need FRL 60/60/60 minimum for compartment floors. The tile layer is "in addition to" the slab rating, not a substitute.

**Q: 42** An acoustic engineer says our inter-unit floor/ceiling assembly must achieve an Impact Sound Rating (ISR) of 55dB and Airborne Sound Rating of 50dB under SANS 10400-T. Our tiles are on a concrete slab with no acoustic underlay. Will this comply?
**A:** A standard 150mm concrete slab with hard porcelain tiles directly bonded will NOT achieve ISR 55dB. Hard surfaces reflect impact sound (footsteps, dropped objects) directly into the ceiling cavity below. To achieve ISR 55dB, you need an acoustic underlayment (resilient layer) between the screed and the tiles, or a floating screed system. Options: (1) 5mm rubber crumb underlay beneath the screed, (2) acoustic matting systems specifically designed for tile installations, (3) raised access floor systems with acoustic infill. The airborne rating of 50dB is achievable with the concrete slab alone. Budget R150–R300/m² additional for acoustic flooring systems. Non-compliance with SANS 10400-T acoustic requirements can result in refusal of occupation certificate.

**Q: 43** We're building a glass tower in Sandton where the façade creates a greenhouse effect. The interior floor tiles reach surface temperatures of 55°C in summer. Does SANS 10400-XA (energy efficiency) regulate tile surface temperatures?
**A:** SANS 10400-XA regulates the building envelope's thermal performance, not interior surface temperatures directly. However, excessive solar heat gain through glazing will cause your building to fail the XA requirements for annual energy consumption. The solution is not the tile selection but the glazing specification — you need high-performance glass with a Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC) below 0.35 and visible light transmittance above 0.50. Tiles with lighter colours and higher reflectance (LRV > 50) will reduce the radiant heat absorbed and re-emitted into the space. Dark tiles (LRV < 20) in a glass building act as heat sinks and increase cooling loads significantly. Specify light-coloured tiles for sun-exposed areas and ensure your HVAC engineer accounts for the additional heat load.

**Q: 44** The energy efficiency assessor says our tiled concrete floors need to be included in the thermal simulation because they form part of the "thermal mass" calculation. Is a tiled floor actually thermal mass?
**A:** Yes. Concrete slabs with ceramic or porcelain tiles have high thermal mass (the ability to store and slowly release heat). Under SANS 10400-XA, thermal mass is beneficial in climates with high diurnal temperature variation (Highveld, Karoo) because it stabilises indoor temperatures. However, in humid coastal climates (Durban, Richards Bay), high thermal mass can work against you — the floor stays cool and damp, increasing condensation risk. The XA simulation must include the floor slab's thermal properties (density, specific heat capacity, thermal conductivity). Tiled floors increase the exposed thermal mass surface area compared to carpeted floors. Your assessor is correct — include it.

**Q: 45** Our building passed the fire inspection but failed the acoustic test between units because the tile layer grouted right up to the wall without leaving a perimeter isolation joint. How does grouting affect acoustic performance?
**A:** Acoustic flanking paths are a common failure mode in multi-unit buildings. When tiles are grouted hard against the wall, impact vibration transmits through the tile-screed-wall junction and re-radiates into the adjacent unit. SANS 10400-T and SANS 10177 (acoustic testing) require that floor finishes include a resilient perimeter isolation joint (minimum 5mm–8mm) filled with acoustic sealant where they abut walls and service penetrations. The tile layer must: (1) install a compressible foam strip around the perimeter before tiling, (2) leave a 5mm gap between the tile edge and the wall, (3) fill the gap with a flexible acoustic sealant (not grout). This simple detail can reduce flanking transmission by 5–10dB — often the difference between pass and fail.

### SECTION G: STORMWATER, ENVIRONMENTAL & HERITAGE ISSUES

**Q: 46** Our development of 200 townhouses will have 8,000m² of tiled and paved surface. The municipality's stormwater department says we need attenuation ponds because the runoff coefficient exceeds their threshold. What's the threshold, and what does this cost?
**A:** Under SANS 10400-R and municipal stormwater bylaws, developments that increase impervious surface area typically require stormwater attenuation. The runoff coefficient threshold varies by municipality but is commonly 0.50–0.70 (meaning 50%–70% of rainfall becomes runoff). Tiled surfaces have a runoff coefficient of approximately 0.85–0.95 (almost all rain runs off). The requirement is to attenuate peak runoff to pre-development levels or to the municipality's design storm standard (typically 1:50 year return period). For 200 townhouses, expect 2–3 attenuation ponds at R300,000–R600,000 each, plus underground attenuation tanks if space is limited. Permeable paving in non-critical areas reduces the requirement — consider open-graded pavers for driveways and walkways.

**Q: 47** We're building in the Bo-Kaap heritage protection overlay zone. The City of Cape Town requires that all external floor finishes be "sympathetic to the heritage character." Can we use modern large-format porcelain tiles, or are we restricted to traditional materials?
**A:** The Bo-Kaap Heritage Protection Overlay Zone (HPOZ) has strict aesthetic controls. External floor finishes in public-visible areas (stoep, courtyard) are typically required to use traditional materials — clay brick pavers, slate, or cement tiles — in keeping with the Victorian and Georgian architectural character. Large-format modern porcelain will almost certainly be rejected by the Heritage Impact Assessment (HIA) process. Internal finishes are less controlled but may require HIA approval if the building is Grade II heritage-protected. Engage a heritage consultant early in the design phase. Attempting to install non-compliant finishes after HIA rejection can result in a demolition order — the City has issued these for unauthorised alterations in heritage zones.

**Q: 48** The Environmental Impact Assessment for our development flagged that tile cutting on-site generates silica dust, which is a listed activity under the National Environmental Management: Air Quality Act. Do we need an atmospheric emissions licence?
**A:** On-site tile cutting using water-cooled wet saws does not typically trigger the need for an atmospheric emissions licence, as the water suppresses dust. However, dry cutting of tiles, stone, or concrete is a listed activity under Category 3 of the listed activities and standards regulations. If your contractors dry-cut tiles on-site, you need an atmospheric emissions licence from the provincial environmental department, or you must ensure all cutting is done off-site at a licensed facility. Best practice: prohibit dry cutting in your contractor specifications, require wet-cutting methods, and include it in your Environmental Management Plan (EMP). Silica dust exposure also triggers Occupational Health and Safety obligations — provide respiratory protection if dust cannot be fully suppressed.

**Q: 49** Our development borders a wetland. The environmental officer says our construction runoff, including grout and adhesive residue from tile washing, cannot enter the wetland. How do we manage this practically?
**A:** Construction runoff containing cement, grout, adhesive, or sealant residues is classified as contaminated water under the National Water Act and cannot discharge into a wetland, river, or watercourse. You must: (1) establish a sealed wash bay area with sediment traps, (2) collect all tile wash water and dispose of it at a licensed waste facility (not into the stormwater system), (3) install silt fences and check dams around the perimeter, (4) prepare a Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan (SWPPP) as part of your Environmental Management Programme. Fines for wetland pollution range from R100,000 to R5 million under the National Environmental Management Act. The Department of Forestry, Fisheries and Environment actively prosecutes developers for wetland damage.

**Q: 50** We're required to conduct a Heritage Impact Assessment before demolishing an existing building on our development site. The building has no heritage status, but it's in an area with heritage sensitivity. How long does this take, and can we start construction while waiting?
**A:** A Heritage Impact Assessment (HIA) for a building with no formal heritage grading but in a sensitive area typically takes 4–8 weeks, including the public participation phase. You CANNOT commence demolition or construction until the HIA is approved by the provincial heritage resources authority (PHRA) — in the Western Cape, this is Heritage Western Cape; in Gauteng, the South African Heritage Resources Agency (SAHRA) regional office. Starting construction before HIA approval is a criminal offence under the National Heritage Resources Act (Section 48). The PHRA can issue a stop-work order and require you to restore any unauthorised demolition. Budget 2–3 months for the full heritage approval process.

### SECTION H: SHOW HOUSE, BODY CORPORATE & SECTIONAL TITLE OBLIGATIONS

**Q: 51** Our show house has imported marble-look porcelain tiles (R850/m²). The standard spec in the sales contract is local ceramic tiles (R120/m²). A buyer who only viewed the show house (never read the contract) claims they were misled. What's our legal exposure?
**A:** Under CPA Section 41 and common law misrepresentation, your exposure is significant. If the buyer can prove they relied on the show house as representative of the standard product (and many buyers do), a court or tribunal may find a misrepresentation. The CPA shifts the burden — you must prove the buyer was not misled. Best defence: demonstrate that the buyer signed a specification schedule at the show house clearly distinguishing upgrades from standard, and that the sales contract was explained. Without this, you may be required to either upgrade the buyer's unit to match the show house or compensate for the difference. The cost of defending a CPA complaint at the National Consumer Tribunal, plus reputational damage, often exceeds the cost of simply upgrading the tiles.

**Q: 52** The body corporate of our completed sectional title scheme wants to change the lobby tiles from the developer's specification to something "more modern." Can they do this without our consent, and do we have any ongoing obligation?
**A:** Once the sectional title scheme is registered and the body corporate is established, the body corporate — not the developer — controls the common property. Under the Sectional Titles Schemes Management Act (STSMA), the body corporate can, by special resolution (75% majority in value), make alterations to common property. As the developer, you have no ongoing obligation to maintain the common property finishes unless your development agreement included a specific warranty period for common areas (which is unusual). However, if the body corporate changes the tiles within the defects liability period and the work is defective, they may claim against you. Once transferred, the body corporate's decisions are their own.

**Q: 53** We sold all units in our sectional title scheme but retained the penthouse as the developer's unit. The body corporate passed a rule requiring all unit owners to use the same tile specification for any renovations to ensure "visual uniformity." Does this rule apply to us as the developer?
**A:** Yes. As a unit owner, you are subject to the conduct rules of the body corporate regardless of your status as the developer. Under STSMA Section 11(3), conduct rules bind all owners and occupiers. If the body corporate has validly passed a rule requiring specific tile specifications for renovations (which must have been approved by the Chief Ombud for Sectional Titles), you must comply. The only exception is if the rule was passed after you took transfer and it imposes a restriction that was not disclosed in the sale agreement — you may have a claim for breach of contract. As developer, consider reserving rights in the development agreement or establishing the conduct rules yourself before selling the first unit.

**Q: 54** A purchaser in our sectional title scheme is refusing to pay levies because they claim the developer-specified lobby tiles are "substandard and reduce the value of their unit." Can they withhold levies for this reason?
**A:** No. Levy payments and complaints about common property quality are separate legal issues. Under STSMA Section 3(1), owners are liable for levies from the date of transfer, regardless of disputes. The purchaser cannot unilaterally withhold levies — doing so exposes them to interest charges, legal costs, and potential foreclosure by the body corporate. Their remedy is to: (1) raise the issue at a body corporate general meeting, (2) propose a special levy for tile replacement, (3) if the tiles are genuinely defective (not merely "substandard" in the owner's subjective opinion), claim against the developer's defects liability warranty. If the tiles meet the specification and SANS standards, the owner has no valid claim.

**Q: 55** We used the same tile specification in the show house as in the delivered units, but the batch numbers differ, and the show house tiles look slightly glossier. A buyer claims this is a "material difference" and wants compensation. Is batch variation a valid claim?
**A:** Batch variation in glaze appearance is an inherent characteristic of ceramic tile manufacturing. SANS 1449 and ISO 10545-16 allow for shade variation within defined tolerances (typically between V1 — uniform appearance — and V4 — substantial variation, as marked on the packaging). If the tiles delivered are from the same manufacturer, the same product line, and within the rated shade variation class, the buyer's claim has no technical basis. Your defence: produce the tile manufacturer's shade variation rating (V1–V4) and explain that the tiles delivered fall within the rated class. If the tiles are from different product lines or the variation exceeds the manufacturer's rating, you may have a legitimate quality issue. Always order the full tile quantity from a single production batch where possible.

### SECTION I: OCCUPANCY CERTIFICATES, PRACTICAL COMPLETION & DEFECTS LIABILITY

**Q: 56** The municipality's building inspector refused to issue an Occupation Certificate because the floor tiles in the fire escape stairwell don't have the required slip rating. We used standard polished porcelain. What slip rating is required for emergency escape routes?
**A:** SANS 10400-T (fire protection) and SANS 10107 require that escape routes have slip-resistant finishes, especially stair treads. For fire escape staircases, the minimum slip resistance is R10 (pendulum test value ≥ 45 wet), with R11 preferred for commercial buildings. Polished porcelain typically achieves only R9 (or less when wet). You must replace the stair tread tiles with R10 or R11 rated slip-resistant tiles, or apply a certified slip-resistant treatment to the existing tiles. The inspector is correct to withhold the Occupation Certificate — an unsafe fire escape is a life safety issue. Budget for replacement; slip treatments are often rejected by inspectors as temporary solutions.

**Q: 57** Our practical completion certificate was issued, but three weeks later, a purchaser found cracked tiles in the main bedroom. The tiling contractor says practical completion means their liability ended. Is this correct?
**A:** No. Practical completion under JBCC or NEC contracts marks the end of the construction period and triggers the defects liability period (DLP), typically 3–12 months. During the DLP, the contractor remains responsible for repairing defects that appear. Cracked tiles appearing three weeks after practical completion are almost certainly latent defects and fall squarely within the DLP. The contractor must repair them at their cost. Notify the contractor in writing within the notification period specified in your contract (typically 7–14 days of discovering the defect). If they refuse, you can engage another contractor and deduct the cost from retention monies or claim against their performance bond.

**Q: 58** We have a 6-month defects liability period in our contracts. A buyer reported a leaking shower 7 months after practical completion. The leak is caused by a failure in the waterproofing beneath the tiles. Who is liable — us, the tiler, or the waterproofing subcontractor?
**A:** After the DLP expires, the developer's contractual liability to the buyer for patent defects ends, but the NHBRC 5-year structural warranty may apply if the leak is caused by structural movement or a major structural waterproofing failure. If the waterproofing system was a separate subcontract ( specialised waterproofing applicator), their warranty typically runs for 10 years and is independent of the DLP. The buyer should claim against the waterproofing subcontractor's warranty first. If the leak is due to poor tile installation (incorrect falls, grout failure, no silicone at junctions), the tiler may be liable under their workmanship warranty. As developer, your liability depends on the cause — engage a forensic plumber to determine whether the leak is structural (NHBRC), waterproofing (10-year warranty), or installation (tiler's liability).

**Q: 59** The municipality won't issue the Occupation Certificate because the tiling contractor hasn't submitted their Occupational Health and Safety (OHS) file to the principal contractor. Can a subcontractor's paperwork block an OC?
**A:** Yes. Under the Construction Regulations 2014 (OHSA), the principal contractor must maintain a consolidated health and safety file for the project, including all subcontractor OHS files. The municipality's building inspector may request proof that the construction health and safety file is complete before issuing the Occupation Certificate — this is within their powers under SANS 10400-A. The tiling subcontractor must submit their OHS file, including risk assessments, method statements, and proof of inductions. This is not the tiler's choice — it's a legal requirement. Withhold final payment until the OHS file is submitted. If they refuse, you may need to reconstruct the file from site records.

**Q: 60** A unit failed final inspection because the tile layer used black grout instead of the specified grey grout. The difference is purely aesthetic. Can an aesthetic deviation block an Occupation Certificate?
**A:** In theory, an Occupation Certificate should only be withheld for non-compliance with the National Building Regulations (structural, fire, health, safety). A grout colour deviation is not a building regulation issue. However, if the approved plans specify "grey grout" and the building inspector is being pedantic, they may refuse the OC on the grounds that the completed building does not match approved plans. The practical resolution: submit a minor amendment application for the grout colour change (if your municipality requires this) or simply re-grout the affected areas to match the specification. For future projects, include a note on plans: "grout colour subject to final selection by architect" to avoid this issue.

### SECTION J: GEOTECHNICAL, CLIMATE & SITE-SPECIFIC ISSUES

**Q: 61** Our Pretoria development is built on expansive clay (smectite). The geotechnical report recommended a suspended floor slab, but we used a ground-bearing slab to save costs. Now tiles are cracking everywhere. What's our liability?
**A:** You are fully liable. Ignoring a geotechnical engineer's recommendation constitutes professional negligence. Expansive clay in Pretoria (the "Pretoria clay" formation) can heave 50mm–100mm seasonally as moisture content changes. A ground-bearing slab on this clay will crack, and no tile installation system can accommodate that movement. The NHBRC will likely classify this as a structural defect and require you to remediate. Remediation options: (1) underpin and convert to suspended slab (extremely expensive), (2) install a structural topping slab over the existing slab with movement joints, (3) in severe cases, demolish and rebuild correctly. Your professional indemnity insurance may cover the rectification cost, but expect a significant excess. Always follow geotechnical recommendations — the cost of a suspended slab is minor compared to remediation.

**Q: 62** We're building on the East Rand near Benoni where there are abandoned mine shafts. The DMR (Department of Mineral Resources) has flagged our site as "undermined." Do we need special foundations, and how does this affect floor tiles?
**A:** Yes. Undermined areas in Gauteng require a DMR mining risk assessment before construction. If the risk assessment confirms undermining, you may need: (1) compacted mine-fill or grout injection to stabilise voids, (2) specially designed foundations (raft or pile foundations) to span potential subsidence zones, (3) flexible building design to accommodate predicted ground movement. For floor tiles, you must use a crack-isolation membrane between the structural slab and the tile bed to accommodate differential movement. Specify a membrane rated for the predicted movement (typically 2mm–5mm). Do NOT use rigid adhesive systems — use a flexible, polymer-modified adhesive (C2TES1 classification). Tile cracking in undermined areas is common; the membrane is your best defence.

**Q: 63** Our Durban beachfront development has tiles lifting off the floor due to salt corrosion of the adhesive. The adhesive was a standard cement-based product. What should we have used?
**A:** Coastal environments with high airborne salt content (within 1km of the coastline) require specialised adhesive systems. Standard cement-based adhesives are susceptible to salt crystallisation within the adhesive bed, which causes expansion and debonding. You should have specified: (1) a cementitious adhesive classified as C2 (improved adhesive) with S1 (deformable) characteristics, (2) an epoxy-based adhesive for critical areas (though more expensive at R80–R120/m² material cost), (3) a salt-resistant primer applied to the concrete substrate before tiling. Additionally, the concrete substrate must have adequate cover over reinforcement (minimum 40mm in coastal zone 5) to prevent substrate spalling that destabilises the tile bed. Durban's coastal climate is one of the most hostile environments for tiling in South Africa.

**Q: 64** We built in the Free State where winter temperatures drop to -8°C. External porcelain tiles on the verandah are spalling (surface flaking) after two winters. Is this a tile quality issue or an installation issue?
**A:** This is likely both. Spalling of tiles in freeze-thaw conditions occurs when: (1) the tiles have high water absorption (>0.5% for porcelain, >3% for ceramic), allowing water to freeze within the tile body and cause surface delamination, (2) the installation did not include adequate expansion joints to accommodate thermal cycling, (3) water is pooling on the surface and penetrating through grout joints. For external tiles in freeze-thaw zones (Highveld, Free State, Northern Cape), you must specify frost-resistant porcelain with water absorption below 0.1% (ISO 10545-12 frost resistance tested). Install expansion joints at maximum 4.5m intervals in both directions, use a flexible grout, and ensure adequate falls (minimum 1:80) to prevent water pooling.

**Q: 65** Our Karoo development has extreme temperature swings — 40°C in summer, -5°C in winter. The tiles are making a "popping" sound during the day. Is this thermal expansion, and will they crack?
**A:** The "popping" sound is likely the tile bed releasing stress as the tiles expand under thermal load. In the Karoo, tiled surfaces can reach 60°C+ in summer, causing significant thermal expansion (porcelain expands approximately 0.006mm/m per °C). If the installation did not include adequate expansion joints, the accumulated stress causes either audible "popping" as the adhesive releases, or actual tile cracking/tenting. The expansion joint spacing should be reduced in extreme thermal environments: maximum 3m intervals in each direction for external tiles, 4.5m for internal tiles with direct solar exposure. Install movement joints NOW using a soft polyethylene rod and flexible polyurethane sealant. Without intervention, cracking is inevitable within one or two thermal cycles.

### SECTION K: SUBCONTRACTOR DEFAULT, LABOUR & DISPUTES

**Q: 66** Our tiling subcontractor went into business rescue after completing 60% of the work. They owe their tile suppliers R800,000, and the suppliers have placed a lien on the remaining tiles on site. Can we use those tiles to finish the project?
**A:** This is legally complex. Under business rescue (Chapter 6 of the Companies Act), a moratorium applies on all legal proceedings against the company, but it does not automatically extinguish supplier rights. If the tile supplier has a valid retention of title (ROT) clause in their supply contract, the tiles on site remain their property until paid for. You cannot simply use them — the supplier could claim theft. Options: (1) negotiate with the supplier to purchase the tiles directly (they may prefer partial payment to a total loss), (2) apply to the business rescue practitioner for release of the materials, (3) if the tiles were fully paid for by the subcontractor before business rescue, they form part of the rescue estate and you need the practitioner's consent. Engage an attorney specialising in business rescue immediately.

**Q: 67** The tiling crew on our site stopped work because they're demanding 13th cheques and the subcontractor says it's not in their contracts. We're 3 weeks from sectional title registration deadline. Can we dismiss them and bring in another crew?
**A:** The workers are employed by your subcontractor, not by you. You cannot directly dismiss them — that would constitute an unfair labour practice and potentially make you a "joint employer" under labour law. However, you CAN instruct your subcontractor to resolve the dispute or face termination of their contract for failure to perform. If the subcontractor's contract includes penalty clauses for delay, enforce them. If you need to engage a replacement subcontractor urgently, you must follow the contractual termination procedure (typically 7–14 days notice of breach with opportunity to remedy). The 13th cheque demand has no legal basis unless it's in the employment contract or an industry collective agreement — the tiling industry does not have a universal 13th cheque obligation under any bargaining council agreement.

**Q: 68** COSATU representatives arrived on our site demanding we sign a "project labour agreement" recognising their shop steward and agreeing to union-scale wages for all workers. Our tiling subcontractor uses non-union casual labour. Are we obligated to engage with COSATU?
**A:** If your development qualifies as a "designated employer" under the Labour Relations Act (typically 50+ employees including subcontractors), you may be subject to organisational rights applications. However, COSATU cannot unilaterally impose a project labour agreement without following the statutory process: they must represent a majority of workers on the site and apply to the CCMA or Labour Court for organisational rights. Your obligation is to your direct employees only. The subcontractor's labour relationship is with them, not you. That said, site disruptions from union activity are a reality. Engage a labour law attorney, document all interactions, and do not sign any agreement under duress. If violence or intimidation occurs, report it to SAPS immediately.

**Q: 69** Our tiling subcontractor's workers went on an unprotected strike, broke tiles on site, and damaged equipment. We have the incident on CCTV. What's our recourse against the workers and the subcontractor?
**A:** Unprotected strike action with property damage constitutes both a disciplinary offence and criminal conduct (malicious damage to property). You should: (1) lay criminal charges with SAPS for the damage, providing CCTV evidence, (2) claim damages from the subcontractor — they are vicariously liable for their employees' conduct during the course of employment, (3) if the subcontractor cannot pay, claim against their public liability insurance or your own Contractors All Risk (CAR) policy, (4) dismiss the striking workers (the subcontractor must follow fair procedure — they can dismiss for unprotected strike and gross misconduct). The subcontractor should also report the workers to the police. Your CAR policy typically covers malicious damage by third parties, subject to the excess.

**Q: 70** We engaged an independent tiling contractor through a labour broker. The broker didn't pay UIF or PAYE for the workers. Now SARS is auditing us as the "responsible party." Are we liable?
**A:** Under the Tax Administration Act and the Fourth Schedule of the Income Tax Act, if you engage workers through a labour broker and the broker fails to pay UIF/PAYE, SARS can hold you jointly and severally liable if you were aware or should have been aware of the non-compliance. The Labour Relations Act Amendment Act also imposes joint and several liability for labour broker clients in certain circumstances. Your defence: demonstrate that you verified the broker's SARS tax clearance and UIF registration before engagement, and that you paid all fees to the broker (who then failed to remit to SARS). However, SARS may still pursue you for the shortfall. Always verify broker compliance monthly and obtain proof of PAYE/UIF payments. Use only reputable, established labour brokers.

### SECTION L: INSURANCE, CURRENCY & FINANCIAL RISK

**Q: 71** Our Contractors All Risk (CAR) policy excludes "defective workmanship" — which means tile installation defects aren't covered. But the policy covers "resultant damage." If defective tiling causes water damage to the ceiling below, is the ceiling repair covered?
**A:** Yes, this is a classic CAR policy interpretation. CAR policies typically exclude the cost of rectifying the defective work itself (re-tiling) but cover the resultant damage caused by the defect (water-stained ceiling, damaged electrical wiring, warped cabinetry). Insurers call this the "defective workmanship exclusion with resultant damage extension." If leaking tiles damage the ceiling below, the insurer should pay for ceiling repair, repainting, and any electrical or furnishing damage, but NOT for re-tiling or re-waterproofing the shower. Always read your policy schedule carefully — some policies have a "total exclusion" that removes the resultant damage extension. Ensure your policy includes the extension.

**Q: 72** We need to import R5 million worth of tiles from Italy, with delivery in 6 months. The Rand/Euro rate is volatile. Should we take a forward exchange contract, and what does it cost?
**A:** A forward exchange contract (FEC) locks in today's exchange rate for a future payment, eliminating currency risk. For a 6-month FEC, the cost is the forward points (the interest rate differential between ZAR and EUR), which is typically 2%–4% worse than the spot rate, depending on the term and market conditions. For R5 million, a 3% premium equals R150,000 — your cost of certainty. Alternative: a currency option (more expensive but allows you to benefit if Rand strengthens), or a 50% hedge (hedge half, leave half exposed). Most major banks (Standard Bank, FNB, Nedbank) offer FECs for importers. You need a trade finance facility. For a 6-month horizon with current Rand volatility, hedging is strongly recommended.

**Q: 73** Our development finance bank requires "cost-to-complete" certificates every quarter. The quantity surveyor says we must include the full value of tiles already purchased but not yet installed. Is this correct?
**A:** Yes. Cost-to-complete (CTC) certificates must reflect the full project cost, including materials on site (MOS) that have been purchased but not yet installed. Tiles delivered to site and verified as present are a legitimate project cost, even if not yet laid. The CTC calculation is: total project budget minus costs incurred to date (including MOS) = cost to complete. The bank uses this to ensure the remaining facility is sufficient to finish the project. Ensure your quantity surveyor physically verifies tile stock on site (count pallets, check batch numbers) — banks may request photographic evidence. Tiles stored off-site (at a supplier's warehouse) are NOT included in CTC unless they are bonded and a valid cession of stock certificate exists.

**Q: 74** A tile fell from a balcony on our construction site and injured a passing pedestrian. Our CAR policy covers "public liability" up to R10 million. But the pedestrian is claiming R15 million. What happens now?
**A:** Your CAR policy's public liability limit is R10 million. The claimant's demand of R15 million exceeds the policy limit by R5 million. Your options: (1) negotiate a settlement within the policy limit (insurer's lawyers will attempt this), (2) if the claim proceeds to court and the judgment exceeds R10 million, you are personally liable for the shortfall (R5 million plus legal costs), (3) you may have separate public liability insurance that provides additional cover — check your corporate insurance portfolio. To avoid this exposure in future, carry a minimum R20 million public liability limit for high-rise construction, or purchase an umbrella liability policy that provides excess coverage above the CAR limit. Also ensure all balcony edges have proper edge protection until handrails are installed — falling objects are a leading cause of construction-related injury claims.

**Q: 75** Our professional indemnity (PI) insurer has excluded "waterproofing design" from our policy after a previous claim. We're now designing all wet area waterproofing ourselves. Are we exposed?
**A:** Yes, significantly. Without PI coverage for waterproofing design, any failure in a waterproofing system you designed exposes your company's balance sheet directly. A single failed basement waterproofing system can result in claims of R2–R5 million for water damage, mould remediation, and content replacement. Options: (1) engage a specialist waterproofing consultant (they carry their own PI) and have them sign off on your designs, (2) obtain a separate PI policy extension for waterproofing (expensive but necessary), (3) use proprietary waterproofing systems where the manufacturer provides a system warranty (e.g., Sika, Mapei) contingent on their approved applicator installing it. Never design waterproofing in-house without either PI coverage or specialist consultant sign-off.

### SECTION M: TRANSFER DUTY, VAT & TAX STRUCTURES

**Q: 76** We're VAT-registered developers selling sectional title units. The sales contract says "VAT inclusive." Do we charge VAT on the full selling price including the tile specification, or is the tile cost somehow separated?
**A:** As a VAT-registered developer, you charge VAT on the full supply of the residential unit, which is a single composite supply. You cannot separate the tile cost and apply a different VAT treatment — the sale of the completed unit is standard-rated at 15% VAT on the total contract price. The tiles form part of the "goods" supplied as part of the construction and sale. However, you can claim input VAT on the tile purchase, the installation labour, and all other development costs. The "VAT inclusive" price means the stated price already includes 15% VAT — the VAT-exclusive price is the stated price divided by 1.15. Ensure your tax invoice to the purchaser clearly states the VAT amount.

**Q: 77** We sold a unit and charged VAT, but the buyer is an individual purchasing their primary residence. They claim we should have charged transfer duty instead of VAT. Which is correct?
**A:** VAT and transfer duty are mutually exclusive on the same transaction. If you are a VAT-registered developer selling a unit in the course of your enterprise, the sale is a VATable supply at 15%, regardless of the buyer's status. The buyer does not pay transfer duty on a VAT transaction — transfer duty only applies to sales by non-vendors (individuals selling their own home, for example). The buyer may be confused because they are used to paying transfer duty on second-hand home purchases. Explain that as a VAT-registered developer, VAT applies, and the advantage to the buyer is that transfer duty (which has its own sliding scale and can exceed VAT on high-value properties) does not apply. The buyer cannot "choose" transfer duty over VAT — the tax treatment is determined by the seller's status.

**Q: 78** We temporarily let 10 completed units for 8 months while waiting for buyers (rent-to-own scheme). SARS says we must pay VAT on the rental income AND the eventual sale is now a "second supply" subject to transfer duty. Is this correct?
**A:** SARS's position is partially correct. Rental income from residential letting is exempt from VAT (not zero-rated — exempt). This means you cannot claim input VAT on costs related to the letting period. When you subsequently sell the unit, if the letting period exceeds 6 months and the unit was used for residential letting, SARS may argue that the subsequent sale is a "second supply of a residential property" which is also VAT-exempt (not standard-rated). This means you cannot charge VAT on the sale price, and you must deregister those units from VAT. The buyer would pay transfer duty instead. This is a major tax trap for developers — always structure rent-to-own schemes with sale agreements signed before letting begins, or ensure the letting period is under 6 months to preserve VAT status. Consult a property tax specialist before implementing any letting strategy.

**Q: 79** Our SPV (special purpose vehicle) developer company has never traded before this project and has no VAT registration. Must we register for VAT, and what's the threshold?
**A:** The compulsory VAT registration threshold is R1 million in taxable supplies over any 12-month period. For a development with total revenue exceeding R1 million, you must register for VAT. You can also register voluntarily if your taxable supplies exceed R50,000 in the past 12 months (which a single unit sale would trigger). Voluntary registration allows you to claim input VAT on development costs from day one, which improves cash flow significantly. Apply for VAT registration at least 2 months before the first unit sale — SARS registration can take 4–6 weeks. If you sell a unit before VAT registration, you cannot charge VAT or claim input VAT on that sale. The sale will be treated as a non-vendor sale subject to transfer duty.

**Q: 80** We imported tiles and paid customs duty plus 15% VAT at the border. Our clearing agent says we can claim the import VAT as input VAT. What's the process?
**A:** Import VAT (paid at customs) is claimable as input VAT on your VAT201 return, provided you have the SARS customs receipt (the SAD 500 form with the VAT payment stamp) and the VAT number is correctly recorded. The clearing agent must ensure your VAT number appears on the customs declaration. You cannot claim VAT on customs duty itself — duty is a cost, not a VAT. The input VAT claim is made in the VAT period when the goods are imported (not when sold). If you are not yet VAT-registered when the tiles are imported, you lose the input VAT claim permanently — another reason to register for VAT before importing materials. Keep all customs documentation for 5 years — SARS audits import VAT claims rigorously.

### SECTION N: CONSTRUCTION WASTE, ASBESTOS & SAFETY

**Q: 81** Our demolition contractor found asbestos-cement floor tiles (19x19cm patterned tiles, typical of the 1970s) under the existing floor covering. What are our legal obligations under the Asbestos Regulations 2020?
**A:** Asbestos-containing floor tiles are regulated under the Asbestos Abatement Regulations, 2020. You must: (1) immediately cease work in the affected area and cordon it off, (2) engage a registered asbestos inspector to confirm the asbestos content (polarised light microscopy), (3) if confirmed, appoint a registered asbestos removal contractor (not a general demolition contractor), (4) notify the Department of Employment and Labour 14 days before removal, (5) prepare an asbestos removal plan and obtain written approval, (6) dispose of the asbestos waste at a licensed hazardous waste facility (not a general landfill — Holfontein near Vereeniging and Vissershok near Cape Town are licensed). Removal costs are R150–R400/m² depending on access and encapsulation requirements. Failure to comply is a criminal offence under OHSA with fines up to R5 million and/or 5 years imprisonment.

**Q: 82** We're generating approximately 30 tonnes of tile offcuts and broken tiles from our development. The waste contractor wants to take it to a general landfill, but we've heard tile waste is classified as "building and demolition waste" with specific disposal requirements. Is this correct?
**A:** Building and demolition waste (including tile offcuts) is classified as General Waste under the National Waste Management Act, not hazardous waste, and can be disposed of at licensed general landfill sites that accept builders' rubble. However, not all landfills accept construction waste — you must use a licensed construction and demolition (C&D) waste facility. Some municipalities (City of Cape Town, City of Johannesburg) require waste manifest documentation tracking the waste from site to landfill. The National Norms and Standards for Assessment of Waste for Landfill Disposal require that you classify the waste using the SANS 10234 waste classification system. Tile offcuts are typically inert (Class A waste) and can be crushed for recycling as aggregate. Consider engaging a recycling facility — crushed ceramic tile is used in road base and concrete aggregate, and recycling is cheaper than landfill.

**Q: 83** A worker on site slipped on tile adhesive that had spilled on the floor and fractured his wrist. He was not wearing the safety boots we provided. Is this a COIDA claim only, or can he sue us for negligence?
**A:** The worker's exclusive remedy is through the Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act (COIDA) — he cannot sue you for damages in civil court (COIDA Section 35 provides the employer immunity from civil claims for workplace injuries). You must report the injury to the Compensation Commissioner within 7 days (Form W.CL.2), and the worker receives compensation through the fund. However, if the Department of Employment and Labour inspects and finds you failed to maintain a safe workplace (spilled adhesive not cleaned up, no housekeeping procedures), you can be prosecuted under OHSA and fined. The fact that he wasn't wearing safety boots is a mitigating factor but does not absolve you of the housekeeping obligation. Improve site housekeeping immediately.

**Q: 84** Our site manager was arrested because a subcontractor's employee fell through an unprotected floor opening where tiles were being laid. The charge is "contravention of OHSA Construction Regulations." What's the potential liability?
**A:** This is extremely serious. Under the Construction Regulations 2014, any person who controls a construction site (including the developer's representative) has a legal duty to ensure health and safety. Falling from an unprotected opening is a "fall from height" incident — the regulations require all openings to be covered or guarded while work is in progress. The site manager faces: (1) criminal prosecution under OHSA with potential fines up to R100,000 and/or 2 years imprisonment for a first offence, (2) if gross negligence is proven (recklessness), up to R2 million and/or 5 years imprisonment, (3) permanent criminal record affecting future employment. The company also faces fines. Engage a criminal attorney specialising in occupational health and safety immediately. The subcontractor shares liability — they are required to have their own health and safety plan and supervision.

**Q: 85** Tile cutting on site generates silica dust. We provided dust masks but workers say they're uncomfortable and don't wear them. A health inspector is visiting next week. What are the consequences if she finds workers cutting tiles dry without masks?
**A:** Silica dust exposure is regulated under the Hazardous Chemical Substances Regulations (HCS) and the OHSA. Respirable crystalline silica is a known carcinogen. If the inspector finds workers exposed to silica dust without respiratory protection: (1) she can issue a prohibition notice — immediate shutdown of tile cutting operations, (2) fines of up to R50,000 per contravention per worker exposed, (3) potential criminal prosecution of the employer, (4) the company may be listed on the "name and shame" register. Best practice: eliminate the hazard by requiring WET cutting only (water suppresses 99% of silica dust), which removes the need for respiratory protection in most cases. If dry cutting is unavoidable, you must conduct air monitoring, provide approved respiratory protection (not dust masks — P2 or FFP3 rated respirators), and train workers on proper use. Wet cutting is the only sustainable solution.

### SECTION O: DISABILITY ACCESS, SECTIONAL TITLE & NEIGHBOUR DISPUTES

**Q: 86** SANS 10400 Part S requires tactile paving at level changes in our apartment building lobby. We've installed polished porcelain tiles throughout. Can we install tactile indicators on top of the existing tiles, or must we replace the floor finish?
**A:** Tactile paving (truncated dome or bar patterns) can be surface-applied to existing tiled floors using self-adhesive polyurethane or stainless-steel tactile indicators. You do not need to replace the floor finish. The indicators must comply with SANS 784 (tactile paving indicators), with a luminance contrast of at least 30% against the background surface, and height of 4mm–5mm. They must be installed at all changes in level (single step, ramp onset), at the top and bottom of staircases, and along accessible routes. Polished porcelain may present a slip hazard if wet — ensure the tactile indicators themselves have a slip rating of at least R10 and that the surrounding floor achieves R9 minimum. Surface-applied indicators cost R80–R150 per unit; a full lobby installation is R3,000–R8,000.

**Q: 87** A purchaser in our wheelchair-friendly unit says the bathroom floor tiles are "too cold" and wants us to install underfloor heating at our cost, claiming it's a "reasonable accommodation" under the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act (PEPUDA). Do we have to comply?
**A:** PEPUDA requires reasonable accommodation for persons with disabilities, but "too cold tiles" does not constitute discrimination or a barrier to equal enjoyment of the accommodation. Under SANS 10400-S, accessible units must meet specific dimensional requirements (door widths, turning circles, grab rail positions, floor surface firmness and stability). Thermal comfort is NOT a specified requirement. If the tiles meet the slip resistance, firmness, and surface stability requirements of SANS 10400-S, you have complied. Underfloor heating is an amenity, not a disability access requirement. If the buyer requires underfloor heating for a specific medical condition (circulatory disorder), they may request it as a reasonable accommodation, but the cost would typically be borne by the requestor unless you agreed otherwise in the sale contract.

**Q: 88** Our development's homeowners association (HOA) constitution requires that all external paving and driveway tiles be "earth-toned clay pavers." A buyer wants to install grey porcelain tiles. Can the HOA enforce this restriction after transfer?
**A:** Yes, if the title deed includes a condition binding the property to the HOA's architectural guidelines, and the buyer was made aware of these restrictions before purchase. The HOA can enforce aesthetic restrictions through the title deed conditions, and non-compliance can result in enforcement action including court orders for removal. However, the restrictions must be reasonable and cannot be discriminatory or capricious. "Earth-toned clay pavers" is a reasonable aesthetic guideline. As developer, you must ensure all buyers are provided with a copy of the HOA design guidelines before signing the sale agreement. Failure to disclose HOA restrictions can result in a CPA claim for misrepresentation — the buyer may rescind the contract if they were unaware of the restrictions.

**Q: 89** A neighbour's property was damaged when our tiling contractor's waterproofing failed and water seeped through the common wall. The neighbour is suing us, not the contractor. Can they do that?
**A:** Yes. Under the neighbour law principles (derived from both common law and the National Building Regulations), an owner of a property is liable for damage caused to an adjoining property by defects in their building, regardless of whether they caused the defect personally. This is based on the principle that a property owner must not use their property in a way that causes harm to neighbours (the sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas principle). You must pay the neighbour's damages and then claim an indemnity from your tiling contractor and/or waterproofing subcontractor. Ensure your sale contracts with buyers include a warranty that the waterproofing complies with SANS 10400-XA and that the buyer must maintain it — this protects you from claims by subsequent owners.

**Q: 90** We're building a row of townhouses with zero-lot-line walls. The tile layer needs to work from the neighbour's side to install tiles on the party wall. The neighbour refuses access. What can we do?
**A:** Under common law, you have no automatic right of access to a neighbour's property unless an access servitude is registered against their title deed. For zero-lot-line construction, you should have secured a temporary access servitude or "building line relaxation" before construction began. Without it: (1) negotiate with the neighbour — offer compensation for the inconvenience, (2) if the neighbour unreasonably refuses, apply to the High Court for a temporary access order under the Access to Neighbouring Land Act, which allows courts to grant access for essential construction work if the neighbour's refusal is unreasonable, (3) as a last resort, use a swing-stage or cherry picker from your side if physically possible. Prevention: always negotiate access servitudes during the planning phase, before breaking ground.

### SECTION P: TITLE DEED RESTRICTIONS, BANKING & ARBITRATION

**Q: 91** The title deed for our development land has a restrictive condition: "No building shall exceed two storeys in height." We want to build four storeys. Can we apply to have the restriction removed, and how long does this take?
**A:** Title deed restrictions can only be removed by: (1) application to the High Court for removal (if the restriction is obsolete or unreasonable), which takes 6–12 months and costs R100,000+, or (2) application to the original creator of the restriction or their successors (if a private party imposed it), or (3) in some cases, the local municipality can assist if the restriction was imposed by a predecessor municipality. If the restriction was imposed by a previous landowner for aesthetic or neighbourhood character reasons, removal is difficult — the court will consider whether the restriction still serves a legitimate purpose and whether neighbouring properties would be prejudiced. Engage a conveyancer specialising in title restrictions before purchasing land with restrictive conditions.

**Q: 92** Our development finance agreement has an arbitration clause referring disputes to AFSA (Association of Arbitrators). A tile supplier has sued us in the Magistrate's Court for non-payment. Can we enforce the arbitration clause and stop the court action?
**A:** If the tile supplier is not a party to your development finance agreement, the arbitration clause does not bind them — they can sue you in court. Arbitration clauses only bind the parties who signed the agreement. If the supplier's contract with you (or your main contractor) also contains an arbitration clause, you can apply for a stay of the court proceedings under Section 6 of the Arbitration Act. The court will grant the stay unless there are compelling reasons not to. However, if there is no arbitration agreement with the supplier, you must defend the court action. For future developments, include arbitration clauses in all supply agreements, not just the finance agreement.

**Q: 93** We appointed an arbitrator through AFSA for a dispute with our tiling contractor. The arbitrator's fees are R4,500 per hour, and the hearing is scheduled for 5 days. The dispute is worth R800,000. Is arbitration cost-effective?
**A:** In this case, probably not. Five days at R4,500/hour equals approximately R180,000 (assuming 8-hour days), plus your attorney's fees (R3,000–R5,000/hour for preparation and hearing), plus the arbitrator's preparation time. Total arbitration costs could reach R400,000–R500,000 for an R800,000 dispute. AFSA has a "fast-track" procedure for disputes under R1 million with capped costs. Alternatively, consider: (1) mediation through AFSA or private mediation (typically R15,000–R30,000 total), (2) expert determination (a single expert decides the technical issue), (3) the Small Claims Court if the amount falls within jurisdiction. Arbitration is excellent for complex, high-value disputes with technical issues, but for smaller disputes, mediation or court litigation may be more cost-effective.

**Q: 94** Our bank won't release the next tranche of development finance because the quantity surveyor's cost report shows we've exceeded the tile budget by 18%. The overrun is due to a specification upgrade. Can the bank legally withhold funding?
**A:** Yes. Development finance agreements typically include a "cost overrun" clause allowing the bank to withhold further drawdowns if the project exceeds budget by more than a specified percentage (commonly 5%–10%). The bank's concern is that their security (the completed development) may be insufficient to cover the loan if costs spiral. To release the tranche, you must either: (1) inject additional equity to cover the overrun, (2) provide additional security (another property or personal surety), (3) reduce costs elsewhere in the project to bring the overall budget back in line, or (4) negotiate a facility increase (new credit application, new valuations). The bank is contractually entitled to withhold funds — this is not a dispute, it's standard facility management. Always obtain bank approval before specification changes that affect cost.

**Q: 95** The bank requires that our development company directors personally guarantee the development loan. We're uncomfortable with this. Is there any way to avoid personal suretyship?
**A:** For first-time or smaller developers, personal suretyship is almost always required by South African banks — it's non-negotiable unless you have significant track record and balance sheet strength. For experienced developers with multiple completed projects: (1) offer a corporate guarantee from a listed parent company instead, (2) increase the loan-to-value ratio (more equity, less debt), (3) provide additional security in the form of unbonded properties, (4) use a mezzanine lender for part of the funding (more expensive but less restrictive on suretyship), (5) as a last resort, negotiate a "limited suretyship" capped at a specific amount rather than unlimited joint and several suretyship. Never sign unlimited suretyship without understanding that your personal assets (house, savings) are at risk if the project fails.

### SECTION Q: WATERPROOFING WARRANTIES, MUNICIPAL CONNECTIONS & ENERGY

**Q: 96** The waterproofing subcontractor provided a "10-year waterproofing guarantee" for our bathroom floors. Who is the guarantee actually in favour of — us as the developer, or the end purchaser?
**A:** Unless the guarantee is specifically ceded to the purchaser, it is a contract between the waterproofing subcontractor and you as the developer. The end purchaser has no direct contractual right to enforce it. However, you can cede the guarantee to the purchaser at transfer — this is best practice and adds value to the sale. The guarantee should specify: (1) what is covered (water ingress through the waterproofing membrane only, not through plumbing leaks or structural cracks), (2) maintenance requirements (re-sealant at junctions every 3–5 years), (3) the claims procedure (notification in writing, inspection by the guarantor), (4) exclusions (damage from structural movement, alterations by the owner). A 10-year guarantee is market standard for torch-on and liquid membranes in South Africa.

**Q: 97** We've been waiting 5 months for electricity connection from City Power. The units are complete, buyers are clamouring, and we're paying interest on the full facility. Can we issue temporary occupation and use generators?
**A:** Issuing occupation without a valid Occupation Certificate is illegal under the National Building Regulations and the municipal by-laws. However, some municipalities will issue a "temporary occupation certificate" or "conditional approval" if the only outstanding item is the electricity connection, provided: (1) fire safety systems are operational, (2) alternative power is provided (generators), (3) the municipality confirms the connection is imminent (within 30–60 days), (4) buyers sign an acknowledgement of temporary conditions. Not all municipalities offer this — City of Johannesburg has been more flexible than Ekurhuleni or Tshwane in recent years. Contact the municipal building control manager and request a meeting. The cost of running generators for common area services (R5,000–R15,000/day for a 50-unit block) is usually less than the interest cost of delayed sales.

**Q: 98** Our development includes a solar PV system for common area power. The body corporate wants to tile the roof area around the panels with porcelain pavers to create a "roof terrace." Will this affect the solar system's performance warranty?
**A:** Installing porcelain pavers around solar panels does not directly affect the PV system's performance if the panels themselves are not shaded or covered. However: (1) the additional dead load of the pavers on the roof slab must be checked by a structural engineer — porcelain pavers at 25kg/m² plus screed and adhesive can add 40–50kg/m², (2) the roof waterproofing must be intact before tiling — any penetration for fixing the paver support system compromises the waterproofing, (3) the solar installer may void their warranty if the panel access for maintenance is obstructed (panels need 500mm clear walkways for cleaning and maintenance), (4) SANS 10400-X requires roof insulation — tiling the roof surface may trap heat and reduce panel efficiency by 5%–10%. Specify elevated pedestal paver systems that allow airflow beneath the tiles.

**Q: 99** The body corporate of our completed scheme wants to install EV charging stations in the basement parking. The floor is tiled with ceramic tiles. Do they need to lift the tiles to run cabling?
**A:** EV charging station cabling can be surface-mounted on walls and columns in the basement without lifting floor tiles, provided the cable route follows existing wall/ceiling cavities. However, if the charging station locations require new floor-mounted distribution boards or floor boxes, the tiles must be lifted in those specific areas. The body corporate should engage an electrician to design a cable route that minimises floor penetration. Any penetrations through the floor slab must be fire-stopped (SANS 10400-T) to maintain fire compartmentation. The cost of lifting and re-tiling is approximately R500–R800/m². For a basement with 50 charging points, budget R200,000–R400,000 for floor-related work if surface routing is not possible.

**Q: 100** We installed a greywater recycling system, and the treated water is being used for toilet flushing. The bathroom floor tiles are showing efflorescence around the toilet bases. Is the recycled water causing this?
**A:** Treated greywater typically has higher dissolved solids and mineral content than potable water, which can accelerate efflorescence if there are any leaks at the toilet base seal. However, the root cause is more likely a failed wax ring or silicone seal at the toilet-to-floor junction, allowing water (grey or otherwise) to seep into the tile bed. The salts in the greywater crystallise as the water evaporates, producing the white efflorescence. Solution: (1) reseal all toilet bases with marine-grade polyurethane sealant, (2) check the toilet pan connectors for leaks, (3) if greywater is indeed leaking, the system must be redesigned to ensure positive sealing at all junctions. Efflorescence from greywater is not a structural defect but an ongoing maintenance issue — the body corporate must budget for annual re-sealing.

### SECTION R: LOAD SHEDDING, ENERGY & CONTINGENCY PLANNING

**Q: 101** Load shedding has delayed our project by 6 weeks. We have a penalty clause of R15,000/day for late completion to the main contractor. Can we claim the full 6 weeks from Eskom or the municipality?
**A:** No. You cannot claim from Eskom or the municipality for load shedding delays unless there is a specific contractual relationship with them (which there isn't for a private development). Your recourse depends on your contract terms. Under JBCC 2014 (and later editions), load shedding is a "change in legislation" or "statutory change" event that entitles the contractor to a time extension but not necessarily cost compensation (check your contract's Schedule of Variables). The penalty clause may not be enforceable against the contractor if the delay was caused by a compensable delay event. Review your contracts: if the penalty clause is "liquidated damages," they must be a genuine pre-estimate of loss — you cannot profit from the penalty. For the contractor's delay claim, you need a concurrent delay analysis to apportion responsibility.

**Q: 102** We installed a generator backup system, but the fuel fumes are discolouring the lobby floor tiles (travertine-look porcelain). The tiles have a matte finish. Is this permanent damage?
**A:** Generator exhaust fumes contain hydrocarbons and sulphur compounds that can deposit as a thin film on porous or textured tile surfaces, causing a yellow-brown discolouration. On porcelain tiles (even matte finish), this is typically a surface deposit, not permanent staining, because porcelain has near-zero porosity. Clean the affected tiles with an alkaline degreaser (pH 11–13) followed by a neutral cleaner. If the tiles are unglazed porcelain with a textured surface, the deposits may have settled in the surface texture — use a stiff nylon brush. To prevent recurrence: (1) extend the generator exhaust pipe to discharge outside the building or above roof level, (2) ensure adequate ventilation in the generator room, (3) consider a catalytic converter on the exhaust. This is not a tile defect — it's an environmental exposure issue.

**Q: 103** During Stage 6 load shedding, our concrete supplier couldn't deliver screed for the tile bed. The screed that was already placed set too quickly and cracked extensively. Is the concrete supplier liable?
**A:** The concrete supplier is liable only if the screed mix was defective (incorrect cement content, excessive water, or inadequate curing time specification). However, rapid setting during load shedding is an environmental issue, not a product defect. The concrete supplier's terms of sale typically exclude liability for delays caused by force majeure events. Your claim would be against your contractor for failing to programme screed placement to avoid load shedding periods, or for failing to have generator backup for critical concrete pours. Under SANS 1200, screed must be cured for a minimum period (typically 7 days for light traffic, 28 days for tiling) — load shedding should not interrupt curing if proper wet-curing methods are used. The cracked screed must be removed and replaced before tiling.

**Q: 104** We designed the building with underfloor heating beneath the tiles. During load shedding, the heating is off for 4–6 hours at a time. Will the thermal cycling (heating/cooling) damage the tiles or adhesive?
**A:** No. Underfloor heating systems cycle on and off naturally as the thermostat controls them. A 4–6 hour off-period during load shedding is no different from the system being off overnight. Standard porcelain tiles with a flexible adhesive (C2TES1) and appropriate expansion joints can handle normal thermal cycling without damage. The only risk is if the underfloor heating is turned on to maximum immediately after load shedding ends, causing rapid thermal shock. Program the thermostat to ramp up gradually (no more than 5°C per hour). Ensure the tile adhesive and grout are rated for underfloor heating (temperature resistance to 45°C surface temperature). Most quality S1 classified adhesives in South Africa are UFH-compatible.

**Q: 105** Load shedding caused our site security system to fail, and thieves stole R400,000 worth of imported tiles from site overnight. Our insurance policy has a "security warranty" requiring 24/7 armed response. Does the power failure void our claim?
**A:** The insurer may attempt to repudiate the claim on the grounds that the security warranty was breached. However, if the security system failure was solely attributable to load shedding (a nationwide event beyond your control) and you had taken reasonable alternative measures (backup battery for the alarm, patrolling guards during outages), you have a strong argument that the breach was not material or wilful. South African courts have increasingly recognised load shedding as an extraordinary circumstance. To support your claim: (1) provide evidence that the alarm battery was functional (it may have simply exceeded capacity during extended Stage 6), (2) show that guards were present but overwhelmed, (3) demonstrate that the security system was maintained and tested regularly. If the insurer repudiates, escalate to the Ombudsman for Short-Term Insurance.

### SECTION S: UNUSUAL EDGE CASES, RURAL ISSUES & TRIBAL LAND

**Q: 106** We're developing on tribal land in KZN under a Permission to Occupy (PTO) from the traditional council. The PTO says nothing about building standards. Do SANS 10400 and the NHBRC still apply?
**A:** The National Building Regulations (SANS 10400) and NHBRC requirements apply to ALL new residential construction in South Africa, regardless of land tenure. A Permission to Occupy does not exempt you from national law. However, enforcement on tribal land is often weaker because the municipal building control office may not have jurisdiction or capacity in those areas. If you are selling units commercially, the NHBRC and CPA definitely apply — you cannot sell homes to the public without NHBRC enrolment and CPA compliance. If the development is for communal or traditional council use, the legal position is greyer, but SANS 10400 standards should still be followed for safety. The Ingonyama Trust and traditional councils have their own approval processes that run parallel to municipal requirements — you need both.

**Q: 107** A traditional council in the Eastern Cape is demanding "ischibi" (a traditional levy of R50,000) before we can start construction on communal land we've already leased. Is this legal?
**A:** The payment of traditional levies or "ischibi" is a customary practice recognised in many rural areas of the Eastern Cape and KZN. Whether it is legally enforceable depends on the terms of your lease agreement with the traditional council and whether the levy was disclosed upfront. If the lease explicitly requires the payment of traditional levies, you are contractually bound. If not, the council cannot unilaterally impose new financial obligations after the lease is signed. However, refusing to pay may result in social and political obstacles that stall your project. Many developers budget for these levies as a cost of doing business in rural areas — they are typically 1%–3% of project value. Obtain a written receipt and ensure the payment is acknowledged in the lease. Do not pay cash without documentation.

**Q: 108** We're building a luxury lodge in a private game reserve near Hoedspruit. There are no municipal services and no building inspector. Do we need to comply with SANS 10400 at all?
**A:** Yes. SANS 10400 is part of the National Building Regulations, which apply throughout South Africa. The absence of municipal building control does not exempt you. If you do not obtain a building plan approval and inspection, you face: (1) inability to obtain insurance for the completed building, (2) liability if any guest is injured due to non-compliant construction, (3) difficulties when selling the property in future (no approved plans means the building is "illegal" and the purchaser cannot obtain a bond), (4) potential prosecution if the local municipality later extends its services and discovers unapproved construction. For game reserves in Limpopo, engage a private building inspector registered with the South African Council for the Project and Construction Management Professions (SACPCMP) to conduct independent inspections and issue a compliance certificate.

**Q: 109** Our development is in a mining town where the mine owns all the land and leases plots to employees. The mine requires us to use their "approved" tile supplier, who charges 40% above market. Is this an abuse of dominance under the Competition Act?
**A:** If the mine has a dominant position in the relevant market (i.e., they control virtually all residential land in the town), requiring the use of a specific supplier at inflated prices may constitute an abuse of dominance under Section 8 of the Competition Act — specifically, "excluding competitors" or "an excessive price." However, proving abuse of dominance requires demonstrating that the mine's conduct is both anti-competitive and has no efficiency justification. The mine may argue that their supplier requirement ensures quality control and bulk purchasing efficiency. Before lodging a complaint with the Competition Commission, gather evidence: (1) the price differential between the approved supplier and market alternatives, (2) any evidence that the mine benefits financially from the arrangement (kickbacks), (3) attempts to negotiate with the mine. Also consider approaching the Minerals Council for mediation — mining companies are sensitive to competition complaints.

**Q: 110** We're building on a farm outside Port Elizabeth that has no municipal connection. We're using a borehole for water and septic tanks for sewage. The tile supplier says their warranty is void if tiles are installed in areas with "aggressive groundwater." How do we test for this?
**A:** "Aggressive groundwater" refers to water with high sulphate content (above 500mg/L), low pH (below 6.5), or high chloride content that attacks cement-based adhesives and grouts. To test: (1) take a groundwater sample from your borehole, (2) send it to a SANAS-accredited laboratory for full chemical analysis (SANS 10802 standard), (3) specifically request sulphate (SO4), chloride (Cl), pH, and total dissolved solids (TDS). If sulphates exceed 500mg/L, you must use sulphate-resistant adhesive (SR classification, available from major manufacturers), or switch to an epoxy-based adhesive system which is chemically inert. If chlorides exceed 200mg/L in a coastal environment, use corrosion-resistant fixings. The test costs approximately R1,500–R3,000 and takes 5–7 working days. It's essential for farm and rural developments.

### SECTION T: COMPLEX LIABILITY, INSURANCE & LEGAL TRAPS

**Q: 111** A tile in a common area lobby came loose, and a visitor tripped and broke their hip. They are suing the body corporate, the developer (us), and the tiling contractor. Who is actually liable?
**A:** Liability depends on when the incident occurred and what caused the tile to come loose. If within the defects liability period: the tiling contractor is primarily liable for defective workmanship; the developer is secondarily liable as the employer. If after the DLP: the body corporate is liable for failure to maintain common property under STSMA Section 3(1)(b), unless the defect is structural (NHBRC 5-year warranty). The developer's liability post-transfer is limited to: (1) undisclosed latent defects known at the time of sale (common law), (2) NHBRC structural warranty claims, (3) CPA claims for defects present at sale. If the tile came loose due to substrate failure (structural), the NHBRC warranty applies and the developer remains liable. If due to poor adhesion or lack of expansion joints, it's the contractor's workmanship. Each defendant's attorney will argue for apportionment under the Apportionment of Damages Act.

**Q: 112** Our development was completed and all units sold. Two years later, a body corporate claims the waterproofing in the basement has failed and water is coming up through the floor tiles. The waterproofing contractor provided a 10-year warranty. Can the body corporate claim directly against the contractor?
**A:** If the 10-year waterproofing warranty was ceded to the body corporate at transfer, yes — they can claim directly. If not, the body corporate must claim against the developer, who then claims against the contractor. However, many developers do not properly cede warranties. Under common law, the body corporate has no direct contractual relationship with the waterproofing contractor unless the warranty was assigned. The practical path: the body corporate claims against the developer, the developer invokes the waterproofing warranty against the contractor, and the contractor repairs. If the developer has dissolved the SPV (common practice), the body corporate may be left with no recourse unless they can prove the developer knew of the defect and failed to disclose it (fraudulent concealment). Always cede all warranties to the body corporate before dissolving the SPV.

**Q: 113** We sold a unit with a "developer's warranty" that covered "all building defects for 5 years." A buyer claims a cracked tile in year 4. We believe it's due to the owner dropping a heavy object. Does the warranty cover impact damage?
**A:** A generic "all building defects" warranty is dangerously broad. Unless the warranty explicitly excludes impact damage, abuse, or accidental damage, a court may interpret it as covering any defect that manifests during the warranty period — including the cracked tile. Your defence: commission an expert assessment of the crack pattern. Impact cracks are typically radial (spider-web) with a central impact point, whereas adhesion failure or substrate movement cracks follow different patterns. If the crack is impact-related, it is not a "building defect" — it is damage caused by the owner's negligence. However, the burden of proving this may fall on you. For future developments, define the warranty precisely: "This warranty covers defects in materials and workmanship only. It excludes damage caused by impact, abuse, negligence, alterations, or failure to maintain."

**Q: 114** A tile installer on site was an independent contractor, not an employee. He injured his back lifting a tile box. He's claiming against our Workers Compensation fund, but we never registered him because he's independent. What now?
**A:** COIDA only covers employees, not independent contractors. If the tile installer is genuinely independent (registered as a business, provides own tools, controls how the work is done, bears his own financial risk), he cannot claim from your COID fund. However, he may sue you in civil court for negligence if you controlled the work environment (e.g., you required him to lift heavy boxes without assistance, the site was unsafe). The line between employee and independent contractor is often blurred in construction. If the Labour Court finds he was a "deemed employee" (you controlled his hours, methods, and provided materials), you face: (1) COIDA penalties for non-registration, (2) liability for the injury, (3) potential prosecution. Always ensure independent contractors have their own COIDA or personal accident insurance, and document the independence relationship in writing.

**Q: 115** We're being sued by a purchaser for "defective tiles" but we didn't manufacture the tiles — we bought them from a local distributor. Can we join the distributor and the Italian manufacturer as third parties to the action?
**A:** Yes. Under the CPA, the consumer has a claim against the supplier in the supply chain (you as the developer/seller), and you have a right of recourse against the upstream suppliers — the distributor and the manufacturer. You can issue a third-party notice joining the distributor and manufacturer to the litigation. The CPA provides that all parties in the supply chain are jointly and severally liable for product defects. If the tiles are genuinely defective (manufacturing flaw, not installation error), the manufacturer or distributor should indemnify you. However, joining an Italian manufacturer is complex — you may need to serve proceedings in Italy, and the manufacturer may argue that South African courts do not have jurisdiction. Your supply agreement with the distributor should include an indemnity for product defects and a jurisdiction clause favouring South Africa.

### SECTION U: PROCUREMENT, SPECIFICATION & DESIGN DECISIONS

**Q: 116** Our architect specified large-format 900x900mm porcelain tiles for the lobby without consulting the structural engineer. The tiles weigh 28kg/m² plus adhesive and screed. The suspended slab was designed for 1.5kPa live load. Have we exceeded the design load?
**A:** 900x900mm porcelain tiles at 28kg/m² plus 15kg/m² for adhesive and screed adds approximately 0.43kPa of dead load. If the slab was designed for 1.5kPa total superimposed load and the original specification accounted for lighter finishes (e.g., carpet at 2kg/m²), you've added approximately 0.4kPa — which may push the total load close to or over the design capacity. The structural engineer must recalculate: (1) total dead load including the new tile specification, (2) live load capacity remaining, (3) deflection under the increased dead load. For large-format tiles, deflection is critical — L/360 is the maximum allowable deflection for rigid tile finishes. If the slab deflects more than this, the tiles will crack. Get the engineer's sign-off before ordering — changing tile specification without structural review is a common and costly mistake.

**Q: 117** The interior designer specified natural stone tiles (travertine) for all bathrooms. We substituted with porcelain lookalike tiles to save cost. The buyer is furious and claims this changes the "character" of the unit. Is "character" a legally enforceable concept?
**A:** "Character" is not a legally defined term unless the sales contract or specification schedule explicitly defines the materials that create it. If the contract specifies "travertine tiles" and you substituted porcelain, you are in breach of contract — but the remedy is damages or specific performance (replacement), not a claim for "character." If the contract used generic terms like "premium stone-look tiles," you are within your rights to substitute. The lesson: never allow designers to specify materials by trade name in the sales contract without a "or equal approved" clause. Also, porcelain performs better than travertine in wet areas (lower porosity, better slip resistance) — you can argue the substitution is an improvement. But without a contract clause allowing substitution, you're exposed.

**Q: 118** Our tiling contractor has proposed using a "tile adhesive with built-in waterproofing" for the shower floors instead of a separate waterproofing membrane. This saves R120/m². Is this compliant with SANS 10400?
**A:** No. SANS 10400-XA (waterproofing) requires a separate, continuous waterproofing membrane in wet areas (showers, bathrooms) beneath the tile bed. "Waterproof" adhesive is water-resistant adhesive — it prevents water penetration through the adhesive bed but does not provide a continuous membrane beneath the screed. Water will still penetrate through grout joints and reach the structural slab. A proper waterproofing system requires: (1) a primed substrate, (2) a torch-on or liquid-applied membrane (minimum 1.5mm thick for sheet membranes), (3) protection board, (4) screed bed, (5) tiles. The R120/m² saving is false economy — a single waterproofing failure costs R15,000–R30,000 to remediate per unit, plus damage to the unit below. Never accept "waterproof adhesive" as a substitute for a proper membrane.

**Q: 119** We want to use recycled glass tiles in our development for sustainability marketing. The supplier can't provide a SANS certificate for the tiles because they're imported and "not tested in South Africa." Can we use them?
**A:** SANS 1449 is a voluntary standard for tile classification in South Africa — it is not legally mandatory. However, under SANS 10400-A, building materials must be "fit for purpose" and must not create health or safety hazards. If the glass tiles meet the ISO 10545 international standard (which the supplier should provide), they can be used, but you should: (1) obtain the ISO test certificates (water absorption, breaking strength, chemical resistance, slip resistance), (2) have an independent laboratory test a sample batch to SANS 1449 parameters for your records, (3) ensure the tiles are rated for the intended application (floor tiles must have higher breaking strength than wall tiles), (4) check that the adhesive manufacturer's warranty covers use with glass tiles. Glass tiles are typically more slippery than porcelain — ensure the slip rating meets SANS 10400-S for the application.

**Q: 120** The main contractor wants to use a "tile on tile" system — tiling over existing tiles in the renovation portion of our project to save time. What's the risk?
**A:** Tiling over existing tiles is technically possible but carries significant risk: (1) the existing tiles must be well-bonded — tap test every tile, remove any hollow-sounding ones, (2) the existing surface must be thoroughly cleaned and degreased, (3) the additional height (typically 15–20mm) affects door thresholds, skirting alignment, and plumbing fixture connections, (4) the double layer increases dead load on the slab — check with the structural engineer, (5) if the original tiles fail (debond) in future, the new tiles fail with them, (6) warranty issues — most adhesive manufacturers will not warrant tile-on-tile installations unless their specific system is used and the substrate is approved. If any original tiles are loose, remove them all and start fresh. Tile-on-tile is a cost-saving shortcut that often results in call-backs.

### SECTION V: NEIGHBOUR ISSUES, EASEMENTS & PARTY WALLS

**Q: 121** The neighbour's building is so close that our tile layer needs to lean a ladder against their wall to tile our external wall. They refuse permission. Can we get a court order for access?
**A:** Under the Access to Neighbouring Land Act, you can apply to the Magistrate's Court or High Court for an order granting temporary access to a neighbour's property if: (1) the work you are doing is necessary and cannot reasonably be done without access, (2) the neighbour's refusal is unreasonable, (3) you offer adequate compensation for any damage or inconvenience. The court will consider the duration of access required, the impact on the neighbour's use of their property, and whether alternative methods exist. For tiling work that requires only a few hours of ladder access, a court is likely to grant the order with compensation of R500–R2,000 per day. However, the court process takes 2–6 weeks — it's faster and cheaper to negotiate a cash payment with the neighbour. Always address access requirements before construction begins.

**Q: 122** Water from our development's balcony is dripping onto the neighbour's driveway because the tile layer didn't install a drip edge. The neighbour is threatening to sue for "aquae pluviae" (rainwater damage). Is this a real cause of action?
**A:** The aquae pluviae action is an ancient Roman-Dutch law remedy that allows a lower landowner to sue an upper landowner for diverting or concentrating rainwater onto their property in a way that causes damage. While this specific cause of action has been superseded by modern neighbour law principles in many contexts, the underlying principle remains: you must not alter the natural flow of water from your property in a way that causes damage to neighbours. The balcony drip edge is a standard detail (SANS 10400-XA requires water to be directed away from the building). You are liable for the cost of installing the drip edge and any damage caused. Fix the drip edge immediately — it's a R50 detail that prevents a R50,000 lawsuit.

**Q: 123** Our townhouse development has party walls between units. The tile layer tiled right across the party wall without leaving a movement joint at the boundary. Now both owners are complaining about cracking. Who pays for the repair?
**A:** Party walls are structural elements shared between two properties. Under the Sectional Titles Schemes Management Act (for sectional title) or common law (for freehold townhouses), both owners share responsibility for the structural wall. The tile layer should have installed a movement joint at the party wall boundary to allow independent movement of the two structural bays. Failure to do so is defective workmanship. The repair cost should be borne by the developer (if within the DLP) or the body corporate (if the wall is common property). If the development is completed and transferred, the affected owners may need to share the repair cost and then claim against the developer if the DLP has not expired. For new developments, specify movement joints at all party wall junctions in the tiling specification.

**Q: 124** A purchaser claims their unit has "noise transmission" from the adjacent unit through the party wall, and the tiles on their side amplify the sound. They want us to install acoustic insulation and re-tile. Is this our responsibility?
**A:** Under SANS 10400-T (Part T for fire and Part K for acoustic control), inter-unit walls in multi-unit residential buildings must achieve minimum airborne and impact sound insulation ratings. The tile finish itself does not provide acoustic insulation — the wall construction does (typically double-skin masonry with cavity fill, or insulated drywall). If the wall meets the SANS 10400 acoustic requirements, the tile finish is irrelevant to the rating. "Amplification" of sound by hard tiles is a room acoustics issue, not a building code issue — the buyer's remedy is rugs, curtains, or wall panels. However, if the wall does not meet the acoustic rating (insufficient mass, missing cavity fill, unsealed penetrations), the developer is responsible for remediation. Commission an acoustic test (SANS 10177) to determine if the wall complies.

**Q: 125** Our estate has a shared driveway paved with clay brick pavers. One homeowner replaced their section with polished porcelain tiles, which are dangerously slippery when wet. The HOA wants them to remove the tiles. Can the HOA enforce this?
**A:** Yes, if the HOA's design guidelines or conduct rules prohibit changes to shared driveways or specify minimum slip ratings for external surfaces. Shared driveways are typically common property or servitude areas, and individual owners cannot unilaterally alter the surface. Even if the driveway is privately owned, the title deed may include aesthetic restrictions or maintenance obligations. Polished porcelain (R9 or lower when wet) on an external driveway is a safety hazard — slip resistance standards (SANS 10107) require R10 minimum for external pedestrian areas and R11 for ramps. The HOA can issue a compliance notice and, if necessary, obtain a court order for removal. The owner may also face civil liability if someone slips and is injured.

### SECTION W: MORE UNUSUAL & AWKWARD DEVELOPER QUESTIONS

**Q: 126** Can we specify different tile grades for different areas — Grade 1 (premium) for show units and Grade 3 for the actual sold units — provided the sales contract doesn't specify the grade?
**A:** While legally possible if the contract is silent on grade, this is ethically questionable and commercially dangerous. SANS 1449 defines tile grades: Grade 1 (minor visual defects only), Grade 2 (minor defects, some colour variation), Grade 3 (visible defects). A Grade 3 tile is fit for purpose but will have noticeable colour variation, pinholes, or size variation. If a buyer discovers they received Grade 3 while the show house had Grade 1, they may claim under CPA Section 55 that the goods are not of "good quality" and not "fit for purpose" for a residential unit. The CPA's "reasonable consumer" test considers the overall context — a buyer of a new home expects consistent quality. If you use different grades, disclose it in the contract. The saving (typically R20–R50/m²) is not worth the reputational risk.

**Q: 127** A buyer wants to install underfloor heating over our tiles after taking occupation. Does this void our NHBRC warranty on the floor slab?
**A:** The NHBRC warranty covers structural defects in the floor slab, not the tile finish. Installing underfloor heating over the tiles does not affect the slab's structural integrity. However, if the underfloor heating installation involves drilling into or cutting the slab (for conduit routing), this could compromise the structural element and void the NHBRC warranty for the affected area. The NHBRC warranty is transferrable to subsequent owners but only covers defects present at construction. Post-occupation alterations are the owner's responsibility. Your sale agreement should include a clause stating that the NHBRC warranty does not cover damage caused by post-transfer alterations. The buyer should ensure their UFH installer has professional indemnity insurance.

**Q: 128** We're required to provide a certain percentage of units as "affordable housing" under the municipal zoning conditions. Can we use lower-specification tiles in the affordable units compared to the market-rate units?
**A:** Municipal affordable housing conditions typically regulate unit size, price point, and income targeting — they do not specify interior finishes. However, using significantly lower finishes in affordable units compared to market-rate units in the same development may expose you to: (1) claims of discriminatory treatment under PEPUDA (Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act), (2) reputational damage and media scrutiny, (3) if the affordable units are visibly inferior, municipal housing officials may refuse to sign off on the affordable housing certificate. Best practice: use the same tile specification across all units, or if cost constraints require a difference, ensure the affordable unit tiles are still durable, attractive, and compliant with all standards. Never use substandard tiles in affordable units — it perpetuates inequality and invites legal challenge.

**Q: 129** A purchaser in our sectional title scheme wants to tile over the existing bedroom carpet with vinyl tiles. The body corporate says this is an alteration to the unit and requires their consent. Is the body corporate correct?
**A:** For internal alterations within a sectional title unit, the owner's freedom depends on the conduct rules. Standard STSMA conduct rules require body corporate consent for alterations that affect the building's structure, common property, or external appearance. Tiling over carpet inside a unit does not affect any of these — it's purely internal. The body corporate cannot unreasonably withhold consent for internal finish changes. However, if the new tiles increase the floor load significantly or involve waterproofing (e.g., converting a bedroom to a wet area), consent may be required. Vinyl tiles on a bedroom floor is a minor internal alteration — the body corporate has no basis to interfere. The owner should cite STSMA Section 13(1)(c) which protects the right to use and enjoy their section.

**Q: 130** We're being audited by SARS and they want to know why our tile procurement cost per unit decreased by 22% between Phase 1 and Phase 2. The truth is we switched to a cheaper supplier. Could this trigger a transfer pricing investigation?
**A:** A 22% cost reduction between phases is unlikely to trigger a transfer pricing investigation on its own — transfer pricing applies to cross-border transactions between connected persons (related parties), not domestic arm's-length supplier switches. SARS is likely checking whether: (1) you inflated Phase 1 costs to reduce taxable income, (2) the cheaper tiles in Phase 2 are reflected in the selling price, (3) there was any side agreement or kickback arrangement. Provide SARS with: (1) the supplier invoices for both phases, (2) the specification sheets showing the tiles are equivalent, (3) the B-BBEE certificates showing the switch was compliance-driven, (4) minutes of the procurement decision. If the switch was legitimate and at arm's length, there is no issue. Keep all procurement documentation for 5 years.

**Q: 131** A tile manufacturer offered us a "marketing contribution" of R50,000 if we specify their tiles exclusively across the entire development. Is this a kickback, and is it legal?
**A:** A "marketing contribution" from a supplier to a developer for exclusive specification is not inherently illegal if: (1) it is properly documented as a sponsorship or marketing agreement, (2) the amount is commercially reasonable and disclosed to the buyer (if material), (3) it does not result in you specifying inferior or overpriced products to the buyer's detriment. However, if the payment is secret, not documented, or results in you charging the buyer the full price while pocketing the contribution, it constitutes fraud. Under the CPA, you have a duty to act in good faith — secret commissions breach this duty. If the tile specification is appropriate and the price to the buyer is fair, document the marketing agreement, declare it in your financial statements, and ensure it does not compromise the buyer's interests. If in doubt, disclose it to the buyer.

**Q: 132** Our tiling specification calls for "white body porcelain." A supplier delivered "red body porcelain" (which is cheaper). The project manager didn't notice and the tiles were installed in 15 units. The red body has higher water absorption. Do we need to replace them?
**A:** Red body (also called "double-fire" or "white-fire red body") porcelain has a different clay composition than white body porcelain. True porcelain should have water absorption below 0.5% (ISO 10545-3). Some "red body" products marketed as porcelain may have water absorption above 0.5%, which technically classifies them as "stoneware" or "fine stoneware" rather than porcelain under SANS 1449. If the water absorption exceeds 0.5%, the tiles do not meet the "porcelain" specification and must be replaced. Even if absorption is below 0.5%, if the contract specified "white body" and "red body" was delivered without approval, it's a breach of contract. Commission independent testing of the installed tiles (cost: R2,000–R3,000 per sample). If they fail the porcelain specification, replace them — the cost of replacement is less than the CPA and reputational risk.

**Q: 133** A buyer has requested that we install "extra reinforcement" in the floor slab beneath the tiles in their unit because they plan to install a grand piano (400kg concentrated load). Who pays for this — us or the buyer?
**A:** Standard residential floor slabs are designed for a distributed live load of 1.5kPa–2.0kPa (approximately 150–200kg/m²). A 400kg grand piano on small castors creates a concentrated load that may exceed the slab's capacity, causing cracking or excessive deflection. If the buyer's request is made before construction, the additional reinforcement (typically a thicker slab or additional reinforcing steel in the piano area) is an alteration to the standard specification. The buyer should pay the additional cost. Document this as a variation order with: (1) the structural engineer's assessment, (2) the reinforcement specification, (3) the cost breakdown, (4) the buyer's written acceptance. If the request comes after slab construction, it's usually impractical to reinforce — the buyer may need a structural engineer to assess whether the existing slab can accommodate the load.

**Q: 134** The body corporate in our completed scheme wants to install CCTV cameras in the common area corridors. The cameras need to be mounted on the tiled walls. Do they need our permission as the developer?
**A:** Once the sectional title register is opened and the body corporate is established, the developer has no ongoing rights over the common property unless specifically reserved in the development agreement. The body corporate can make alterations to common property by special resolution (STSMA Section 5(1)(h)). Mounting CCTV cameras on tiled walls is a minor alteration. They do not need your permission. However, the installation must be done by a competent contractor who does not damage the waterproofing or fire-rating of the wall. If the camera installation damages tiles, the body corporate is responsible for repair. As a courtesy, the body corporate should notify all owners of the camera locations for privacy reasons under POPIA (Protection of Personal Information Act).

**Q: 135** We're building student accommodation near a university. The target market is students who are notoriously hard on finishes. Can we use commercial-grade tiles (Class 5 PEI) in a residential application, and does this affect our NHBRC warranty?
**A:** Yes, you can use Class 5 PEI (Porcelain Enamel Institute) tiles in residential applications — Class 5 is the highest wear rating, designed for heavy commercial traffic (shopping centres, hotel lobbies). Using Class 5 tiles in student accommodation is actually excellent practice — it reduces maintenance and replacement costs over the building's life. The NHBRC warranty is not affected by tile classification — it covers structural defects, not finish wear. The only consideration is cost: Class 5 tiles are 20%–40% more expensive than Class 3 (standard residential). For student accommodation, this premium pays for itself within 3–5 years through reduced maintenance. Specify dark-coloured, matte-finish Class 5 tiles with R10 slip rating — students will appreciate the durability and safety.

**Q: 136** Our development includes a mosque with prayer hall floors. The Imam requires that the floor tiles be "clean and appropriate for prayer" — meaning no images, no gold, and no animal motifs. Is this a reasonable accommodation under religious freedom laws?
**A:** Under Section 15 of the Constitution (freedom of religion) and PEPUDA (promotion of equality), religious accommodation is a protected right. If you are building a mosque for a Muslim community, specifying tiles that comply with Islamic aesthetic principles is not just reasonable — it's necessary. Tiles with geometric patterns, calligraphy (Quranic verses), or plain designs are appropriate. Tiles with human figures, animals, or gold leaf would violate Islamic principles and potentially constitute indirect discrimination if insisted upon. For any religious building, consult the religious authority on appropriate finishes. This applies equally to churches, temples, and synagogues — each has specific requirements that must be respected. Document the religious requirements in the specification to avoid disputes.

**Q: 137** A purchaser wants to know if our tiles contain "toxic chemicals" because they read online that ceramic tiles emit radiation. How do we respond?
**A:** Ceramic and porcelain tiles are made from natural clay and minerals that contain trace amounts of naturally occurring radioactive elements (uranium, thorium, potassium-40) — this is true of all natural stone and clay products. However, the levels are extremely low and well within international safety standards. Tiles do not emit harmful "chemicals" — they are inert after firing at 1,200°C. Provide the buyer with the tile manufacturer's safety data sheet (SDS) and any available radiation test certificates (some European manufacturers provide these). The Health Physics Society states that the radiation exposure from ceramic tiles is negligible compared to background radiation. If the buyer remains concerned, offer an independent radiation test (cost approximately R5,000), but this is almost certainly unnecessary and the buyer should bear the cost.

**Q: 138** We're building a retirement village and the occupational therapist recommends non-slip tiles with "high visual contrast" at level changes to assist residents with vision impairment. Does SANS 10400-S mandate this?
**A:** SANS 10400-S (facilities for persons with disabilities) requires luminance contrast (visual contrast) at critical locations: stair nosing edges, tactile indicators, door frames, and level changes. For retirement villages and care facilities (Class H3 occupancy), the requirements are more stringent than standard residential. The standard specifies a minimum luminance contrast of 30% between the critical surface and adjacent surfaces. For floor level changes, a tile with LRV (Light Reflectance Value) difference of 30 points or more from the surrounding floor meets this requirement. For example, if the main floor tiles have LRV 50, the step edge tiles should have LRV ≤ 20 (dark) or ≥ 80 (light). Many tile manufacturers publish LRV data. If unavailable, a colour consultant can measure it (cost: R3,000–R5,000 for a report). This is a requirement for retirement village approval.

**Q: 139** Our development has an underground parking basement where the floor tiles are constantly damp. The waterproofing contractor says it's "rising damp" and not covered by their warranty. Is rising damp from the ground covered?
**A:** Rising damp (capillary moisture from the water table or subsoil) is a hydrostatic pressure issue, not a waterproofing membrane defect — unless the waterproofing system was explicitly designed to resist hydrostatic pressure and has failed. Standard waterproofing warranties cover water ingress through the membrane due to installation defects or material failure. They do NOT cover water that bypasses the membrane through construction joints, tie holes, or inadequate subsoil drainage. For underground basements, the correct approach is: (1) positive-side waterproofing (external membrane on the outside of the slab and walls), (2) subsoil drainage system (French drains, sump pumps), (3) vapour barrier beneath the screed. If you only have an internal membrane, rising damp will eventually penetrate. The solution is external remediation (digging around the basement) or installing a dehumidification system. Neither is cheap — R500–R1,500/m² for external remediation.

**Q: 140** A tile manufacturer's representative offered to "certify our installation" if we use their adhesive system, saying this gives us a "10-year system warranty." What does this actually mean?
**A:** A "system warranty" from a tile manufacturer means that the manufacturer warrants the performance of their tiles, adhesive, grout, and specified installation method AS A SYSTEM for the warranty period. This is valuable because: (1) if anything fails, there's a single point of accountability — the manufacturer cannot blame the adhesive or the installer, (2) the warranty is typically backed by the manufacturer's product liability insurance, (3) the manufacturer will inspect the installation at key stages and issue a compliance certificate. However, read the fine print: (1) the warranty may only cover material defects, not installation errors by your contractor, (2) the manufacturer may require use of their certified installers, (3) the warranty may be voided by structural movement, incorrect cleaning methods, or failure to maintain expansion joints, (4) claim procedures may require immediate notification and site access. A system warranty typically costs 5%–10% more than buying components separately. For large developments, it's worth it.

**Q: 141** We're developing a floating apartment building (houseboat marina) in the Vaal River. Do SANS 10400 building regulations apply, and what tile adhesive works on a constantly moving substrate?
**A:** SANS 10400 applies to "buildings" defined as "any structure," which includes houseboats and floating structures used for residential purposes. The local municipality (Emfuleni or Metsimaholo, depending on the Vaal location) will require building plan approval. For a constantly flexing substrate, standard cement-based adhesives will fail. You need: (1) a structural engineer to calculate the maximum deflection and movement at the floor level, (2) a marine-grade flexible adhesive (polyurethane or epoxy-based, classified as R2 or S2), (3) a decoupling membrane between the structural deck and the tile bed to absorb differential movement, (4) silicone-filled expansion joints at maximum 2m intervals in both directions. Marine environments also require stainless steel or brass tile trim — aluminium will corrode rapidly. Budget 40%–60% more for marine-grade installation systems compared to standard residential.

**Q: 142** Our development borders a nature reserve, and the environmental management plan prohibits any light pollution that could affect nocturnal animals. Do polished tiles contribute to light pollution, and do we need to use matte finishes only?
**A:** Polished tiles have a high gloss factor (90+ GU) and can reflect artificial light, potentially contributing to light pollution if used on external surfaces. However, light pollution from floor tiles is negligible compared to direct light sources (floodlights, security lighting). The environmental management plan likely focuses on upward light spill from fixtures, not floor reflectance. That said, if external walkways are illuminated at night, polished tiles will create glare that disorients nocturnal animals. Specify matte or textured external tiles (LRV 30–50, gloss factor below 15 GU) for any areas visible from the nature reserve. Internal polished tiles are not an environmental issue. Consult the environmental management plan's specific light pollution clauses — they typically specify lumens and fixture direction, not floor finishes.

**Q: 143** A buyer wants to pay for a tile upgrade directly to the tiling contractor (cash) to avoid paying our markup. The contractor has agreed. Should we allow this?
**A:** No. Allowing a buyer to contract directly with your subcontractor creates multiple problems: (1) the subcontractor's priority shifts to the paying customer rather than the developer, (2) quality control becomes impossible — the upgraded tiles may not meet the project's overall aesthetic, (3) the NHBRC warranty may be compromised if non-approved materials are used, (4) your insurance may not cover defects arising from unsupervised work, (5) the buyer may later claim against you if the direct-contract work is defective, (6) the contractor may neglect your standard units to focus on the lucrative upgrade. All upgrades should go through you as the developer. Charge a reasonable administration fee (10%–15% of upgrade cost) for processing the variation. Include a formal variation order process in your sale agreements.

**Q: 144** We're building a gated estate and the HOA wants to mandate that all driveways use the same tile pattern to maintain "architectural harmony." Can an HOA actually enforce tile colour rules on private property?
**A:** Yes, if the architectural guidelines are registered as a condition in the title deeds of all properties within the estate. Title deed conditions are enforceable against all subsequent owners. The HOA can enforce through: (1) written notice requiring compliance, (2) fines for non-compliance (if the HOA rules provide for this), (3) court interdict for removal of non-compliant finishes, (4) in some cases, the HOA can perform the work and recover costs from the owner. However, the restrictions must be reasonable and must have been disclosed to the buyer at the time of purchase. As developer, you must ensure all buyers receive and acknowledge the architectural guidelines before transfer. Unreasonable restrictions (e.g., mandating a specific supplier at inflated prices) may be challengeable, but aesthetic uniformity rules have been upheld by South African courts.

**Q: 145** A tile batch we received has a subtle but visible pattern misalignment — the veining on the porcelain tiles doesn't flow consistently. The manufacturer says this is "within tolerance for a natural stone look." The buyer says it's unacceptable. Who is right?
**A:** For tiles marketed as "natural stone look" or "marble effect," variation in veining and pattern is expected and desirable — it mimics natural stone, which is inherently variable. However, the tiles should still achieve a harmonious overall appearance when laid. SANS 1449 and ISO 10545-16 address shade variation but not pattern alignment. The key is the manufacturer's published specification: if they state "tiles are designed for random laying and pattern variation is a feature," the buyer's claim is weak. If the manufacturer markets the tiles as "book-matched" or "consistent veining," they must deliver that. Best practice: require the tiling contractor to dry-lay a representative area (2m x 2m minimum) before fixing, and obtain the buyer's approval of the layout. This manages expectations and prevents disputes.

**Q: 146** Our project manager authorised the tile layer to work overtime on a Saturday to catch up on the programme. The overtime rate is double time. The tile layer's weekly earnings now exceed the BCEA overtime threshold. Are we in breach of the Basic Conditions of Employment Act?
**A:** The BCEA (Basic Conditions of Employment Act) limits overtime to 10 hours per week and requires payment at 1.5x the normal rate (or double time on Sundays/public holidays). There is no "overtime threshold" that prohibits payment — the threshold determines whether the employee is covered by certain BCEA provisions. If the tile layer is a "commission earner" or earns above the BCEA earnings threshold (currently R224,080 per annum as of 2024), some BCEA provisions (like overtime limits) do not apply. However, if they are an ordinary employee, the 10-hour weekly overtime limit applies regardless of earnings. Exceeding this limit without a specific exemption from the Department of Labour is a breach. The exemption process takes 2–4 weeks and requires justification. Plan your programme to avoid unplanned overtime.

**Q: 147** We have a unit where the floor slope is 1:120 (slightly steeper than the 1:150 ideal for tiling). The tile layer says the tiles will "slide" during adhesive curing. Is this a real risk, and what's the solution?
**A:** A 1:120 slope (approximately 0.48°) is not steep enough to cause tile sliding with any normal adhesive system. Even a 1:50 slope (1.15°) can be tiled with standard C2 adhesive without sliding. The tile layer's concern suggests they are unfamiliar with sloped installations or are using an adhesive with poor initial grab (open time too long). The solution: (1) use a C2TES1 adhesive (improved adhesive with deformable and non-slip properties) — the "T" classification includes thixotropic properties that prevent sagging on walls and slopes, (2) work in smaller sections (2–3m² at a time) so the adhesive doesn't skin over before the tile is placed, (3) use a contact adhesive system (two-coat method) for very steep slopes. A 1:120 slope is standard for wet area falls — any competent tiler should handle it without issue. If they can't, find a different tiler.

**Q: 148** A buyer has threatened to post a video on social media showing cracked tiles in their unit "unless we fix it immediately." The unit is past the defects liability period, and the cracks are from the owner dragging furniture. Is this extortion, and how do we respond?
**A:** Threatening to publish damaging information unless a demand is met can constitute extortion under the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act (PRECCA) if the threat is accompanied by an unlawful demand. However, a consumer posting a legitimate complaint about perceived defects (even if they are wrong about the cause) is protected consumer speech under the CPA. The line is crossed if the buyer explicitly demands payment or benefits "or else" the video goes viral. Your response: (1) do not be pressured into unwarranted repairs by social media threats, (2) document your assessment (cracks caused by furniture impact, not defective workmanship), (3) offer a goodwill inspection by an independent expert at your cost, (4) if the threat is explicit extortion, report it to SAPS, (5) engage a defamation attorney if the published statements are false and damaging. Consider a written response setting out the factual basis for your position. Social media shakedowns are increasingly common — stand your ground if the facts support you.

**Q: 149** Our development is in a complex with mixed-use zoning (retail on ground floor, residential above). The retail tenant wants to install heavy display shelving on the tiled floor. Do we need to reinforce the slab, and does the tenant need our permission?
**A:** The retail tenant's right to install fixtures depends on their lease agreement. Typically, the tenant needs landlord consent for any fixture that affects the structure or permanently alters the premises. Heavy display shelving (concentrated loads) may exceed the standard retail floor loading of 3.0–5.0kPa. You need a structural engineer to assess: (1) the weight of the shelving plus maximum stock loading, (2) the load distribution (point loads from shelving legs are more critical than distributed loads), (3) whether the existing slab can accommodate the load. If reinforcement is needed, the tenant typically pays for it (subject to lease negotiation). The tiled floor finish may need to be lifted to install reinforcement plates or additional support — this is at the tenant's cost with landlord approval. Always require a structural assessment before granting consent for heavy retail fixtures.

**Q: 150** We're building 30km from the nearest town and the municipal building inspector refuses to travel to our site for inspections. We've completed the building and the tiles are installed. How do we get an Occupation Certificate?
**A:** Municipalities are legally obligated to provide building control services within their jurisdiction, regardless of location. If the inspector refuses to travel, escalate to: (1) the Municipal Manager, (2) the MEC for Local Government in the province, (3) the National Department of Public Works and Infrastructure. In practice, for remote sites, you can: (1) engage a private building surveyor registered with SACPCMP to conduct inspections and issue a compliance certificate, (2) request the municipality to appoint the private surveyor as an "authorised person" under the Building Control Act, (3) in some provinces (Western Cape, Gauteng), accredited private building control officers can issue compliance certificates directly. The NHBRC can also conduct inspections independently. Document all requests to the municipality — if they fail to inspect within a reasonable time, their refusal may be challengeable in court as administrative failure.

### SECTION X: EVEN MORE OBSCURE DEVELOPER QUESTIONS

**Q: 151** Our tiling contractor wants to use a "rapid-set" adhesive that allows grouting after 2 hours instead of the standard 24 hours. This would save 3 days on the programme. Is there any risk?
**A:** Rapid-set adhesives (classified as F — fast-setting under EN 12004) achieve initial set in 2–3 hours and can be grouted within 3–6 hours under ideal conditions. The risk is that rapid-set adhesives have a shorter open time (the time between spreading and placing the tile), typically 10–15 minutes compared to 20–30 minutes for standard adhesives. In hot or windy conditions, the adhesive may skin over before the tile is placed, resulting in poor adhesion. They also have higher cement content, which increases shrinkage stress. Rapid-set is excellent for time-critical projects IF: (1) the tiler is experienced with rapid-set products, (2) ambient temperature is between 15°C and 25°C, (3) batch sizes are small, (4) the substrate has been properly primed. For a 3-day programme saving, the risk is probably worth it — but do a trial area first.

**Q: 152** A purchaser claims the tiles in their unit "resonate" when they walk on them, creating an echoing sound. They want the entire floor replaced. Is "resonance" a recognised tile defect?
**A:** "Resonance" or hollow-sounding tiles is not a defect per se — it's an acoustic phenomenon caused by a partial void between the tile and the substrate. If the tile is adequately bonded (adhesion strength above 0.5 MPa as per SANS 10107), a slight hollow sound is not a defect. However, if the void exceeds 10% of the tile area or the adhesion strength is below 0.5 MPa, it's a debonding defect that must be repaired. Commission a pull-off test (destructive test on a spare tile) to measure adhesion strength. If the adhesion meets the standard, the "resonance" is a characteristic of the installation system and not a defect. Explain to the buyer that the tile is securely bonded and the sound is cosmetic. Offer to inject epoxy resin into any significant voids as a goodwill gesture. Full replacement is not warranted.

**Q: 153** We have a heritage-protected building in the Cape Town CBD where the National Monuments Council requires us to retain the original 1920s floor tiles. But the tiles are cracked and loose. Can we replace them with replicas?
**A:** Under the National Heritage Resources Act, any alteration to a heritage-protected building requires a permit from Heritage Western Cape (or the relevant Amafa/Heritage agency). If the original tiles are damaged beyond repair, you can apply for a permit to remove and replace them. The application must include: (1) a condition assessment by a heritage consultant, (2) photographic documentation of every tile, (3) a sample of the original tile for matching, (4) details of the proposed replacement tile (reproduction cement tiles are available from specialist manufacturers in Cape Town and Durban), (5) methodology for removal and reinstallation. Heritage authorities generally prefer repair over replacement. If 70%+ of the tiles can be salvaged and re-laid, they will likely require this. Budget R2,000–R4,000/m² for heritage tile restoration versus R300–R500/m² for standard replacement. Unauthorised removal of heritage tiles is a criminal offence.

**Q: 154** Our development's basement parking has epoxy floor paint, but the body corporate now wants to tile it with porcelain for "a premium look." The basement has no natural ventilation. Is this safe?
**A:** Tiling a basement parking area is technically possible but raises several concerns: (1) exhaust fumes from vehicles contain carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides, and hydrocarbons — a tiled floor does not absorb these fumes the way an epoxy or concrete floor might (though this is minor), (2) tiled surfaces are more slippery than textured epoxy when wet (oil, water, coolant spills) — specify R11 or R12 slip-rated textured tiles, (3) tyre marks are harder to remove from textured tiles than from smooth epoxy, (4) the additional dead load of tiles plus screed (approximately 40–50kg/m²) must be checked against the suspended slab capacity, (5) cost: tiling a basement costs R350–R600/m² versus R80–R150/m² for epoxy. For a premium look, consider polished concrete or a high-build epoxy system with decorative flake — it achieves the aesthetic without the slip and maintenance issues of tiles in a parking environment.

**Q: 155** A tile supplier has offered us "factory seconds" at 40% discount for use in the service areas (plant rooms, storage). The tiles have minor cosmetic defects. Is this allowed under SANS 10400?
**A:** SANS 10400 does not prohibit the use of second-grade tiles in non-habitable areas. Factory seconds are fit for purpose in plant rooms, storage areas, and service corridors where aesthetics are not a priority. However: (1) the tiles must still meet structural requirements (breaking strength, thickness), (2) they must be suitable for the intended loading (floor tiles, not wall tiles, in floor applications), (3) if the area is accessible to the public or building users, the slip rating must still comply with SANS 10107, (4) do NOT use seconds in any area covered by the NHBRC warranty for the habitable portion of the dwelling — the warranty requires "workmanlike finish" in all areas. Clearly segregate the seconds and mark them for specific non-critical areas. Never use seconds in areas visible to purchasers or in any wet area where water resistance is critical.

**Q: 156** We're building in an eco-estate where the environmental rules prohibit any cement-based products because cement production has a high carbon footprint. Can we install tiles without cement adhesive?
**A:** Cement-free tile installation is possible but challenging. Alternatives to cement-based adhesive include: (1) epoxy adhesives (100% solids, no cement, extremely strong and chemical-resistant, but expensive at R60–R120/m² material cost), (2) polyurethane adhesives (flexible, good for timber or metal substrates, R50–R80/m²), (3) silicone-based adhesives (for lightweight applications only, not for floor tiles), (4) mechanical fixing systems (clips and rails, used in industrial applications). For a residential eco-estate, epoxy adhesive is the most viable cement-free option, but it requires specialist application (trained installers, limited open time, temperature-sensitive). The carbon footprint argument against cement is questionable when applied to small-scale construction — the cement in tile adhesive for a single home is approximately 200–300kg, which is negligible compared to structural concrete. Challenge the rule on practical grounds or seek an exemption for tile adhesive specifically.

**Q: 157** A buyer wants to install a jacuzzi on their balcony. The balcony floor is tiled with standard porcelain. The jacuzzi weighs 1,200kg when full. The balcony was designed for 2.0kPa live load. Can the balcony take the weight?
**A:** A 1,200kg jacuzzi on a typical 6m² balcony creates a distributed load of approximately 2.0kPa, which meets the design live load — BUT this assumes the load is evenly distributed. A jacuzzi concentrates the load at the base points (four or six contact points), creating point loads that may exceed the slab's punching shear capacity. Additionally, the dynamic load from water movement adds approximately 20%–30% to the static weight. You need a structural engineer to assess: (1) the exact balcony slab dimensions and reinforcement, (2) the jacuzzi's contact point loads, (3) whether a load-distribution pad is needed beneath the jacuzzi base. Many balcony slabs are cantilevered (only supported from one side), which further reduces their capacity. The tiles are irrelevant to the structural question — they will crack if the slab fails. Do not approve any balcony jacuzzi installation without a structural engineer's sign-off.

**Q: 158** Our development has a central courtyard with a water feature. The water feature's edge is tiled, but children from the estate keep climbing on it. A child fell and the parents claim the tiled edge is "too slippery." Are we liable if the water feature wasn't designed as a play area?
**A:** Liability depends on whether the water feature constitutes an "attractive nuisance" and whether reasonable safety measures were in place. Under South African common law (delict), an occupier of premises owes a duty of care to lawful visitors, including children. Even if the water feature was not designed as a play area, if it is accessible to children and presents a foreseeable risk of injury, you may be liable. Defence factors: (1) the tiles meet the applicable slip rating for the application (R10 minimum for pool surrounds), (2) signage was posted warning against climbing, (3) the water feature design included safety features (shallow depth, rounded edges), (4) the parents were negligent in supervising the child. To mitigate risk: ensure pool surround tiles achieve R11 slip rating, install child-resistant barriers if the water depth exceeds 300mm, and post visible warning signage. Consider adding textured anti-slip strips to the edge.

**Q: 159** The tiling subcontractor's insurance lapsed mid-project. We only discovered this after a tile fell and damaged a car in the basement. Our insurance covers the car damage, but can we claim the excess back from the subcontractor?
**A:** If the subcontractor's public liability insurance lapsed due to their failure to pay premiums, they are personally liable for any damages caused by their negligence. However, collecting from an uninsured subcontractor is often difficult — they may not have sufficient assets. Your contract with the subcontractor should include: (1) a requirement to maintain public liability insurance throughout the project, (2) a clause making them liable for all claims arising from their work, (3) a right to deduct damages from payments due, (4) a personal suretyship from the subcontractor's directors for contractual obligations. For this incident: (1) claim from your own insurer (they will likely subrogate against the subcontractor), (2) deduct the excess and any other costs from the subcontractor's final account, (3) do not allow the subcontractor back on site until they provide proof of reinstated insurance. Always verify subcontractor insurance monthly — don't rely on the initial certificate.

**Q: 160** We have a dispute with a tile supplier over batch shade variation. The contract says disputes are resolved by "expert determination." How does this work, and who pays the expert?
**A:** Expert determination is a form of alternative dispute resolution where an independent expert (typically a senior quantity surveyor, architect, or tile technologist) is appointed to decide the dispute based on their expertise. The process is: (1) the parties jointly appoint an expert or, if they cannot agree, each nominates one and the two nominees appoint a third, (2) the expert reviews evidence (tile samples, specifications, SANS standards), (3) the expert issues a binding or non-binding determination (depending on the contract clause), (4) the determination is typically final unless there was procedural unfairness or the expert acted outside their mandate. Cost: the expert's fees (R3,000–R8,000 per day) are usually shared equally by the parties, unless the expert orders otherwise. Expert determination is faster (4–8 weeks) and cheaper than arbitration or litigation. For technical disputes like shade variation, it's highly effective.

**Q: 161** Our development has units with "voids" (double-volume spaces). The tile layer wants to charge a height premium for tiling the walls above 3m. Is this standard practice?
**A:** Tiling at heights above 3m requires scaffolding, scaffolding erection costs, and additional safety measures (harnesses, edge protection). A height premium is standard industry practice. The premium typically kicks in at 3.0m (standard working height) and increases at 4.5m and 6.0m. Premiums range from 15% above 3.0m to 50% above 6.0m, reflecting the increased labour cost, equipment hire, and risk. Scaffolding hire for interior work costs R30–R60/m² per week, and the tile layer needs to include erection and dismantling time. If the wall tiling above 3m is purely decorative (a feature wall in a double-volume living room), consider whether it's necessary — painted plaster above 3m achieves a similar aesthetic at much lower cost. If the tiles are required for acoustic or fire-rating purposes, the premium is unavoidable.

**Q: 162** A buyer wants us to install tiles with underfloor heating and specify that the system must be "smartphone controlled." Does smart thermostatic control affect the tile installation warranty in any way?
**A:** The smartphone control is a thermostat feature and does not affect the tile installation warranty. The warranty covers adhesion, grout integrity, and tile performance — not the control system. However, you should specify that the underfloor heating system (including thermostat) is warranted by the UFH manufacturer/installer, not by the tiling contractor or developer. The tiling warranty remains valid provided: (1) the UFH system is installed according to the manufacturer's guidelines, (2) the maximum floor surface temperature does not exceed 27°C for continuous heating or 29°C for intermittent heating, (3) the heating is brought online gradually (5°C per day increase), (4) the tiles are suitable for UFH application (most porcelain is). Include a clause in the sale agreement stating that smart home systems are provided by a third party and are subject to the third party's warranty terms.

**Q: 163** Our estate has a communal swimming pool. The body corporate wants to replace the existing mosaic tiles with large-format porcelain. The pool contractor says large-format tiles will "pop off" because they don't accommodate the pool's structural movement. Is this correct?
**A:** Swimming pools experience significant structural movement due to: (1) hydrostatic pressure changes (full vs empty), (2) thermal expansion of the water and surrounding structure, (3) ground movement. Traditional pool tiles are small-format mosaics (25mm x 25mm or 48mm x 48mm) with numerous grout joints that accommodate movement. Large-format tiles (300mm x 300mm or larger) have fewer joints and less flexibility — they are more prone to debonding in a pool environment. While it is possible to use large-format tiles in a pool with a specialised flexible adhesive and waterproofing system, it requires: (1) a highly flexible adhesive (S2 classification), (2) a crack-isolation membrane between the pool shell and the tile bed, (3) movement joints at maximum 3m intervals. Even with these measures, large-format pool tiles have a higher failure rate than mosaics. Most pool specialists recommend against it — stick with mosaics for pools.

**Q: 164** We're developing in a "smart city" precinct where the municipality requires all buildings to have IoT sensors embedded in the floor slab. Can these sensors be installed beneath tiles, and will they affect the tile warranty?
**A:** IoT sensors (temperature, occupancy, footfall counters) can be embedded in the floor screed beneath tiles. The installation process involves: (1) casting the sensor into the concrete slab or embedding it in the screed layer, (2) ensuring the sensor surface is flush with the screed top, (3) tiling over the sensor location with standard adhesive. The sensor itself does not affect the tile warranty provided: (1) the screed thickness is maintained (no depressions where the sensor sits), (2) the sensor wiring is properly protected in conduit, (3) the sensor location is marked for future access. If the sensor fails and needs replacement, the tile above it must be lifted. Plan for this: install the sensor at a tile joint (not in the centre of a tile) if possible, or use a "sacrificial" tile that can be replaced. Coordinate with the IoT system provider and the tiling contractor to ensure compatibility.

**Q: 165** A tile manufacturer has offered us a "buy-back guarantee" — they'll buy back any unused tiles at 80% of the purchase price. Is this a genuine offer, or are there hidden conditions?
**A:** Buy-back guarantees from tile manufacturers are genuine but typically have significant conditions: (1) the tiles must be in original, unopened packaging in saleable condition, (2) the buy-back must be exercised within a specific period (typically 6–12 months from purchase), (3) there may be a minimum quantity threshold (e.g., only full pallets), (4) transport costs to return the tiles are usually the developer's responsibility, (5) discontinued lines may be excluded, (6) the manufacturer may require exclusive supply of the development as a condition. Calculate the net benefit: if you return R200,000 worth of tiles at 80%, you receive R160,000 minus transport costs (R5,000–R15,000). The R40,000 loss is your cost of over-ordering insurance. For large developments where tile quantity estimation is uncertain, a buy-back guarantee is valuable. Read the fine print and ensure the conditions are achievable.

**Q: 166** Our development's specifications require "calibrated" tiles. The supplier delivered "rectified" tiles, claiming they are the same thing. Are they?
**A:** No, they are different. "Calibrated" tiles have been sorted into batches with consistent dimensions — each batch has tiles of the same size, but different batches may vary slightly. "Rectified" tiles have been mechanically cut after firing to precise, uniform dimensions (typically ±0.2mm variation). Rectified tiles are more precise and allow narrower grout joints (1.5mm). Calibrated tiles still require standard grout joints (3mm–5mm) because of the remaining size variation. If your specification calls for calibrated tiles and the supplier delivers rectified, you've actually received an upgrade — but if the reverse happens (spec says rectified, you get calibrated), the tiles will not achieve the designed joint width and the finished floor may have lippage. Always verify that the delivered product matches the specification exactly. Rectified tiles cost 10%–20% more than calibrated.

**Q: 167** A buyer has installed a home gym on their tiled floor with heavy free weights. The tiles are cracking under the weight. They claim the tiles are "not fit for purpose." Are we liable?
**A:** Standard residential floor tiles are designed for domestic foot traffic (PEI Class 3 or 4, corresponding to approximately 500–1,500 Newtons point load). Heavy free weights (20kg+ dumbbells) dropped from height create point loads exceeding 3,000 Newtons, which can crack standard tiles. The SANS 1449 classification system does not rate tiles for gym use — commercial gym floors use rubber matting or specialised high-impact flooring. Your tiles were fit for the intended residential purpose; the buyer's use (home gym with dropped weights) is outside the design parameters. You are not liable unless the sales contract specifically warranted the tiles for "any use including heavy impact exercise" (which would be unusual). Offer the buyer guidance on installing rubber gym matting over the tiles to protect the floor — this is the standard solution for home gyms.

**Q: 168** Our development includes a wine cellar in the basement. The interior designer wants to install natural stone tiles. The wine cellar will be kept at 12°C with 70% humidity. Will stone tiles survive these conditions?
**A:** Natural stone tiles (travertine, marble, limestone) in a wine cellar at 12°C and 70% humidity face two challenges: (1) high humidity promotes mould and mildew growth on organic residues in the stone, (2) some stones (particularly green marbles and certain limestones) contain minerals that react with moisture and can cause surface spalling or discolouration. If the stone is properly sealed with a penetrating sealer (reapplied every 2–3 years) and the cellar has adequate ventilation, these issues are manageable. Better alternatives for wine cellars: (1) porcelain tiles with stone-look finish (impervious to moisture, no sealing required), (2) clay brick pavers (traditional, breathable, no condensation issues), (3) sealed concrete. If natural stone is insisted upon, specify a dense, low-porosity stone (granite or quartzite) rather than porous travertine or limestone. The stone must be installed with a waterproof adhesive and grout.

**Q: 169** The body corporate wants to install electric fencing on top of the perimeter wall. The wall is capped with decorative tiles. Will the electric fence installation damage the tiles?
**A:** Electric fence installation on a tiled wall cap requires drilling through the tiles to mount the fence brackets. This creates: (1) tile drilling points that must be sealed to prevent water ingress, (2) risk of tile cracking during drilling (especially if the tiles are porcelain or natural stone), (3) potential compromise of the wall's waterproofing capping. The installation should be done by a contractor experienced with tiled surfaces: (1) use diamond-tipped drill bits for clean holes, (2) apply epoxy sealant around each bracket fixing, (3) install a continuous mounting plate rather than individual brackets to reduce the number of penetrations, (4) ensure the electric fence installer has public liability insurance that covers tile damage. The body corporate should obtain three quotes and select the contractor with the least invasive mounting system. Wall cap tiles will likely need replacement around the drill points over time.

**Q: 170** We're building a recording studio as part of a mixed-use development. The acoustic consultant says the floor must be "floating" with an air gap. Can we still tile a floating floor?
**A:** Yes, but it requires a specialised system. A floating floor is isolated from the structural slab by resilient mounts or a continuous resilient layer (rubber or spring isolators). The floating floor typically consists of: (1) structural steel or timber frame, (2) acoustic insulation within the cavity, (3) plywood or cement-board topping, (4) tile adhesive and tiles on top. The total construction depth is 150mm–300mm. Critical considerations: (1) the floating floor must not contact the surrounding walls (isolation gap of 25mm minimum filled with acoustic sealant), (2) the additional dead load of tiles (20–25kg/m²) must be included in the floating floor structural design, (3) services (electrical, plumbing) must pass through the isolation gap with flexible connections, (4) the tile adhesive must be flexible (S1 classification) to accommodate any residual movement. This is a specialist installation — engage an acoustic flooring contractor. Budget R3,000–R6,000/m² for a complete floating floor with tile finish.

### SECTION Y: FINAL BATCH — THE MOST OBSCURE EDGE CASES

**Q: 171** Our development has a "show day" every Sunday where potential buyers view the show house. One Sunday, a viewer dropped a bottle of wine on the tiled floor, staining the grout. Who pays for the cleaning — us or the estate agent managing the show house?
**A:** The cost of preparing and maintaining the show house is a marketing expense borne by the developer unless the estate agent's mandate specifically includes cleaning and maintenance. A wine stain on grout is typically removable with an alkaline cleaner or hydrogen peroxide solution — it's a minor cleaning cost (R200–R500 for a professional clean). However, if the stain is permanent and the grout needs replacement, the cost increases to R2,000–R5,000. For regular show days, include a "show house maintenance" budget in your marketing plan (R3,000–R8,000/month for cleaning, consumables, and minor repairs). The estate agent should supervise visitors and enforce a "no food or drink" policy in finished areas. If the agent was negligent in supervision, they may share the cost, but pursuing this for a minor stain is not worth the relationship damage.

**Q: 172** We have a penthouse unit with a rooftop terrace. The waterproofing membrane is under the tiles. A satellite dish installer drilled through the tiles and membrane to mount a dish, causing a leak. The purchaser says we must fix it because the waterproofing is under warranty. Are we responsible for third-party damage?
**A:** The waterproofing warranty covers defects in the original installation, not damage caused by subsequent alterations by the owner or their contractors. If the satellite dish installer (engaged by the purchaser) drilled through the membrane, this is third-party damage and is not covered by the waterproofing warranty. The purchaser must: (1) claim from the satellite installer's insurance, (2) pay for the membrane repair themselves, or (3) claim from their homeowner's insurance. As developer, your obligation is limited to providing waterproofing that was defect-free at the time of handover. You should, however, provide the purchaser with the waterproofing system details and membrane location so they (and their contractors) know where not to drill. Consider including a "membrane location plan" in the handover documentation.

**Q: 173** Our development's basement has a vehicular ramp with a 1:8 slope. The architect specified polished porcelain tiles. The building inspector failed the ramp because of slip resistance. What slip rating is required for vehicle ramps?
**A:** Vehicular ramps (garage entrances, basement ramps) require a minimum R12 slip rating (pendulum wet value ≥ 55) under SANS 10107, and some insurers require R13 for commercial ramps. Polished porcelain achieves R9 at best — it is completely unsuitable for any sloped surface exposed to water or oil. The architect's specification is non-compliant and dangerous. Suitable alternatives: (1) textured concrete with carborundum strips, (2) R12 or R13 rated quarry tiles or paver tiles, (3) epoxy systems with anti-slip aggregate broadcast, (4) aluminium or brass stair nosing with anti-slip inserts at the ramp entrance. Reject the architect's specification and substitute a compliant finish. If the ramp is already tiled with polished porcelain, it must be replaced — there is no coating or treatment that reliably upgrades R9 to R12 on a long-term basis.

**Q: 174** A purchaser in our sectional title scheme is a "prepper" who has stockpiled 5,000 litres of water in their unit (in tanks on the tiled floor). The body corporate says this exceeds the floor loading and is a safety risk. Who is right?
**A:** 5,000 litres of water weighs 5,000kg. On a typical 15m² tiled floor area, this creates a distributed load of 3.3kPa — which exceeds the standard residential floor design load of 1.5kPa and may exceed the slab capacity, especially if the tanks create point loads through their support legs. The body corporate is correct to be concerned. Under STSMA Section 13(1)(a), an owner must use their section in a manner that does not cause a nuisance or damage to common property. If the water tanks damage the floor or create a structural risk, the body corporate can issue a compliance notice. The owner should either: (1) distribute the tanks across multiple support points to reduce point loads, (2) place them on a load-spreading platform, (3) relocate them to the garage (typically designed for higher loads), or (4) reduce the quantity. A structural engineer's assessment will determine the actual risk.

**Q: 175** We installed underfloor heating beneath tiles in all units. A buyer read online that electromagnetic fields (EMF) from heating cables are "dangerous" and wants the system removed at our cost. Is there any scientific basis for this claim?
**A:** No. Underfloor heating cables produce extremely low-frequency electromagnetic fields (ELF-EMF) at levels well below international safety guidelines (ICNIRP standards). The EMF from underfloor heating is typically less than 0.3 microtesla at floor level, which is lower than the Earth's natural magnetic field (25–65 microtesla) and far below the ICNIRP exposure limit of 100 microtesla for the general public. The World Health Organisation states there is no convincing scientific evidence that low-level ELF-EMF causes health effects. Provide the buyer with the UFH manufacturer's EMF test certificate and the WHO fact sheet on electromagnetic fields. If the buyer persists, offer an independent EMF measurement (cost: R2,000–R3,500) — the results will almost certainly show compliance. Removing the UFH system is not a reasonable request and you should not accommodate it unless a genuine hazard is proven.

**Q: 176** Our estate has a rule that all exterior tiles must be "cool roof" rated with minimum Solar Reflectance Index (SRI) of 29. Our specified tiles have SRI 20. What is SRI, and how do we comply?
**A:** Solar Reflectance Index (SRI) measures a surface's ability to reflect solar heat — higher SRI means cooler surface temperatures. SANS 10400-XA energy efficiency regulations recommend SRI 29 or higher for roof surfaces in hot climates. Some progressive estates have extended this to external floor and wall tiles to reduce the urban heat island effect. To comply: (1) specify light-coloured tiles (white, beige, light grey) which naturally have higher SRI, (2) some manufacturers produce "cool pigment" tiles that achieve higher reflectance without being white, (3) request the tile manufacturer's SRI test data — most don't test for this, but you can have it independently tested (cost: R8,000–R15,000). If your specified tiles genuinely cannot achieve SRI 29, apply to the estate's architectural review committee for a variance with supporting thermal analysis. For courtyard and walkway tiles, the SRI requirement is less critical than for roof tiles — argue for a lower threshold.

**Q: 177** A tile installer on site was caught stealing tiles and selling them privately. We dismissed him and withheld his final payment. He's threatening to report us to the Department of Labour for "unfair dismissal." Can he do this if he's an independent contractor?
**A:** If the tile installer is genuinely an independent contractor (registered business, provides own tools and equipment, controls how the work is done, bears financial risk), he has no recourse under the Labour Relations Act for unfair dismissal — the LRA protects employees, not independent contractors. His remedy for withheld payment is a civil claim (Magistrate's Court) for breach of contract. However, if he was in fact a de facto employee (you controlled his hours, provided materials, supervised his work), he can refer an unfair dismissal dispute to the CCMA. To protect yourself: (1) document the theft with evidence (CCTV, witness statements, recovered tiles), (2) report the theft to SAPS, (3) withhold payment in terms of the contract's set-off clause, (4) if he was a contractor, counterclaim for damages. The threat of Labour Department reporting is a bluff if he is truly independent. Ensure your subcontract agreements clearly define the independent contractor relationship.

**Q: 178** Our development includes a synagogue. The rabbi requires a "mikvah" (ritual bath) with specific tiling requirements — all-white tiles, no grout lines visible, and absolutely no leakage. What installation system achieves this?
**A:** A mikvah has strict religious requirements: (1) the water must be "living water" (rainwater or spring water) mixed with drawn water in a specific ratio, (2) the pool must hold water without leakage (the water level must not drop), (3) all surfaces must be smooth and white (no visible joints preferred). To achieve this: (1) use large-format white porcelain tiles (minimum 600x600mm) to minimise grout joints, (2) use white epoxy grout (colour-matched to the tile, does not stain), (3) install a full tanking system (epoxy or cementitious waterproofing) beneath the tiles with 100% coverage, (4) seal all grout joints with a clear epoxy sealant, (5) pressure-test the pool before tiling (fill and monitor for 7 days), (6) ensure the pool shell is structurally independent from the building to prevent differential movement. Budget R4,000–R8,000/m² for a mikvah installation — it's significantly more expensive than a standard pool due to the religious and technical requirements.

**Q: 179** We have a dispute where the tile supplier delivered 2,000m² of tiles but the delivery note was signed by our security guard (who can't read English) and the quantity was never verified. We later discovered we were short 300m². Who bears the loss?
**A:** The signature of your security guard on the delivery note creates a presumption that the goods were received as stated. However, if the guard was not authorised to verify quantities (security guards typically only check for visible damage and confirm vehicle exit), you may argue that his signature was for "safe receipt" only, not "quantity verification." Your contract terms with the supplier govern: if the contract requires the buyer to verify quantities within 24 hours and report discrepancies, and you failed to do so, the loss is likely yours. If the supplier's own driver counted the pallets incorrectly, the supplier bears the loss. Best practice: (1) authorise specific site personnel to count and sign for deliveries, (2) require the supplier to provide a packing list with tile counts per pallet, (3) verify pallet counts before signing, (4) photograph each delivery. For this dispute, check if the supplier's driver logs or CCTV footage show fewer pallets than invoiced.

**Q: 180** Our development's sectional title plan was approved with all floor finishes shown as "tiles." The surveyor now says we must amend the sectional title plan if we change any unit to vinyl flooring. Is this true?
**A:** No. The sectional title plan shows the boundaries of each section and the common property — it does not specify floor finishes. The surveyor may be confusing the sectional title plan with the building plans submitted to the municipality. Building plans may show floor finishes as part of the specification, but changing from tiles to vinyl within a unit does not affect the sectional title boundaries or the sectional plan. The body corporate's conduct rules may regulate finish changes in common areas, but not within private units unless the change affects structural elements, common services, or external appearance. If the surveyor insists on a sectional plan amendment, they are incorrect — escalate to the Surveyor-General's office for clarification. A sectional plan amendment is only required for structural alterations that change the boundaries or participation quotas.

**Q: 181** We're being audited by the CIDB (Construction Industry Development Board) and they've asked for our "tiling subcontractor's CIDB grade." Our tiler is a one-man business with no CIDB registration. Are we in trouble?
**A:** CIDB registration is required for contractors undertaking construction works above specified thresholds. For a private residential development (not government-funded), CIDB registration of subcontractors is NOT legally mandatory — the CIDB Act primarily regulates public sector procurement. However, some developers voluntarily require CIDB registration as part of their contractor qualification process. If your development is privately funded, the CIDB audit is likely related to your own registration status or a specific contractual requirement, not a statutory obligation for the tiler. If your development received any government subsidy or was procured by a public entity, then CIDB registration becomes mandatory for all contractors above the threshold. For a one-man tiling subcontractor on a private job, CIDB registration is not required by law. Verify why the CIDB is auditing you — if it's a voluntary audit for your own Grade 9 registration, explain that the subcontractor was below threshold.

**Q: 182** A tile manufacturer's catalogueshows a tile with a beautiful grey pattern. The actual tiles delivered have a much more pronounced pattern. The manufacturer says "shade variation is normal" and refers to V3 on the box. The buyer hates it. What does V3 mean?
**A:** V3 is the shade variation classification printed on tile packaging. The scale is: V1 = uniform appearance (minimal variation), V2 = slight variation, V3 = moderate variation (obvious differences in texture and colour), V4 = substantial variation (each tile looks different). If the catalogue showed a V1 or V2 appearance and the delivered tiles are V3, the manufacturer may have misrepresented the product. However, if the box is clearly marked V3 and the buyer's expectation was set by a single catalogue photograph, the manufacturer has a defence. Best practice for developers: (1) never rely on catalogue images — view a full display board with multiple tiles, (2) specify the exact V-rating in the contract, (3) require the tiler to dry-lay a representative area before fixing and obtain buyer approval. For this dispute, if the contract specified V1 or V2 and V3 was delivered, you have a legitimate claim against the supplier.

**Q: 183** Our development has an internal courtyard with a glass roof. The interior designer wants to install dark-coloured tiles to create a "dramatic effect." The glass roof creates a greenhouse effect — will dark tiles make the courtyard unbearably hot?
**A:** Yes. Dark tiles (LRV < 20) in a glass-roofed courtyard will absorb solar radiation and re-radiate it as heat, significantly increasing the courtyard temperature. In direct sunlight, dark tile surfaces can reach 60°C–70°C, making the space unusable and increasing the air conditioning load for adjacent rooms. Under a glass roof with no UV filtering, the tiles will also be exposed to concentrated UV, which can degrade some sealants and grouts over time. Recommendations: (1) use medium-tone tiles (LRV 40–60) for a balance of aesthetics and thermal comfort, (2) install solar-control glass or a retractable shading system, (3) specify tiles with high solar reflectance (cool pigments), (4) ensure adequate ventilation (extractor fans or operable roof panels). The interior designer should produce a thermal simulation before finalising the specification — aesthetics must not override thermal comfort.

**Q: 184** We're building a multi-generational estate where some units are designated for retirees and others for young families. Can we use different tile specifications for the "retirement" units versus the "family" units?
**A:** Yes, you can use different specifications for different unit types, provided this is disclosed in the sales documentation. In fact, it's good design practice: retirement units benefit from R10 slip-rated tiles (wet slip resistance), higher LRV (lighter tiles for better visibility), and matte finishes (reduced glare). Family units may prioritise durability (Class 4 PEI) and stain resistance over slip rating. However, be careful not to create a perception that retirement units are "lower spec" — frame the differences as "tailored design" rather than cost-cutting. If the retirement units are sold at a lower price point, the different specification is expected and justifiable. Document the rationale for each specification in case of queries. Never use lower-grade tiles in accessible units — SANS 10400-S requires higher performance standards for units designed for persons with disabilities.

**Q: 185** A buyer has requested that we leave the bathroom floors "untiled" so they can install their own tiles later. The NHBRC requires that wet areas be completed before enrolment. Can we accommodate this request?
**A:** The NHBRC requires that wet areas (showers, bathrooms, kitchens) have completed waterproofing before enrolment, but they do not strictly mandate that tiles must be installed — provided the waterproofing membrane is protected and functional. However, an unfinished bathroom floor will not pass a municipal final inspection for an Occupation Certificate — the building must be "completed" as per approved plans. Options: (1) install a temporary protective screed over the waterproofing that the buyer can remove later, (2) install standard tiles with a "buyer upgrade" variation order, (3) explain to the buyer that the bathroom must be finished for OC purposes and offer a credit against the purchase price for the tile cost, allowing them to re-tile later. Option 3 is the most practical — the buyer pays for the standard installation, receives a credit, and can upgrade after transfer. Ensure the waterproofing warranty is not voided by subsequent re-tiling.

**Q: 186** Our tiling contractor has proposed using a "tile levelling system" (plastic clips and wedges) to achieve perfect lippage-free results. This adds R15/m² to the cost. Is it worth it?
**A:** For large-format tiles (600x600mm and above), a tile levelling system (TLS) is highly recommended and often essential. TLS devices hold tiles at a consistent level during adhesive curing, eliminating lippage (height differences between adjacent tiles). The benefits: (1) perfectly flat floor or wall surface, (2) reduced risk of trip hazards, (3) professional aesthetic, (4) faster installation (tiler doesn't need to manually level each tile), (5) reduced tiler fatigue (less bending to check levels). For tiles below 400x400mm, TLS is less critical. At R15/m² on a 100-unit development (approximately 500m² per unit = 50,000m²), the total cost is R750,000. For a luxury development where finish quality is a key selling point, this is money well spent. For affordable housing, manual levelling by a skilled tiler may be sufficient. Specify TLS for all tiles 600x600mm and above.

**Q: 187** The estate's architectural review committee has rejected our specified tiles because they're "not on the approved finishes schedule." But the finishes schedule was written 10 years ago and most of those tiles are discontinued. What can we do?
**A:** An outdated finishes schedule creates a deadlock. The developer (you) or the body corporate can amend the architectural guidelines through the mechanism specified in the estate's constitution — typically a special resolution of the body corporate or developer approval. If you are still the developer and haven't transferred control to the body corporate, you have the authority to update the finishes schedule. If control has transferred: (1) submit a motivation to the architectural review committee for an amendment, (2) provide three alternative tile options that meet the aesthetic intent of the original schedule, (3) propose a formal review of the entire finishes schedule (many estates have this on their agenda annually). A 10-year-old schedule is legally challengeable if it forces the use of discontinued products — this is an unreasonable restriction that may not hold up in court. However, negotiation is always preferable to litigation in HOA matters.

**Q: 188** We're developing on a site that was previously a petrol station. The environmental consultant has cleared the site for hydrocarbon contamination, but warns that "sealed floor surfaces" (like tiles) can trap residual vapours. Is this a real concern?
**A:** Yes. If residual hydrocarbons (petrol, diesel, benzene) remain in the soil beneath the site, they can volatilise (turn to vapour) and migrate upward. A sealed floor surface like tiles with epoxy grout can trap these vapours in the sub-floor space, creating a potential explosion hazard or health risk if vapours find a pathway into the building through service penetrations. The correct approach for former petrol station sites: (1) the environmental consultant must conduct soil vapour assessment, (2) if vapour intrusion is a risk, install a sub-slab depressurisation system (SSDS) — a perforated pipe network beneath the slab that vents vapours to atmosphere, (3) ensure all service penetrations through the slab are sealed with vapour-resistant sealant, (4) the tile installation itself is not the problem — the slab design and sub-slab management is. Budget R200–R400/m² for SSDS installation. Do not proceed with construction until the environmental consultant issues a clean site closure certificate.

**Q: 189** A buyer wants to install a wood-burning fireplace on their tiled lounge floor. The fireplace weighs 250kg and will sit directly on the tiles. Will the tiles crack from the weight or heat?
**A:** A 250kg fireplace creates a distributed load of approximately 2.5kPa if the base is 1m² — this is within the capacity of a standard residential floor slab (1.5–2.0kPa live load plus dead load capacity). However, the heat is the main concern: a wood-burning fireplace can generate floor surface temperatures of 80°C–120°C near the base, which exceeds the thermal tolerance of standard tile adhesive (typically 70°C maximum). The tile may also crack from thermal shock if the heat is concentrated. Solution: (1) install a non-combustible hearth pad (stone, concrete, or steel plate) between the fireplace and the tiles, extending at least 300mm beyond the fireplace base on all sides, (2) use a heat-resistant adhesive (epoxy or specialised high-temperature adhesive) in the fireplace footprint area, (3) ensure the hearth pad is level and distributes the weight evenly. The buyer should engage a fireplace installer who understands hearth requirements — SANS 10400-V (space heating) specifies minimum hearth dimensions.

**Q: 190** Our development has a communal braai area. The body corporate wants to tile the braai area floor with the same tiles as the pool surround. Is this appropriate given the grease and ash exposure?
**A:** A braai area is exposed to grease, ash, fat drips, and high temperatures — none of which are kind to tiles or grout. Grease stains porous grout, ash is alkaline and can etch some natural stones, and dropped hot coals (800°C+) can crack porcelain or ceramic tiles through thermal shock. If the body corporate insists on tiling: (1) use dark-coloured, matte, vitrified porcelain tiles (minimum Class 4 PEI), (2) use epoxy grout (stain-proof, heat-resistant), (3) install a stainless steel or stone drip tray beneath the braai grid to catch fat and coals, (4) seal the tiles with a penetrating sealer rated for high heat, (5) budget for more frequent cleaning and eventual re-grouting. Better alternatives: (1) sealed concrete with saw-cut patterns, (2) clay brick pavers (traditional, forgiving, easily replaced), (3) natural stone like granite or slate (heat-resistant but requires sealing). The pool surround tiles are designed for chlorine and water exposure, not grease and heat — they will deteriorate faster in a braai area.

**Q: 191** We sold a unit to a foreign buyer who now says they want to export the tiles (remove them and ship them overseas) because they have "sentimental value." The tiles are fixed to the floor. Can they do this?
**A:** Once tiles are fixed to the floor, they become "fixtures" and part of the immovable property. Under South African property law, fixtures cannot be removed by the seller or buyer unless specifically excluded in the sale agreement. If the sale agreement did not exclude the tiles, the buyer has no right to remove them. Even if the buyer argues sentimental value, the tiles belong to the property and cannot be unilaterally removed. If the buyer attempts removal, it constitutes damage to the property and you could claim damages. This is an unusual request — most likely the buyer is testing boundaries or has misunderstood something. Politely explain that fixed floor finishes are part of the property and cannot be removed. If they insist, request a formal legal opinion from their attorney — no attorney would support this claim.

**Q: 192** Our development includes a gym with a free weights area. The gym operator wants rubber flooring, but the architect specified tiles. Can rubber flooring be installed over tiles, and does it need a separate subfloor?
**A:** Rubber gym flooring (10mm–25mm thick) can be installed directly over properly prepared tiles. The tile floor provides a flat, hard substrate that is ideal for rubber flooring. The installation process: (1) clean the tile floor thoroughly (degrease if necessary), (2) apply a pressure-sensitive adhesive or use interlocking rubber tiles that don't require adhesive, (3) install the rubber flooring in sheets or tiles. No separate subfloor is needed — the existing tiles serve as the subfloor. The benefits of rubber over tiles in a free weights area: (1) impact absorption (protects the slab and reduces noise transmission to units below), (2) equipment protection (dumbbells won't crack rubber), (3) athlete safety (reduces injury from falls). If the architect specified tiles, request a variation — rubber is the correct specification for a gym free weights area. Budget R350–R800/m² for quality rubber gym flooring.

**Q: 193** A tile delivery arrived with packaging that says "Made in China" but the sales order said "Italian Design." The buyer claims this is false advertising. Is "Italian Design" a legally acceptable description for Chinese-made tiles?
**A:** "Italian Design" is a marketing term that describes the aesthetic origin, not the manufacturing origin. It is legally acceptable if: (1) the design was genuinely created by an Italian design studio or based on Italian aesthetic traditions, (2) the packaging clearly states the country of manufacture ("Made in China"), (3) there is no attempt to conceal the manufacturing origin. However, if the buyer was genuinely misled into believing the tiles were manufactured in Italy, and this belief influenced their purchase decision, they may have a claim under CPA Section 41 for misleading representation. The test is whether a reasonable consumer would understand "Italian Design" to mean "designed in Italy, made in China" or "made in Italy." To avoid disputes, use clearer language: "Designed in Italy, Manufactured in China" or specify the exact factory location. Transparency prevents complaints.

**Q: 194** Our development's common area tiles were installed with an epoxy grout that has now discoloured after 18 months (yellowing in high-traffic areas). The grout manufacturer says this is "normal UV degradation." Is epoxy grout unsuitable for lobby areas?
**A:** Epoxy grout is generally superior to cement grout for stain resistance and durability, but some formulations (particularly white and light colours) can yellow when exposed to UV light or when subjected to high-temperature cleaning. In lobby areas with large windows and direct sunlight, UV exposure accelerates yellowing. The manufacturer's claim of "normal UV degradation" is partially valid — not all epoxy grouts are UV-stable. Solutions: (1) for future developments, specify "UV-stable epoxy grout" (formulated with aliphatic hardeners that resist yellowing), (2) use darker grout colours that don't show yellowing, (3) apply a UV-resistant clear sealer over the grout lines, (4) if the yellowing is severe, the grout can be ground out and replaced (cost: R80–R150/m²). For south-facing lobbies with high sun exposure, cement grout with a grout colourant/sealer may be more practical than standard epoxy.

**Q: 195** We're building on land that has a "mineral rights" reservation in the title deed. The mineral rights holder wants to conduct exploration drilling through our basement floor. Can they do this after we've built?
**A:** If the title deed reserves mineral rights to a third party, those rights remain enforceable even after you build. The Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act (MPRDA) governs the relationship between surface landowners and mineral rights holders. The mineral rights holder must: (1) give reasonable notice, (2) compensate you for any damage caused, (3) follow reasonable practices to minimise disruption. However, they generally have the right to access minerals beneath your land. If drilling through your basement is proposed, you can negotiate: (1) the timing (before tiling and fit-out, not after), (2) the location (minimise structural impact), (3) compensation for loss of use and remediation costs, (4) a bond to ensure they restore the property. If the drilling occurs after construction, they must repair all damage, including re-tiling the basement floor. Engage a mining attorney to negotiate the access agreement — mineral rights disputes can be highly complex.

**Q: 196** Our estate has a rule that "no alterations may be made without prior written consent of the developer." We've sold 90% of the units and the remaining 10% are still in our name. A sold-unit owner wants to retile their bathroom. Do we still have authority to approve or refuse?
**A:** If the title deed condition reserves approval rights to "the developer," and you remain the developer (the development company still exists and holds unsold units), you retain the approval authority. However, this power must be exercised reasonably — you cannot unreasonably withhold or delay consent for internal alterations that do not affect the building's structure, common property, or external appearance. Retiling a bathroom is an internal alteration that does not require developer consent unless the title deed specifically includes floor finishes in the restricted alterations. As a practical matter, most developers delegate this authority to the body corporate once the scheme is established. Continuing to exercise micro-control over sold units creates resentment and potential legal challenges. Consider amending the title deed conditions to transfer alteration approval to the body corporate for all internal works.

**Q: 197** We're required to install "tactile paving" at all pedestrian crossings within our estate. The quantity surveyor has priced this at R850/m². We can buy tactile indicator studs for R45 each and install them on the existing tiles. Which approach is correct?
**A:** Tactile paving can be installed as either: (1) full tactile paving tiles (typically R600–R1,000/m² supplied and installed), or (2) individual tactile indicator studs surface-mounted on existing paving or tiles (R150–R350/m² including installation). SANS 784 and SANS 10400-S accept both methods provided the indicators meet the dimensional and luminance contrast requirements. Surface-mounted studs on existing tiles are compliant, significantly cheaper, and easier to replace if damaged. At R45 per stud with approximately 12 studs per m² (spaced at 300mm centres), the material cost is R540/m² plus installation at R80–R120/m² = R620–R660/m² total. This is still less than R850/m². Challenge the QS's pricing — they may have specified full tactile tiles without considering the stud alternative. For estate roads and parking areas, surface-mounted studs are the standard solution.

**Q: 198** A tile installer on site mixed different brands of adhesive and grout, claiming "they're all the same." The installation is now showing efflorescence and soft grout. Is mixing brands the cause?
**A:** Mixing different manufacturers' products is not recommended and often voids warranties. Each manufacturer formulates their products to work as a system — the adhesive, grout, primer, and sealer are chemically compatible. Cross-brand mixing can cause: (1) chemical incompatibility (one product's additives react with another's), (2) efflorescence (different salt compositions migrate at different rates), (3) adhesion failure (different flexibility characteristics create stress), (4) colour inconsistency in grout. The efflorescence and soft grout are likely caused by using a cement grout with an incompatible admixture or by using grout that was not designed for the joint width installed. The defective work must be removed and reinstalled using a single manufacturer's system. Claim the rectification cost from the tile installer — this is defective workmanship, not a product defect. Specify "single manufacturer system" in your installation specifications going forward.

**Q: 199** Our development includes a helipad on the roof of the building. The civil engineer says the helipad surface must withstand 4,000kg point loads from helicopter landing gear. Can we tile a helipad?
**A:** A helipad surface must withstand: (1) concentrated point loads from landing gear (2,000–4,000kg per wheel depending on helicopter class), (2) jet blast (downward rotor wash at high velocity), (3) fuel and hydraulic fluid spillage, (4) extreme weather exposure. Standard tiles cannot withstand these loads — a Bell 407 helicopter's landing gear creates approximately 1,000kg point loads per strut, and heavier helicopters create significantly more. The helipad surface is typically: (1) reinforced concrete with a non-slip epoxy or paint finish, (2) aluminium or steel grating, (3) specialised helipad matting systems. Tiles would crack under point loads and create dangerous debris from jet blast. Do NOT tile a helipad. The SANS 10400-B structural requirements for helipads are specific and substantial — engage an aviation structural engineer. The helipad must also comply with CAA (Civil Aviation Authority) regulations for marking, lighting, and fire suppression.

**Q: 200** We've reached the end of our 200 questions. As a final catch-all: what is the single most common mistake SA developers make with floor tiles that costs them the most money in callbacks and legal disputes?
**A:** The single most costly mistake is inadequate or incorrect waterproofing in wet areas, compounded by failure to install movement joints at proper spacing. The sequence of failure is: (1) waterproofing membrane is either omitted, poorly applied, or incompatible with the tile adhesive, (2) water penetrates through grout joints (which are never fully waterproof) to the substrate, (3) without movement joints at maximum 4.5m intervals, thermal and moisture expansion creates stress, (4) the tiles debond, crack, or tent, (5) water ingress damages the ceiling and finishes of the unit below, (6) the NHBRC warranty, waterproofing warranty, and CPA claims all converge, creating multiple liability pathways. The rectification cost for a single failed shower is R15,000–R30,000; for a basement waterproofing failure, R500,000+. The prevention cost is R50–R80/m² for proper waterproofing and movement joints. The rule is simple: never compromise on waterproofing, and always install movement joints — the savings from cutting these corners are false economies that always cost more later.
