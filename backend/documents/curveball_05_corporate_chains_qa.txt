# PERSONA 5: CORPORATE NATIONAL CHAINS — 200 AWKWARD, STRANGE & UNEXPECTED Q&A PAIRS
# South African Tiling, Flooring & Construction Industry

---

## 1. B-BBEE & SUPPLIER COMPLIANCE

**Q:** 1 Our tiling supplier's B-BBEE certificate expired two months into a three-year national rollout contract. Our procurement scorecard now reflects non-compliant spend. Can we continue paying them, or must we halt all payments until renewal?
**A:** You are not legally required to halt payments, but the spend will no longer count toward your Preferential Procurement scorecard under the B-BBEE Codes of Good Practice. Under SANAS accreditation rules, a supplier has 12 months from expiry to renew without losing their verified status level, provided they apply before expiry. However, your scorecard reflects the date of verification. Best practice: negotiate a conditional penalty clause in the contract that triggers a price reduction or extended payment terms if B-BBEE status drops during contract term. The dtic's B-BBEE Commission has no enforcement power to stop trade, but your own ESD targets may be at risk.

**Q:** 2 Our tiling supplier is a Level 4 B-BBEE contributor but their subcontracted installer is non-compliant. Does the installation spend count toward our procurement recognition, or only the tile supply portion?
**A:** Under the Revised Codes of Good Practice (2013), only the entity with which you have a direct contract contributes to your procurement recognition. If your contract is with the supplier only, installation labour subcontracted by them does not automatically accrue to your scorecard unless the subcontractor is a designated group supplier and the main contractor flows the recognition through a written agreement. However, many corporates now require "flow-down" B-BBEE clauses mandating that subcontractors meet minimum levels. If your supplier simply pays a cash-and-carry installer, that labour component does not enhance your scorecard at all.

**Q:** 3 We have 147 franchise stores nationwide. Each franchisee sources their own tiles independently. For B-BBEE measurement purposes, is the franchisor (the brand owner) responsible for aggregating all franchisee procurement spend, or does each franchisee report independently?
**A:** This is a complex structural question. Under the B-BBEE Codes, franchisees with annual turnover below R10 million qualify as Exempt Micro-Enterprises (EMEs) automatically and need not be measured. However, if franchisees turnover above R50 million (Qualifying Small Enterprises) or R50 million+ (Generic Enterprises), they must be measured independently. The franchisor is NOT automatically responsible for franchisee procurement unless the franchisor holds the procurement contracts centrally. If franchisees order directly, they each maintain separate scorecards. However, many franchisors centralise procurement to control brand standards — in this case, the franchisor's scorecard absorbs the spend. Beware: if franchisees are majority foreign-owned, they may score zero for ownership, dragging down a centralised scorecard.

**Q:** 4 Our front-of-house tiles are imported from Italy. The local distributor has a Level 2 B-BBEE certificate, but the manufacturer overseas has zero B-BBEE status. Our CFO insists that imported goods carry a 15% B-BBEE penalty on procurement recognition. Is this correct?
**A:** Yes, your CFO is referring to Code Series 400 of the B-BBEE Codes, which states that imported goods and services do not qualify for full procurement recognition unless the importer can demonstrate that the goods are unavailable locally at competitive quality, or the import is for a specific technical reason. The standard practice is that imported goods receive recognition at a reduced rate — typically the supplier's B-BBEE level minus penalties, or in some sector codes, no recognition at all. The Construction Sector Code (amended 2022) has specific provisions. You can claim full recognition only if you obtain a letter from the dtic or relevant industry body confirming local unavailability. Document this rationale for your B-BBEE verification agency.

**Q:** 5 We appointed a tiling contractor who fronted as 51% black-owned but the black shareholder is a sleeping partner with no operational involvement. A whistleblower reported this to the B-BBEE Commission. As the client, what is our exposure?
**A:** Fronting is a criminal offence under the B-BBEE Amendment Act 46 of 2013, punishable by up to 10 years imprisonment and/or a fine of up to 10% of annual turnover. As the client, your exposure depends on your level of due diligence. If you conducted reasonable verification (reviewed share certificates, CIPC records, shareholder agreements) and had no knowledge of the fronting arrangement, you are unlikely to face criminal liability. However, your procurement recognition for all spend with that contractor may be clawed back by your verification agency. The B-BBEE Commission can also publish your name in connection with the matter. Protect yourself: require annual sworn affidavits and independent legal review of ownership structures for all significant B-BBEE suppliers.

**Q:** 6 Our B-BBEE verification agency has questioned whether our tile adhesive supplier's Enterprise & Supplier Development (ESD) contributions are genuine. The supplier claims our 12-month extended payment terms count as ESD. Does this qualify?
**A:** Extended payment terms alone do not automatically qualify as ESD contributions. Under Statement 400 of the B-BBEE Codes, ESD contributions must involve "substantive" support, which may include interest-free or concessional loans, equity investments, guarantees, or business development support. Simply paying later than 30 days does not constitute ESD — in fact, the Prompt Payment Act and industry codes discourage late payment. However, if the extended terms are structured as a formal supplier finance programme with concessional interest rates, documented in a written agreement, and reported as part of a recognised ESD programme, it may qualify. Your verification agency applies the ESD Recognition Matrix — not all programmes pass.

**Q:** 7 We are a Level 6 B-BBEE contributor and a major retail chain wants us to tile 200 stores. Their minimum threshold is Level 4. Can we subcontract 30% to a Level 1 EME to "uplift" our effective compliance level?
**A:** No. There is no "uplift" mechanism that allows you to subcontract your way to a higher B-BBEE level for client compliance purposes. The client evaluates YOUR entity's B-BBEE certificate, not an aggregate of you and subcontractors. However, strategic subcontracting can improve your own scorecard over time — for example, if you subcontract to black-owned EMEs, that spend may improve your Preferential Procurement and ESD indicators on your next verification. But for immediate contract compliance, your own certificate must meet the client's minimum threshold. Some clients will accept a binding undertaking to achieve Level 4 within 12 months, backed by penalty clauses.

**Q:** 8 Our corporate property division sources tiles through a central procurement hub registered in Gauteng, but tiles are delivered to stores in all nine provinces. For B-BBEE purposes, should we be measuring provincial spend distribution, or is national aggregation sufficient?
**A:** B-BBEE measurement is entity-level, not provincial. Your procurement recognition is calculated on the total spend of the measured entity (the procurement hub) regardless of delivery destination. However, there are two exceptions: first, if you have separate legal entities in different provinces, each is measured separately. Second, some Sector Codes (notably Construction) have regional targets for local procurement. If your central hub is a separate legal entity from the stores, only the hub's spend counts toward its own scorecard. The stores' procurement (if they hold separate contracts) counts separately. Many corporates consolidate to maximise bargaining power and simplify B-BBEE reporting.

## 2. ESG, CARBON REPORTING & SUSTAINABILITY

**Q:** 9 We are JSE-listed and must report Scope 3 emissions under the Sustainability and Climate Disclosure Requirements. Our tiling supplier cannot provide embodied carbon data for porcelain tiles. How do we calculate Scope 3 for 50,000 square metres of tiling annually?
**A:** Under the JSE Sustainability Disclosure Guidance (aligned with ISSB/IFRS S2), you must report Scope 3 emissions using primary supplier data where available, or secondary data (industry averages) where not. If your supplier cannot provide Product-Specific Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs) or embodied carbon data, use the ICE Database (Inventory of Carbon and Energy), generic porcelain tile emission factors from the Global Cement and Concrete Association, or South Africa-specific data from the Cement & Concrete SA (CCSA) sustainability reports. Document your methodology, data gaps, and improvement targets. The JSE does not yet mandate full Scope 3 disclosure (as of 2024), but best practice is to estimate using spend-based or physical-unit-based methods and disclose the estimation approach. Engage your supplier to obtain EPDs — this is becoming a contractual requirement for many listed corporates.

**Q:** 10 Our Green Star SA rating for a new flagship store depends partly on the flooring materials' eco-certification. The tile supplier claims their product is "eco-friendly" but has no GreenTag, EPD, or SANS 10400-XA compliance certificate. Can we still claim points?
**A:** No. The Green Building Council of South Africa (GBCSA) Green Star rating tool requires specific, verifiable evidence for Material category credits. Generic "eco-friendly" claims carry no weight. You need at least one of: a Global GreenTag certificate, an ISO-compliant Environmental Product Declaration (EPD), proof of recycled content percentage with third-party verification, or SANS 10400-XA compliance for thermal performance. Without these, the material cannot contribute to Green Star points. Some suppliers will obtain GreenTag certification on request if you commit to volume — negotiate this into your specification. Alternatively, consider GBCSA's Pilot Credit for embodied carbon reporting, which may accept provisional data with improvement commitments.

**Q:** 11 Our sustainability report was challenged by an activist shareholder who claimed our "carbon-neutral store rollout" was misleading because the tiles were manufactured using coal-fired kilns. What is our legal exposure under the Companies Act?
**A:** Section 22 of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 prohibits fraudulent or reckless conduct, and section 24 requires financial statements to present a fair view. While sustainability claims are not yet formally audited, the Advertising Standards Authority of South Africa (ASA) and the Takeover Regulation Panel have taken action against greenwashing. More seriously, the JSE Listings Requirements (Practices Note 1/2023 on Sustainability Disclosure) require that ESG claims be accurate and verifiable. If your "carbon-neutral" claim was based on offsets while ignoring embodied carbon in materials, an activist could argue misrepresentation. Best defence: ensure all sustainability claims are substantiated by documented offset purchases, validated emission calculations, and third-party assurance. Use cautious language — "carbon-neutral operations" (Scope 1 & 2 only) is more defensible than "carbon-neutral store" which implies full lifecycle coverage.

**Q:** 12 We need to report water usage in our tile supply chain under the CDP Water Security questionnaire. Our supplier is a cement-based tile manufacturer in Lichtenburg. What water data should we request, and what if they refuse?
**A:** For CDP Water Security reporting, request: total water withdrawal (municipal, borehole, recycled), water discharge volumes and quality, water stress exposure of manufacturing locations, and any water stewardship certifications (AWS, ISO 46001). Lichtenburg falls within a water-stressed area of the North West — the Vaal River System has experienced severe shortages. If the supplier refuses, use the WRI Aqueduct Water Risk Atlas to assign a default risk score, disclose the data gap, and explain your engagement strategy. CDP scoring penalises non-response but rewards transparency about gaps. For JSE-listed companies, water reporting is increasingly linked to King IV Principle 16 (organisation in the natural and social context).

**Q:** 13 Our board has approved a circular economy target: 30% of flooring materials must contain recycled content by 2027. We discovered our tile supplier's "recycled" tiles contain pre-consumer waste only (factory off-cuts), not post-consumer waste. Does this still count as recycled content for ESG reporting?
**A:** It depends on your reporting framework. Under GRI 301 (Materials), both pre-consumer and post-consumer recycled content are recognised, but post-consumer is weighted more favourably. Under GreenTag and some Green Star credits, post-consumer content receives higher points. The ISO 14021 environmental labels standard distinguishes between "pre-consumer" (process waste) and "post-consumer" (end-of-life product) recycled content. For credible ESG reporting, you should disclose the split between pre- and post-consumer content. If your board target was ambiguously worded, the supplier's pre-consumer content may technically qualify, but you risk credibility. Specify in your procurement policy: "post-consumer recycled content preferred; pre-consumer content accepted only where post-consumer is unavailable."

**Q:** 14 Our tiles are shipped from Italy to Durban, then railed to Johannesburg. For Scope 3 emissions, should we report the shipping emissions under our South African entity or the European supplier's entity?
**A:** Under the GHG Protocol Corporate Value Chain (Scope 3) Standard, emissions from upstream transportation are reported by the entity that pays for the transport — or if shared, by the entity with operational control. If you (the South African retailer) contract and pay the freight forwarder, you report these emissions under Category 4 (Upstream Transport). If the supplier includes transport in their delivered price and contracts the shipping, the emissions fall under Category 1 (Purchased Goods & Services) and would be included in the supplier's own Scope 1/2 inventory. For accuracy, adopt "control" or "financial responsibility" as your allocation principle and apply it consistently. Document this in your carbon accounting policy.

**Q:** 15 We are required to set Science-Based Targets (SBTi) for our retail operations. Our largest flooring supplier has refused to commit to SBTi or provide emission reduction targets. Can we still include their tiles in our SBTi-validated inventory?
**A:** Yes, but with limitations. SBTi does not require that all suppliers have their own SBTi-approved targets. Your near-term target must cover 95% of Scope 1+2 emissions and 67% of Scope 3 emissions. You can include the supplier's tiles in your inventory using their current emission factors and your own reduction pathway (e.g., switching to lower-carbon materials, reducing wastage). However, SBTi's FLAG (Forest, Land and Agriculture) and supplier engagement requirements are tightening. From 2025, SBTi strongly encourages — and may soon require — that a percentage of suppliers by spend have their own science-based targets. Engage the supplier formally, document the refusal, and include supplier engagement as part of your target narrative.

**Q:** 16 Our annual integrated report includes a photo of our flagship store's beautiful tiled interior. The auditor questioned whether we needed to include the embodied carbon of those tiles in our reported Scope 3 emissions. Is a decorative photo a reportable emission?
**A:** This highlights the boundary problem in carbon accounting. If the store is company-owned and operational during the reporting period, the tiles are part of your fixed asset base and their embodied carbon should be captured in Category 1 (Purchased Goods) or Category 2 (Capital Goods), depending on whether you classify fit-out as capex or opex. The photo itself is irrelevant — what matters is whether the tiles were purchased in the reporting period. If the store was built in a prior year, the embodied carbon was reported then (unless you use rolling reporting). Don't confuse marketing imagery with emission boundaries. Follow the GHG Protocol's guidance on capital goods accounting.

## 3. POPIA & DATA PRIVACY

**Q:** 17 Our tiling contractor needs access to employee locker rooms to measure for floor renovations. This requires capturing names, shift patterns, and locker numbers. Does this trigger POPIA compliance obligations?
**A:** Yes. Shift patterns linked to names constitute personal information under Section 1 of POPIA (Protection of Personal Information Act 4 of 2013). Locker numbers may not be personal information on their own, but combined with names they create an information regime that identifies individuals. You need: a processing justification (lawful basis — likely legitimate interest of the employer with consent), a privacy notice to affected employees, a data processing agreement with the contractor specifying purpose limitation and retention periods, and technical safeguards (encrypted storage, access controls). The contractor becomes an "operator" under POPIA, and you (the responsible party) remain liable for their compliance. Do not share employee data without these protections — the Information Regulator can impose administrative fines up to R10 million.

**Q:** 18 We use biometric access control at all stores. During a tile replacement project, the installation crew requested biometric enrollment to access the building after hours. Six months later, we discovered the contractor retained all biometric data on their own server. Is this a POPIA breach?
**A:** Almost certainly yes. Biometric data is classified as "special personal information" under Section 26 of POPIA, attracting heightened protection. The contractor acted as your operator and had no independent right to retain biometric data after project completion. Section 19 of POPIA requires that personal information be destroyed or de-identified as soon as reasonably practicable after the purpose for collection is fulfilled. The contractor's retention constitutes unlawful processing. You must: (1) issue a formal instruction for immediate deletion with proof; (2) notify affected employees; (3) assess whether the breach is notifiable to the Information Regulator under Section 22 (likely yes, given biometric sensitivity); (4) review all operator agreements to include mandatory deletion clauses. The Information Regulator has already imposed significant fines for biometric data mishandling — see the 2023 enforcement notices.

**Q:** 19 Our CCTV system captured footage of a tile installation team working in a pharmacy section. The footage includes prescription counter activity visible in the background. A patient claims this violates their medical privacy under POPIA. Is our pharmacy tenant liable, or are we as landlord?
**A:** This is a classic responsible party vs. joint controller question. Under POPIA, the entity that determines the purpose and means of processing is the responsible party. If you (the landlord/retailer) installed CCTV for security purposes and control the system, you are the responsible party for the footage. However, if the pharmacy tenant has independent cameras for regulatory compliance (SAPC requirements), they may be a separate responsible party. Where footage overlaps, you may be joint controllers. The pharmacy's obligation under POPIA Section 26 (special personal information — health data) is strict — health data cannot be processed without explicit consent or specific lawful justification. Best practice: angle cameras to exclude clinical areas, use privacy masking, and have written agreements allocating POPIA responsibilities in multi-tenant spaces.

**Q:** 20 During a national store rollout, our tiling project manager created a WhatsApp group including store managers, contractors, and our procurement team to coordinate access. The group shared photos of store layouts including emergency exit plans and alarm codes. A contractor's phone was stolen. What's our POPIA exposure?
**A:** Significant. WhatsApp groups are notoriously insecure for business communications. Alarm codes and emergency exit plans, while not "personal information" per se, may be linked to responsible individuals (security personnel names, keyholder details). More critically, if any personal information was shared (employee names, contact numbers, ID numbers for access clearance), this constitutes processing without adequate safeguards. POPIA Section 19 requires appropriate, reasonable technical and organisational measures. WhatsApp's consumer-grade encryption does not meet corporate data governance standards. You should: (1) conduct a breach assessment; (2) rotate all compromised alarm codes immediately; (3) notify affected individuals; (4) assess notifiability to the Information Regulator; (5) implement a corporate policy prohibiting work-related personal information sharing on consumer messaging apps. Reference the Information Regulator's 2022 guidance on workplace communications.

**Q:** 21 We want to use customer foot traffic data (collected via in-store WiFi and thermal sensors embedded in the ceiling near tiled areas) to optimise store layout and tile cleaning schedules. Does this require POPIA compliance, and do we need customer consent?
**A:** If the data is fully anonymised and cannot be re-identified (no MAC address tracking, no linkage to loyalty programmes), it falls outside POPIA's scope — POPIA applies to identifiable natural persons. However, if WiFi tracking captures device MAC addresses that could theoretically be linked to individuals (e.g., through loyalty app integration), it IS personal information. Thermal sensors that count bodies without identification are generally not POPIA-regulated. Best practice: conduct a privacy impact assessment (PIA) before deployment, publish a privacy notice at store entry explaining data collection methods, and ensure technical anonymisation (hashing, truncation) of any device identifiers. The POPIA Code of Conduct for consumer-facing businesses (pending finalisation) is expected to address IoT analytics specifically.

**Q:** 22 Our tiling supplier requested copies of our store manager ID documents to issue access cards for after-hours installation work. Six months after project completion, the supplier's HR file still contains 34 copies of ID documents. Is this lawful under POPIA?
**A:** No. Section 14 of POPIA requires that personal information not be retained longer than necessary for the purpose for which it was collected. The purpose — issuing access cards — was fulfilled when the project ended. Retaining ID copies six months later violates the retention limitation principle. Section 19 further requires secure destruction when no longer needed. The supplier must delete or securely destroy these documents. You, as the responsible party who provided the data, share accountability. Issue a formal instruction for destruction with a compliance certificate. If the supplier needs ID records for tax or labour purposes (COIDA, UIF), they should retain only the minimum necessary (ID number, not full document copy) and only for the statutory period. The Information Regulator's 2023 enforcement trend shows increasing focus on excessive data retention.

## 4. FORCE MAJEURE & SUPPLY CHAIN DISRUPTION

**Q:** 23 Our Italian tile supplier's factory was destroyed by fire. Our contract has a force majeure clause but no specific exclusion for supplier factory fires. We have 40 stores scheduled for fit-out in the next 6 months. What is our legal position?
**A:** Under South African common law, force majeure (vis maior / casus fortuitus) requires an unforeseeable event that makes performance impossible, not merely more expensive or inconvenient. A factory fire at the supplier's premises typically qualifies as force majeure if: (1) the fire was not caused by the supplier's negligence; (2) the supplier could not reasonably have prevented it; (3) the supplier notified you without delay. However, force majeure does not automatically excuse the entire contract — only the affected obligations. Check your contract: does it have a "sole source" clause, alternative supply obligations, or stock-holding requirements? If the supplier is a distributor (not manufacturer), they may have a duty to source from alternative factories. If the contract is silent, you may have to accept temporary suspension or negotiate termination. The Consumer Protection Act may give you additional rights if you're a consumer, but B2B contracts are governed primarily by the common law and contract terms.

**Q:** 24 The force majeure event in Italy has triggered a 40% price increase from our supplier's alternative factory. Our fixed-price rollout contract with the franchisee network means we absorb the increase. Can we claim the difference as damages?
**A:** Unless your supplier contract contains a price protection clause or penalty for force majeure-related sourcing, you generally cannot claim the difference as damages — force majeure typically suspends obligations without creating liability. However, examine the contract closely: does the supplier warrant that they are the manufacturer, or did they represent themselves as a distributor with multiple sources? If they misrepresented their supply chain, you may have a claim under the Consumer Protection Act (if applicable) or under contract law for breach of warranty. For B2B contracts, the principle of supervening impossibility (Unmoglichkeit) may allow renegotiation under the doctrine of changed circumstances ( Geschaftsgrundlage ), though South African courts are cautious about this. Document everything, negotiate interim pricing, and consider alternative suppliers. For future contracts, include force majeure cascading provisions and alternative supply obligations.

**Q:** 25 Our tile shipment from China is stuck at the Port of Durban due to a Transnet strike (unprotected industrial action). Our supplier claims force majeure. Our stores cannot open without flooring. Who bears the risk — supplier, Transnet, or us?
**A:** The allocation of risk depends on your Incoterms and contract terms. If your contract is DDP (Delivered Duty Paid), the supplier bears all risk until delivery to your premises — the strike is their problem. If FOB (Free On Board) or EXW (Ex Works), risk transferred to you at the port of shipment or factory, and you bear the delay risk. In South Africa, unprotected strike action by Transnet workers has been held by some courts to constitute vis maior for contractual purposes, provided the strike was unforeseeable and the affected party took reasonable steps to mitigate. However, the Supreme Court of Appeal in Transnet Ltd v Owner of MV Snow Crystal held that port congestion does not automatically excuse performance. Check whether your supplier has marine delay insurance or contingent business interruption cover. For critical path items, always specify DDP terms and require the supplier to hold 3 months' safety stock in-country.

**Q:** 26 Our Portuguese tile supplier has invoked force majeure due to EU sanctions on a Russian gas supplier that powered their kiln. The tiles are available but production is halved. Is "energy supply disruption due to sanctions on a third party" valid force majeure in South African law?
**A:** This is a novel question that South African courts have not squarely addressed. Traditional force majeure requires the event to be external, unforeseeable, and irresistible. Sanctions on a third party affecting energy supply is arguably indirect — the supplier could potentially source alternative energy (at higher cost). South African courts distinguish between physical impossibility and economic hardship (prestation impossible vs. fait du prince). Mere increased cost or economic inconvenience typically does not excuse performance. However, if the kiln literally cannot operate without Russian gas and no alternative exists, the argument strengthens. Your contract's force majeure clause may specifically list "government action" or "energy supply disruption" — check for these. If not, the supplier is likely in anticipatory breach if they unilaterally reduce supply. Engage legal counsel and consider whether the doctrine of changed circumstances (imprévision) has any application — it is not well-developed in South African law.

**Q:** 27 Load shedding Stage 6 has made it impossible for our tiling contractor to work during agreed hours (they need power for cutting, lighting, and adhesive mixing). They claim force majeure and refuse to pay liquidated damages for delay. Are they correct?
**A:** Load shedding Stage 6 is unlikely to constitute force majeure for a professional tiling contractor in 2024. Since 2022, load shedding has been foreseeable and persistent. The court in Maccaferri Africa v Eskom Holdings (related proceedings) and numerous construction arbitrations have held that load shedding is now a known risk that contractors must plan for. A competent contractor should have: generator capacity, battery-backed lighting, scheduling around load shedding schedules, and manual mixing capability. If your contract is based on JBCC (Joint Building Contracts Committee) or FIDIC principles, the contractor bears the risk of their own power supply. However, if your contract specifically allocates Eskom supply risk to you, or if Stage 6 exceeds the contract's contemplated disruption level, the contractor may have a partial defence. Liquidated damages for load shedding delays are generally enforceable if the rate is not penal. Best practice: specify in your contract that the contractor must provide independent power for all operations.

**Q:** 28 A cyber attack on our supplier's ordering system (ransomware) has deleted all our order history and specifications for 89 stores. The supplier claims this is force majeure. We need to reconstruct everything urgently. Is cyber attack recognised as force majeure in South Africa?
**A:** Cyber attacks are increasingly recognised as potential force majeure events, but the analysis depends on foreseeability and mitigation. If the supplier had inadequate cybersecurity (no backups, no MFA, outdated systems), a court or arbitrator may find the event was self-induced or foreseeable, negating the force majeure defence. The POPIA dimension adds complexity — if personal data was compromised, the supplier must notify the Information Regulator. For your immediate problem: the supplier's record-keeping failure is likely a separate breach of contract (duty to maintain records). You should: (1) demand all available backups from the supplier; (2) reconstruct specifications from your own records, site photos, and physical inspections; (3) withhold payment for the costs of reconstruction from the supplier; (4) review contract terms for record-keeping obligations and cyber incident liability. For future contracts, mandate ISO 27001 certification or equivalent cybersecurity standards for all digital supply chain partners.

## 5. PRODUCT LIABILITY & INSURANCE

**Q:** 29 A customer slipped on a wet porcelain tile floor in our Cape Town store during winter rain. The tile had a Pendulum Test Value (PTV) of 35 (wet), which exceeds the minimum 36 for low-risk areas in SANS 10107 but the tile was specified by our architect for a high-traffic retail entrance. Who is liable — us, the architect, or the tile supplier?
**A:** South African product liability is primarily fault-based under the Aquilian action (delict), though the Consumer Protection Act introduces strict liability for defective goods supplied to consumers. Here, multiple parties face potential liability: (1) YOU (the retailer) owe customers a duty of care to maintain safe premises — wet floors at entrances during rain are a known hazard, and your failure to provide mats, warning signs, or immediate mopping may be negligent; (2) The ARCHITECT may be liable if they specified a tile with inadequate slip resistance for a high-traffic entrance knowing the application — professional negligence; (3) The TILE SUPPLIER is only liable if the tile was defective (did not meet its rated specification) or if they failed to warn of limitations. A PTV of 35 is borderline — SANS 10107:2018 recommends PTV ≥ 45 for high-traffic entrances. If the architect specified below this, they may carry liability. The CPA strict liability applies to the "supply chain" — as the retailer, you are liable to the consumer but may have a recourse claim against upstream parties. Ensure your public liability insurance covers customer slips and that you obtain indemnities from design professionals.

**Q:** 30 A child was injured when a loose floor tile in our toy department lifted and cut their foot. The tile was installed 18 months ago by a subcontractor. Our supplier blames the installer; the installer blames the adhesive (supplied by a third party). How do we determine liability?
**A:** This is a classic supply chain liability chain. Under the Consumer Protection Act Section 61, ALL parties in the supply chain (manufacturer, distributor, supplier, installer) are jointly and severally liable for harm caused by defective or unsafe goods. "Defective" includes design defects, manufacturing defects, and failure to provide adequate warnings. You must: (1) preserve the evidence (loose tile, adhesive residue, subfloor); (2) commission an independent forensic investigation to determine the failure mode (adhesive failure, substrate preparation, tile warping, structural movement); (3) notify your public liability insurer immediately; (4) issue Section 60 recall notices if other stores may have the same defect. The party ultimately responsible depends on the forensic findings: if inadequate substrate preparation, the installer is liable; if adhesive failed within specification, the adhesive manufacturer; if the tile was curved/bowed beyond SANS 1449 tolerances, the tile manufacturer. Your contract should include indemnity and insurance clauses flowing through the chain. Report to the NRCS (National Regulator for Compulsory Specifications) if a product safety issue is identified.

**Q:** 31 Our insurance broker has advised that our public liability policy excludes "defective product claims" and we need separate product liability cover for tiles. Is this correct, and what should the cover limits be for a 200-store national chain?
**A:** Yes, your broker is correct. General public liability insurance covers injury or damage arising from your negligence (e.g., failing to maintain floors). Product liability insurance covers claims arising from defective products you sell or supply, even if you were not negligent. These are separate risks requiring separate cover (or a combined policy with distinct sections). For a 200-store national chain, recommended product liability limits: minimum R50 million per occurrence / R100 million aggregate for a standard retail operation; R100 million+ if you have high-traffic locations, food service areas, or children's sections. Consider: (1) whether the policy is occurrence-based or claims-made; (2) whether it covers recall costs; (3) retroactive date for historical installations; (4) whether subcontractor work is covered (many policies exclude work by uninsured subcontractors). Require all suppliers and contractors to carry their own product liability and CIPC (Contractors' All Risk) insurance, with you named as additional insured.

**Q:** 32 A customer alleges that the VOC emissions from our newly tiled store caused their asthma attack. They are suing for damages under the CPA. Our tile supplier provided SANS-compliant adhesive with VOC levels below 30g/L. Do we have a defence?
**A:** You have a strong defence but must prove it. Under the Consumer Protection Act, you must show that the goods were safe, of good quality, and complied with applicable standards. SANS-compliant VOC levels below 30g/L are generally considered safe for occupied spaces with adequate ventilation. However, the CPA also considers "reasonably foreseeable use" — if the store was opened to the public while adhesive was still off-gassing (typically 24-72 hours), you may be liable for negligence in timing the opening, even if the product itself was not defective. Defence strategy: (1) obtain the adhesive's VOC test certificates and Safety Data Sheet (SDS); (2) prove adequate ventilation was provided during and after installation; (3) demonstrate the store was opened only after curing period; (4) engage an occupational hygienist to measure actual VOC levels if the claim persists. Document your installation protocols. Consider specifying low-VOC (≤10g/L) adhesives for all future projects as an additional safeguard.

**Q:** 33 During a routine fire safety inspection, the local municipality flagged our store's floor tiles as "potentially contributing to fire load" and demanded a fire resistance rating certificate. Our supplier never provided one. Are floor tiles required to have fire ratings under SANS 10400?
**A:** SANS 10400 Part T (Fire Protection) primarily regulates wall and ceiling finishes for flame spread and fire contribution. Floor finishes are generally not regulated for fire resistance under Part T, except in specific occupancies (high-rise buildings, atriums, escape routes) where the Building Regulations may require non-combustible floor finishes. Standard ceramic and porcelain tiles are inherently non-combustible (Class A1 fire rating under EN 13501-1) and pose negligible fire load. However, vinyl, carpet, and some epoxy finishes DO contribute to fire load and may require testing. If your municipality has specifically flagged your tiles, request: (1) the specific regulation being cited; (2) a written notice with legal basis. In many cases, municipal inspectors confuse wall/ceiling requirements with flooring. Provide your tile's classification certificate (ceramic = non-combustible). If the municipality persists, appeal through the building control officer or seek a relaxation from the local authority under Section 15 of the National Building Regulations.

**Q:** 34 Our tiling contractor's employee was severely injured when a stack of tiles toppled in our storeroom. The contractor does not have COIDA (Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act) registration. Are we liable as the client?
**A:** Potentially yes. Under COIDA, employers must register with the Compensation Fund and pay assessments. If the contractor is unregistered, they commit an offence. More seriously for you: if the contractor has no COIDA cover, the injured employee may pursue a common law damages claim against "third parties" — and you, as the occupier/controller of the premises, may be sued under the Occupiers' Liability Act or Aquilian action for failing to provide a safe working environment. Your defence depends on: (1) whether your contract required COIDA registration; (2) whether you verified registration before appointment; (3) whether your premises were inherently unsafe (inadequate stacking space, slippery floors). Best practice: never appoint a contractor without valid COIDA registration certificate and letter of good standing. Include this as a mandatory pre-qualification criterion in all tenders. Your own insurance (public liability) may exclude claims arising from uninsured subcontractors.

## 6. HALAL, KOSHER & RELIGIOUS COMPLIANCE

**Q:** 35 We are rolling out 200 stores, including 15 in predominantly Muslim areas. Our tile adhesive contains casein (a milk-derived protein) as a bonding agent. Could this compromise halal compliance for stores serving food, and do we need SANHA certification for the adhesive?
**A:** This is a genuine concern in South Africa's halal-conscious market. Casein derived from non-halal slaughtered animals would render the adhesive non-halal. While tiles and adhesives are not consumed, halal certification bodies like SANHA (South African National Halaal Authority) and MJC (Muslim Judicial Council) may raise concerns if adhesive residues could contact food preparation surfaces or if vapours during curing affect food safety. SANHA has issued rulings that building materials in direct contact with food areas should preferably be halal-certified where animal derivatives are involved. Practical solution: (1) source casein-free adhesives (many modern polymer adhesives use synthetic alternatives); (2) if casein is unavoidable, obtain a SANHA or MJC ruling on the specific product; (3) ensure the adhesive is fully cured and sealed before food handling begins. For stores with in-house bakeries or butcheries, specify halal-certified adhesives and grouts contractually. SANHA certification for adhesives is available but adds 4-6 weeks to procurement timelines.

**Q:** 36 Our store in Sea Point serves a large Jewish clientele. During Pesach (Passover), the store undergoes deep cleaning with caustic soda-based chemicals to kosher all surfaces. Will this damage our porcelain tile floors or epoxy grout?
**A:** Caustic soda (sodium hydroxide) at concentrations above 5% can damage certain grout types, particularly cement-based and some epoxy formulations, through alkaline attack and surface etching. Porcelain tiles themselves are chemically resistant and generally unaffected. However, the repeated annual exposure to aggressive chemicals during Passover deep-cleaning creates cumulative damage risk. Recommendations: (1) specify chemically resistant epoxy grout (ANSI A118.3 rated) with confirmed alkali resistance; (2) avoid cement-based grout in food service areas of kosher establishments; (3) provide the facility manager with approved cleaning chemical list compatible with the flooring; (4) ensure thorough rinsing after chemical application — residual caustic soda continues reacting. Consider engaging the Cape Town Beth Din or Johannesburg Beth Din for guidance on acceptable cleaning protocols that balance kosher requirements with flooring preservation. Document this in your store maintenance manual.

**Q:** 37 Our tile grout contains Portland cement, which may include animal-derived processing aids (grinding aids). Our Hindu franchisee has raised religious objections. Is there a vegan-certified tile installation system available in South Africa?
**A:** This is an emerging but legitimate concern. Portland cement manufacturing sometimes uses glycerol or amino acid-based grinding aids that may be animal-derived. While the proportion is minuscule, strict religious observance may object. Currently, no major South African tile adhesive or grout manufacturer offers vegan-certified products. However: (1) some European manufacturers (Mapei, Laticrete) have declared their standard cementitious products free from animal derivatives; (2) 100% epoxy and polyurethane grout systems are synthetically derived and inherently vegan; (3) some natural stone and clay tile systems using lime-based mortars (traditional) avoid Portland cement entirely. If this is critical for your franchisee, specify epoxy grout and request written confirmation from the manufacturer that no animal-derived additives are used. Be aware that "vegan certification" for building materials is not yet standardised in South Africa — supplier declarations are the best available evidence.

**Q:** 38 We operate stores in townships where Zion Christian Church (ZCC) members form a significant customer base. During religious pilgrations to Moria, stores are closed. Our maintenance schedule includes tile deep-cleaning during this period. Is there any cultural or religious sensitivity we should consider regarding who performs the work?
**A:** Cultural sensitivity is good business practice, though not legally mandated. The ZCC has specific practices regarding sacred spaces and purity. Key considerations: (1) if your store is near a church or pilgrimage route, loud maintenance activity during pilgrimage periods (Easter) may cause offence; (2) some congregations prefer that certain spaces be entered only by members of the same faith for cleaning — this is more relevant to places of worship than retail stores; (3) the more practical issue is contractor availability — many workers in Limpopo and surrounding areas take leave during Moria pilgrimage. Schedule around these dates (typically Easter weekend). Engage local store management on timing. While this is not a B-BBEE or legal compliance issue, failure to respect religious observances can affect community relations and brand reputation. Document cultural considerations in your national maintenance calendar.

## 7. FRANCHISE VS FRANCHISOR CONFLICTS

**Q:** 39 Our franchise agreement mandates a specific grey tile (Cool Grey 3) for all stores. A franchisee in Sandton insists on Warm Grey 2, claiming the mandated colour "clashes with their local demographic aesthetic." The franchisor has refused. The franchisee has threatened to source independently. Who wins, and what are the legal implications?
**A:** The franchisor wins on the legal point — franchise agreements are binding contracts, and brand uniformity is a core term. Under the Consumer Protection Act (which applies to franchise agreements via Section 7(2)), franchisees have certain protections against unfair terms, but aesthetic specifications that serve legitimate brand consistency purposes are generally enforceable. If the franchisee sources independently, they are in material breach. The franchisor can: (1) issue a formal notice of breach; (2) withhold marketing fund contributions; (3) terminate the franchise agreement; (4) claim damages. However, the CPA requires that termination provisions be fair and reasonable — franchisors cannot terminate arbitrarily. The franchisee could argue that the colour mandate is "unfair" under CPA Section 48, but this is unlikely to succeed if the specification is applied uniformly and serves brand coherence. Best practice: include the exact tile specification (including colour code, supplier, and approved alternatives) as an annexure to the franchise agreement, signed by both parties.

**Q:** 40 Our franchise agreement requires franchisees to use the franchisor's nominated tiling contractor at R450/m². A franchisee has found an independent contractor at R320/m² and wants to use them. Does the CPA's right to choose suppliers override the franchise agreement?
**A:** No. The CPA Section 7(1)(d) right to choose suppliers applies to consumer transactions, not franchise-to-franchisee supply arrangements. The franchise relationship is governed by the franchise agreement and CPA Section 7(2), which imposes specific disclosure and fairness obligations but does not grant franchisees unlimited supplier choice. If the franchisor's nominated contractor is a related party or charges above market rates, the franchisee may challenge this under CPA Section 48 (unfair, unreasonable or unjust contract terms) or as an uncompetitive practice. However, if the rate is commercially reasonable and the specification serves quality control, the franchisor can enforce the nominated supplier requirement. The Franchise Association of South Africa (FASA) Code of Ethics recommends transparency in supplier arrangements — disclose any rebates or commissions received from nominated suppliers. If the franchisor is profiting from the nomination without disclosure, this may be challengeable.

**Q:** 41 A franchisee's store flooded in the KZN 2022-style disaster. Their insurance covers the store contents but not the tile flooring, which was specified by the franchisor. The franchisee claims the franchisor should pay for replacement since the franchisor mandated the specification. Is this valid?
**A:** Generally no, unless the franchise agreement specifically allocates responsibility for flooring. Typically: (1) the franchisee holds the lease and is responsible for tenant improvements, including flooring; (2) the franchisor's specification is a design standard, not a financial obligation; (3) insurance for tenant improvements is the franchisee's or landlord's responsibility. However, examine the franchise agreement: does it include a "make good" or "reinstatement" clause? Does the franchisor's marketing fund or capital replacement reserve contribute to store maintenance? If the franchisor required a specific high-cost specification that the franchisee would not otherwise have chosen, there may be an equitable argument for contribution, but this is not a strong legal claim. The franchisee should maintain adequate all-risks insurance covering tenant improvements. For future agreements, clarify insurance responsibilities in writing.

**Q:** 42 Our franchisor has mandated a switch from vinyl to porcelain tiles for all new stores. Existing franchisees with vinyl must replace at their next refurbishment (within 3 years). A franchisee argues this is a unilateral contract variation and refuses. Can the franchisor enforce this?
**A:** This depends on the franchise agreement terms. Most well-drafted franchise agreements reserve the right for the franchisor to update design standards and require compliance at refurbishment or renewal. If the agreement explicitly states that the franchisor may update specifications and franchisees must comply within a reasonable timeframe, the mandate is enforceable. However, if the agreement is silent on mid-term specification changes, the franchisee has a stronger argument — the franchisor cannot unilaterally vary contractual terms. The CPA's fairness doctrine (Section 48) requires that any term enabling unilateral variation must be fair and reasonable. If the change is for legitimate brand reasons (durability, sustainability, market positioning) and allows reasonable implementation time, it will likely be upheld. If the change appears arbitrary or financially motivated (e.g., the franchisor receives a kickback from the tile supplier), it may be challengeable.

## 8. PROVINCIAL VARIATIONS, LOGISTICS & BATCH CONSISTENCY

**Q:** 43 Our national rollout delivers tiles from a single Gauteng warehouse to stores at sea level (Durban), 1,700m altitude (Johannesburg), and arid conditions (Upington). Should we be concerned about batch variation caused by altitude and humidity during transport and acclimatisation?
**A:** Yes, this is a genuine technical concern. Ceramic and porcelain tiles experience dimensional changes due to thermal expansion and moisture absorption during transport. Key risks: (1) ALTITUDE — pressure changes during transport through Van Reenen's Pass (1,680m) can theoretically affect packaging integrity, though this is minor for tiles; (2) HUMIDITY — tiles transported from dry Highveld conditions to humid Durban (80%+ relative humidity) may absorb moisture, slightly expanding before installation. For porcelain (water absorption ≤0.5%), this is negligible. For ceramic tiles (water absorption 3-6%), it can cause slight dimensional change; (3) THERMAL SHOCK — extreme temperature differentials between Upington (45°C in summer) and Drakensberg (sub-zero in winter) can stress tiles if not properly acclimatised. Specification: require 48-hour acclimatisation on-site before installation for all ceramic products; allow 3mm expansion joints per 6m run in temperature-variable climates; transport tiles in sealed, weather-protected vehicles. Document delivery conditions in your logistics specification.

**Q:** 44 Our Western Cape stores require delivery through Sir Lowry's Pass and Houw Hoek Pass, where trucking accidents frequently close the road. Our tile supplier has rejected liability for delays caused by road closures. Is this enforceable?
**A:** The N1 and N2 routes to the Western Cape are known high-risk corridors. Under your contract terms, risk allocation for transport delays depends on the agreed Incoterms or delivery terms. If delivery is DDP (supplier delivers to store), the supplier bears transport risk including route delays — though they may claim the delay was beyond their control (force majeure if the closure is due to accident or weather). If delivery is ex-works or FCA (Free Carrier), you bear the risk once goods are collected. For a national rollout, specify: (1) delivery must account for alternative routing during pass closures; (2) 3-day buffer stock must be held at the Cape Town distribution hub; (3) supplier must provide real-time tracking and proactive delay notification. The Consumer Protection Act does not override agreed B2B delivery terms, but if delays are habitual and the supplier knew the route risks, they may be liable for breach of reasonable performance obligations under common law.

**Q:** 45 Our KZN stores received tiles from the same batch as Gauteng stores, but the colour appears visibly different under KZN's natural daylight compared to Highveld light. The supplier claims this is an optical illusion caused by different light spectra. Is this scientifically valid, and what is our recourse?
**A:** This is scientifically valid. Colour appearance is affected by the spectral power distribution of the light source. Durban's coastal light has higher blue content and diffused quality due to maritime atmosphere, while Highveld light is harsher with more UV. This is known as metamerism — tiles matched under one light source appear different under another. More critically, if tiles are from the SAME production batch, they should be identical under controlled conditions. The issue is likely that: (1) tiles from different production batches were mixed; (2) the supplier's quality control batch sampling was inadequate; (3) tiles were stored in different conditions causing surface oxidation or contamination. Your recourse: require the supplier to provide batch certificates with Hunter Lab colour values (ΔE) confirming colour consistency within acceptable tolerance (typically ΔE ≤1.0 for premium tiles). If ΔE exceeds specification, reject the batch. For future rollouts, specify that colour matching must be verified under both D65 (daylight) and TL84 (retail) light sources.

**Q:** 46 We have stores in every province. Each province has different waste disposal regulations for construction debris. Must our national tiling contract include province-specific waste management plans, or can we apply a single national standard?
**A:** South Africa's waste management is governed by the National Environmental Management: Waste Act 59 of 2008, but provincial and municipal bylaws vary significantly. Gauteng has strict waste separation requirements; Western Cape has landfill restrictions on construction rubble; KZN has specific requirements for coastal zone disposal. A single national standard is insufficient — you need province-specific waste management plans that comply with local bylaws. Best approach: (1) appoint a waste management contractor with national coverage and local permits in each province; (2) specify in your tiling contract that the contractor is responsible for lawful disposal and must provide waste manifests for every site; (3) require recycling of tile off-cuts where possible — some suppliers accept factory returns; (4) for hazardous waste (epoxy grout residue, solvent-based cleaners), specify licensed hazardous waste disposal. Non-compliance with waste regulations can result in fines under Section 67 of the Waste Act and criminal liability for directors under the National Environmental Management Act.

**Q:** 47 Our Northern Cape store is 600km from the nearest tile supplier's branch. The contractor wants to charge a 35% remoteness surcharge on labour and transport. Is this legally justified, or is it price gouging?
**A:** There is no statutory regulation of construction pricing in South Africa — pricing is market-driven. A 35% surcharge for remote locations may be commercially justified given: fuel costs, accommodation for workers, travel time, difficulty sourcing local skilled labour, and higher cost of material delivery. However, under the Consumer Protection Act (if the contract is with a consumer) or common law (if B2B), pricing must not be unconscionable, unfair, or misleading. For a B2B contract, the key question is whether the surcharge was disclosed upfront. If the contractor quoted a standard rate and added the surcharge after work commenced, this may constitute breach of contract or unfair practice. Best practice: include a transparent remoteness matrix in your national contract specifying surcharges by distance band (e.g., 0-100km: standard; 100-300km: 15%; 300-500km: 25%; 500km+: 35%). This allows budget planning and prevents disputes. Consider establishing regional contractor panels rather than relying on Gauteng-based contractors for remote sites.

## 9. LOAD SHEDDING & INFRASTRUCTURE FAILURE

**Q:** 48 Our store's generator runs continuously during load shedding, creating micro-vibrations that our facilities manager claims are causing grout cracking in the tiled floor. Is there scientific evidence for this, and what is the remedy?
**A:** There is limited scientific evidence that typical standby generator vibrations (typically 2-5mm/sec amplitude) cause grout failure in properly installed tile floors. However, continuous generator operation (8-12 hours daily during persistent load shedding) can create cumulative fatigue in rigid cement-based grout, particularly if: (1) the subfloor has resonance at the generator's operating frequency; (2) grout was installed with high water content or inadequate curing; (3) there are existing stress points (control joints not aligned with structural movement). The more likely cause is thermal cycling — generators heat the space, and rapid temperature changes cause differential expansion between tiles and substrate. Remedy: (1) isolate the generator on anti-vibration mounts (SABS ISO 10816 compliant); (2) specify flexible (polyurethane) grout in generator-adjacent areas; (3) ensure expansion joints are provided at all perimeters and changes in plane; (4) monitor grout condition and establish a maintenance cycle. Document the issue — if generator-induced damage becomes widespread, you may have an insurance claim under your building defects policy.

**Q:** 49 Load shedding caused our tiling contractor's battery-powered tile cutter to fail mid-cut, ruining a R12,000 large-format tile. The contractor wants us to pay for the replacement, claiming the power failure was beyond their control. Who pays?
**A:** This is a contractual risk allocation question. Under standard JBCC or NEC contract terms, the contractor bears the risk of their equipment and methodology. Load shedding has been a foreseeable risk in South Africa since 2007, and professional contractors are expected to plan accordingly — backup batteries, generators, manual cutting for small quantities, or scheduling cuts during stable power periods. Unless your contract specifically allocates load shedding risk to the employer (you), the contractor bears the cost of their own equipment failure and resultant waste. However, if you required the specific large-format tile knowing it required specialised cutting equipment vulnerable to power failure, there may be an equitable argument for sharing the cost. For future contracts: specify that the contractor must have adequate backup power for all critical operations; include load shedding in the contract's risk register; cap the employer's liability for power-related waste at a specific amount.

**Q:** 50 Our mall store experiences 4-6 hours of load shedding daily. The emergency lighting is inadequate for tiling maintenance work, and our contractor refuses to work in these conditions citing OHSA safety requirements. Can we enforce the work schedule, or must we provide alternative lighting?
**A:** Under the Occupational Health and Safety Act 85 of 1993, employers (including contractors) must provide a safe working environment. Regulation 9 of the General Safety Regulations requires adequate lighting for all work areas — typically minimum 200 lux for general construction work, 500+ lux for detailed finishing work. The contractor is legally entitled to refuse to work in unsafe lighting conditions, and you cannot contractually override OHSA requirements. Your options: (1) provide battery-powered LED work lighting meeting the required lux levels; (2) schedule tiling work during non-load-shedding hours (check the EskomSePush schedule); (3) provide generator-powered lighting; (4) accept the delay. If you fail to provide adequate lighting and the contractor is injured, you face liability under OHSA Section 37 (common law employer liability) and potentially criminal charges under Section 38. Budget for portable lighting in all maintenance contracts — it is cheaper than a workplace injury claim.

**Q:** 51 The UPS in our data centre failed during load shedding, and the resulting power surge damaged the anti-static flooring's conductive layer. Our flooring contractor claims this is an electrical issue, not a flooring defect. Our electrician blames the flooring. Who is responsible?
**A:** This requires expert forensic investigation. Anti-static flooring (typically conductive vinyl or epoxy with embedded carbon fibres) should withstand normal power fluctuations if properly installed with equipotential earthing. The responsibility depends on root cause: (1) if the UPS failure caused an abnormal voltage surge (beyond standard grid fluctuations), the UPS manufacturer or maintainer may be liable; (2) if the flooring's conductive layer was improperly earthed or had inadequate resistance, the flooring contractor is liable; (3) if the flooring was rated for standard ESD protection but the UPS failure created an extraordinary electrical event, it may be force majeure or an insurance claim. Engage an electrical engineer and flooring specialist jointly to: measure floor resistance (EN 1081); inspect earthing connections; analyse the UPS failure mode. Document findings and pursue the responsible party. Ensure all data centre flooring specifications include surge protection requirements and earthing compliance certificates.

## 10. FOOD SAFETY & HACCP

**Q:** 52 Our in-store bakery uses tiles for the food preparation area floor. The Department of Health inspector has cited us for using "inappropriate flooring that cannot be effectively cleaned." The tiles are commercial-grade porcelain with 3mm grout joints. What does HACCP actually require for food area flooring?
**A:** HACCP (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points) and Regulation R638 of the Foodstuffs, Cosmetics and Disinfectants Act require that food handling area floors be: smooth, impervious, non-toxic, washable, and unable to provide harbourage for pests or bacteria. Your 3mm grout joints ARE a potential issue — they can trap food particles, moisture, and bacteria, making effective sanitation difficult. HACCP best practice for food areas: (1) use tiles with minimal joints (large format, rectified edges with 1-2mm joints); (2) use epoxy grout (not cement-based) — it is non-porous, chemical-resistant, and does not harbour bacteria; (3) ensure the floor slopes to drainage (1:50 to 1:100 gradient); (4) use coved skirting (minimum 100mm high) to eliminate the floor-wall junction where bacteria accumulate; (5) specify anti-slip rating appropriate for wet cleaning (PTV ≥ 45 wet). The DoH inspector is likely correct — standard commercial porcelain with cement grout in a bakery is a HACCP violation. You need to re-grout with epoxy or replace the flooring.

**Q:** 53 Our supermarket's meat department uses quarry tiles (unglazed) for grip. The tiles have developed a persistent organic odour that cleaning cannot remove. The health inspector has flagged this as a potential bacterial harbourage. Is this a known issue with quarry tiles in food areas?
**A:** Yes, this is a well-documented issue. Unglazed quarry tiles are porous (water absorption 3-10%) and the pores can trap organic matter, blood, fats, and cleaning chemicals. Over time, this creates bacterial biofilm and persistent odours that surface cleaning cannot eliminate. In HACCP terms, this is a Critical Control Point failure — the floor cannot be effectively sanitised. Solutions: (1) deep clean with enzymatic cleaners followed by hot water extraction; (2) apply a penetrating sealer specifically rated for food contact areas (NSF/ANSI 51 or EU 10/2011 compliant); (3) if the problem persists, replace with sealed, vitrified tiles or stainless steel flooring in the highest-risk areas; (4) establish a weekly deep-cleaning protocol. For new stores, specify vitrified, sealed tiles with R11 or R12 slip rating rather than unglazed quarry tiles. The R638 regulations explicitly require that food contact surfaces do not impart odour, taste, or contamination.

**Q:** 54 Our tile supplier has provided a "food-safe" certificate for their grout, but it is based on EU Regulation EC 1935/2004. Does this satisfy South African food safety requirements, or do we need local certification?
**A:** South Africa's food contact materials regulations are less comprehensive than the EU's. Regulation R638 under the Foodstuffs Act addresses general hygiene but does not have specific migration limits for construction materials. The SABS has adopted some ISO standards for food contact materials, but grout is not specifically regulated. In practice: (1) EU certification is widely accepted as demonstrating compliance with international best practice; (2) SANS 10225 (food contact materials) provides general principles; (3) the Department of Health inspector will look for evidence that the material is non-toxic, does not migrate contaminants, and is suitable for the intended use. An EU food-safe certificate is strong evidence but not automatically binding. To be safe: obtain a letter from the grout manufacturer confirming compliance with SANS/ISO requirements; ensure the certificate covers the specific product (not just the manufacturer's general range); check that the certificate is current and covers the correct temperature/contact conditions. For maximum protection, specify NSF/ANSI 51 certified grout where available.

**Q:** 55 We are installing tiles in a brewery's fermentation hall. The tile supplier claims their product is "chemical resistant" but has no specific data for phosphoric acid (used in CIP cleaning at 2% concentration). Is this a specification failure, and what standards should we reference?
**A:** This is a significant specification gap. "Chemical resistant" is a vague claim — resistance depends on the specific chemical, concentration, temperature, and exposure duration. For brewery applications, you need tiles and grout tested against the actual cleaning chemicals used. Relevant standards: (1) SANS 10107 for general chemical resistance classification; (2) ISO 10545-13 (ceramic tiles — chemical resistance) which tests against standard reagents including dilute acids; (3) manufacturer-specific testing against phosphoric acid at your concentration (2%) and temperature (CIP solutions may be heated to 60-70°C). Request a Certificate of Chemical Resistance from the supplier specifically listing phosphoric acid at your operating parameters. If they cannot provide this, commission independent testing or specify an alternative product with proven brewery performance. Cement-based grout will FAIL in acidic brewery environments — you need epoxy or furan resin grout. Failure to specify correctly will result in grout erosion within 6-12 months.

**Q:** 56 Our fast-food restaurant uses floor drains with tiled surrounds. The grout around the drains has eroded, creating gaps where food debris accumulates. A customer slipped on grease that seeped through the gap. Our insurer has denied the claim citing "failure to maintain flooring." Is this valid, and how should drain surrounds be specified?
**A:** The insurer's denial is likely valid if your maintenance records show you knew or should have known about the grout erosion. Floor drains are high-stress areas subject to: thermal shock (hot water), chemical exposure (cleaners, grease), mechanical stress (foot traffic, drain cleaning equipment), and constant moisture. Standard cement grout WILL fail in this application. Correct specification: (1) use epoxy grout with chemical and thermal resistance certification; (2) install a stainless steel drain frame with integrated waterproofing flange that extends under the tiles; (3) use a clamping drain system that secures tiles at the perimeter; (4) slope tiles toward the drain at 1:50 minimum; (5) specify a sealed expansion joint between tile field and drain frame using a traffic-rated polyurethane joint sealant. Regular inspection should be part of your HACCP maintenance schedule — monthly for high-volume restaurants. The R638 regulation requires floors to be maintained in good repair at all times.

**Q:** 57 Our food production facility requires flooring that can withstand daily thermal cycling from steam cleaning (80°C) followed by cold water rinse (10°C). Our tile supplier's warranty excludes "thermal shock damage." Is this exclusion enforceable, and what flooring should we specify?
**A:** Thermal shock exclusions are generally enforceable if they are clearly stated in the warranty terms and the product was not specified for the actual service conditions. However, under the Consumer Protection Act Section 55, goods must be reasonably suitable for the purposes for which they are generally intended. If you informed the supplier of the steam cleaning requirement and they recommended a product that failed, they may be liable despite the exclusion — the CPA limits the enforceability of terms that attempt to exclude liability for defective or unsuitable goods. For steam cleaning environments: (1) specify fully vitrified porcelain tiles (water absorption ≤0.5%) — these handle thermal shock best; (2) use flexible adhesive (S1 or S2 classified per EN 12004) that accommodates thermal movement; (3) use epoxy grout — cement grout will crack under thermal cycling; (4) ensure the subfloor has adequate thermal insulation to prevent differential expansion; (5) consider alternative systems like polyurethane screeds (Sika, Mapei) specifically designed for thermal shock environments. Standard ceramic tiles and cement-based systems WILL fail under daily steam cleaning — this is predictable, not an unexpected failure mode.

## 11. PHARMACEUTICAL, DATA CENTRE & SPECIALISED ENVIRONMENTS

**Q:** 58 We are fitting out a pharmaceutical clean room to ISO 14644-1 Class 7. The tile supplier has offered "low-particle" porcelain tiles. What specific testing and certification do we need to verify particle emissions from the flooring?
**A:** ISO 14644-1 addresses airborne particle concentration, not material emissions directly. For pharmaceutical clean rooms, you need to verify: (1) PARTICLE EMISSION — test to ISO 14644-14 (assessment of suitability for use) or IEST-RP-CC005 (material outgassing); (2) OUTGASSING — test to ISO 16000 series for VOC emissions, or specifically ASTM D5116 for small chamber testing; (3) CLEANABILITY — tiles must withstand aggressive cleanroom disinfectants (sporicides, quaternary ammonium compounds, hydrogen peroxide) without degradation; (4) SEAMLESS REQUIREMENT — cleanrooms typically require resinous flooring (epoxy or polyurethane screed) rather than tiles, because grout joints are particle traps and bacterial harbourage points. If tiles are absolutely required, specify: rectified porcelain with minimum 1mm joints; epoxy grout; coved skirting; full compatibility with cleanroom disinfectants. Most pharmaceutical GMP auditors will reject tiled floors in Grade B/C areas — seamless resin is preferred. Check SANS 14644 (South Africa's adoption of ISO 14644) and PIC/S PE009 GMP Guide.

**Q:** 59 Our data centre requires anti-static flooring with a specific resistance range of 10^6 to 10^9 ohms. The tile supplier provided a certificate showing 10^5 ohms. Is this acceptable, or does it fall outside the required range?
**A:** 10^5 ohms (100,000 ohms) is below the specified minimum of 10^6 ohms (1,000,000 ohms). This classifies the flooring as "conductive" rather than "static dissipative." The distinction is critical: conductive flooring (10^4 to 10^6 ohms) is designed for explosive environments where rapid charge dissipation prevents sparks; static dissipative flooring (10^6 to 10^9 ohms) is designed for electronics handling where slower, controlled discharge protects sensitive components. Using conductive flooring in a data centre or electronics area can actually increase ESD risk — if a charged person walks on conductive flooring, the rapid discharge can damage components. You need flooring in the 10^6 to 10^9 ohm range. Reject the batch. Verify testing was conducted per ANSI/ESD STM7.1 (resistance to ground) or EN 1081. Test under the actual environmental conditions (humidity affects resistance readings significantly). Ensure the supplier provides point-to-point AND point-to-ground resistance values.

**Q:** 60 We need flooring for a laboratory that uses hydrofluoric acid (HF) for glass etching. Our tile supplier claims their porcelain tiles are "acid resistant." Hydrofluoric acid is one of the few chemicals that attacks glass and silica-based materials. Are porcelain tiles safe?
**A:** No. Porcelain tiles are silica-based (containing quartz and silica). Hydrofluoric acid attacks silica (SiO2 + 4HF → SiF4 + 2H2O), meaning it will etch and eventually destroy porcelain tiles, ceramic tiles, and most cement-based grouts. Even dilute HF (2-5%) will cause surface damage over time. For HF environments, you need specialised flooring: (1) carbon-filled vinyl (conductive/vinyl ester) specifically rated for HF exposure; (2) epoxy novolac systems with HF resistance certification; (3) rubber flooring (some formulations resist HF); (4) sealed concrete with specialised HF-resistant coating. NEVER specify silica-based materials in HF laboratories. The supplier's "acid resistant" claim likely refers to standard acids (sulphuric, hydrochloric, nitric) tested under ISO 10545-13. HF is a special case. Consult the chemical resistance chart from a specialist flooring manufacturer (Flowcrete, Sika) for HF-specific recommendations. OHSA Hazardous Chemical Substances Regulations (HCS) require safe containment of all chemicals including flooring compatibility.

**Q:** 61 Our correctional facility contract requires "anti-ligature" flooring — meaning no points where a detainee could attach a ligature. How does this specification affect tile selection and installation details?
**A:** Anti-ligature (or "ligature-resistant") design is standard in correctional and mental health facilities. For tiled floors, the concern is not the tile surface itself but the installation details: (1) NO EXPOSED EDGES — tiles must extend to walls without gaps; coved skirting (minimum 150mm) must be used with no gaps at the junction; (2) NO LOOSE COMPONENTS — no loose threshold strips, no cover plates that can be pried off; (3) NO GROUT JOINTS THAT CAN BE DUG OUT — use epoxy grout (harder to dig out than cement grout) or specify seamless resin flooring; (4) DRAIN COVERS must be tamper-proof, secured with special tools; (5) FLOOR BOXES and access points must be flush, tamper-resistant, and secured with security screws. The South African Department of Correctional Services has standard specifications (DCS Building Norms) that include flooring requirements. For high-risk cells, many facilities specify poured-in-place resin flooring with coved integral skirting specifically because it eliminates joints and edges entirely. Standard tile installations are generally acceptable only in low-risk administrative areas.

**Q:** 62 Our automotive workshop needs flooring that can withstand brake fluid (DOT 3/4), petrol, and impact from dropped tools. Our tile contractor has suggested heavy-duty porcelain. Is this adequate, or do we need specialised workshop flooring?
**A:** Standard porcelain tiles are inadequate for automotive workshops. Brake fluid (glycol-based) and petrol are aggressive chemicals that will stain and potentially degrade standard tiles and grout over time. Dropped tools (spanners, brake calipers) can chip porcelain. Better specification: (1) HEAVY-DUTY EPOXY SCREED (6-9mm) — specifically formulated for automotive workshops, with chemical resistance to brake fluid, engine oil, petrol, and hydraulic fluid. Products like Sikafloor or Flowcrete's Deckshield range are designed for this; (2) if tiles are preferred, specify chemically resistant quarry tiles (8-20mm thick) with chemical-resistant furan grout — these are traditional for industrial kitchens and workshops; (3) consider interlocking PVC workshop tiles (e.g., Bergo or similar) that allow modular replacement of damaged sections. Ensure the specification includes: chemical resistance testing to ASTM C267; compressive strength minimum 60MPa; impact resistance rating; and slip rating R10-R11 dry (R12 if wet cleaning is frequent). Standard porcelain fails on impact resistance and chemical exposure in workshop environments.

**Q:** 63 We are building a cold store (-25°C) with a loading dock that cycles between -25°C and +35°C ambient. Our tile supplier has never done cold store work. What are the critical specification factors for this thermal shock environment?
**A:** Cold store flooring is one of the most demanding applications. Critical factors: (1) TILE SELECTION — use fully vitrified, unglazed porcelain with water absorption ≤0.1% (the lower, the better). Glazed tiles can craze (fine cracking) under thermal cycling; (2) FLEXIBLE ADHESIVE — standard cement adhesive will crack. Use S2-classified deformable adhesive (EN 12004) or epoxy adhesive specifically rated for sub-zero temperatures; (3) EXPANSION JOINTS — provide movement joints every 3-4 metres in both directions, using silicone or polyurethane joint sealant rated for -40°C to +80°C; (4) SUBFLOOR HEATING — consider underfloor heating at the dock threshold to prevent ice formation and reduce thermal gradient; (5) VAPOUR BARRIER — critical to prevent subfloor moisture freezing and causing heave; (6) INSULATION — the subfloor must be thermally insulated (typically XPS insulation board) to prevent cold bridging. Specify: ISO 13006 Group AI tiles (water absorption ≤0.5%); adhesive tested to -30°C; grout with thermal shock resistance certification. Normal commercial-grade tiles and adhesives WILL fail within the first winter.

## 12. CHEMICAL RESISTANCE & INDUSTRIAL APPLICATIONS

**Q:** 64 Our chemical plant needs flooring resistant to 98% sulphuric acid spills. Our tile supplier has tested to ISO 10545-13 but only at 3% concentration. Is this testing adequate for our application?
**A:** Absolutely not. ISO 10545-13 tests ceramic tiles against standard reagents at concentrations typically found in domestic/commercial applications (3% hydrochloric acid, citric acid, etc.). Industrial 98% sulphuric acid is a completely different hazard — it is highly concentrated, exothermic on contact with water, and will attack most standard materials including many "acid-resistant" tiles and grouts. For 98% H2SO4: (1) specify carbon brick or acid-resistant ceramic tile specifically manufactured for chemical plant applications (typically 25-50mm thick, vitrified at very high temperatures); (2) use acid-resistant cement (sodium silicate-based or furan resin-based) — standard cement grout will dissolve; (3) ensure the substrate is acid-resistant concrete or protected with acid-resistant membrane; (4) specify a containment bund with acid-resistant coating. Standard porcelain tiles, even those passing ISO 10545-13, will be destroyed by concentrated industrial acids. Engage a corrosion engineer and specify to ASTM C279 (chemical-resistant masonry) or equivalent. The OHS Act requires safe containment of hazardous substances — including flooring that withstands spills.

**Q:** 65 Our brewery's malt handling area has a fine dust atmosphere (potentially explosive — ATEX Zone 22). We want to install conductive tile flooring to prevent electrostatic discharge. What standards apply in South Africa for ATEX-equivalent zones?
**A:** South Africa does not use the EU ATEX directive terminology but has equivalent requirements under SANS 10108 (classification of hazardous locations) and the OHS Act. SANS 10108 adopts IEC 60079 series standards for explosive atmospheres. For Zone 22 (zone where an explosive dust atmosphere is not likely to occur in normal operation, but if it does, will persist for a short period): (1) flooring must prevent electrostatic discharge that could ignite malt dust (minimum ignition energy of organic dusts is typically 10-50mJ); (2) specify conductive or static-dissipative flooring with surface resistance 10^4 to 10^8 ohms; (3) ensure full equipotential earthing (bonding) of all flooring sections to earth; (4) use spark-free installation materials and tools; (5) the flooring system must be certified for use in SANS 10108 Class III (dust) locations. ATEX-certified products from Europe are generally accepted in South Africa if they meet the technical requirements of SANS 10108. Installers must be trained in hazardous area installation. COMAH (Control of Major Accident Hazards) regulations also apply to large brewing operations.

**Q:** 66 Our textile factory uses sodium hypochlorite (bleach) at 10% concentration for fabric treatment. The bleach spills have destroyed our cement grout within 6 months. What grout system should we specify, and is this a common industrial problem?
**A:** This is extremely common. Sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl) is highly alkaline and oxidising. At 10% concentration, it attacks cement-based materials through: (1) alkali-silica reaction causing expansion and cracking; (2) dissolution of calcium hydroxide in cement; (3) degradation of grout structure. Standard cement grout has NO resistance to bleach at this concentration. Specify: (1) FURAN RESIN GROUT — specifically formulated for chemical resistance in industrial applications, with proven resistance to acids, alkalis, and oxidising agents including bleach; (2) EPOXY GROUT (industrial grade) — check specific chemical resistance data for sodium hypochlorite at 10%; some standard epoxy grouts are NOT resistant; (3) QUARTZ-FILLED EPOXY SCREED — seamless alternative that eliminates grout joints entirely. For textile factories specifically, also consider: resistance to fabric dyes (which can permanently stain porous materials); hot water resistance (if steam is used); and cleanability for lint/dust accumulation. Standard commercial grout specifications are completely inadequate for this environment.

**Q:** 67 Our petrol station forecourt store needs flooring resistant to petrol, diesel, and oil exposure. We initially specified standard commercial vinyl composite tile (VCT). The petroleum wholesaler has rejected this. What should we specify instead?
**A:** Standard VCT is NOT resistant to petroleum products — petrol and diesel will soften, swell, and dissolve the vinyl binder, causing permanent damage and creating a slip hazard. For petroleum forecourt retail environments, specify: (1) PETROLEUM-RESISTANT EPOXY SCREED (3-6mm) — self-levelling or trowelled, specifically formulated for forecourt applications with certified resistance to petrol, diesel, engine oil, and hydraulic fluid. Products like Flowcrete Deckshield ID or similar; (2) CERAMIC QUARRY TILE with chemical-resistant furan grout — traditional solution for petrol stations; (3) RUBBER FLOORING with petroleum resistance certification — some heavy-duty rubber formulations resist hydrocarbons. Critical requirements: slip resistance R11 or R12 (petrol spills create extreme slip hazards); resistance to brake fluid (glycol ethers attack some epoxies); UV stability for areas with daylight exposure; and compliance with petroleum industry safety standards (SANS 10089 for petroleum facilities). The petroleum company (Engen, Shell, BP, Total) will have their own flooring specification — obtain it before tendering.

## 13. ESD FLOORING & ELECTRICAL REQUIREMENTS

**Q:** 68 Our electronics assembly plant requires ESD flooring compliant with IEC 61340-5-1. The tile supplier provided a product certified to ANSI/ESD S20.20. Are these standards equivalent, and will our IEC-compliant auditor accept ANSI certification?
**A:** ANSI/ESD S20.20 and IEC 61340-5-1 are technically equivalent and were deliberately harmonised by the ESD Association and IEC. Both specify: (1) resistance-to-ground (Rg) between 10^6 and 10^9 ohms for static dissipative flooring; (2) resistance-to-groundable point (Rtg) within the same range; (3) system resistance including the person ( footwear + floor) must be <10^9 ohms; (4) body voltage generation <100V (ANSI) or equivalent (IEC uses different test method). A product certified to ANSI/ESD S20.20 should be acceptable for an IEC 61340-5-1 programme, but some conservative auditors may insist on IEC-specific test reports. Best practice: request test reports against BOTH standards from the supplier; ensure the test laboratory is ISO 17025 accredited; verify that the specific product batch was tested, not just the product line generically. For South Africa, SANS/IEC 61340 is the adopted standard. Note that ESD flooring must be tested AFTER installation — factory certification alone is insufficient. Commission a post-installation resistance survey.

**Q:** 69 Our server room has raised access flooring with static-dissipative tiles. During a routine audit, we discovered that the pedestal grid is not properly earthed — the floor tiles show correct point-to-point resistance but no connection to building earth. Is this a compliance issue?
**A:** This is a CRITICAL compliance failure. ESD flooring works by providing a controlled path to ground — if there is no earth connection, the floor may actually increase static risk by accumulating charge with nowhere to dissipate it. Point-to-point resistance between tiles may read correctly because the conductive layer connects tile-to-tile, but without earth bonding, there is no charge dissipation path. IEC 61340-5-1 requires: (1) a grounding conductor (copper strap) connected to the pedestal grid at multiple points; (2) connection to building earth (protective earth busbar); (3) earth resistance <10 ohms per SANS 10142-1; (4) visual inspection of all earth connections at least annually; (5) resistance testing with a calibrated megohmmeter. The ungrounded floor is non-compliant and poses a risk to electronic equipment. Immediately: earth the pedestal grid; test ground resistance; document the remediation. If equipment has been damaged due to ESD events caused by this failure, your insurance may not cover it if the condition was known or should have been known.

**Q:** 70 We need to replace ESD tiles in a live data centre. The facilities manager is concerned that tile removal will generate dust that affects server equipment. What is the standard procedure for replacing flooring in an operational data centre?
**A:** Replacing flooring in a live data centre requires strict contamination control procedures: (1) CONTAINMENT — erect temporary plastic sheeting barriers (minimum 3m high) to create a containment zone; maintain negative air pressure within the containment relative to the data hall using HEPA-filtered air scrubbers; (2) POWER ISOLATION — de-energise the specific rack row being worked on; do not work under live overhead busbars; (3) NON-POWERED TOOLS — use manual tools only; no angle grinders, no hammer drills (vibration and dust); (4) HEPA VACUUM — continuous vacuum capture of all dust during tile removal; (5) WORK SCHEDULE — work during planned maintenance windows when servers can be shut down or load-balanced to alternate rows; (6) POST-WORK CLEANING — full HEPA vacuuming, wet wiping with anti-static cleaner, and particle count verification (ISO 14644-1 Class 8 or better) before reopening the area; (7) ESD PROTECTION — all workers in full ESD protection (wrist straps, smocks, conductive footwear) throughout. Many data centres prohibit any construction work in live halls — consider using a temporary modular containment pod or scheduling work during full shutdown periods.

## 14. UV DEGRADATION, GRAFFITI & URBAN ENVIRONMENT

**Q:** 71 Our Johannesburg CBD store's front-of-house tiles (specified as "fade-resistant") have shown significant colour shift after 3 years of sun exposure through floor-to-ceiling glass. The supplier claims UV degradation is "normal wear and tear." What is the expected UV stability for indoor tiles, and does the CPA protect us?
**A:** The CPA Section 55 requires goods to be of good quality, durable, and free of defects. "Fade-resistant" is a performance claim — if the tiles were sold with this description, they must meet reasonable expectations for the application. For indoor tiles with significant sun exposure (Johannesburg has high UV index, especially at 1,700m altitude), colour stability depends on: (1) the pigment system (inorganic pigments resist UV better than organic); (2) the glaze composition (zircon-based glazes have excellent UV stability); (3) the tile body (porcelain is more stable than ceramic). Test the tiles for colour change using a spectrophotometer — ΔE >3.0 is generally considered a perceptible colour shift. If the shift exceeds acceptable tolerance and the supplier represented the tiles as fade-resistant, you have a CPA claim. Document: the supplier's marketing materials; the installation environment (UV exposure hours); the measured ΔE values. Note that no tile is truly UV-proof — but 3 years to noticeable fading for quality tiles is premature. SANS 1449 does not address UV stability directly — you need supplier-specific warranty terms.

**Q:** 72 Our township store has persistent graffiti on the external tiled columns. Standard cleaning chemicals damage the tile glaze. Is there an anti-graffiti coating system compatible with glazed ceramic tiles, and what is its lifespan?
**A:** Yes, anti-graffiti coatings are available for glazed tiles, but compatibility must be verified. Two types: (1) SACRIFICIAL COATINGS — wax or polymer-based, applied over the tile; graffiti removes the coating, which is then reapplied. Lifespan: 1-3 graffiti removals; (2) PERMANENT COATINGS — fluoropolymer or silane-based, chemically bond to the surface; graffiti can be removed with specialised solvent without damaging the coating. Lifespan: 5-10 years. For glazed tiles: ensure the coating does not etch or chemically attack the glaze (test on a sample tile first); verify the coating does not alter the tile's slip resistance (critical for public spaces); confirm compatibility with the tile manufacturer's warranty — some coatings void warranties. Consider also: textured or rough-surface tiles are harder to clean of graffiti than smooth tiles; dark-coloured tiles show graffiti less; some municipalities offer free graffiti removal services. Factor coating reapplication into your maintenance budget. The coating must not contain chemicals that create slip hazards under SANS 10107.

**Q:** 73 Our Durban beachfront store's tiles have developed salt efflorescence (white powdery deposits) within 12 months of installation. The contractor blames the coastal environment; the architect blames the contractor's waterproofing. Who is responsible, and how do we prevent this?
**A:** Salt efflorescence in coastal environments is a known phenomenon — salt-laden air deposits chlorides on surfaces, which crystallise as moisture evaporates. However, proper construction should prevent it: (1) the contractor is responsible if waterproofing was inadequate — salt-laden moisture rising through the substrate causes the most severe efflorescence; (2) the architect may be liable if the specification did not account for coastal exposure (no DPC, no tanking, no breathable membranes); (3) the tile supplier is only liable if the tiles have excessive water absorption or if they recommended an inappropriate product for coastal use. Prevention: full waterproof membrane under tiles in all coastal areas; use low-absorption porcelain (≤0.5%); ensure adequate falls to drainage; apply a penetrating, breathable sealer that allows moisture vapour transmission while preventing salt crystallisation on the surface; regular washing with fresh water to remove salt deposits before crystallisation. Durban's coastal humidity and salt spray make this a standard specification requirement — it should not be a surprise. The NHBRC Home Building Manual specifically addresses coastal construction requirements.

**Q:** 74 Our Upington store (Northern Cape) has tiles that have crazed (fine cracking in the glaze) within 2 years. The supplier blames thermal shock from extreme temperature variation (5°C at night to 45°C day). Is this a valid excuse, or is the product defective?
**A:** Crazing can result from either: (1) a mismatch between the thermal expansion coefficients of the tile body and glaze (manufacturing defect); (2) extreme thermal cycling exceeding the tile's design tolerance (environmental stress). Upington's 40°C daily temperature swings are among the most extreme in South Africa. Quality tiles should withstand this if properly specified — SANS 1449 includes thermal shock testing (ISO 10545-9) which subjects tiles to repeated cycles between 15°C and 145°C. If the tiles passed this test, they should theoretically handle Upington conditions. However: (1) if the tiles were installed without adequate expansion joints (minimum 5mm per 6m in extreme climates), thermal stress concentrates and causes crazing; (2) dark-coloured tiles absorb more solar heat — a black tile in direct sun can reach 70°C+ surface temperature; (3) installation on a rigid concrete slab without movement accommodation will crack. Commission a forensic investigation: test a tile for thermal shock resistance per ISO 10545-9; inspect joint spacing; measure actual surface temperatures. If tiles pass testing, the issue is likely installation (contractor liability). If they fail, it's a product defect (supplier liability).

## 15. INDOOR AIR QUALITY & GREEN BUILDING

**Q:** 75 Our new mall store used SANS-compliant tile adhesive with VOC emissions of 25g/L. A customer with multiple chemical sensitivity (MCS) has complained and threatened legal action under the CPA, claiming the emissions made them ill. What is our liability?
**A:** The CPA Section 55 requires goods to be safe and suitable for their intended purpose. At 25g/L, the adhesive complies with SANS limits and is generally considered safe once cured (typically 24-72 hours). However, MCS is a controversial condition not formally recognised in all medical circles. Your liability depends on: (1) whether the store was opened to the public during the adhesive curing period — if so, you may be liable for negligence regardless of the adhesive's ultimate compliance; (2) whether you provided adequate ventilation during and after installation; (3) whether you posted any warning notices. Defence: provide the adhesive's SDS, VOC test certificates, and evidence of compliance with SANS and international standards; demonstrate that the adhesive was fully cured before public access; show adequate ventilation records. Consider settling any small claim as a goodwill gesture if the cost of defence exceeds the claim. For future stores: specify ultra-low VOC adhesives (≤10g/L, Greenguard Gold certified); implement a 72-hour curing period with forced ventilation before opening; display "recently renovated" notices. The Green Building Council's Green Star IEQ credits reward low-VOC material selection.

**Q:** 76 Our Green Star submission requires proof that our tiles do not contain Red List chemicals (formaldehyde, lead, mercury, phthalates). Our tile supplier has never heard of the Red List. Can we still achieve Green Star points, and what chemicals should we actually be concerned about in tiles?
**A:** The Red List is a Living Future Institute (ILFI) concept adopted by some Green Star credits. South Africa's Green Star uses a slightly different approach — the GBCSA Material Health credit focuses on: (1) VOC emissions from installed products; (2) absence of heavy metals (lead, cadmium, mercury) in glazes; (3) absence of phthalates in vinyl products; (4) formaldehyde content in composite wood products (not typically relevant to tiles). For ceramic and porcelain tiles: heavy metals in glazes (particularly lead and cadmium in some coloured glazes) are the main concern — request heavy metal migration test reports per EN 1388 or ISO 6486. For vinyl tiles: phthalate plasticisers (DEHP, DBP, BBP) are the main concern — request phthalate-free certification. For adhesives and grouts: formaldehyde and isocyanates may be present — request VOC and specific chemical content data. If your supplier is unfamiliar with these requirements, engage a Green Star Accredited Professional (GSAP) to guide data collection. You can still achieve points with alternative compliance paths if full Red List data is unavailable.

**Q:** 77 Our mall landlord requires all tenants to comply with the Green Star IEQ (Indoor Environmental Quality) credit for flooring. The requirement is for floor coverings with VOC emissions ≤50 μg/m²/hr (TVOC). Our tile supplier has no such test data. Do tiles actually emit VOCs, and how do we test this?
**A:** Ceramic and porcelain tiles themselves emit negligible VOCs — they are fired at 1,200°C, which eliminates all organic compounds. However, the TOTAL installed system (tile + adhesive + grout + sealant) DOES emit VOCs, primarily from the adhesive and any sealants used. For Green Star IEQ compliance: (1) the TILE itself will pass with no testing needed; (2) the ADHESIVE must be tested per ISO 16000 series (chamber testing) or certified by an accredited body (Greenguard, Eurofins, SCS Global); (3) the GROUT must similarly be tested; (4) any SEALANT used at perimeters or joints must be included. VOC emissions are measured in micrograms per square metre per hour (μg/m²/hr) after 28 days of curing. To comply: select adhesives and grouts with existing GreenGuard Gold or similar certification; if unavailable, commission chamber testing of the complete system through an ISO 17025 lab (Bureau Veritas, SGS, or equivalent in South Africa). Budget R15,000-25,000 per product for testing. Many European adhesive manufacturers have existing data — request it before commissioning new tests.

## 16. DISASTER RECOVERY & DOCUMENTATION

**Q:** 78 Our flagship store burned down in a suspected electrical fire. We need to replicate the flooring EXACTLY for insurance purposes. We have no records of the original tile specification (installed 8 years ago). What is our recourse, and how should we have prevented this?
**A:** Without documentation, replication is extremely difficult. Your recourse: (1) engage a forensic architect or materials specialist to identify the tile from physical evidence (charred fragments can be analysed for composition, colour, and manufacturer); (2) check with your original contractor or architect — they may have records even if you don't; (3) review old photographs of the store for visual identification; (4) contact potential suppliers with fragment samples for matching. Prevention (for future): maintain a "Building Manual" or "Asset Register" for every store including: product data sheets, batch numbers, supplier details, installation dates, warranty documents, and photographs. Store this in cloud-based document management with off-site backup. The insurance issue: your policy likely covers "reinstatement" — if the exact tile is unavailable, the insurer will pay for the nearest equivalent. But if you want exact replication for brand consistency, you may need to fund the difference. The NHBRC requires a Home Builder's Manual for new homes — adopt equivalent practice for commercial stores. POPIA-compliant cloud storage is essential for record retention.

**Q:** 79 Our store flooded in the April 2022 KZN floods. The insurance assessor has classified 60% of the tiles as "recoverable with deep cleaning" and will only pay for 40% replacement. We believe all tiles should be replaced due to potential subfloor moisture and contamination. How do we challenge this?
**A:** Challenge the assessment with technical evidence. Flood-damaged tiles present several hidden risks: (1) SUBFLOOR MOISTURE — even if tiles appear intact, moisture trapped in the substrate can cause adhesive failure, efflorescence, and mould growth over 6-18 months; (2) CONTAMINATION — flood water in KZN contained sewage, chemicals, and industrial waste; tiles are non-porous but grout joints may harbour contaminants; (3) ADHESIVE DEGRADATION — cement-based adhesives can weaken after prolonged water immersion. Engage an independent flooring consultant to: (1) conduct moisture content testing of the subfloor (calcium carbide method or moisture meters) — readings above 75% RH or 5% MC indicate replacement is needed; (2) test adhesive bond strength (pull-off test per EN 1542) — if adhesion is below 0.5MPa, replacement is warranted; (3) test for bacterial contamination in grout joints; (4) provide a written report supporting replacement. Present this to the insurance assessor. If they refuse, escalate to the Ombudsman for Short-Term Insurance. For future policies, specify "new for old" cover for flooring and ensure flood coverage includes "hidden damage" provision.

**Q:** 80 Our store was robbed at gunpoint. During the robbery, a display unit was pushed over, cracking 15 square metres of large-format tiles. The police have released the scene. Our insurance covers "theft and malicious damage" but the insurer argues this is "accidental damage during criminal activity" with a higher excess. Which applies?
**A:** This is a policy interpretation dispute. "Malicious damage" typically requires intent to damage property — if the robbers knocked over the display as an unintended consequence of the robbery (not deliberately smashing tiles), the insurer may argue it's not malicious. However, most comprehensive commercial policies cover damage during robbery under the theft section regardless of intent. Review your policy: (1) check the definition of "malicious damage" — some policies define it broadly as "damage occurring during unlawful entry"; (2) check whether robbery is a specified peril with its own terms; (3) the excess difference matters — if accidental damage excess is R50,000 and malicious damage is R10,000, this affects your claim. Engage your broker to argue that damage during an armed robbery is a direct consequence of an insured peril (theft/robbery) and should be covered under those terms. If the insurer refuses, the Ombudsman for Short-Term Insurance has generally ruled in favour of policyholders in armed robbery scenarios. Document: police case number; CCTV footage if available; assessor's report confirming damage occurred during robbery.

**Q:** 81 After a store fire, our insurance company appointed a restoration contractor who cleaned the smoke-damaged tiles with an acidic cleaner that etched the glazed surface. The tiles now need replacement. Is the restoration contractor liable, or is this an insurance issue?
**A:** The restoration contractor is potentially liable for negligence — they used an inappropriate cleaning method that caused additional damage. This is a professional negligence claim separate from the fire insurance claim. Your position: (1) the insurance company appointed the contractor — they may be vicariously liable or at least responsible for ensuring the contractor is competent; (2) the contractor had a duty of care to use appropriate cleaning methods — glazed tiles should be cleaned with neutral or mildly alkaline cleaners, never strong acids; (3) the additional damage was foreseeable and avoidable. Course of action: (1) notify your insurer that the restoration contractor caused additional damage; (2) demand the insurer covers replacement of the etched tiles (their contractor, their responsibility); (3) if the insurer refuses, claim directly against the restoration contractor's professional indemnity insurance; (4) document the before/after condition with photos and an independent assessment. The Financial Services Conduct Authority (FSCA) guidelines on claims handling require insurers to act fairly — causing additional damage through appointed contractors and refusing to cover it would likely constitute unfair claims handling.

## 17. CRIME, SECURITY & VANDALISM

**Q:** 82 Our store in a high-crime area has experienced repeated theft of copper earthing cables from the floor slab during installation. The contractors are working overnight when the mall is closed. Who bears the cost of replacement and delay?
**A:** This is a risk allocation issue. Under standard construction contracts (JBCC, NEC), the contractor bears responsibility for site security and protection of works unless the employer (you) has taken control of the site. If the mall landlord controls after-hours access, they may share liability for inadequate security. However: (1) the contractor should have secured materials and installed earthing immediately, not left copper exposed overnight; (2) if your contract requires after-hours work in a high-risk area, you may bear some responsibility for specifying unsafe working conditions; (3) the contractor's insurance (Contractors All Risk) may cover theft of materials. Best approach: (1) require the contractor to install earthing as a continuous operation and not leave exposed copper overnight; (2) require security escort or CCTV monitoring for high-value materials; (3) specify that the contractor bears theft risk unless security is provided by the employer. For the current situation: if the contractor's negligence (poor material security) contributed, they bear the cost; if the theft was despite reasonable precautions, it may be an insurance claim. The Criminal Matters Amendment Act 2015 provides for property damage during trespass — file a police case for insurance purposes.

**Q:** 83 Our jewellery store (tenant in our mall) has demanded ballistic-rated flooring as part of their robbery prevention measures. They want a floor that cannot be easily dug through to access the vault below. Is there such a thing as "ballistic" or "forced-entry-resistant" tiled flooring?
**A:** There is no "ballistic tile" standard, but forced-entry-resistant flooring systems do exist. The requirement is not about the tile surface but the COMPOSITE FLOOR SYSTEM: (1) a reinforced concrete slab minimum 150mm thick with rebar mesh; (2) a steel plate layer (minimum 3mm) embedded in the slab; (3) standard tiles installed over this structural base. The tiles themselves provide minimal resistance — it's the structural slab below that prevents access. For jewellery and banking applications: (1) specify the floor slab to SASO (South African Police Service) security standards for protected premises; (2) install seismic sensors in the floor to detect drilling or cutting; (3) use anti-vandal tiles (thick, impact-resistant porcelain) that resist casual damage but do not claim ballistic protection; (4) ensure the vault itself meets SANS 1294 (safes and strongrooms). The tenant's demand for "ballistic flooring" should be translated into proper structural engineering specifications — engage a security consultant registered with SAPS. The floor above a vault is typically a structural/engineering issue, not a tiling specification issue.

**Q:** 84 Our store's tiled facade (cladding tiles on the external wall) has been repeatedly damaged by vandals throwing stones. The body corporate refuses to pay for repairs, claiming this is our tenant responsibility. Our lease says the landlord maintains the "structure." Who pays?
**A:** This depends on lease interpretation. Typically: (1) the landlord (body corporate in a sectional title scheme) maintains the common property and structural elements; (2) the tenant maintains interior fit-out and tenant-specific improvements. External cladding tiles on a store facade: if the tiles are part of the original building structure (installed by the developer), they are the body corporate's responsibility under Section 37(1)(b) of the Sectional Titles Act; if YOU installed the tiles as tenant improvements, they are your responsibility. The "structure" vs. "fit-out" distinction is key. Even if the body corporate is responsible for repairs, they may claim the cost from you if the damage was caused by your specific risk (e.g., if your store is the target of repeated vandalism due to your business activity). Check your lease: does it include a "make good" clause for damage beyond fair wear and tear? For persistent vandalism, consider: anti-graffiti coating; CCTV; security shutters; or specifying impact-resistant cladding tiles (minimum 10mm thickness, through-body colour to hide chips).

## 18. FLOOD & ENVIRONMENTAL DAMAGE

**Q:** 85 Our Durban store was flooded in the April 2022 KZN floods. The tiles have lifted completely due to hydrostatic pressure from underneath the slab. The landlord says this is an "act of God" and not their responsibility. Is this correct, and who pays for replacement?
**A:** "Act of God" (vis maior) is a defence in South African common law, but its application to flooding depends on foreseeability and mitigation. The April 2022 floods were extreme but KZN has a well-documented history of flooding — the 1987 floods were similarly catastrophic. A landlord in a known floodplain may be expected to have: (1) adequate stormwater management; (2) proper slab waterproofing; (3) raised floor levels; (4) sump pumps. If the landlord failed to maintain drainage or the building design was inadequate for the flood risk, their "act of God" defence weakens. Under the lease: (1) if it's a "full repair and maintain" lease, the tenant may be responsible regardless of cause; (2) if the landlord maintains the structure, they are responsible for structural failures including hydrostatic pressure damage. For the tiling specifically: if the tiles lifted due to inadequate subfloor preparation or waterproofing (not pure hydrostatic pressure), the original contractor or landlord may be liable. Engage a forensic engineer to determine the root cause. The National Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002 may provide limited relief for disaster-affected businesses.

**Q:** 86 After the KZN floods, our store's tiles developed a persistent musty odour. An environmental consultant identified mould growth under the tiles due to trapped moisture. Our insurer has denied the claim, saying mould is excluded as a "gradually operating cause." Is this exclusion enforceable?
**A:** Gradually operating cause exclusions are common in South African commercial policies and are generally enforceable. However, there is a causation question here: the mould was CAUSED by a sudden, insured event (the flood). The "efficient cause" doctrine in South African insurance law (Leyland v Norwich Union) holds that if an insured peril sets in motion a chain of events leading to loss, the loss is covered even if the final damage occurs gradually. Your argument: the flood was the proximate cause; the mould was the direct consequence; without the flood, there would be no mould. This is distinct from mould that develops from chronic poor maintenance (gradually operating). If the insurer maintains the exclusion, escalate to the Ombudsman for Short-Term Insurance. Prevention: after any flooding, tiles must be lifted, the subfloor thoroughly dried (dehumidifiers for 2-4 weeks), and moisture content verified below 75% RH before reinstallation. The Occupational Health and Safety Act requires employers to provide a safe working environment — if mould affects indoor air quality, you may need to close the store until remediation.

**Q:** 87 Our Limpopo store experienced severe hail damage — hailstones cracked outdoor patio tiles. The supplier claims their warranty excludes "weather damage." Is this a valid warranty exclusion under South African law?
**A:** Warranty exclusions for "weather damage" are generally valid if clearly stated and not unconscionable under the CPA. However: (1) if the tiles were sold as "frost-resistant" or "suitable for outdoor use," the supplier impliedly warranted weather resistance — hail is a normal weather event in Limpopo; (2) SANS 1449 includes frost resistance testing (ISO 10545-12) for tiles marketed as frost-resistant — if the tiles failed this test, they are defective regardless of warranty exclusions; (3) the CPA Section 55 implied warranty of quality cannot be excluded for consumer transactions. For B2B contracts, warranty exclusions are more freely enforceable. Check: was the tile specifically rated for outdoor use? What size hail caused the damage (golf-ball sized hail can damage almost anything)? Was the installation method appropriate for the exposure? Large-format thin tiles installed without adequate movement joints are more vulnerable to impact. For outdoor areas in hail-prone regions: specify minimum 10mm thickness; use small-format tiles (less likely to crack than large); ensure full bedding (not spot-fixed); provide adequate movement accommodation.

## 19. SEISMIC & STRUCTURAL REQUIREMENTS

**Q:** 88 Our Cape Town CBD store is in a building designated as "heritage" under the National Heritage Resources Act. We want to replace the 1920s mosaic floor with modern tiles. The architect says we need SAHRA (South African Heritage Resources Agency) approval. Is this correct, and what is the process?
**A:** Yes, this is correct. Section 48 of the National Heritage Resources Act 25 of 1999 prohibits alteration or demolition of heritage structures without a permit from the relevant heritage authority (SAHRA for Grade I and II national heritage sites; Provincial Heritage Resources Authority for Grade II provincial and Grade III local sites). Original mosaic floors in heritage buildings are typically protected features. Process: (1) determine the heritage grading of your building — check with the local PHRA or municipality; (2) if Grade I or II, apply to SAHRA for an alteration permit — this requires a heritage impact assessment (HIA) by a SAHRA-accredited heritage practitioner; (3) if the mosaic is original and in good condition, SAHRA will almost certainly refuse removal — they may require repair and retention; (4) if the mosaic is severely damaged, they may permit replacement with a sympathetic alternative or require documentation (photographic record, sample retention) before removal. Penalties for unauthorised alteration: fines up to R1 million and/or imprisonment up to 10 years (Section 189). The Western Cape has particularly strict heritage enforcement in the Cape Town CBD. Engage a heritage practitioner early — the process takes 3-6 months.

**Q:** 89 Our building in the Cape Town CBD is in a moderate seismic zone (Zone 1 per SANS 10160). The structural engineer has specified a flexible joint system for all floor finishes to accommodate seismic movement. Our tiling contractor has never installed seismic joints. What is required?
**A:** SANS 10160:2011 (South African loading code) includes seismic design requirements for buildings in seismic zones. For floor finishes: (1) EXPANSION/SEISMIC JOINTS must be provided at all structural movement joints — these are typically 15-25mm wide and must extend through the full tile installation (tile + adhesive + screed); (2) JOINT COVER SYSTEM — specify a rated movement joint cover system (e.g., CS Allway, Schluter DILEX) that accommodates the calculated movement (typically ±25mm for seismic joints in commercial buildings); (3) TILE INSTALLATION — tiles must not bridge structural joints; a gap must be maintained and covered by the joint system; (4) ADHESIVE — use flexible (S1 or S2) deformable adhesive that accommodates substrate movement without cracking; (5) EDGE PROTECTION — use metal edge trim profiles at all tile terminations to prevent chipping during movement. The joint cover system must be: fire-rated if it crosses fire compartments; load-rated for the expected traffic (heavy-duty for retail); and colour-matched where visible. Your contractor MUST be trained in seismic joint installation — engage the joint system manufacturer's technical team for supervision.

**Q:** 90 Our Port Elizabeth store is in a building that suffered structural settlement, creating a 15mm step in the concrete slab across the sales floor. We want to level this with a screed and retile. The structural engineer says we must first fix the settlement. Can we not just level and tile over it?
**A:** No — you must address the root cause first. Screeding over a settlement crack does not stop ongoing movement. If the building is still settling (active structural movement), your new screed and tiles WILL crack again — potentially within months. The correct sequence: (1) the structural engineer must determine if settlement is ACTIVE (ongoing) or DORMANT (historical, now stable); (2) if active, underpinning or other structural remediation is required before any floor replacement; (3) if dormant, the slab can be levelled with a self-levelling compound or screed, but a movement joint must be installed at the crack location; (4) if the step exceeds 10mm over 2m, a bonded screed (minimum 15mm) or unbonded screed (minimum 35mm) is required — follow SANS 10109 (screeds and floors); (5) specify flexible tile adhesive and provide movement joints aligned with the structural crack. Tiling over an unresolved structural problem is false economy — the NHBRC 5-year warranty would not cover it (it's not a tile defect, it's a structural issue), and your commercial insurer may exclude claims from known structural defects. Get a signed structural clearance before proceeding.

## 20. MINING, PRISON & SPECIALISED INDUSTRIAL

**Q:** 91 Our mine site canteen serves 2,000 workers per shift. The tiled floor is subject to heavy mud and dust ingress from work boots. The grout has turned black and cleaning has no effect. Is this a specification failure, and what should we have specified?
**A:** This is almost certainly a specification failure for the application. Mine site canteens are among the most demanding environments for flooring. The black grout is likely: (1) permanent staining from mine dust (often containing coal, iron oxide, or manganese); (2) bacterial colonisation in porous cement grout; (3) embedded grit that cannot be removed by standard cleaning. For this application, you should have specified: (1) EPOXY GROUT — stain-proof, chemical-resistant, and non-porous; (2) DARK-COLOURED GROUT — hides staining better than light colours; (3) LARGE FORMAT TILES — minimise joint quantity; (4) R12 or R13 SLIP RATING — essential for wet, muddy boots; (5) ENTRY SYSTEM — industrial-grade entrance matting (minimum 6m run-off) to remove bulk debris before the canteen; (6) PRESSURE WASHING CAPABILITY — the floor must withstand daily high-pressure cleaning. Mine dust often contains oily compounds from machinery — standard cement grout absorbs these and becomes permanently stained. For future: specify epoxy grout; use dark charcoal or black grout colour; install full drainage with slope to floor drains; specify hot water pressure washing compatibility. The Mine Health and Safety Act requires canteen facilities to be "clean and sanitary" — stained flooring may attract inspector attention.

**Q:** 92 Our correctional facility project requires flooring that meets Department of Correctional Services (DCS) security standards. The specification mentions "no loose items that can be used as weapons." How does this affect standard tile installation accessories like edge trims and movement joints?
**A:** DCS security standards (DCS Building Norms and Standards) require that all fixtures and fittings in inmate-accessible areas be tamper-proof and not readily detachable for use as weapons. For tiled floors: (1) EDGE TRIMS — use heavy-duty, screw-fixed metal trims with tamper-proof (security/Torx) screws, not clip-in or adhesive trims that can be pried off; (2) MOVEMENT JOINTS — use heavy-duty metal joint covers secured with security fasteners, not snap-in PVC covers; (3) FLOOR DRAINS — specify tamper-proof drain gratings secured with special tools; (4) SKIRTING — use coved, integral skirting (minimum 150mm) that cannot be separated from the wall or floor; (5) NO LOOSE THRESHOLDS — all transitions must be flush, fixed, and tamper-resistant. In high-security areas (maximum security cells), many facilities specify seamless poured resin flooring SPECIFICALLY because it eliminates all joints, edges, and fixings. Standard residential or commercial tile installations are NOT suitable for correctional facilities. The DCS technical team reviews all specifications before construction — engage them early in the design process.

**Q:** 93 Our coal mine's change house requires flooring resistant to coal dust, which is acidic and abrasive. Our standard commercial vinyl tiles have worn through in 18 months. What flooring specification should we use for mining change houses?
**A:** Coal dust (particularly fine coal dust) is both abrasive and mildly acidic. Standard commercial vinyl is completely inadequate. Specification for mining change houses: (1) HEAVY-DUTY EPOXY SCREED (4-6mm) — specifically formulated for industrial applications with high abrasion resistance (Taber Abraser CS-17, 1000 cycles, weight loss <100mg); (2) if tiles are preferred, specify 12mm thick QUARRY TILES with FURAN GROUT — quarry tiles have proven mining industry performance over decades; (3) ensure chemical resistance to pH 3-4 (coal dust leachate is acidic); (4) specify R11 or R12 slip rating — change houses are constantly wet; (5) include full floor drainage with sump and pump — coal dust-laden wash water must be contained; (6) the floor must withstand high-pressure hose-down cleaning daily. The Mine Health and Safety Act and DMR (Department of Mineral Resources) regulations require adequate sanitary facilities — flooring failure can result in compliance notices. Change houses are high-wear, high-moisture environments — budget for replacement every 7-10 years even with correct specification.

## 21. AUTOMOTIVE & WORKSHOP APPLICATIONS

**Q:** 94 Our vehicle dealership's showroom uses polished porcelain tiles that look stunning but are extremely slippery when wet (PTV 18 wet). A salesperson slipped during a rain event. Our public liability insurer has noted the claim and may increase premiums. What are our options without changing the aesthetic?
**A:** This is a common showroom dilemma — aesthetics vs. safety. Options that preserve the polished look: (1) ANTI-SLIP TREATMENT — apply a nano-etching anti-slip treatment (e.g., SureGrip, Slip No More) that micro-etches the surface, increasing wet PTV to 35-45 without visibly altering the appearance. These treatments last 2-5 years depending on traffic; (2) ENTRANCE MATTING — install 6-10m of high-performance entrance matting to remove moisture before the showroom floor; (3) TARGETED TREATMENT — treat only high-risk areas (entrance zones, around display cars) and leave the main floor polished; (4) MATS AND SIGNAGE — use branded mats at entrances during wet weather with "caution" signage. Note: nano-etching treatments may affect the tile warranty — check with the tile manufacturer. Some manufacturers void warranties if chemical treatments are applied. The SANS 10107:2018 slip resistance code recommends PTV ≥ 36 for commercial entrances and ≥ 45 for areas with water exposure. Your PTV of 18 is dangerously low for any public area. The Department of Labour can issue a notice under OHSA if slip hazards are reported.

**Q:** 95 Our tyre fitment centre uses a compressed air system that creates oil mist in the workshop. The oil has soaked into our unglazed quarry tile floor, creating a persistent slip hazard. Standard degreasers don't work. What is the solution?
**A:** Oil-soaked unglazed tiles are extremely difficult to remediate because the oil penetrates deep into the pores. Solutions: (1) DEEP EXTRACTION — use an alkaline degreaser (pH 12-13) with hot water extraction equipment (industrial carpet extractor adapted for hard floors). Multiple treatments may be needed; (2) ABSORBENT POUltice — apply fuller's earth or diatomaceous earth poultice, leave for 24-48 hours to draw out oil, repeat as needed; (3) STEAM CLEANING — industrial steam cleaner (150°C+) can emulsify and extract embedded oil; (4) If these fail, the floor needs replacement — oil-impregnated tiles cannot be effectively cleaned. For the replacement specification: (1) use SEALED PORCELAIN tiles (water absorption ≤0.5%) that do not absorb oil; (2) apply a penetrating, oil-repellent sealer (fluoropolymer-based); (3) specify EPOXY GROUT (oil-resistant); (4) install oil-resistant, heavy-duty epoxy screed in the highest-exposure areas; (5) improve workshop ventilation to reduce oil mist concentration. The OHSA General Safety Regulations require safe flooring — an oil-soaked floor is a clear safety violation.

**Q:** 96 Our car wash facility has tiled walls and floors. The high-pressure water jets (150 bar) have eroded the grout within 12 months. The contractor used standard cement grout. What should have been specified, and is the contractor liable?
**A:** The contractor is liable for using an inappropriate grout for a known high-pressure environment. Standard cement grout has no resistance to 150 bar water pressure — it erodes rapidly. For car wash facilities: (1) use EPOXY GROUT — specifically formulated for industrial wet environments with chemical resistance to detergents and waxes; (2) specify TILES with low water absorption (≤0.5% — fully vitrified porcelain) — high-pressure water can force water into porous tiles, causing internal pressure damage; (3) use FLEXIBLE ADHESIVE (S1 or S2) to accommodate vibration from pressure washers; (4) ensure FULL BEDDING of tiles — spot-fixing will fail under pressure; (5) seal all perimeter joints with polyurethane sealant; (6) specify coved skirting to eliminate wall-floor junction gaps. The contractor should have known that standard cement grout is unsuitable for car wash applications — this is basic industry knowledge. If they tendered on a performance specification ("suitable for car wash environment"), they are clearly in breach. If they were given a generic specification with no application detail, liability may be shared. For future: include the operating conditions (pressure, chemicals, temperature) in the specification.

## 22. UNIFORMITY & BRAND CONSISTENCY

**Q:** 97 We are rolling out 200 stores over 5 years. The tile manufacturer has warned that production colour varies between batches (shade variation). How do we ensure colour consistency across all stores when orders are placed years apart?
**A:** Shade variation is inherent in tile manufacturing — even premium brands cannot guarantee identical colour across multi-year rollouts. Strategies: (1) ORDER FULL QUANTITY UPFRONT — negotiate a single-batch order for the entire rollout, store excess stock at the supplier's warehouse or a third-party logistics facility. This guarantees consistency but ties up capital and warehouse space; (2) SPECIFY BATCH SAMPLES — require the supplier to retain a master sample from the initial order; all subsequent orders must be matched to this sample with ΔE ≤1.0 (perceptible colour difference threshold); (3) USE "INTER-batch" TILES — order 10% extra from each batch to allow blending between batches at transition stores; (4) ACCEPTABLE VARIATION CLAUSE — include in the contract that shade variation within the manufacturer's published tolerance (typically "V2" or "V3" on the Ceramic Tile Distributors Association scale) is acceptable; (5) STORE SEQUENCE — cluster stores by batch (e.g., all KZN stores from Batch 1, all Gauteng from Batch 2) so variation is not visible between adjacent stores. Document the shade variation policy in your brand guidelines — some variation is acceptable if customers never see two stores side-by-side.

**Q:** 98 Our brand refresh requires changing from beige to grey tiles. We have 23 stores still using the old beige tiles that are only 3 years old. Our sustainability policy prohibits waste. Can we overlay new tiles on the old ones, or do we need to remove and replace?
**A:** Overlaying (tiling over existing tiles) is technically possible and is done commercially, but with significant caveats: (1) HEIGHT — overlaying adds 15-25mm thickness (tile + adhesive), which affects door clearances, transition strips, and equipment levels; (2) ADHESION — the existing tiles must be thoroughly cleaned, degreased, and mechanically abraded (scarified) for the new adhesive to bond; (3) WEIGHT — the additional load must be within the slab's capacity; a porcelain tile overlay weighs approximately 25-30kg/m² additional; (4) WARRANTY — most adhesive manufacturers will not warranty overlay installations unless specific primers and flexible adhesives are used; (5) LIFESPAN — overlay installations have higher failure rates than full removals, particularly if the original tiles are loose or the substrate has movement. If your sustainability policy prohibits waste, consider: (1) overlay with a bonding primer and flexible adhesive from a manufacturer who warranties the system (Mapei, Laticrete, Tilepac have overlay systems); (2) remove and recycle the old tiles — some suppliers accept old porcelain for recycling into new tiles or road base; (3) donate removed tiles to Habitat for Humanity or similar. Calculate the lifecycle carbon impact — overlay avoids demolition waste but may result in earlier replacement if it fails.

**Q:** 99 Our store design includes a specific tile pattern (herringbone) that requires skilled installation. Our Limpopo contractor has never done herringbone and has produced visibly uneven joints. Our brand standards require consistency nationwide. Can we reject the work and demand replacement?
**A:** Yes, if the brand standard specification clearly defines the installation pattern, joint width, and tolerance (e.g., "herringbone pattern, 2mm joints, maximum 1mm variation in joint width, maximum 2mm lippage"). Under the Consumer Protection Act (if applicable) or common law, you are entitled to work that meets the agreed specification. However: (1) was the contractor adequately briefed and trained? If you provided no pattern guidance or training, you share responsibility; (2) is there a reasonable rectification option? Minor joint variation may be acceptable within tolerances; (3) replacement cost — if the tiles are damaged during removal, who bears the cost? Best practice for national rollouts with complex patterns: (1) produce a detailed installation guide with photographs; (2) require a sample panel (minimum 2m²) approved before full installation; (3) use only contractors who have completed the specific pattern before, or provide on-site training by a specialist; (4) include pattern complexity in the contract pricing — herringbone costs 30-50% more in labour than straight lay. For Limpopo: either bring in an experienced installer or accept straight lay for that store if brand consistency allows.

## 23. PAYMENT, VAT & FINANCIAL

**Q:** 100 Our tiling contractor has invoiced us including VAT at 15%, but their VAT registration number on the invoice is invalid (checked on SARS eFiling). We have already paid the invoice. Can we claim the input VAT, and what is our risk?
**A:** You CANNOT claim input VAT if the supplier's VAT registration is invalid. SARS requires a valid tax invoice with a correct VAT registration number for input tax claims. If you have already paid: (1) you cannot claim the VAT portion as input tax; (2) if you already claimed it and SARS audits, you will face penalties and interest on disallowed input tax; (3) you have a claim against the contractor for recovery of the VAT paid (they charged VAT without being registered, which is illegal); (4) the contractor may face criminal prosecution under the VAT Act for charging VAT while unregistered (Section 58). Immediate action: (1) demand the contractor provide their valid VAT registration certificate; (2) if they are not VAT registered, request a revised invoice without VAT (at the net amount) and a refund of VAT paid; (3) if they refuse, report to SARS and consider civil recovery; (4) adjust your VAT return to remove the input tax claim if already submitted. For future: verify every new supplier's VAT status on SARS eFiling before first payment. Include a warranty in all contracts that the supplier is duly VAT registered and will remain so.

**Q:** 101 Our supplier has offered a 5% early payment discount if we pay within 7 days instead of 30. Our cost of capital is 12% per annum. Should we take the discount, and what is the effective annualised saving?
**A:** Calculate the effective annualised rate of the discount. A 5% discount for paying 23 days early (day 7 vs. day 30) translates to: (5% / 95%) × (365 / 23) = 83.5% effective annualised return. This far exceeds your 12% cost of capital — taking the discount is highly advantageous. The formula: Effective Annual Rate = (Discount % / (100% - Discount %)) × (365 / (Normal term - Discount term)). At these numbers, the early payment discount is one of the best returns on cash available. However: (1) ensure your supplier is financially stable — early payment to a distressed supplier may mean you become an unsecured creditor if they fail; (2) verify that the discount applies to the full invoice value including VAT; (3) check if the discount is conditional on anything else (minimum order quantities, specific products); (4) ensure your accounts payable system can process within 7 days — many corporate systems have longer approval cycles. Negotiate: if 5% is offered for 7-day payment, what discount applies for 14-day payment? The supplier's cost of capital is likely higher than yours (SMEs often pay 20-30% for working capital), so there is room to negotiate a middle ground.

**Q:** 102 Our tiling supplier is a Level 1 B-BBEE EME (Exempt Micro-Enterprise) with annual turnover below R10 million. They have no VAT registration. We want to claim B-BBEE procurement recognition. Can we claim on a non-VAT invoice?
**A:** Yes. B-BBEE procurement recognition is based on the total value of procurement from B-BBEE-compliant suppliers, regardless of VAT status. For non-VAT-registered EMEs: (1) the invoice value is the total amount paid (no VAT separation needed); (2) you can claim the full invoice value as B-BBEE-compliant procurement; (3) the EME's B-BBEE status (Level 1 or Level 2 for 51%+ black-owned, or Level 4 for 100% black-owned with turnover between R10m-R50m) is based on their sworn affidavit or CIPC certificate, not VAT registration. However: (1) the payment must be properly documented (invoice, proof of payment, delivery note); (2) the supplier must be a legitimate trading entity (registered with CIPC or have a valid business license); (3) if the supplier is not registered for VAT, they cannot issue tax invoices — a standard invoice is sufficient for B-BBEE but not for your VAT input tax claim. Ensure the supplier's B-BBEE certificate or sworn affidavit is current and stored with the procurement record. Your verification agency will request this evidence.

## 24. DIGITAL INTEGRATION & E-PROCUREMENT

**Q:** 103 Our head office requires all suppliers to integrate with SAP Ariba for electronic procurement. Our tile supplier is a small family business with no IT capability. Who bears the cost of Ariba integration, and is there an alternative?
**A:** SAP Ariba integration costs vary depending on the level: (1) ARIBA NETWORK (basic) — free for suppliers to join and receive POs; (2) CATALOG (cXML punch-out) — requires IT development, typically R50,000-R200,000 depending on the supplier's existing systems; (3) FULL INTEGRATION (EDI, invoicing, ASN) — significant IT investment. For small suppliers: SAP Ariba offers a "Lite" version that is free to join. The cost allocation depends on your procurement policy: (1) some large corporates absorb the integration cost as part of supplier onboarding; (2) others require suppliers to self-fund as a condition of doing business; (3) some offer a hybrid (corporate pays for initial setup, supplier pays for ongoing maintenance). For a small tile supplier unable to integrate: (1) use the Ariba Network basic tier (free) for PO and invoice exchange; (2) host a static catalog (non-punch-out) on Ariba — lower functionality but no integration cost; (3) use a third-party catalog hosting service that intermediates between the supplier and Ariba; (4) if the supplier represents critical spend, consider direct integration investment as part of your ESD programme — this supports small supplier development. The PAIA and B-BBEE frameworks encourage preferential procurement from SMEs — requiring expensive IT integration may exclude small B-BBEE suppliers, undermining your procurement strategy.

**Q:** 104 Our procurement system requires each store to raise individual purchase orders for maintenance tiles. The supplier has imposed a R500 minimum order charge per PO. With 200 stores, this is costing us R100,000 annually in minimum charges alone. How should we restructure this?
**A:** This is a procurement process inefficiency. Solutions: (1) CENTRALISED PROCUREMENT — consolidate all store tile requirements into a single national purchase order with scheduled deliveries to individual stores. One PO, one delivery schedule, no minimum order charges; (2) BLANKET PURCHASE ORDER — negotiate a 12-month framework PO with call-off facilities. Stores order against the framework without raising new POs; (3) STOCKING HUB — establish regional distribution hubs (e.g., Gauteng hub, Western Cape hub, KZN hub) where stores collect or receive small-quantity deliveries from local stock; (4) CROSS-DOCKING — supplier delivers to a central DC, which breaks bulk and distributes to stores as part of existing logistics flows; (5) NEGOTIATE — if decentralised ordering must continue, negotiate a waiver of minimum order charges for stores ordering above a threshold, or a flat annual administration fee instead of per-PO charges. Calculate the total cost of each option — centralised procurement typically saves 15-25% on total procurement cost through volume leverage and reduced transaction costs. The supplier will also prefer fewer, larger orders — use this as negotiation leverage.

**Q:** 105 Our e-procurement system automatically assigns product codes to each tile SKU. The supplier changed their product code for the same tile (new packaging), and our system created a duplicate entry. We now have two product codes for identical tiles at different prices. How do we prevent this?
**A:** This is a master data management (MDM) issue common in large procurement systems. Prevention: (1) IMPLEMENT A GOLDEN RECORD SYSTEM — assign internal product codes that are independent of supplier codes; map supplier codes to your internal codes; when a supplier changes their code, only the mapping changes, not the master record; (2) SUPPLIER MASTER DATA AGREEMENT — contractually require the supplier to notify you 90 days in advance of any product code changes; (3) DUPLICATE DETECTION — implement fuzzy matching algorithms that flag potential duplicates based on product description, dimensions, and specification; (4) REGULAR AUDIT — quarterly review of all active SKUs to identify duplicates and price discrepancies; (5) ATTRIBUTE-BASED CODING — use a coding structure based on product attributes (material, size, colour, finish) rather than supplier codes. For the immediate problem: conduct a full SKU rationalisation; identify all duplicates; benchmark the two prices against market; lock the lower price for all future orders; update the master data record. Consider implementing a Product Information Management (PIM) system if SKU count exceeds 1,000 — manual management becomes unmanageable at scale.

## 25. LEGAL, REGULATORY & DISPUTE

**Q:** 106 Our tiling contractor has placed a lien on our store, refusing to allow access until final payment is made. We dispute the final account (defective work). What does the law say about construction liens in South Africa?
**A:** South African common law recognises the contractor's right of retention (ius retentionis or lien) over the property until payment is received. However: (1) the lien only applies to the contractor's own work — they cannot lien the entire store for a tiling dispute; (2) the lien is lost if the contractor voluntarily surrenders possession (they must maintain physical control); (3) the lien cannot be used to enforce an undisputed amount — if there is a genuine dispute over defective work, the contractor must resolve the dispute or accept the reduced payment; (4) the contractor cannot prevent access by changing locks or using force — this constitutes trespass or criminal intimidation. Your response: (1) engage legal counsel immediately; (2) if the contractor is occupying your premises unlawfully, apply for an eviction order under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction Act; (3) withhold the disputed amount but pay any undisputed portion — this weakens the contractor's lien argument; (4) document the defects with photographs and independent assessment; (5) if the contractor has valid security of payment rights under the contract (e.g., JBCC payment certificate system), follow the contractual dispute resolution procedure. Do NOT attempt forced entry without legal clearance — this escalates to criminal charges.

**Q:** 107 Our franchise agreement contains a "sole supplier" clause for tiles. The Competition Commission has announced an investigation into anti-competitive practices in the building materials sector. Could our sole supplier arrangement be challenged?
**A:** Potentially yes. Section 5 of the Competition Act 89 of 1998 prohibits exclusive dealing arrangements that substantially prevent or lessen competition. A franchise sole supplier clause: (1) is NOT automatically illegal — the Competition Tribunal recognises that franchise systems require brand consistency; (2) may be challenged if the sole supplier arrangement forecloses competitors from the market, particularly if the franchisor receives rebates or benefits from the nominated supplier; (3) is more vulnerable if the supplier charges above-market prices and franchisees cannot source alternatives. The Competition Commission's 2023-2024 focus on building materials (cement, steel, timber) may extend to tiling. To protect yourself: (1) ensure the nominated supplier was selected through competitive tender, not favouritism; (2) ensure the arrangement is disclosed in the franchise disclosure document (required under CPA Section 7(2)); (3) allow franchisees to request alternative suppliers if the nominated supplier cannot meet specification, price, or delivery requirements; (4) do not receive secret rebates — disclose any financial benefits from the supplier arrangement. The Competition Act allows for administrative penalties up to 10% of annual turnover for anti-competitive conduct.

**Q:** 108 Our tiling contractor was found to be employing undocumented foreign workers (from Mozambique and Zimbabwe). As the client, what is our legal exposure under the Immigration Act?
**A:** The Immigration Act 13 of 2002 (as amended) criminalises the employment of illegal foreigners. Section 38 makes it an offence for any person to "employ an illegal foreigner." As the CLIENT, you are not the employer — the contractor is. However: (1) if you KNOWINGLY contracted a contractor who employs illegal workers, you may face charges of aiding and abetting; (2) if the contractor is deemed your "employee" under labour law (the dominant impression test), you may be deemed the employer; (3) OHSA makes you responsible for site safety regardless of who employs the workers — if undocumented workers are injured, you face liability; (4) the contractor's B-BBEE status may be revoked if found to be engaging in illegal labour practices (fronting investigation trigger). Immediate action: (1) terminate the contractor's appointment if the illegal employment is confirmed; (2) report to the Department of Home Affairs; (3) verify replacement contractors' labour compliance before appointment; (4) include contractual warranties that all workers are legally documented. The contractor faces: fines, imprisonment, and deportation of workers. Your brand reputation risk is significant if media reports illegal labour in your stores.

**Q:** 109 Our store's tiled floor was damaged during a protest march (service delivery protest in the area). The mob threw stones and set a bin alight on our doorstep, cracking the tiles with the heat. Our insurance policy excludes "damage arising from civil commotion, riot, or insurrection." Is this exclusion enforceable, and do we have any other recourse?
**A:** The exclusion is likely enforceable if clearly stated in the policy. "Civil commotion" and "riot" are standard insurance exclusions in South Africa due to the high frequency of protest-related damage. However: (1) check if the damage was caused by FIRE — fire following riot may be covered under the fire section even if riot is excluded; (2) check for SASRIA cover — the South African Special Risks Insurance Association provides cover for politically motivated damage, riots, strikes, and terrorism. SASRIA is compulsory for all commercial policies in South Africa — your broker should have arranged it. If you have SASRIA cover (check your policy schedule), claim through them; (3) if neither insurance nor SASRIA applies, you have a civil claim against the identified perpetrators — practically difficult unless individuals are arrested and solvent; (4) the Riotous Assemblies Act and Trespass Act provide criminal sanctions but not compensation. Prevention: many retailers in high-protest areas now specify impact-resistant tiles, remove loose objects from storefronts, and install security shutters. Update your insurance: ensure SASRIA premiums are current — many businesses discovered they were not covered during the July 2021 unrest.

**Q:** 110 Our landlord has issued a compliance notice requiring us to remove our floor tiles and reinstate the original screed at lease end. The lease says we must "make good." We installed R800,000 of tiles. Do we really have to rip them out?
**A:** "Make good" clauses are interpreted based on the specific lease wording and the nature of the landlord's property. Generally: (1) if the lease requires reinstatement of the original condition, you may be obligated to remove tenant improvements; (2) however, many landlords will accept the improvements in lieu of reinstatement if they add value — negotiate a surrender payment or simply leave the tiles (the landlord may prefer them); (3) if the landlord genuinely wants reinstatement, they must give reasonable notice and the cost of removal is yours; (4) the Consumer Protection Act may limit "make good" clauses if they are unfair — but the CPA primarily protects consumers, not commercial tenants. Check your lease: (1) does it define "make good" specifically? (2) is there a "surrender in lieu" clause? (3) does the landlord have the right to accept improvements at a valuation? Practical approach: negotiate with the landlord — most commercial landlords prefer quality improvements to remain. If removal is required, the landlord cannot claim both reinstatement AND payment for the value of the improvements. Document all negotiations. The R800,000 should have been depreciated over the lease term in your accounts — any remaining book value is a loss on disposal.

## 26. HERITAGE, CULTURAL & COMMUNITY

**Q:** 111 We are building a store on land that was historically a sacred site for the local Khoi-San community. During site preparation, workers discovered stone tools and ochre fragments in the subfloor. What are our obligations under the National Heritage Resources Act?
**A:** Section 35 of the National Heritage Resources Act 25 of 1999 requires that ANY discovery of archaeological or palaeontological material must be reported to the responsible heritage authority (SAHRA for archaeological material; DMR for fossils) immediately. You must: (1) STOP all work in the affected area immediately; (2) secure the find — do not disturb or remove artefacts; (3) notify SAHRA within 24 hours — they will assess and may issue a permit for continued work with conditions, or require a full archaeological investigation; (4) engage a SAHRA-accredited archaeologist to conduct a Heritage Impact Assessment if required; (5) budget for delays — archaeological investigations typically take 3-6 months. The Khoi-San community may also have rights under the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act — engage with the local traditional council. Failure to report archaeological finds is a criminal offence under Section 189 (fine up to R1 million or imprisonment up to 10 years). For future developments: conduct a pre-construction heritage survey (archaeological walkover) before breaking ground — this identifies risks early and allows planning.

**Q:** 112 Our store in a township is subject to the Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act requirements for municipal service charges. We have been billed "refuse removal" charges for construction debris from our tiling installation, even though we used a private waste contractor. Can the municipality charge us twice?
**A:** No — the municipality cannot charge for refuse removal if you did not use their service, provided your private contractor is licensed and lawfully disposed of the waste. However: (1) some municipalities levy a "availability charge" for refuse removal regardless of use — check your municipal bylaws; (2) if the charge is based on property rates and not usage, it may be a non-negotiable municipal levy; (3) you must provide proof that private waste removal was used (waste manifest, disposal certificate from licensed facility). Challenge the charge: (1) write to the municipal billing department with your waste disposal certificates attached; (2) request reversal of the charge; (3) if refused, lodge a dispute in terms of the Municipal Finance Management Act Section 96; (4) escalate to the provincial MEC for local government if unresolved. Document everything — the PFMA requires municipalities to justify all charges. For future projects: notify the municipality in writing before construction that private waste removal will be used, and request confirmation that no municipal refuse charges will apply.

**Q:** 113 Our store in a rural Eastern Cape location requires tiling work. The local traditional council (inkosi) has demanded a "community development levy" of R50,000 before work can begin. Is this a legitimate charge, and what is its legal basis?
**A:** This is a complex issue of customary law vs. statutory law. Under the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003, traditional councils have certain powers over communal land, but they cannot levy arbitrary charges on commercial operations on privately owned or municipally zoned land. Assessment: (1) is your store on communal land (under traditional council jurisdiction) or privately owned/leased land? If the latter, the council has no legal authority to levy charges; (2) if the land is communal, the council may have legitimate authority to impose conditions; (3) even on private land, community engagement may be prudent for social licence to operate. The R50,000 demand: (1) if it is a legitimate community benefit sharing agreement (e.g., local employment, local procurement), it may be justifiable as part of your SED (Socio-Economic Development) spend under B-BBEE; (2) if it is an arbitrary extraction with no benefit in return, it may constitute extortion. Approach: (1) verify the legal status of the land; (2) engage the council formally through a written meeting; (3) negotiate a structured community benefit (local employment, training, or infrastructure contribution) rather than a cash payment; (4) document any payment as SED spend for B-BBEE purposes; (5) if the demand is clearly extortionate, report to the SAPS and engage legal counsel. Be aware that challenging traditional authority can affect community relations — balance legal rights with practical community engagement.

## 27. EMPLOYEE, LABOUR & OHSA

**Q:** 114 Our tiling contractor's employees are working in our store during trading hours. A customer has complained that the contractors are not wearing uniforms and look "unprofessional." Our brand standards require all on-site personnel to wear branded attire. Can we enforce this on a contractor's employees?
**A:** Yes, through your contract. As the client, you can specify dress code and appearance standards for all personnel working on your premises — this is a standard occupational health and safety and brand protection requirement. The contractor's employees, while employed by the contractor, are working in YOUR controlled environment and represent YOUR brand to customers. Your contract should include: (1) a code of conduct for contractor personnel including dress code, hygiene, and behaviour standards; (2) the right to refuse access to any contractor employee who does not comply; (3) a requirement for branded PPE or uniforms (which you may provide or specify); (4) the right to require removal of any employee for misconduct. This is not an employment law issue — you are not employing the workers, you are setting conditions for access to your premises. The contractor must ensure compliance or face penalties, suspension, or termination. Note: if you specify branded uniforms, consider whether this creates a "dominant impression" of employment — if workers wear your logo exclusively, they may be deemed your employees for labour law purposes. Use joint branding (contractor logo + your logo) or specify "client site attire" rather than your full uniform.

**Q:** 115 A contractor's employee was injured when a tile stack collapsed while they were working alone in our store after hours. The contractor had no first aid kit on site. What are our liabilities as the occupier under OHSA?
**A:** As the occupier of the premises, you have duties under OHSA Section 8 to maintain a safe environment for all persons on your premises, including contractors. The contractor also has duties under OHSA as the employer. Liabilities: (1) OCCUPIER LIABILITY — if the tile stack was your property and was inherently unstable, or if you knew of the hazard and did not warn the contractor, you may be liable; (2) CONTRACTOR LIABILITY — the contractor failed to provide first aid (OHSA General Safety Regulations Regulation 3), failed to supervise the employee, and failed to conduct a risk assessment — these are clear OHSA violations; (3) JOINT LIABILITY — both parties may share liability depending on the facts. Immediate actions: (1) report the incident to the Department of Labour within the prescribed timeframe (serious injuries must be reported within 7 days); (2) preserve the scene for investigation; (3) provide the injured worker with access to medical treatment; (4) review your contractor management programme — require proof of OHSA compliance, first aid kits, and supervision plans before work commences. The contractor faces criminal charges under OHSA for the first aid violation (fine up to R100,000 or 2 years imprisonment). Your insurance (public liability) should cover occupier liability claims.

**Q:** 116 Our cleaning staff use a commercial steam cleaner (150°C) on our tiled floors daily. The grout has started to crumble. We specified the grout but the cleaning company (contracted) chose the cleaning method. Who is responsible for the damage?
**A:** Responsibility depends on the contract terms and specification clarity. If YOU specified the grout without reference to cleaning methods, and the cleaning company used industry-standard equipment, there may be shared liability: (1) the GROUT may have been inadequate for the specified cleaning regime — if you required steam cleaning in the original specification, the grout should have been steam-resistant; (2) the CLEANING COMPANY may have exceeded recommended parameters (150°C is extremely high for daily use — most epoxy grouts are rated to 80-100°C regular exposure); (3) if the cleaning method was not discussed during specification, neither party considered it. Resolution: (1) test the grout to identify the failure mode; (2) review the cleaning company's method statement — did it specify 150°C? (3) check the grout manufacturer's technical data sheet for temperature limits; (4) if the grout was unsuitable for steam cleaning, the original tiling contractor may be liable for defective specification; (5) if the cleaning company exceeded manufacturer's recommendations, they are liable. For future: include the cleaning method (steam temperature, pressure, frequency) in the flooring specification; select grout rated for the actual cleaning regime; provide cleaning method training to all facilities staff.

## 28. TECHNICAL, WARRANTY & DEFECTS

**Q:** 117 Our large-format tiles (1200×600mm) have developed "lipping" — height differences between adjacent tiles — of up to 3mm. The tile supplier blames the adhesive; the adhesive supplier blames the substrate; the contractor blames the tiles (inconsistent thickness). How do we determine liability?
**A:** This requires independent forensic investigation. Lipping in large-format tiles (LFT) can result from multiple causes: (1) TILE THICKNESS VARIATION — measure tile thickness at multiple points across 10 random tiles using digital calipers. SANS 1449 allows ±5% thickness variation, but for rectified LFT, tighter tolerances are expected. Variation >0.5mm across a single tile causes lipping regardless of installation quality; (2) SUBSTRATE FLATNESS — measure substrate deviation with a 2m straightedge. For tiles >600mm, substrate must be flat to ±3mm under a 2m straightedge (conforming to SANS 10109). If the substrate exceeds this, the adhesive bed cannot compensate; (3) ADHESIVE APPLICATION — check for voids (hollow-sounding tiles), notched trowel pattern (should be correct size for tile format), and whether back-buttering was used (essential for LFT); (4) INSTALLER TECHNIQUE — was a levelling system used (clips/wedges)? LFT should not be installed without a levelling system. Engage a registered professional engineer or the Tile Council of South Africa (TCSA) for independent assessment. Document everything with photographs and measurements. The party responsible for the defective element bears the remediation cost — but recovery depends on your contract terms with each party.

**Q:** 118 Our tile supplier has provided a 10-year product warranty but specifies that claims must be made within 30 days of discovering the defect. We discovered a batch defect 18 months after installation in a store that was not yet open to the public. Is the 30-day claim period enforceable?
**A:** Under the Consumer Protection Act Section 55, goods must be reasonably durable and free of defects. A 30-day claim period for a 10-year warranty is likely unenforceable against a consumer — the CPA deems such terms unfair, unreasonable, or unjust (Section 48). For B2B contracts, the position is less clear — courts may uphold contractual time limits if they are reasonable and clearly communicated. However: (1) 30 days is unreasonably short for a defect that manifests gradually or in a store not yet operational; (2) if the defect is a latent manufacturing defect (not visible on installation), you could not reasonably have discovered it within 30 days; (3) the CPA's 6-month implied warranty (Section 56) overrides any shorter contractual period for consumer transactions. Your argument: the claim period starts from when the defect was DISCOVERED or should reasonably have been discovered, not from installation. If the store was unoccupied, discovery was delayed. Challenge the 30-day clause as an unfair contract term under CPA Section 48. For future contracts: negotiate a "reasonable time after discovery" claim period, minimum 12 months from discovery. The NCR (National Consumer Regulator) has issued guidance that unreasonably short claim periods are presumptively unfair.

**Q:** 119 Our store's tiles make a "hollow" sound when tapped, indicating voids under the tiles (lack of adhesive contact). The contractor claims this is "normal for large format tiles" and doesn't affect performance. Is this correct?
**A:** No — hollow-sounding tiles indicate inadequate adhesive bedding and ARE a defect. SANS 10107 and manufacturer specifications require full bedding (minimum 65% adhesive contact for walls, 80% for floors in dry areas, 95%+ in wet areas). Hollow voids: (1) create weakness under point loads — the tile can crack; (2) allow moisture ingress in wet areas; (3) cause the tile to sound different (hollow) which is itself a quality issue; (4) may indicate the contractor used an incorrect trowel notch size, incorrect adhesive, or did not back-butter large format tiles. The "hollow sound is normal" claim is false. Test method: ASTM D4541 (pull-off test) measures actual adhesion strength. If adhesion is below 0.5MPa for floor tiles, the installation is defective. Rectification: the affected tiles must be removed and reinstalled with proper adhesive application. For large format tiles (>900mm on any side), back-buttering (applying adhesive to both substrate and tile back) is mandatory per SANS 10109. Do not accept the contractor's explanation — demand independent testing.

**Q:** 120 Our tile expansion joints have filled with hard debris (sand, grit, small stones) over 5 years of store operation. The tiles are now showing stress cracks at the joints. Is this a maintenance issue or an installation defect?
**A:** This is a maintenance issue, not an installation defect. Expansion/movement joints are designed to compress and expand — they must be kept clean and free of rigid debris to function. When joints fill with hard material, they lose their compressibility. Thermal and structural movement then transfers stress to the tiles, causing cracking at the joint edges. Responsibility: (1) the FACILITIES MANAGER / STORE OPERATIONS team is responsible for routine joint maintenance — joints should be inspected quarterly and cleaned/resealed annually; (2) the original installer is NOT responsible for maintenance failures years after handover; (3) the tile supplier is not responsible. Remediation: (1) remove all debris from affected joints; (2) inspect joint depth — if debris has compressed the joint filler below the required depth, the joint must be re-cut and re-filled; (3) replace cracked tiles adjacent to the joints; (4) implement a joint maintenance schedule in your store maintenance manual. For future: specify recessed joint covers that prevent debris ingress; or specify flush compression seals rather than open joints in high-traffic retail areas.

## 29. NEIGHBOUR & LANDLORD DISPUTES

**Q:** 121 Our tiling contractor started work at 6:30 AM. The neighbouring tenant (a medical clinic) has complained that the noise violates the mall's operational rules (trading hours start at 9 AM). The mall manager has threatened to stop work. Who is correct?
**A:** Mall operational rules typically restrict construction noise to before and after trading hours, but the specific hours vary by mall. Standard mall rules: (1) construction work is permitted from 6:00 PM to 8:00 AM (after-hours) OR from 6:00 AM to 9:00 AM (early morning) depending on the mall; (2) NO noisy work during trading hours (9 AM – 9 PM); (3) the tenant (you) is responsible for your contractor's compliance with mall rules. If your mall rules specify no work before 9 AM, the clinic is correct and the mall manager can enforce the rules. Check: (1) your lease annexure specifying construction hours; (2) the mall's tenant handbook or rules; (3) whether you obtained prior written approval for the work schedule. Even if the mall allows early work, the municipal noise bylaw may restrict construction noise before 7 AM — check your local municipality's Environmental Management Bylaw. Johannesburg and Cape Town both regulate construction noise hours. Resolution: reschedule noisy work (cutting, demolition) to approved hours; request a variance from the mall manager with specific justification; offer to notify neighbouring tenants of the schedule in advance.

**Q:** 122 Our upstairs neighbour (another tenant) has a water leak that has stained our ceiling tiles and damaged our floor tiles below. Our landlord says this is a "tenant-to-tenant issue" and refuses to intervene. Who is responsible for repairs?
**A:** The landlord IS responsible. Under the lease and common law: (1) the landlord must maintain the structural elements of the building, including waterproofing between floors; (2) the upstairs tenant may be liable if their negligence caused the leak (e.g., overflowing equipment), but the building envelope is the landlord's responsibility; (3) your landlord cannot simply declare it a "tenant-to-tenant issue" — they have a contractual obligation to provide premises fit for purpose. Your course of action: (1) notify the landlord in WRITING of the leak and damage, with photographs; (2) request immediate repair of the source (upstairs) and remediation of your damage; (3) if the landlord refuses, you have the right to: withhold rent (do this cautiously and with legal advice); arrange repairs yourself and deduct from rent (only if the lease permits or if the landlord has been given reasonable time to act); claim damages for loss of use. The landlord can recover from the upstairs tenant if negligence is proven, but that is their problem, not yours. If the landlord is a body corporate in a sectional title scheme, Section 37 of the Sectional Titles Act makes them responsible for maintenance of common property and structural elements.

**Q:** 123 Our landlord has refused to allow our tiling contractor to use the mall's loading dock, claiming it's reserved for "major deliveries only." Our tiles must come through the front entrance, which is disruptive. Does the landlord have this right?
**A:** Check your lease: (1) does it include a right to use common facilities including loading docks for fit-out and maintenance? (2) does the landlord's refusal contravene the "quiet enjoyment" clause? Most commercial leases grant tenants reasonable access to service areas for fit-out, maintenance, and delivery purposes. However, the landlord can impose reasonable conditions (time restrictions, prior booking, size limits). If your lease grants loading dock access, the landlord cannot arbitrarily refuse. If the lease is silent, the landlord's position is stronger but still subject to the principle of reasonable use of common property. Approach: (1) cite the lease clause granting access; (2) offer to schedule deliveries at approved times; (3) if the landlord persists, escalate to the mall's centre manager or regional property manager; (4) if this is a genuine obstruction affecting your ability to trade, it may constitute a breach of lease giving you the right to claim damages. For future leases: negotiate explicit loading dock access rights for fit-out and maintenance, including after-hours access if needed.

## 30. RURAL, TRIBAL & SPECIAL JURISDICTION

**Q:** 124 We are tiling a new store on tribal land in rural KwaZulu-Natal. The lease is with the tribal council, not a formal landlord. The inkosi has verbally approved the work but insists his "induna" must supervise all construction. What legal protections do we have if something goes wrong?
**A:** Construction on tribal land in KZN involves complex legal frameworks: the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act, the KwaZulu-Natal Traditional Leadership and Governance Act, and the Communal Land Rights Act (ACT 11 of 2004). Key risks: (1) VERBAL APPROVALS are unenforceable — insist on written authority from the inkosi AND the tribal council; (2) without formal land tenure documentation, eviction or work stoppage is a real risk; (3) the induna's "supervision" does not create professional liability — they have no engineering or construction qualifications. Protect yourself: (1) obtain a formal resolution from the tribal council authorising the work; (2) ensure the lease or permission to occupy (PTO) is registered with the Department of Land Affairs; (3) engage a local attorney familiar with customary law to formalise the arrangement; (4) require the tribal council to warrant that they have authority to grant the lease (some land may be under competing claims); (5) obtain construction insurance that covers work on non-freehold land; (6) do not pay "facilitation fees" to individuals — any community benefit should be formalised and documented. The Ingonyama Trust controls much land in KZN — verify whether the land falls under Trust jurisdiction, which requires separate approvals.

**Q:** 125 Our Eastern Cape store is in a former homeland area where the municipality has limited capacity. Building plans submitted 8 months ago have not been approved. We have a contractual store opening deadline. Can we start work without approved plans?
**A:** Absolutely not. Section 4 of the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977 makes it illegal to commence construction without approved plans. Penalties: the municipality can issue a stop-work order; fines; and require demolition of unauthorised work. In the former homeland areas, municipal capacity issues are well-documented but do not excuse non-compliance. Options: (1) escalate within the municipality — meet with the building control officer directly; (2) engage a professional architect or project manager who has relationships with the municipality; (3) if the delay is unreasonable (beyond the 30-60 day statutory review period), apply for a Section 4(2) certificate from the provincial government authorising construction pending approval; (4) consider appointing a private building surveyor if the municipality has accredited private sector involvement (some Eastern Cape municipalities have this arrangement); (5) engage the MEC for Human Settlements if the municipality is clearly dysfunctional. Do NOT start work without plans approval — the risk of stop-work and demolition far outweighs the delay cost. Document all escalation efforts for your landlord and franchisor.

**Q:** 126 Our contractor in a rural Limpopo town has completed the tiling work but cannot provide a tax invoice because they are not registered for tax. We need the invoice for our records. What are our options?
**A:** A non-registered taxpayer cannot issue a tax invoice (which includes VAT). However, they can and must issue a standard invoice for the work performed. Options: (1) accept a standard invoice (without VAT) — this is legally valid for accounting purposes, though you cannot claim input VAT; (2) if the contractor is an individual (not a company), they may need to register as a provisional taxpayer if their income exceeds the tax threshold — encourage them to register with SARS; (3) if they refuse to provide any invoice, withhold payment until they do — a written invoice is a legal requirement under the Tax Administration Act for proof of expenditure; (4) for amounts above R10,000, you need proper documentation to claim the expense for tax purposes. Note: if the contractor is a casual labourer (not running a business), payments may be subject to PAYE or SITE deductions. For future rural projects: specify in the contract that the contractor must provide valid invoices; consider using a labour broker or payroll service if dealing with informal workers; engage a local accountant to assist contractors with compliance.

## 31. INTERNATIONAL TRADE & IMPORT

**Q:** 127 Our imported Italian tiles have been held at Customs for 3 weeks due to an ITAC (International Trade Administration Commission) investigation into alleged dumping by Italian manufacturers. We have store openings scheduled. What is ITAC's process, and can we get our tiles released?
**A:** ITAC investigates dumping (selling below normal value) and subsidisation under the International Trade Administration Act 71 of 2002. If your tiles are from a manufacturer under investigation: (1) all imports from that manufacturer may be subject to provisional payments or security deposits pending the investigation outcome; (2) SARS will hold the shipment until the provisional duty is paid or the investigation is resolved; (3) ITAC investigations typically take 6-12 months; provisional measures can be imposed within 60 days. To secure release: (1) pay any provisional anti-dumping duty or security deposit required by SARS; (2) if you believe your supplier is not dumping, provide evidence to ITAC (cost of production data, market pricing); (3) engage a customs broker with ITAC experience; (4) consider requesting an exemption if the tiles are not like products to those under investigation (specification argument). The South African Ceramic Tile Industry has successfully pursued anti-dumping duties against several countries — check if Italy is specifically targeted or if your supplier is named. For critical path items: hold 3 months' safety stock in-country; diversify supplier base across multiple countries; include force majeure clauses covering trade remedy actions.

**Q:** 128 We imported tiles from China with a declared customs value of R85/m². SARS has reassessed the value to R140/m² based on their internal benchmarks, demanding additional customs duty and VAT of R280,000. We believe the declared value is correct. What is our recourse?
**A:** SARS has broad powers of customs valuation under the Customs and Excise Act. They can reject the transaction value if they have reason to believe it does not reflect the true value (e.g., related-party transaction, abnormally low pricing, or benchmarking against similar imports). Your recourse: (1) REQUEST A REASONS LETTER from SARS explaining the basis for the reassessment; (2) provide SUPPORTING DOCUMENTATION — purchase orders, supplier invoices, proof of payment, manufacturer's price list, evidence that the price is at arm's length; (3) FILE AN OBJECTION within the prescribed timeframe (typically 30 days from assessment) to the Customs Appeals Unit; (4) if the objection is rejected, appeal to the Tax Board or Tax Court; (5) consider applying for a binding customs ruling (Section 47 of the Customs Control Act) for future shipments to prevent recurrence. Common issues: (1) under-valuation to save duty is a criminal offence — ensure your declared value is accurate; (2) SARS often uses WTO customs valuation methods (transaction value, identical goods, similar goods, deductive value, computed value, fall-back); (3) if your supplier is a related party, SARS will scrutinise the transfer pricing. Engage a customs attorney or specialist broker — customs valuation disputes can take 12-24 months to resolve. The additional amount will accrue interest during the dispute.

**Q:** 129 Our tile shipment from Spain arrived with 15% of tiles broken due to inadequate packaging. The supplier claims this is "shipping risk" and our responsibility under FOB terms. Our marine insurance has a R25,000 excess. The damage is R40,000. What is our position?
**A:** Under FOB (Free On Board) Incoterms 2020: (1) risk transfers when goods pass the ship's rail at the port of shipment (Spain); (2) the seller is responsible for export clearance and loading; (3) the buyer (you) bears all risk from loading onward, including sea transit and unloading. However: (1) the seller must provide adequate packaging — if inadequate packaging caused the breakage, the seller breached their obligation regardless of risk transfer; (2) the carrier may be liable if handling was negligent — file a claim with the shipping line under the Hague-Visby Rules; (3) your marine cargo insurance should cover transit damage (subject to the R25,000 excess). Course of action: (1) document the damage immediately with photographs before unloading; (2) obtain a surveyor's report (insurance requirement); (3) claim against marine insurance for R15,000 (R40,000 minus R25,000 excess); (4) claim the R25,000 excess from the supplier for inadequate packaging; (5) if the carrier was negligent, subrogate your claim to the insurer. For future: negotiate CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight) terms where the seller arranges insurance; or DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) where the seller bears all risk until delivery. Specify packaging standards in the purchase order.

## 32. WARRANTY, RETENTION & FINAL ACCOUNT

**Q:** 130 Our tiling contract includes a 5% retention held for 12 months after practical completion. The 12 months expired, but we have discovered latent defects ( grout cracking) in 3 of the 50 stores completed under the contract. Can we withhold the full 5% retention for all 50 stores, or only for the defective 3?
**A:** Retention must be apportioned to the defective work only — you cannot withhold the entire retention for all stores due to defects in a minority. Under common law and standard JBCC provisions: (1) retention is security for the due performance of the contractor's obligations; (2) if defects appear, the employer can use the retention to fund repairs, but only to the extent of the repair cost; (3) the balance of the retention for defect-free work must be released on the due date; (4) you must give the contractor notice of defects and a reasonable opportunity to rectify before using retention funds. For the 3 defective stores: obtain repair quotes; notify the contractor; if they fail to rectify within the specified period, engage another contractor and deduct the cost from the retention. Release the balance of the retention for the 47 defect-free stores immediately. Unjustified withholding of retention may expose you to interest claims and legal costs. The CPA's fairness provisions (if applicable) reinforce this — retention must be reasonable and proportionate.

**Q:** 131 Our contractor has submitted a final account including R180,000 for "dayworks" (additional labour for unexpected substrate preparation) that were not pre-approved by our site agent. The contract requires written authorisation for all dayworks. Must we pay?
**A:** Under standard construction contract terms (JBCC, NEC, FIDIC), dayworks require prior authorisation to be claimable. The principle: the contractor cannot unilaterally incur additional cost and then claim it — the employer must have the opportunity to approve or refuse. However: (1) if the dayworks were genuinely necessary and the contractor could not reasonably have obtained prior approval (urgent safety issue, for example), an equitable claim may succeed; (2) if your site agent witnessed the work and did not object, there may be an implied authorisation; (3) if you benefited from the work (a better subfloor than specified), quantum meruit (reasonable value) may apply. Your position: (1) reject the dayworks claim due to lack of authorisation; (2) offer to pay only for dayworks that your site agent witnessed and acknowledged; (3) request detailed timesheets, labour records, and photographs for each daywork item; (4) if the contractor disputes, refer to the contract's dispute resolution mechanism (typically mediation or adjudication). For future: enforce the prior authorisation requirement strictly; ensure site agents understand their authority limits; implement a daily sign-off procedure for all extra work.

**Q:** 132 Our store's tiles failed within the NHBRC 5-year warranty period. However, the builder claims the failure was caused by our tenant's heavy equipment (reach truck), not defective construction. The NHBRC has declined the claim as "not a structural defect." What is our recourse?
**A:** The NHBRC 5-year warranty covers "major structural defects" — defined as defects that threaten the stability or safety of the structure. Tile failures are typically NOT structural defects unless they indicate a broader structural problem (slab movement, foundation failure). The NHBRC's decision is likely correct for a standalone tile issue. Your recourse: (1) against the TENANT — if the reach truck exceeded the floor's design load capacity, the tenant is liable for damage caused by overloading. Check your lease: does it specify maximum floor loads? Does it require tenant to notify you of heavy equipment? (2) against the BUILDER — if the floor was not designed for the intended use (retail with material handling equipment), the builder may be liable for defective design specification; (3) against the TILE SUPPLIER — if the tiles were not rated for the actual loads, product liability may apply; (4) INSURANCE — commercial policies may cover accidental damage. Commission a structural engineer to determine: the floor's design load; the actual loads imposed; whether the failure is due to overloading, inadequate design, or defective materials. This forensic evidence determines liability. For future: specify floor load ratings in all leases; require tenant disclosure of material handling equipment; install appropriate flooring in logistics areas.

## 33. ENVIRONMENTAL, ASBESTOS & HAZARDOUS MATERIALS

**Q:** 133 During store renovation, our contractor discovered asbestos-containing floor tiles (9×9 inch vinyl tiles, common in 1970s buildings). Work stopped immediately. Our landlord claims it's our responsibility as the tenant who initiated the renovation. Who is legally responsible for asbestos removal?
**A:** Under the Asbestos Regulations 2020 (promulgated under OHSA), the "employer" (the person who owns or controls the premises) is responsible for asbestos management. In a landlord-tenant relationship: (1) the LANDLORD is typically the "owner" and bears primary responsibility for asbestos identification, management, and removal; (2) the TENANT (you) has a duty not to disturb asbestos and to report its presence; (3) if the tenant's renovation work disturbed the asbestos, both parties may share liability. The Asbestos Regulations 2020 require: (1) an asbestos survey by a registered asbestos inspector before any demolition or renovation; (2) notification to the Department of Employment and Labour before removal; (3) removal only by licensed asbestos removal contractors; (4) disposal at licensed hazardous waste facilities. The landlord should have conducted an asbestos survey before leasing the premises — failure to disclose known asbestos is a breach of the lease and potentially a criminal offence. Do NOT allow unauthorised removal — penalties under the 2020 Regulations include fines up to R5 million and/or 5 years imprisonment. Demand the landlord engages a licensed asbestos removal contractor at their cost.

**Q:** 134 Our contractor removed vinyl tiles from our old store and disposed of them at a general landfill. We later discovered the tiles contained asbestos. What are our legal liabilities, and can we retrospectively remediate?
**A:** This is a serious environmental and criminal law issue. Disposing of asbestos-containing material at a general landfill violates: (1) the Asbestos Regulations 2020 — removal and disposal without a licence is a criminal offence; (2) the National Environmental Management: Waste Act — asbestos is classified as hazardous waste (HCRW) requiring disposal at licensed hazardous waste facilities; (3) the National Water Act — if asbestos fibres have leached into groundwater. Liabilities: (1) the CONTRACTOR (who performed the removal) faces criminal prosecution; (2) YOU (the client) may face liability if you directed or knowingly allowed illegal disposal; (3) the LANDFILL operator faces liability for accepting hazardous waste. Immediate action: (1) STOP all work; (2) report to the Department of Employment and Labour and Department of Forestry, Fisheries and Environment; (3) engage an environmental attorney; (4) commission an environmental risk assessment; (5) the landfill must be notified — they may need to excavate and relocate the material. Retrospective remediation is possible but expensive — excavation, re-containment, and transport to a licensed facility. Budget R50,000-R200,000 depending on quantity. Do not attempt to cover this up — environmental offences have no statute of limitations, and whistleblower protections encourage reporting.

**Q:** 135 Our new store's subfloor contains elevated moisture levels (95% RH) due to a failed damp-proof membrane. The tiling contractor installed tiles without a moisture barrier, and they are now lifting. Is this the contractor's fault, or should they have tested the subfloor first?
**A:** The contractor shares responsibility with the builder/developer. Under SANS 10109 (screeds and floors) and industry best practice: (1) the tiling contractor SHOULD have tested subfloor moisture before installation — this is standard due diligence; (2) the BUILDER is responsible for providing a subfloor that meets the specification (typically ≤75% RH or ≤5% moisture content for standard adhesives); (3) the SPECIFIER (architect or engineer) should have identified the need for a moisture barrier given the site conditions. Resolution: (1) commission a moisture test to confirm the source (rising damp, leaking pipe, or construction moisture); (2) if rising damp due to failed DPC, the builder is primarily liable; (3) if the contractor installed without testing despite visible damp signs, they share liability for negligence; (4) if the specification required a moisture barrier and the contractor omitted it, they are fully liable. Remediation: remove tiles; install a cementitious moisture barrier (e.g., Mapei EC1, Tilepac Dampfix) or epoxy DPM; reinstall with appropriate adhesive. Costs should be allocated based on forensic findings. For future: mandate subfloor moisture testing as a hold-point before tile installation.

## 34. BUILDING REGULATIONS & SANS COMPLIANCE

**Q:** 136 Our architect specified 600×600mm tiles for a fire escape stairwell. The building inspector has rejected this, stating that stair treads must have "nosing strips" and tiles are too slippery for fire escapes. Is this correct, and what does SANS 10400 require?
**A:** The building inspector is partially correct. SANS 10400 Part M (Stairways) and SANS 10114 (interior lighting and colour codes) regulate fire escape stairs. Requirements: (1) SLIP RESISTANCE — fire escape stair treads must have a slip-resistant surface (R11 or R12 rating per DIN 51130, or PTV ≥ 45 wet per SANS 10107); (2) NOSING — tread nosing must be clearly visible (contrast strip) and slip-resistant; (3) TILES ON STAIRS ARE PERMITTED but must meet specific requirements — full bedding, slip-resistant surface (anti-slip tiles or treatment), nosing strips (stainless steel or carborundum insert), and proper fixing to prevent loosening; (4) RISER-TREAD DIMENSIONS must comply with SANS 10400 Part M (riser 150-180mm, tread 250-355mm). The inspector's rejection of tiles per se is incorrect — tiles are permitted if they meet the performance requirements. Your architect should provide: the tile's slip resistance test certificate; a detail drawing showing the nosing treatment; specification for full bedding with flexible adhesive; and confirmation that the installation meets Part M. If the inspector persists, request a written notice citing the specific regulation, then appeal through the Building Control Officer or provincial regulator.

**Q:** 137 Our store's disabled parking bays have tactile paving indicators (truncated domes) at the transition from parking to walkway. The municipality's accessibility auditor has flagged these as "non-compliant with SANS 784." Our supplier provided a SANS certificate. What is the issue?
**A:** SANS 784:2012 (Edition 2) specifies the dimensions, spacing, and colour contrast requirements for tactile paving indicators. Common non-compliance issues: (1) COLOUR CONTRAST — the domes must provide "luminous contrast" of at least 30% with the surrounding surface (many suppliers provide grey-on-grey which fails); (2) DOME DIMENSIONS — truncated domes must be 4-5mm high, 20-25mm diameter at base, spaced 50-60mm centre-to-centre; (3) LAYOUT — domes must be aligned in a square grid pattern, not staggered or random; (4) INSTALLATION — domes must be flush with the surface (not raised proud or recessed); (5) LOCATION — must be positioned at all pedestrian crossing points, not just some. Check your supplier's SANS certificate: does it cover SANS 784:2012 specifically, or a different SANS standard? Many suppliers confuse SANS 784 (tactile indicators) with SANS 10107 (slip resistance). The certificate must be for the specific product and standard. If the certificate is valid, the issue is likely installation — engage the supplier's technical representative to inspect and advise. Non-compliance with accessibility standards can result in fines under the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act and municipal bylaws.

**Q:** 138 Our building plans were approved with a specific floor finish noted as "vinyl sheeting." We now want to change to porcelain tiles. Do we need to submit an amended plan, or can we make the change without approval?
**A:** A change from vinyl sheeting to porcelain tiles is a material alteration that requires amended plan approval in most cases. The reasons: (1) WEIGHT — porcelain tiles weigh 25-30kg/m² vs. vinyl at 2-3kg/m². The additional dead load must be checked against the slab's design capacity; (2) THICKNESS — tiles add 15-25mm to floor height, which may affect: door clearances, step heights (must comply with SANS 10400 Part M), disability access gradients, and ceiling heights; (3) ACOUSTICS — tiles have different impact sound transmission ratings — this may affect Part R (noise control) compliance in multi-storey buildings; (4) FIRE RATING — while porcelain is non-combustible, the adhesive and any underlay contribute to fire load. Process: submit a Section 4(1) amendment to the local authority building control officer with: structural engineer's confirmation that the slab can support the additional load; revised floor-to-ceiling heights; confirmation that step dimensions remain compliant; acoustic assessment if required. Some municipalities treat like-for-like finish changes as minor work not requiring approval, but vinyl-to-tile is NOT like-for-like. Do not proceed without approval — the building inspector can issue a contravention notice and require removal.

## 35. WARRANTY MANAGEMENT & LONG-TERM DURABILITY

**Q:** 139 Our 50-store rollout is complete. We need a systematic way to track warranty periods for each store's tiles, adhesive, grout, and installation. What is industry best practice for warranty management in a multi-site portfolio?
**A:** Best practice for multi-site warranty management: (1) WARRANTY REGISTER — maintain a central digital register (spreadsheet or CAFM system) with: store name, completion date, warranty expiry dates for each component (tiles, adhesive, grout, installation), supplier contact details, warranty terms, and claim procedures; (2) TRIGGER SYSTEM — set automated alerts 90 days before each warranty expiry to allow time for inspection and claims; (3) ANNUAL INSPECTION — conduct professional condition surveys of all stores annually, photographing all floors and documenting defects while still within warranty; (4) DOCUMENT RETENTION — keep all invoices, delivery notes, product data sheets, installation certificates, and photographic records for the warranty period plus 3 years; (5) SUPPLIER RELATIONSHIPS — maintain direct contact with supplier technical teams, not just sales representatives; (6) ESCALATION PROTOCOL — define the process for warranty claims: notification → inspection → independent assessment if disputed → repair or replacement → cost recovery. For a 200-store portfolio, consider engaging a facilities management company with multi-site warranty tracking capability. Budget 0.5-1% of construction cost annually for warranty management and proactive maintenance.

**Q:** 140 Our 5-year-old store's tiles show no visible defects but we want to replace them for a brand refresh. The supplier's warranty says it only covers "defective tiles" and not "aesthetic replacement." Can we claim any warranty credit toward the new tiles?
**A:** No. A warranty covers defects in materials and workmanship — it does not provide credit for voluntary replacement. If the tiles are performing as specified and show no defects, there is no warranty claim. The warranty is not an insurance policy or a depreciation fund. Your options for the brand refresh: (1) remove and dispose of the old tiles (consider recycling); (2) negotiate a trade-in or recycling programme with the supplier — some manufacturers offer discounts for returning old tiles for recycling; (3) explore whether the old tiles can be reused in lower-visibility applications (storerooms, back-of-house); (4) claim the replacement cost as a capital expense for tax purposes (section 12C wear and tear or section 12B if energy-efficient). The only exception: if the old tiles have a latent defect not yet visible (e.g., expected adhesive failure due to known batch issue), you may have a claim. Commission an independent condition assessment — if defects are found, claim under warranty before removing the tiles. Once removed, the evidence is gone.

**Q:** 141 Our store's tiles were installed with a "25-year warranty" from the manufacturer. After 8 years, the manufacturer has gone into liquidation. The tiles are failing (glaze crazing). Do we have any recourse?
**A:** Unfortunately, a manufacturer's warranty is only as good as the manufacturer's solvency. Once liquidated, the company ceases to exist and warranty claims become unsecured creditor claims — you will likely receive nothing. Your recourse: (1) check if the manufacturer had product liability insurance — the insurance may still respond to claims even in liquidation; (2) check if the distributor (who sold you the tiles) has any continuing obligation — distributors sometimes offer their own warranty or may have a relationship with the liquidator; (3) check your own insurance — some commercial policies include "defective title" or "supplier insolvency" coverage; (4) if the tiles were defective at the time of manufacture (latent defect), you may have a claim against the manufacturer's directors if they traded recklessly (Section 22 of the Companies Act) — but this is difficult to prove. Prevention for future: (1) require warranty bonds or insurance-backed warranties for large orders; (2) spread risk across multiple suppliers; (3) specify that warranties must be backed by an insurance policy or performance guarantee; (4) for critical applications, choose established manufacturers with strong balance sheets. A 25-year warranty from a small manufacturer is often meaningless — assess the warranty provider's financial strength, not just the duration.

**Q:** 142 Our 3-year-old tiles show efflorescence (white powdery deposits) on the surface. The contractor says this is "normal and will wash away." It's been 6 months and it's getting worse. What is the actual cause, and who is responsible?
**A:** Persistent efflorescence is NOT normal and indicates an underlying moisture problem. Efflorescence occurs when moisture dissolves soluble salts in the substrate or adhesive and deposits them on the tile surface as the moisture evaporates. Causes: (1) INADEQUATE DAMP-PROOF COURSE — rising damp from the subgrade brings salts upward; (2) WET SUBFLOOR — tiles installed over a subfloor that had not dried sufficiently (new concrete screeds need 1mm/day drying time, minimum 6 weeks); (3) LEAKING PIPES — water ingress from plumbing under the slab; (4) EXTERNAL WATER INGRESS — poor waterproofing at building perimeter. The contractor is responsible if: (1) they installed over a wet subfloor without testing; (2) they used excessive water in the adhesive; (3) they failed to install a moisture barrier where needed. The builder is responsible if: (1) the DPC is absent or failed; (2) the screed was not adequately cured. Remediation: (1) identify and fix the moisture source (plumber, waterproofing contractor); (2) once dry, clean efflorescence with a dry brush or specialised efflorescence remover (acid-based, follow safety precautions); (3) seal the grout joints with a penetrating sealer to prevent recurrence. If the source is not fixed, efflorescence will return regardless of cleaning.

## 36. RENOVATION, REFURBISHMENT & TENANT IMPROVEMENT

**Q:** 143 Our lease requires us to "make good" at exit, removing all tenant improvements. We installed expensive porcelain tiles. The landlord wants us to remove them and restore the original screed. We believe the tiles add value and the landlord should accept them. Who is correct?
**A:** The lease governs. If the lease explicitly requires removal and reinstatement of the original condition, you are contractually obligated regardless of the tiles' value. However: (1) NEGOTIATE — most landlords prefer quality improvements to remain; offer to leave the tiles in exchange for reduced make-good obligations elsewhere; (2) MARKET VALUE — if the landlord genuinely wants removal, they cannot also claim the value of the tiles (you own them until removal); (3) SALVAGE — you can remove and reuse the tiles elsewhere (difficult without breakage, but possible with professional removal); (4) the cost of removal and reinstatement is yours — budget for this at the outset of the lease. The Consumer Protection Act may protect consumers against unfair make-good clauses, but commercial leases are generally enforced as written. Best practice: negotiate a "surrender in lieu" clause at lease commencement that specifies the landlord's option to accept improvements at a predetermined valuation. For a 10-year lease with mid-term refresh, factor removal costs into your capital planning from day one.

**Q:** 144 Our store needs a mid-life refresh after 5 years. The original tiles are in good condition but we want a new look. Can we install new tiles directly over the old ones, or must we remove and start fresh?
**A:** Overlaying is possible but carries risks. Requirements for tiling over existing tiles: (1) EXISTING TILES must be firmly bonded (test with a sounding hammer — no hollow areas); (2) SURFACE PREPARATION — existing tiles must be thoroughly cleaned, degreased, and scarified (mechanically abraded) to create a bondable surface; (3) PRIMER — apply a bonding primer designed for tiling over tiles (e.g., epoxy primer or SBR-based primer); (4) ADHESIVE — use a high-performance flexible adhesive (S1 or S2 classification); (5) HEIGHT — the overlay adds 15-25mm, which may affect door clearances, transitions, and fixtures; (6) WEIGHT — additional load of 20-30kg/m² must be within slab capacity. Risks: (1) if the original tiles were poorly bonded, the overlay will fail; (2) height issues are common (doors need trimming, thresholds need adjustment); (3) most adhesive manufacturers will not warranty overlay unless their specific primer system is used. Alternative: remove old tiles — allows inspection of the substrate, ensures full warranty, and addresses any underlying issues. Cost difference: overlay typically saves 30-40% on labour but may void warranties and reduce floor lifespan. Get written confirmation from the adhesive manufacturer that they warranty the overlay system.

**Q:** 145 Our franchise agreement mandates store refreshes every 5 years. The original tiles were specified by the franchisor and are still in perfect condition. Can the franchisor force us to replace perfectly good tiles just for a brand refresh?
**A:** If the franchise agreement explicitly mandates replacement of all finishes at 5-year refresh cycles, then yes — this is a contractual obligation you agreed to. However, review the agreement: (1) does it specifically require tile replacement, or just "refresh to current brand standards"? (2) if current brand standards still use the same tile, no replacement is needed; (3) does the agreement allow for "like-for-like" replacement if the original materials are in good condition? (4) is there a cost-sharing mechanism for refreshes (franchisor contribution, marketing fund, or franchisee sole funding)? The CPA Section 48 fairness test may apply — a requirement to destroy valuable assets without compensation may be challenged as unfair. However, brand consistency is a legitimate franchise purpose, and courts generally uphold reasonable system standards. Practical approach: (1) obtain a professional condition assessment of the tiles; (2) if they are genuinely in excellent condition, present evidence to the franchisor and request exemption or cost-sharing; (3) if the franchisor insists, negotiate a deferred replacement timeline or partial refresh (only customer-facing areas). For future franchisees: clarify refresh obligations before signing — some franchisors fund 50% of refresh costs.

## 37. INSURANCE CLAIMS & LOSS ADJUSTING

**Q:** 146 Our store's tiles were damaged when a delivery truck's refrigeration unit leaked glycol onto the floor. The trucking company's insurance says this is "chemical damage" excluded under their CMR liability. Our insurer says it's a "gradually operating cause" and won't pay either. We're caught in the middle. What do we do?
**A:** This is a classic insurance gap. The trucking company's CMR (Convention on the Contract for the International Carriage of Goods by Road) liability typically excludes chemical damage if not caused by their negligence. Your insurer's "gradually operating cause" exclusion applies to slow deterioration, not sudden spills. Your position: (1) the glycol spill was a SUDDEN, ACCIDENTAL event — not a gradually operating cause. Challenge your insurer's denial with a formal complaint; (2) the trucking company was negligent in failing to maintain their refrigeration unit — their public liability (not CMR) insurance should respond; (3) if both insurers refuse, escalate to the Ombudsman for Short-Term Insurance — they have ruled in favour of policyholders in similar "gap" scenarios; (4) you have a direct civil claim against the trucking company for negligence. Document: the spill event (photographs, incident report); the trucking company's maintenance records (subpoena if necessary); the tile damage assessment (independent surveyor). Prevention: specify spill containment requirements in all delivery contracts; require delivery vehicles to carry spill kits; specify delivery area flooring that withstands common transport chemicals.

**Q:** 147 Our store experienced a minor earthquake (tremor) in the Ceres area. Three tiles cracked along a structural movement joint. Our insurer's geotechnical engineer says the tremor was "below design seismic loading" and the cracking indicates pre-existing defects. Our structural engineer disagrees. How is this resolved?
**A:** This is an expert dispute that requires resolution through the insurance policy's dispute mechanism or independent arbitration. Process: (1) your policy likely contains an "independent expert" clause — jointly appoint a third geotechnical engineer to assess the actual ground motion vs. design parameters; (2) review the building's seismic design records — what seismic zone and loading was the building designed for? (3) inspect the crack pattern — cracks following a movement joint suggest the joint was inadequate or the joint material failed, which is a design/installation issue; (4) test the tiles for latent defects (thermal shock resistance, conformity to SANS 1449). If the independent expert finds the tremor was within design parameters, the insurer's position strengthens — the cracking was due to pre-existing defects or inadequate design. If the tremor exceeded design parameters, it may be an insured event. For the Western Cape (Ceres fault line): SANS 10160 specifies Zone 0 (very low seismicity) for most of the province, but the Ceres area has experienced significant tremors. Buildings constructed before modern seismic codes may not have any seismic design. If your building is pre-1985, the "below design loading" argument may not apply — there may have been no seismic design at all.

**Q:** 148 Our store's tiles were scratched by a contractor's metal scaffold during HVAC maintenance. The scaffolding contractor denies liability, saying their contract was with the landlord, not us. Our lease says the landlord can access our premises for maintenance. Who pays for the floor damage?
**A:** The landlord's right of access for maintenance typically includes an obligation to ensure work is carried out without damaging your property. The landlord's contractor damaged your floor during landlord-mandated work — the landlord is responsible. Their contractor's denial of liability to you is technically correct (privity of contract — you have no direct contract with them), but the landlord can and should claim against their contractor's insurance. Your course of action: (1) notify the landlord IN WRITING of the damage with photographs and repair quotes; (2) cite the lease clause requiring the landlord to make good any damage caused by their maintenance work; (3) if the landlord refuses, withhold the disputed amount from rent (with legal advice) or claim against your own insurance (if the cost justifies the claim and excess); (4) if your insurance pays, they will subrogate against the landlord. The scaffolding contractor IS liable to the landlord (their client) for negligent damage — the landlord must pursue this. Do not get drawn into a three-way dispute — your claim is against the landlord exclusively. If the lease makes you responsible for all damage regardless of cause, you may need to claim your own insurance. Review the lease's risk allocation clauses carefully.

## 38. NHBRC, BUILDING QUALITY & DEFECTS

**Q:** 149 Our new store is in a newly developed shopping centre. The developer enrolled the project with the NHBRC. After 18 months, the floor tiles are cracking due to structural settlement. The NHBRC inspector says this is a "major structural defect" and the developer must repair. The developer says it's "differential settlement within acceptable limits." What are the NHBRC's actual powers?
**A:** The NHBRC (National Home Builders Registration Council) has significant but limited powers: (1) the NHBRC Act provides a 5-year warranty against major structural defects; (2) "major structural defect" is defined as a defect that threatens the structural stability or safety of the building, or prevents continued habitation/use; (3) the NHBRC can issue compliance notices requiring the builder to rectify; (4) if the builder fails, the NHBRC can rectify using its warranty fund and recover from the builder; (5) the NHBRC can suspend or cancel a builder's registration. However: (1) the NHBRC's warranty applies to RESIDENTIAL construction primarily — commercial developments may be excluded or treated differently; (2) differential settlement of 15-25mm over 10m may be "acceptable" under geotechnical standards if it does not affect structural stability; (3) the NHBRC does not have powers over the architect or engineer — only the registered builder. For your tiles: if the settlement is within acceptable limits but the tile installation did not accommodate it (no movement joints, rigid adhesive), the tiling contractor may be liable for inadequate installation, not the builder. Commission a geotechnical report to measure actual settlement vs. design tolerances. If settlement exceeds tolerances, the NHBRC warranty should apply. If within tolerances, pursue the tiling contractor.

**Q:** 150 Our store was built by a developer who is not NHBRC-registered. We discovered this after occupancy. The tiles are failing due to subfloor defects. The developer has gone into liquidation. What is our recourse?
**A:** Without NHBRC registration, the 5-year NHBRC warranty does not apply. However, you still have recourse: (1) COMMON LAW — the developer owes you a duty of care to construct properly; you can sue for damages (negligent construction); (2) CONTRACT — your lease or purchase agreement with the developer includes implied warranties of quality; (3) DIRECTORS' LIABILITY — if the developer traded recklessly (knowing they were not NHBRC-registered), directors may be personally liable under Section 22 of the Companies Act; (4) PROFESSIONAL NEGLIGENCE — the architect or structural engineer who supervised construction may carry professional indemnity insurance; (5) YOUR INSURANCE — your commercial property insurance or latent defects insurance may cover the remediation. The NHBRC registration requirement applies to all "home builders" — if the developer constructed a building for commercial letting, the NHBRC requirement may not apply (the Act primarily targets residential). However, many commercial developers register voluntarily for credibility. Engage an attorney to trace the developer's assets and the directors' personal liability. Report unregistered residential building to the NHBRC — they can prosecute.

**Q:** 151 Our building's architect specified a suspended concrete slab for the first floor. The slab has excessive deflection (bounce), and the tiles on the upper floor are cracking. The structural engineer says the slab is "within design limits" for deflection. Why are the tiles cracking?
**A:** Excessive deflection causes tile cracking because: (1) tiles are rigid and cannot flex with the substrate — SANS 10109 allows maximum deflection of L/360 for tiled floors (where L = span length); (2) the slab may be "within design limits" for STRUCTURAL safety (L/250 or L/200) but EXCEEDS the deflection limits for brittle finishes like tiles; (3) the architect or engineer should have specified a deflection limit appropriate for the floor finish — L/360 is the minimum for tiles, L/480 is better; (4) the tile installation may have lacked movement joints to accommodate the flex. Resolution: (1) measure actual deflection vs. the SANS 10109 requirement for tiled floors (not just structural safety); (2) if the slab exceeds L/360, the structural design was inadequate for the specified finish — the engineer is liable; (3) if the slab meets L/360 but tiles still crack, the installation was inadequate (no movement joints, rigid adhesive) — the tiling contractor is liable; (4) remediation options: install a decoupling membrane (e.g., Schluter Ditra) over the existing tiles to isolate from substrate movement; or replace tiles with a flexible flooring system (vinyl, carpet). Prevention: always specify the floor finish on structural drawings so the engineer can design for appropriate deflection limits. The gap between structural safety limits and finish suitability limits is a common design failure.

## 39. SECTIONAL TITLE & BODY CORPORATE

**Q:** 152 We own a retail unit in a sectional title scheme. The body corporate has voted to retile all common area walkways using our unit's levy contribution. We believe the existing tiles are fine and the cost is unnecessary. Can we object, and what are our rights?
**A:** Under the Sectional Titles Schemes Management Act 8 of 2011: (1) the body corporate is responsible for maintenance of common property (Section 3(1)(a)); (2) maintenance decisions are made by trustees, subject to owner approval for "major" decisions; (3) a special resolution (75% majority) is required for improvements to common property that are not necessary maintenance; (4) if the retiling is genuine maintenance (existing tiles are damaged/dangerous), the trustees can authorise it; (5) if it is an aesthetic upgrade, a special resolution is needed. Your rights: (1) request the trustees' motivation for the retiling — what is the condition assessment? (2) demand to see the maintenance plan (required under STSMA regulation); (3) if you believe the expenditure is wasteful, you can: apply to the Community Schemes Ombud for an order restraining the expenditure; request a meeting to challenge the decision; withhold payment of the levy portion (risky — you remain liable for levies and can be sued or have your unit attached); (4) if the retiling proceeds despite your objection, you must pay your levy contribution — you cannot unilaterally opt out of body corporate decisions. The Ombud Service is your best recourse if you believe the trustees are acting outside their authority.

**Q:** 153 Our sectional title retail unit's exclusive use area (patio) tiles are lifting. The body corporate says exclusive use areas are the owner's responsibility. Our lease says the landlord (us) is responsible for maintenance. Who pays — us or our tenant?
**A:** In a sectional title scheme: (1) exclusive use areas are maintained at the owner's cost (STSMA Section 3(1)(c)) — the body corporate can do the work and charge you; (2) your lease with your tenant determines who bears this cost internally. If your lease says you (landlord) are responsible for maintenance of the premises including exclusive use areas, you pay. You cannot pass the body corporate's charge to the tenant unless the lease specifically allows recovery of body corporate maintenance costs. Options: (1) pay for the repair yourself; (2) if the tile failure is due to body corporate negligence (e.g., failed waterproofing in common areas above your patio), claim against the body corporate; (3) review your tenant's lease to determine if you can recover the cost as an operating expense — most retail leases allow recovery of maintenance costs as part of operating costs; (4) if the body corporate is responsible for the waterproofing membrane beneath the tiles, they should repair the membrane and you repair/replace the tiles (split responsibility is common). Check your sectional title plan to confirm what is exclusive use vs. common property — the plan governs.

**Q:** 154 We are the anchor tenant in a shopping centre. The landlord (body corporate) has retiled the common mall areas with tiles that do not match our store's entrance threshold, creating a 15mm trip hazard. The landlord says we must adjust our threshold. Who is responsible?
**A:** The landlord is responsible for ensuring common area finishes are safe and do not create hazards at tenant interfaces. A 15mm height difference at a threshold is a trip hazard that fails SANS 10107 (level changes >6mm require bevelled edges) and potentially the National Building Regulations accessibility requirements. Your position: (1) notify the landlord in WRITING of the trip hazard with photographs; (2) request immediate remediation (bevelled transition strip or threshold adjustment); (3) cite SANS 10107 and accessibility requirements; (4) if the landlord refuses, notify your public liability insurer (they need to know of the hazard); (5) if someone trips and is injured before remediation, the landlord is primarily liable but you may share liability if you knew of the hazard and did nothing. Installing a temporary ramp or warning signage protects both parties. If the landlord genuinely cannot change the common area tiles, negotiate a cost-sharing arrangement for threshold modification. However, the legal responsibility for safe transitions lies with the party controlling the common area — the landlord/body corporate.

## 40. CONSUMER PROTECTION & RETAILER LIABILITY

**Q:** 155 A customer slipped on a just-mopped floor in our store. The cleaner had placed a "wet floor" sign 2 metres from the spill. The customer claims the sign was not visible from their approach angle. Are we liable under the CPA?
**A:** The CPA Section 55 requires that services (including cleaning and maintenance) be performed in a manner that is safe. The "wet floor" sign placement is a key factor: (1) a sign 2 metres from the spill may be inadequate — best practice is to place signs at ALL approach angles to the wet area, not just one point; (2) if the customer approached from a direction where the sign was not visible, your warning was insufficient; (3) if the cleaner was still actively mopping and the area was clearly cordoned, your defence is stronger; (4) if the floor remained wet for an extended period without drying measures (fans, dry mopping), you may be negligent. Under South African delictual law, the test is whether you took reasonable steps to prevent harm. A single sign 2 metres away is arguably insufficient for a busy retail environment. The courts have found retailers liable for slip-and-fall where warning measures were inadequate. Your public liability insurance should respond, but the insurer may increase premiums or impose conditions. Best practice: multiple signs at all approach angles; yellow barrier tape for large wet areas; immediate drying; staff training on spill response.

**Q:** 156 Our store displays a "lifetime warranty" on our tiles. A customer has claimed that "lifetime" means THEIR lifetime, not the product's expected life. The CPA requires warranties to be clear. Is our "lifetime warranty" compliant?
**A:** "Lifetime warranty" is ambiguous and potentially misleading under the CPA. Section 56 of the CPA creates implied warranties of quality that cannot be overridden by unclear supplier warranties. The problem: "lifetime" of what? The customer's lifetime? The building's lifetime? The product's reasonable lifespan? The CPA and ASA (Advertising Standards Authority) have ruled that "lifetime" claims must specify what "lifetime" refers to. Best practice: define "lifetime" as "the reasonable expected lifespan of the product under normal use conditions — typically 25 years for porcelain tiles in residential applications." If you cannot define it, do not use the term. The customer's interpretation (their lifetime) is unreasonable for a building product, but the ambiguity works against you under the CPA's contra proferentem rule (ambiguity interpreted against the drafter). Update all warranty documentation to specify the actual warranty period in years and the conditions. The NCC (National Consumer Commission) has investigated retailers for misleading warranty claims.

**Q:** 157 We sell "scratch-resistant" tiles. A customer has scratched the tiles by dragging a refrigerator across the floor and demands replacement under warranty. Our warranty excludes "damage caused by moving heavy appliances without protection." Is this exclusion enforceable under the CPA?
**A:** Under CPA Section 55, goods must be durable and free of defects. "Scratch-resistant" does not mean "scratch-proof" — tiles can be damaged by extreme abuse. The CPA allows suppliers to exclude liability for damage caused by abnormal use, provided the exclusion is clear and not unfair. Key questions: (1) was the warranty exclusion brought to the customer's attention BEFORE purchase (on the website, in-store signage, or receipt)? (2) is "moving heavy appliances without protection" a foreseeable use that should be warned against? (3) did the tiles actually meet their scratch resistance specification (e.g., Mohs hardness >6) — if they were defective (softer than specified), the exclusion does not apply to the defect itself. Defence: if the tiles were properly manufactured and met scratch resistance specifications, and the customer caused damage through unreasonable use (dragging a fridge without sliders or boards), the exclusion is likely enforceable. However, if the customer's use was reasonable (normal furniture moving), the exclusion may be unfair under CPA Section 48. Provide evidence of the tile's actual scratch resistance test results. For future: clarify in marketing that "scratch-resistant" refers to normal foot traffic, not heavy appliance dragging; provide care guides with all purchases.

## 41. LEASE, LANDLORD & TENANT

**Q:** 158 Our lease specifies that the tenant (us) is responsible for "all internal finishes including floor coverings." The landlord has now demanded we replace the common area corridor tiles as well, claiming they are "internal finishes." Is the corridor common property or part of our leased premises?
**A:** The corridor is almost certainly common property, not part of your leased premises. In a multi-tenant building: (1) your lease defines your demise (leased area) — typically shown on a lease plan; (2) common corridors, lobbies, and restrooms are shared facilities maintained by the landlord; (3) the landlord's maintenance obligation for common areas cannot be transferred to tenants through vague lease wording. Your position: (1) request the lease plan showing the demise — if the corridor is not hatched/coloured as your area, it is common property; (2) the landlord is responsible for common area maintenance under the lease's service charge provisions (you pay a service charge / CAM charge for this purpose); (3) "internal finishes" refers to finishes WITHIN your demise, not common areas. If the landlord persists: (1) cite the lease plan and CAM charge provisions; (2) offer to pay only your proportional share through the service charge (which you already do); (3) if the landlord attempts to withhold services or access, this is a breach of lease. Note: some "gross" leases or "triple net" leases transfer more responsibility to tenants — check your specific lease type. In a typical South African commercial lease (AIT or SAPOA template), common area maintenance is the landlord's responsibility funded by service charges.

**Q:** 159 Our lease includes a "yield-up" clause requiring us to remove all tenant improvements and restore the premises to "base building shell" condition. We installed a heated tile floor system (electrical underfloor heating). The landlord now claims the heating system must remain as it is "attached to the building" and has become the landlord's property. Is this correct?
**A:** This depends on the lease wording and the nature of the installation. Under common law: (1) fixtures attached to the building become part of the landlord's property unless the lease specifies otherwise; (2) tenant's removable trade fixtures can be removed if they can be detached without causing substantial damage. For underfloor heating: (1) the heating elements embedded in the screed are clearly fixtures — they cannot be removed without destroying the floor; (2) the thermostat and control units are removable (chattels); (3) the lease's yield-up clause governs — if it requires removal of ALL improvements, you must remove what you can and repair damage; if it has an exception for "landlord's fixtures," the embedded heating stays. Check: (1) does the lease define "tenant improvements" vs. "landlord's fixtures"? (2) did you obtain landlord consent for the underfloor heating installation? (3) is there a clause addressing mechanical and electrical installations? Best practice: clarify the status of all improvements before installation; obtain written agreement on what stays and what goes; photograph the base building condition at lease commencement. For the heating system: negotiate with the landlord — they may want it to remain and may pay a salvage value; or you may be required to remove the screed and restore to original condition (expensive). Budget for yield-up obligations from year one of the lease.

**Q:** 160 Our landlord has sold the building. The new landlord has refused to honour the remaining 3 years of our lease, claiming they were not a party to the original agreement. Can they evict us?
**A:** No. Under South African law, a lease is binding on successors in title. Section 2(1) of the Formalities in Respect of Leases of Land Act and common law principle of huur gaat voor koop (lease prevails over sale) protect tenants. The new landlord steps into the shoes of the old landlord and must honour the lease terms for the remaining period. They cannot evict you without cause. However: (1) this protection applies only if the lease was registered against the title deeds OR the purchaser had knowledge of the lease; (2) if the lease was not registered and the purchaser was a bona fide buyer without knowledge, the position is weaker — but in a commercial building with an operating tenant, knowledge is almost always imputed; (3) if the new landlord wants you out, they must negotiate a buy-out or wait until lease expiry. Your rights: (1) remain in occupation and pay rent to the new landlord; (2) if the new landlord interferes with your occupation, apply for an interdict; (3) if they attempt eviction without a court order, this is illegal under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction Act. The new landlord's remedy is limited to: enforcing lease terms; not renewing at expiry; or negotiating an early termination with compensation. Ensure your lease is registered against the title deeds for maximum protection — registration costs are minimal and provide definitive security.

## 42. OHSA, SAFETY & RISK MANAGEMENT

**Q:** 161 Our tiling contractor has refused to provide a Fall Protection Plan for work on a mezzanine level, saying "it's only 2.5 metres high, so it's not a fall risk." Is this correct under OHSA?
**A:** No. The Construction Regulations 2014 (under OHSA) define a "fall risk" as any work at a height of 2 metres or more above ground level OR at any height where a fall could cause injury. A 2.5-metre mezzanine is definitely a fall risk. Requirements: (1) a Fall Protection Plan is mandatory for ALL construction work at height; (2) the plan must identify fall hazards and specify control measures (guardrails, harnesses, safety nets); (3) the contractor must appoint a fall protection planner (competent person); (4) workers must be trained in fall protection. If the contractor refuses to comply: (1) do not allow work to proceed — you face liability as the client; (2) report to the Department of Labour if the contractor continues to refuse; (3) engage a contractor who complies. As the client, you have duties under Construction Regulation 5 (client responsibilities) — you must ensure the contractor has a health and safety plan and complies with OHSA. A contractor who does not understand basic fall protection requirements is likely non-compliant across the board and should not be on your site. The Department of Labour conducts regular construction site inspections — non-compliance can result in prohibition notices and fines.

**Q:** 162 A customer was injured when a loose tile edge trim lifted and tripped them. Our maintenance contractor had inspected the store 2 weeks prior and noted the trim as "requires attention" but we had not yet authorised the repair. Are we liable?
**A:** Yes, you are likely liable. The test for occupier's liability is whether you knew or should have known of the hazard and failed to take reasonable steps to address it. You knew of the hazard (the maintenance report flagged it) and failed to act within a reasonable time (2 weeks is likely unreasonable for a known trip hazard). Under the Aquilian action and the Occupiers' Liability Act, you owe a duty of care to all lawful visitors. The fact that you "had not yet authorised the repair" is not a defence — a reasonable occupier would have authorised immediate repair or implemented temporary measures (tape, sign, barrier). Your public liability insurance should respond, but: (1) the insurer may apply a higher excess for known hazards not addressed; (2) your premiums may increase; (3) if the maintenance contractor identified the hazard but did not make it safe or warn customers, they may share liability. Best practice: implement a 24-hour response time for all trip hazards identified in maintenance inspections; authorise emergency repairs up to a threshold (e.g., R5,000) without prior approval; document all hazard responses. The Department of Labour can also issue a notice under OHSA if the hazard affects employees.

**Q:** 163 Our contractor used a cutting machine that generated significant silica dust from tile cutting. No dust extraction or respiratory protection was provided. A worker has since been diagnosed with silicosis. What is our liability as the client who appointed the contractor?
**A:** This is extremely serious. Silicosis is a progressive, irreversible lung disease caused by inhaling crystalline silica dust. Under OHSA: (1) the CONTRACTOR (employer) is primarily responsible for providing safe working conditions, including dust extraction and PPE; (2) YOU (the client) have duties under Construction Regulation 5 to ensure the contractor's health and safety plan is adequate and implemented; (3) if you knew or should have known that dust-generating work was being done without protection, you share liability. Silica dust is classified as a hazardous chemical substance under the HCS Regulations — exposure limits apply. Immediate actions: (1) STOP all dust-generating work until controls are implemented; (2) report to the Department of Labour immediately (this is a serious occupational disease); (3) ensure the affected worker receives compensation through COIDA; (4) engage an occupational hygienist to assess exposure levels; (5) review ALL contractor health and safety plans for dust control measures. The contractor faces: criminal prosecution under OHSA; COID Act compensation liability; and potential civil claims. As the client, your brand reputation is at risk — issue a statement on your contractor safety standards. Prevention: mandate wet-cutting for all tile cutting (suppresses dust); require on-site dust extraction; provide respiratory protection (P2 minimum) as standard; conduct regular safety audits.

## 43. FORCE MAJEURE, INSOLVENCY & BUSINESS CONTINUITY

**Q:** 164 Our tile supplier has been placed under business rescue. We have paid a 50% deposit for tiles for 30 stores but no delivery has been made. The business rescue practitioner says we are an unsecured creditor. Can we get our tiles or our money back?
**A:** Under the Companies Act Chapter 6 (Business Rescue): (1) when a company enters business rescue, a moratorium applies — no legal proceedings can be commenced against the company without the practitioner's consent or court approval; (2) you become a creditor in the business rescue process; (3) deposits paid for undelivered goods make you an unsecured creditor (unless you have a security interest or retention of title); (4) the business rescue plan may propose: continued supply (if the business continues trading); return of deposits (unlikely, as cash is usually needed for rescue); or write-off of unsecured claims. Your position: (1) file a PROOF OF CLAIM with the business rescue practitioner immediately; (2) if you paid by credit card or EFT with specific reference, document this as evidence; (3) if the practitioner rejects your claim, apply to court for relief; (4) if the rescue fails and liquidation follows, unsecured creditors typically receive cents on the rand. Prevention for future: (1) pay deposits into a trust account or escrow, not directly to the supplier; (2) use bank guarantees or insurance-backed deposits for large orders; (3) stagger payments against delivery milestones rather than paying large deposits upfront; (4) monitor supplier financial health — credit checks, financial statements, payment behaviour. The business rescue process typically takes 3-6 months — don't count on receiving your tiles during this period. Source alternative suppliers immediately.

**Q:** 165 Our major tile supplier has been acquired by a competitor. The new owner has announced they are discontinuing our specified tile line. We have 47 stores still to build with this specification. What are our contractual rights?
**A:** Your rights depend on the contract terms and the acquisition structure: (1) if the acquisition was a SHARE SALE, the company remains the same legal entity and all contracts continue unchanged; (2) if the acquisition was an ASSET SALE, the new owner may not be bound by your contract unless they expressly assumed it; (3) if your contract includes a "change of control" clause, it may give you termination rights or renegotiation rights. For the discontinued product line: (1) if the contract specified this EXACT tile as a binding specification, discontinuation may be a breach if they cannot supply; (2) if the contract allowed for "equivalent alternatives," the supplier may substitute; (3) if the contract was silent on product continuity, the supplier can discontinue but must supply existing orders or refund. Your strategy: (1) review the contract for change of control, product continuity, and force majeure clauses; (2) demand fulfilment of all confirmed orders — a share sale acquisition inherits these obligations; (3) if the new owner refuses, they are in breach — claim damages (cost difference of substitute tiles, delay costs); (4) negotiate a transition: buy all remaining stock; negotiate a licensing arrangement for the product line; or accept a phased transition with the new owner's equivalent product. For future contracts: include a "product continuity" clause requiring 12 months' notice of discontinuation; include change of control provisions giving you termination or renegotiation rights.

**Q:** 166 A nationwide transport strike has prevented our tiles from reaching any stores for 3 weeks. Our rollout schedule is critically delayed. Our landlord is charging us penalty rents for late store openings. Can we claim these penalties from the tile supplier under force majeure?
**A:** The transport strike may qualify as force majeure if it was unforeseeable and made performance impossible. However: (1) transport strikes in South Africa are not entirely unforeseeable — this affects the force majeure assessment; (2) your supplier's force majeure clause may or may not cover third-party transport disruptions — check the specific wording; (3) even if force majeure applies, it typically suspends obligations without creating liability for consequential losses (like landlord penalties); (4) the landlord penalties are YOUR contractual risk — unless you specifically allocated them to the supplier in your contract (unlikely). Your landlord penalties: (1) claim from your own business interruption insurance if the policy covers supplier delays (many do not — check your policy); (2) if the supplier could have mitigated (alternative routing, air freight, local sourcing), they may be liable for failure to mitigate; (3) negotiate with the landlord — most landlords will waive or reduce penalties for delays beyond the tenant's control, especially if documented. For future: include "delay damages" or "liquidated damages" clauses in supplier contracts that flow through landlord penalties; maintain 4-6 weeks' safety stock; diversify transport providers; include strike action as a specific risk in your rollout risk register with mitigation plans.

## 44. PROCUREMENT, TENDER & CONTRACT LAW

**Q:** 167 Our procurement team ran a competitive tender for 200 stores. The winning bid was 40% cheaper than the second-place bid. Our facilities manager suspects "bid rigging" or a "suicide bid." What are the warning signs, and what should we do?
**A:** Bid rigging (collusive tendering) is a criminal offence under the Competition Act Section 73. Warning signs: (1) a bid significantly below all others (40% below suggests unsustainable pricing or corner-cutting); (2) bidders who regularly bid against each other suddenly submit similar prices; (3) the winning bidder has no track record with projects of this scale; (4) the winning bidder's price breakdown shows unrealistically low labour rates or material costs; (5) bidders rotate wins across different contracts. Actions: (1) DO NOT AWARD the tender yet — investigate first; (2) request a detailed price breakdown from the winning bidder and ask them to explain the pricing; (3) check if the bid is genuinely deliverable (site visits to previous projects, reference checks); (4) report suspicions to the Competition Commission — they have a leniency programme for whistleblowers; (5) if the bid is simply aggressive but legitimate, require a bid bond or performance guarantee to protect against failure; (6) consider a "best and final offer" round to test pricing. A "suicide bid" (below-cost bidding to win market share) will result in: corner-cutting, disputes, contractor insolvency mid-project, and ultimately higher total cost. The Competition Commission has successfully prosecuted construction industry bid rigging — the penalties are severe (up to 10% of turnover and criminal prosecution).

**Q:** 168 Our standard contract template has a "sole remedy" clause stating that the supplier's only liability for defective tiles is replacement of the tiles. A store had to close for 3 days during remediation, losing R120,000 in revenue. Can we claim the lost revenue despite the sole remedy clause?
**A:** The enforceability of a "sole remedy" clause depends on: (1) whether the contract is B2B or B2C — the CPA limits exclusion of consequential loss for consumer transactions but allows more freedom in B2B contracts; (2) whether the clause was clearly brought to the supplier's attention (standard terms and conditions buried in fine print may not be enforceable); (3) whether the defect was caused by the supplier's gross negligence or wilful misconduct — sole remedy clauses typically do not protect against gross negligence; (4) whether the lost revenue was foreseeable at the time of contracting — if the supplier knew you were a retail store and closure would cause revenue loss, consequential damages may be claimable. For a B2B contract: (1) the clause is likely enforceable unless it is unconscionable; (2) R120,000 for a 3-day closure is arguably foreseeable and should be claimable; (3) negotiate with the supplier — many will contribute to avoid dispute; (4) if they refuse, you can challenge the clause under common law (it may be an unreasonable penalty or exclusion). For future: amend the contract to allow recovery of direct and foreseeable consequential damages, capped at a percentage of contract value (e.g., 100% of annual spend). This provides protection while giving the supplier a clear liability cap.

**Q:** 169 Our tiling contract includes a "pay when paid" clause — we only pay the contractor when our client (the property developer) pays us. The developer has delayed payment for 6 months. The contractor has stopped work and is threatening liquidation. Is the "pay when paid" clause enforceable?
**A:** "Pay when paid" clauses are common in construction but their enforceability is limited. Under the common law, these clauses are interpreted as timing mechanisms (we will pay you when we are paid) rather than absolute conditions (we will only pay you if we are paid). However: (1) if the clause is clearly worded as a condition precedent, it may be enforceable in B2B contracts; (2) the Consumer Protection Act may render such clauses unfair for SMEs or consumers; (3) the clause cannot operate indefinitely — if the upstream payment never comes, the downstream contractor is still entitled to payment after a reasonable time. Key cases: Makhamba v Johannesburg City Council and similar precedents hold that "pay when paid" does not extinguish the debt — it merely delays it. If the contractor stops work, they may be justified if they have not been paid for completed work. Your options: (1) pay the contractor what is due — you cannot use the developer's non-payment to justify non-payment to the contractor indefinitely; (2) if you genuinely cannot pay, negotiate a payment plan; (3) if the contractor proceeds to liquidation, you become a creditor in their estate for any amounts you owe. The "pay when paid" clause does not protect you from the contractor's insolvency. Best practice: avoid "pay when paid" clauses — they create supply chain fragility. Instead, maintain payment certainty and finance your own working capital gap.

## 45. FINAL EDGE CASES — THE TRULY UNEXPECTED

**Q:** 170 Our store's floor tiles have become a popular TikTok trend — customers are filming themselves "tile-tapping" (making music by tapping the tiles). Some tiles have chipped from aggressive tapping. Can we claim this as vandalism on our insurance?
**A:** This is a novel claim scenario. Standard commercial insurance covers "malicious damage" (intentional damage by a third party) and "accidental damage." TikTok tile-tapping damage falls into a grey area: (1) if customers are deliberately striking tiles hard enough to chip them, this arguably constitutes malicious damage — the intent may not be to damage, but the damage is a foreseeable result of their actions; (2) if the damage is from normal foot-tapping that happens to chip poor-quality tiles, it is wear and tear, not insurable damage. Your claim depends on: (1) the policy wording — does "malicious damage" require intent to damage, or just intent to perform the act that caused damage? (2) police reports — file a case for malicious damage to property (Criminal Procedure Act); (3) evidence of the social media trend driving the behaviour; (4) whether the tiles were fit for purpose (if they chip from foot-tapping, they may be defective). For the immediate problem: post signage discouraging the behaviour; consider anti-graffiti film or surface protection; engage with the mall security. The insurance claim is worth pursuing but expect pushback — insurers may classify this as a social media phenomenon exclusion (not yet standard). Claim under public property damage and let the insurer classify it.

**Q:** 171 A swarm of bees has established a hive under the external tiled steps of our store. The tiles are lifting due to the weight of the hive and honeycomb. Pest control says they need to break the tiles to access the hive. Who pays — us, pest control, or the landlord?
**A:** This is a property maintenance and pest control issue. Responsibility: (1) if the steps are common property (landlord's responsibility), the landlord arranges and pays for pest removal and tile repair; (2) if the steps are part of your demise, you are responsible; (3) pest control is typically the occupier's responsibility unless the building structure itself is infested; (4) the tile damage is a consequence of necessary pest removal — reasonable force to access the hive is not negligence by the pest control contractor, provided they use appropriate methods. Cost allocation: if you engaged the pest control company, you pay them; if the tiles are landlord's property, they pay for tile repair; if the tiles are yours, you pay for everything. Insurance: check if your policy covers "animal damage" — some commercial policies exclude it, others include it as an insured peril. Prevention: seal all gaps under external steps during construction; conduct quarterly pest inspections. The Western Cape and KZN have active bee populations — this is a foreseeable risk in coastal areas. Note: bees are protected in South Africa — removal must be done by a registered beekeeper, not extermination, unless the hive poses an immediate safety risk.

**Q:** 172 Our store was used as a location for a film shoot. The production company used heavy dollies and camera equipment that scratched the polished tile floor. Our insurance excludes "damage caused by filming activities." The production company refuses to pay, claiming the tiles were "too soft for professional use." What is our recourse?
**A:** The production company hired your premises and caused damage — they are liable under delict (negligence) or contract (the location agreement). Their claim that tiles were "too soft" is a defence of contributory negligence (your product was defective), which they must prove. Your position: (1) the tiles met their specification and were suitable for normal retail use — film production equipment is not normal use; (2) the production company should have used floor protection (plywood, Masonite boards) — failure to do so is negligence; (3) review the location agreement — did it include a damage waiver or require insurance? Most production companies carry location damage insurance. Actions: (1) demand the production company's insurance details; (2) obtain an independent assessment of the damage and repair cost; (3) if they refuse, issue a letter of demand; (4) if still refused, sue in Magistrates' Court (if under R400,000) or High Court. Their argument about tile softness is weak unless they can prove the tiles were defective (below SANS 1449 hardness standards). Polished porcelain typically has Mohs hardness 5-6 — sufficient for normal use but vulnerable to metal equipment without protection. For future location hires: require proof of location insurance; conduct pre- and post-shoot condition surveys; specify floor protection requirements in the location agreement.

**Q:** 173 Our store's tiles are being used as a reference point by local Uber drivers — "pickup at the blue tile store." The tiles are actually grey. We want to replace them to stop the confusion. Can we claim this as a business necessity for tax deduction purposes?
**A:** No. "Uber driver confusion over tile colour" is not a legitimate business expense for tax purposes. Section 11(a) of the Income Tax Act allows deductions for expenditure incurred in the production of income, provided it is not of a capital nature. Tile replacement to change colour is capital expenditure (improving or altering an asset) and would be depreciated over time, not deducted in full. The reason for the replacement must be business-related — brand refresh, wear and tear, or lease compliance. "Uber confusion" is not a business rationale. However, if you are doing a legitimate brand refresh and the colour change is part of that, the cost is deductible as a capital allowance (section 12C wear and tear over the useful life, typically 5 years for shop fittings). Claiming a tax deduction based on the Uber rationale would attract SARS scrutiny and potential understatement penalties. Be honest about the business purpose — SARS audits are increasing, and frivolous claims carry risk.

**Q:** 174 Our store is located below a gym. The constant dropping of weights has caused vibrations that are cracking our ceiling tiles and creating dust. The gym says they have soundproofing mats and the vibrations are "within acceptable limits." What are our rights as the downstairs tenant?
**A:** This is a neighbour law (voetstoots and nuisance) issue. Your rights: (1) every property owner/occupier has the right to use and enjoy their property without unreasonable interference from neighbours; (2) vibrations that cause physical damage are likely "nuisance" — an unreasonable interference with your use of the property; (3) "acceptable limits" for the gym may not be acceptable for you if damage is occurring. Course of action: (1) document the damage with photographs and dates; (2) engage an acoustic engineer to measure vibration levels and compare to SANS 10103 (noise and vibration control) and SANS 10109 (impact on building elements); (3) notify the landlord (if both units are in the same building) — they have a duty to ensure tenants do not unreasonably interfere with each other; (4) notify the gym in writing of the damage and request remediation (better shock absorption, weight limits, rubber flooring); (5) if the gym refuses, apply for an interdict (court order restraining the nuisance); (6) claim damages for repair costs and any business interruption. The gym's "soundproofing mats" may reduce airborne noise but not impact vibration — they need structural isolation (floating floors, spring isolators). The landlord is often the best intermediary in multi-tenant buildings — they can require the gym to upgrade their flooring as a lease condition.

**Q:** 175 Our store's tiled floor has been designated as a "public art installation" by a local artist who claims the tile pattern (our standard corporate herringbone) infringes their copyrighted design. They have demanded royalties and threatened legal action. Is there any validity to this claim?
**A:** Almost certainly not. The herringbone pattern is a centuries-old geometric design that is not subject to copyright — it is in the public domain. Copyright protects ORIGINAL creative works, not standard geometric patterns or common design elements. The artist's claim would only be valid if: (1) they created a SPECIFIC, ORIGINAL variation of herringbone that is substantially similar to your pattern; (2) your pattern is an EXACT copy of their copyrighted design; (3) they registered the design and can prove you had access to it. Standard corporate herringbone is not protectable by copyright. Your response: (1) do not pay royalties — this would validate a baseless claim; (2) respond in writing stating that herringbone is a public domain pattern and you will not entertain the claim; (3) if they persist, engage an intellectual property attorney to send a cease-and-desist letter; (4) if they actually sue (unlikely), the court will almost certainly dismiss the claim — but defend it to prevent precedent. Be aware of "copyright trolling" — individuals making spurious copyright claims hoping for nuisance-value settlements. Do not settle baseless claims — it encourages more. Document that your herringbone specification predates any knowledge of the artist's work.

**Q:** 176 A competitor has opened a store next door and installed identical tiles to ours, creating customer confusion. Our tile specification is not trademarked. Do we have any legal protection for our store design under South African law?
**A:** South African law does not protect store design per se unless specific intellectual property rights are registered: (1) TRADE MARK — you can register a distinctive store layout or colour scheme as a trade mark under the Trade Marks Act if it is distinctive of your brand (difficult for standard tiles); (2) PASSING OFF — if customers are genuinely confused into believing the competitor's store is yours, you may have a passing-off claim under common law — but you must prove goodwill, misrepresentation, and damage; (3) COPYRIGHT — the store design (photographs, drawings) may be copyrighted, but this does not prevent someone from creating a similar physical space; (4) ACPA — the Consumer Protection Act prohibits misleading conduct, but installing similar tiles is not inherently misleading. Your practical options: (1) accept that tile similarity is not protectable and focus on brand differentiation (signage, lighting, layout, service); (2) if the competitor is deliberately copying your entire store concept (not just tiles), a passing-off claim may succeed; (3) negotiate with the landlord — some mall leases include "exclusivity of concept" clauses preventing direct competitors from copying tenant fit-outs. For future: register distinctive elements of your store design as trade marks; include store design photographs in your brand guidelines; build brand recognition that transcends physical similarities. The cost of litigation likely exceeds the benefit for tile similarity alone.

**Q:** 177 Our store in Mpumalanga is near a wildlife reserve. Monkeys have entered the store through an open door and damaged floor tiles by dropping heavy items (stones, fruit) on them. Is this an insurable event under our commercial policy?
**A:** This is an unusual but genuine claim scenario. Standard commercial policies cover damage from specific perils: fire, theft, storm, water, accidental damage. Animal damage is often excluded or not specifically mentioned. Check your policy: (1) does it cover "animal damage" specifically? Some policies include it; others exclude it under a general exclusion for damage caused by animals, birds, or insects; (2) if the policy covers "accidental damage," monkey-dropped stones may qualify — the damage was accidental (not deliberate by you or the monkey); (3) if the policy covers "theft and malicious damage," this probably does not apply — monkeys are not acting with malice. Your claim strategy: (1) claim under the broadest available coverage ("all risks" if you have it); (2) argue that the damage was fortuitous and not excluded; (3) if the insurer declines, escalate to the Ombudsman — they have ruled on unusual claims before. Prevention: automatic doors; security mesh on windows; signage reminding staff to keep doors closed; ultrasonic animal deterrents. Note: the monkeys are likely protected species — harm to them is a criminal offence under the National Environmental Management: Biodiversity Act. Focus on prevention, not retaliation.

**Q:** 178 Our tiling contractor has been blacklisted by the CIDB (Construction Industry Development Board) for non-compliance with grading requirements but continues to operate under a different company name. We discovered this after paying a deposit. What is our recourse, and how do we verify contractor legitimacy?
**A:** Operating under a different name to avoid CIDB blacklisting is fraudulent. The CIDB Act requires all contractors undertaking construction works to be registered and to work within their grade. Your recourse: (1) report the contractor to the CIDB immediately — they can investigate and impose penalties including debarment; (2) if the "new" company is a front for the blacklisted contractor (same directors, same premises, same staff), the CIDB can link them and debar the new entity too; (3) if you have suffered financial loss, report fraud to the SAPS; (4) if the contractor is performing work while unregistered, they are committing an offence — you may be able to terminate the contract and claim your deposit back. Verification before appointment: (1) check the CIDB register online (cidb.org.za) — verify the contractor's registration number, grade, and status; (2) check CIPC records for director details — cross-reference with known blacklisted contractors; (3) check the contractor's B-BBEE certificate and tax clearance status; (4) request a bank guarantee or performance bond for the deposit. Prevention: never pay deposits to contractors without verifying CIDB registration, checking references, and confirming physical business premises. The CIDB has a public register — use it for every appointment.

**Q:** 179 Our national tile supplier has advised that due to load shedding, they are switching their kiln firing from electricity to diesel. They want to pass on a "fuel surcharge" of 8% on all tiles. Our contract has fixed pricing for 12 months. Can they do this?
**A:** No — if your contract has fixed pricing for 12 months, the supplier cannot unilaterally add surcharges unless the contract specifically allows for price adjustments (escalation clause, force majeure, or change in law). A fuel surcharge due to load shedding is not a standard contractual adjustment. Your position: (1) reject the surcharge and demand supply at the contract price; (2) if the supplier refuses to supply without the surcharge, they are in breach of contract; (3) you can: source from an alternative supplier and claim the cost difference; apply for specific performance (court order requiring supply at contract price); or accept the surcharge under protest while reserving your rights. The supplier's argument: (1) if they claim force majeure, load shedding is NOT force majeure in 2024 — it is a foreseeable risk that businesses must manage; (2) if they claim "change in circumstances" (imprévision), this doctrine is not well-developed in South African contract law; (3) diesel cost increase is a business risk — they should have priced it into the contract or hedged. Negotiation: if the supplier is genuinely distressed and you value the relationship, consider a modest increase (2-3%) as a goodwill gesture, documented as a contract amendment. Do not accept an 8% surcharge on a fixed-price contract without significant concession elsewhere.

**Q:** 180 Our internal audit has discovered that our procurement manager accepted a "commission" (kickback) from our tile supplier in exchange for awarding a 3-year exclusive supply contract. The contract is legally signed. What is our position, and what laws have been broken?
**A:** This is serious corruption and potentially criminal. Laws broken: (1) PRECCA (Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004) — both the procurement manager (accepting a bribe) and the supplier (offering a bribe) commit offences punishable by up to life imprisonment; (2) the COMPANIES ACT — the procurement manager breached their fiduciary duty to the company; (3) COMMON LAW — the contract may be voidable due to corruption (illegality). Your position: (1) the contract is VOIDABLE at your option — you can cancel it and claim restitution (return of all payments, return of tiles); (2) you can sue the procurement manager for damages and report them to the SAPS; (3) you can sue the supplier for damages and report them; (4) the supplier can also be prosecuted under PRECCA. Immediate actions: (1) suspend the procurement manager pending investigation; (2) engage forensic auditors to quantify the full extent of the corruption; (3) report to the SAPS Commercial Crimes Unit; (4) report to the NPA if evidence is strong; (5) review the contract — if the pricing was inflated to fund the kickback, you have a claim for overpayment; (6) consider whether to cancel the contract or renegotiate at arm's length pricing. Protection of whistleblowers: the Protected Disclosures Act protects employees who report corruption. The supplier may be blacklisted from government and corporate procurement if convicted.

## 46. THE FINAL 20 — ULTIMATE CURVEBALLS

**Q:** 181 Our store's floor tiles were installed over a radio frequency identification (RFID) antenna system embedded in the subfloor. The tiles are blocking the RFID signal, preventing stock tracking. The flooring contractor blames the RFID installer; the RFID installer blames the tile thickness. Who is responsible?
**A:** This is a systems integration failure. RFID readers typically operate at frequencies that are affected by: metal content in tiles, tile thickness, and adhesive composition. Responsibility: (1) the RFID installer should have specified maximum floor covering thickness and material restrictions BEFORE the flooring was installed; (2) the flooring contractor installed per the building specification — if the RFID requirement was not in the flooring spec, they are not at fault; (3) the PROJECT MANAGER or architect who coordinated the two systems is responsible for the clash. Resolution: (1) test RFID signal strength with different tile types — some tiles are more transparent to RF signals; (2) relocate RFID antennas to ceiling or shelf level instead of floor; (3) use UHF RFID instead of HF (better penetration); (4) cut back tiles at antenna locations and install RF-transparent covers. Prevention: include ALL building systems (RFID, security, fire, data) in the flooring specification; require electromagnetic compatibility testing before finalising finishes. There is no SANS standard for RFID compatibility with flooring — this requires project-specific testing.

**Q:** 182 Our tile supplier has been placed on "business rescue" and cannot deliver. Our contract includes a "material adverse change" clause. Can we terminate and source elsewhere without penalty?
**A:** Business rescue constitutes a material adverse change (MAC) in most contracts. Under the Companies Act, when a company enters business rescue: (1) a moratorium applies — you cannot terminate the contract or enforce obligations without court approval or the practitioner's consent; (2) however, if the contract includes a MAC clause that specifically covers business rescue or insolvency, you may have termination rights; (3) even without a MAC clause, the common law doctrine of supervening impossibility may apply if the supplier cannot perform. Your options: (1) file a claim with the business rescue practitioner as a creditor; (2) apply to court for an order terminating the contract and allowing you to source elsewhere; (3) negotiate with the practitioner — they may consent to termination if it helps the rescue (e.g., if you are a major creditor); (4) if the rescue fails and liquidation follows, you are an unsecured creditor. Do not unilaterally terminate without legal advice — the practitioner can set aside actions taken in contravention of the moratorium. For future contracts: include specific termination rights for business rescue, insolvency, and failure to deliver within specified timeframes.

**Q:** 183 Our store in Sandton City requires after-hours delivery. The mall's security has refused entry to our tile supplier's truck because the driver does not have a " security clearance certificate" issued by the mall. Our supplier has never heard of this. Is this a legitimate requirement?
**A:** Major malls (Sandton City, Mall of Africa, V&A Waterfront) have their own security protocols for after-hours access. These are private security policies, not statutory requirements, but they are enforceable as conditions of access to private property. "Security clearance certificates" for delivery personnel are increasingly common — they involve: criminal record check; ID verification; photo badge issuance; induction training on mall safety procedures. If Sandton City requires this, your supplier must comply or deliver during trading hours. Your options: (1) arrange for the supplier's driver to undergo the clearance process (typically takes 3-5 days); (2) use a courier or logistics company that already has Sandton City clearance; (3) arrange delivery during trading hours with the mall manager's approval; (4) use the mall's approved contractor list — some malls require all after-hours contractors to be pre-approved. This is not a statutory requirement but a condition of the mall's lease and operational rules — compliance is mandatory if you want after-hours access. Factor security clearance lead times into your project schedule.

**Q:** 184 Our Zimbabwean border store needs tiles that can withstand being driven over by heavy trucks (the store has a drive-through section). Our standard commercial porcelain is rated for pedestrian traffic only. What is the loading specification for vehicular traffic tiles?
**A:** Vehicular traffic requires tiles rated for much higher loads than pedestrian traffic. Specification for drive-through areas: (1) COMPRESSIVE STRENGTH — minimum 80MPa (pedestrian tiles are typically 35-50MPa); (2) THICKNESS — minimum 20mm for light vehicles, 30mm+ for heavy trucks; (3) BEDDING — tiles must be fully bedded on a 50-100mm concrete base (not adhesive on a screed); (4) JOINT WIDTH — minimum 5mm joints filled with flexible jointing sand or grout; (5) SLIP RESISTANCE — R12 or R13 for wet vehicle tyres; (6) FROST RESISTANCE — if the area is exposed to temperature variation. Standard products: 20mm outdoor porcelain pavers (e.g., Mirage, Keope, local equivalents); concrete pavers (Interlocking or pressed); clay pavers (Corobrik or similar). These are installed on a compacted sand or mortar bed, not adhesive. Using standard 9-10mm commercial tiles in a drive-through area will result in immediate cracking under vehicle loads. The pavement design must follow COLTO (Committee of Land Transport Officials) guidelines for light commercial vehicle loading. Ensure the sub-base is designed for the actual axle loads — a typical delivery truck has 8-12 ton rear axle load.

**Q:** 185 Our store's tiles were damaged by a swarm of locusts during the 2021 Eastern Cape locust plague. The insects themselves didn't damage the tiles, but the chemical spray used by the Department of Agriculture to kill them stained the surface. Is this an insurable event?
**A:** This is a claim for damage caused by government action (pest control spraying). Standard commercial policies cover "damage caused by civil authority action" or "damage caused by government order" as an insured peril in some policies, but not all. Check your policy: (1) if the government sprayed without your consent and the chemical caused staining, this may be covered under "damage caused by external forces" or "accidental damage"; (2) if the policy covers "government action," the spraying may qualify; (3) if the policy excludes "damage caused by chemical or biological agents," the insurer may decline. Alternative claim: claim directly from the Department of Agriculture — if their contractor used inappropriate chemicals or applied them in a manner that caused damage to your property, they may be liable under the State Liability Act. Document: the locust plague (newspaper reports); the spraying (photographs, dates); the damage (before/after photographs, independent assessment); your cleaning attempts. The Department's defence will be that the spraying was necessary emergency action — but they may still be liable for damage caused by negligent application. Prevention: there is none for locust plagues — this is a pure insurance and risk transfer issue.

**Q:** 186 Our tiling contractor has invoiced us for "profit and attendance" on items we supplied (the tiles themselves). We purchased the tiles directly and only engaged the contractor for installation. Is charging profit on client-supplied materials legitimate?
**A:** "Profit and attendance" on client-supplied materials is a legitimate charge in some contract structures, but it must be disclosed upfront. In a traditional procurement model (contractor supplies all materials), the contractor's profit is embedded in the material supply and installation rates. In a labour-only model (you supply materials), the contractor should not charge profit on materials they did not supply or handle — unless: (1) they are providing "attendance" services (receiving, storing, protecting, and managing your materials on site); (2) they assumed liability for the materials while on site; (3) this was agreed in the contract. If the contract was purely for installation labour with no mention of attendance on your materials, the charge is not justified. Challenge it: request a breakdown of what "attendance" services were provided; if none (materials delivered directly to site by your supplier), reject the charge. For future: clarify in the contract whether labour-only or full procurement; if labour-only, state that no attendance charges will apply to client-supplied materials. Under JBCC contracts, the distinction between "contractor supplied" and "employer supplied" is clearly defined — profit on employer-supplied items is not claimable unless the contract specifically provides for it.

**Q:** 187 Our store in the Western Cape wine lands is in a heritage-protected building. The heritage officer has required that we use traditional lime-based mortar for all floor bedding instead of modern cement adhesive. Our tile supplier says their tiles are not compatible with lime mortar. What is the solution?
**A:** Heritage buildings in the Western Cape (particularly in the Cape Winelands and Cape Town CBD) often require traditional materials and methods under SAHRA or PHRA direction. The conflict: modern porcelain and ceramic tiles are designed for cement-based or epoxy adhesives, not traditional lime mortar. Lime mortar has: low compressive strength (0.5-2MPa vs. 20-40MPa for cement adhesive); slow curing (weeks vs. 24 hours); and different thermal expansion characteristics. Solutions: (1) use TRADITIONAL TILES — terracotta, clay pavers, or slate that ARE compatible with lime mortar and appropriate for the heritage context; (2) use a HYBRID SYSTEM — a lime-cement mortar bed ( NHL 5 natural hydraulic lime mixed with cement) that provides some traditional character with adequate strength for modern tiles — check with the heritage officer if this is acceptable; (3) use a DECOUPLING MEMBRANE over the lime mortar bed, then install modern tiles with appropriate adhesive — this separates the traditional substrate from the modern finish; (4) engage a heritage architect to negotiate with SAHRA — they may accept a concealed modern system if the visible finish appears traditional. The heritage officer's requirement is legitimate — find a technical solution that satisfies both heritage and functional requirements. Never ignore a SAHRA directive — penalties are severe.

**Q:** 188 Our store's floor tiles have been identified as containing uranium oxide (used in some glazes for colour, particularly red and orange). A customer has raised health concerns about radiation exposure. Is this a genuine risk, and do we need to take action?
**A:** Uranium oxide has been used in ceramic glazes (particularly vintage tiles from the 1950s-1970s) to produce vibrant red, orange, and coral colours. The radiation levels from such tiles are extremely low — generally well below background radiation levels and not considered a health hazard for normal occupancy. However: (1) if the tiles are deteriorating (dust generation), there is a minor inhalation risk — uranium is chemically toxic at high doses; (2) if you are selling the property, disclosure may be required; (3) for food preparation areas, there may be specific regulations about materials in contact with food. Testing: (1) a Geiger counter survey can confirm radiation levels — if below 1 μSv/hr above background, no action is needed; (2) if levels are elevated, contact the National Nuclear Regulator (NNR) for guidance. Modern tiles (post-1990) rarely use uranium oxide due to cost and regulatory scrutiny. If your tiles are vintage: (1) the risk is negligible for normal retail use; (2) if concerned, seal the tiles with an epoxy coating to prevent any dust generation; (3) do not sand, grind, or demolish without dust controls. Document your risk assessment — this protects against future claims. The NNR regulates ionising radiation in South Africa under the Nuclear Energy Act 46 of 1999.

**Q:** 189 Our contractor has installed tiles with a "random linear" pattern that is supposed to look organic. Our brand manager says it looks "messy and unprofessional" and wants it removed. The contractor says it was installed per the specification drawing. The drawing is ambiguous. Who pays for the removal and replacement?
**A:** This is a specification ambiguity dispute. Under common law and standard contract interpretation: (1) if the drawing is ambiguous, the risk typically falls on the party who prepared it — YOU (the employer/client); (2) if the contractor installed in good faith based on a reasonable interpretation of the drawing, they are not at fault; (3) however, if the installation is clearly substandard (uneven joints, lippage beyond tolerance, inconsistent pattern), the contractor may share liability for poor workmanship regardless of drawing clarity. Resolution: (1) review the drawing with an independent architect or designer — is the pattern genuinely ambiguous or is the contractor's work poor? (2) if the drawing is ambiguous and the work is technically competent, the employer bears the cost of removal if a different pattern is desired; (3) negotiate a compromise — perhaps the pattern is acceptable in back-of-house areas and only customer-facing areas need replacement; (4) for future: always require a SAMPLE PANEL (minimum 2m²) for complex patterns, approved by the brand manager BEFORE full installation; include reference photographs in the specification showing acceptable and unacceptable outcomes. A picture is worth a thousand words for aesthetic specifications.

**Q:** 190 Our store is in a multi-storey building with a post-tensioned concrete slab. The structural engineer has prohibited ANY drilling or cutting of the slab. Our tiles need movement joints cut through to the subfloor. How do we provide movement joints without cutting the slab?
**A:** Post-tensioned slabs contain high-strength steel tendons under tension — drilling or cutting can sever tendons, causing catastrophic structural failure. This is a genuine constraint. Solutions for movement joints without cutting the slab: (1) SURFACE-APPLIED JOINTS — install movement joints that extend only through the tile and adhesive layer, sitting on top of the slab. Use a surface-mounted joint system (e.g., Schluter DILEX-AHK or similar) that provides expansion accommodation without penetrating the slab; (2) PREFORMED JOINTS — leave gaps during tile installation and fill with flexible joint sealant rated for traffic loads; (3) CONTROL JOINT ALIGNMENT — align all tile joints with the slab's construction joints or pour strips (the only places where cutting is permitted with engineer approval); (4) LARGE-FORMAT TILES with minimal joints — reduces the need for movement accommodation; (5) DECOUPLING MEMBRANE — allows the tile layer to move independently of the slab. The structural engineer's prohibition is absolute — no exceptions without their written approval and PT strand locating (ground-penetrating radar survey). Never cut a post-tensioned slab without professional engineering supervision. SANS 10100-1 (concrete structures) provides guidance on post-tensioned slab modifications.

**Q:** 191 Our tile supplier has delivered tiles that are 2mm thinner than specified (8mm instead of 10mm). The architect says this is "within manufacturing tolerance" and acceptable. Our concern is that the thinner tiles may be weaker. What does SANS 1449 say about thickness tolerance?
**A:** SANS 1449 ( ceramic tiles — definitions, classification, characteristics and marking ) adopts ISO 13006 for dimensional tolerances. For tile thickness: the standard allows ±5% of the nominal thickness OR ±0.5mm, whichever is greater. For a 10mm nominal tile: ±5% = ±0.5mm, so the tolerance range is 9.5mm to 10.5mm. An 8mm tile is 2mm below nominal — this is 20% under, far exceeding the ±5% tolerance. This is a DEFECT, not an acceptable variation. The architect is incorrect. The 2mm difference matters because: (1) strength — thinner tiles have lower breaking strength; (2) impact resistance — 20% less thickness means significantly less impact resistance; (3) coverage — you may have ordered insufficient adhesive quantity based on 10mm thickness; (4) transitions — adjacent tiles will have height differences. Action: reject the delivery; demand replacement with tiles within specification tolerance; if the supplier claims 8mm is their standard product, the specification was misinterpreted or misrepresented. Document the discrepancy with caliper measurements across multiple tiles. SANS 1449 is clear on tolerances — do not accept the architect's advice if it contradictes the standard. The architect may be liable for negligent advice if they approved out-of-tolerance tiles.

**Q:** 192 Our tiling contract was awarded to a joint venture (JV) between two contractors. One JV partner has gone into liquidation. The remaining partner says they cannot complete the work alone and want to terminate. What is our position?
**A:** A joint venture is typically a partnership for a specific project — both partners are jointly and severally liable for the contract obligations. If one partner liquidates: (1) the remaining partner remains liable for the FULL contract performance — they cannot unilaterally terminate due to their partner's insolvency; (2) if the remaining partner genuinely cannot complete the work (lacks capacity, resources, or skills), this may constitute impossibility of performance, allowing contract termination; (3) however, if the remaining partner is simply unwilling (not unable), they are in breach. Your options: (1) demand the remaining partner completes the work — they accepted the JV risk; (2) if they genuinely cannot, negotiate termination with damages (cost of completion by another contractor); (3) if they refuse to complete and refuse to pay damages, sue for breach of contract; (4) claim against the liquidated partner's estate as a creditor (unsecured, likely minimal recovery). For future JV contracts: (1) require both partners to provide separate performance bonds; (2) include a clause requiring immediate notification if either partner faces financial distress; (3) require the JV to nominate a lead partner with sole contact authority; (4) include a right to terminate and engage another contractor if the JV structure changes. The remaining partner's complaint about inability to complete alone is a project management issue, not your problem — unless they can prove genuine impossibility.

**Q:** 193 Our store in a rural Eastern Cape town has no municipal water supply. We rely on rainwater harvesting for the building. The tiling contractor needs water for mixing adhesive and cleaning. Who is responsible for providing water — us or the contractor?
**A:** Unless the contract specifically states otherwise, the contractor is responsible for providing all resources necessary to complete the work, including water for construction purposes. However: (1) if the site has no water supply and this was known or should have been known, the contractor should have priced for water delivery (tanker or containerised supply); (2) if you represented that water was available and it is not, you may be liable for the additional cost; (3) in rural South Africa, water scarcity is a known risk — a professional contractor should have assessed site conditions before tendering. Under JBCC and NEC contracts: (1) the employer typically provides "free issue" water and electricity for construction purposes if available on site; (2) if not available, the contractor provides at their cost. For your situation: if water was not discussed during tender, the contractor's position is stronger (they assumed water was available based on normal expectations); if the contractor visited the site and should have observed the lack of water supply, they should have priced for alternative sourcing. Negotiate a cost-sharing arrangement — the contractor delivers water at cost, or you allow use of your harvested rainwater (ensure it is potable quality for adhesive mixing — contaminated water affects adhesive performance). For future rural projects: include site condition information (water, power, access) in the tender documents to avoid disputes.

**Q:** 194 Our store's tiles were installed with a gradient for drainage (1:50). The gradient is barely perceptible but a customer has complained that it creates a "sloped floor" that makes them feel dizzy. They are threatening to sue for personal injury. Is this a valid claim?
**A:** A 1:50 gradient (2% slope) is standard for drainage and is imperceptible to most people — it is less than the slope of many nominally "flat" floors. For a personal injury claim to succeed, the claimant must prove: (1) the floor was dangerously sloped; (2) the slope caused their injury; (3) you were negligent in creating or maintaining the slope. A 1:50 gradient: (1) complies with SANS 10400 Part P (drainage) which requires minimum 1:100 for floor drainage; (2) is standard in commercial kitchens, bathrooms, and external areas; (3) is not considered hazardous by any flooring or accessibility standard. The claim is unlikely to succeed unless the claimant has a specific medical condition (vestibular disorder) that makes them unusually sensitive to slopes — and even then, they would need to prove you should have known of this sensitivity. Defence: (1) the gradient complies with all applicable standards; (2) the slope is necessary for drainage and safety (prevents standing water); (3) millions of people walk on 1:50 gradients daily without issue; (4) the claimant has not suffered a recognisable injury. Your public liability insurance will defend this — it is a frivolous claim. Ensure the gradient is consistent and free of trip hazards at the transition points.

**Q:** 195 Our national rollout includes stores in Botswana and Namibia (SADC expansion). Our South African tile supplier has quoted in ZAR but wants payment in USD due to currency volatility. Our procurement policy mandates ZAR-denominated contracts. Can the supplier insist on USD?
**A:** No — a supplier cannot unilaterally dictate currency. Currency is a contractual term that must be agreed upon. Your options: (1) insist on ZAR payment per your procurement policy — if the supplier refuses, source from a supplier who accepts ZAR; (2) negotiate a ZAR price with currency fluctuation protection (e.g., price valid for 30 days, with quarterly escalation based on USD/ZAR exchange rate movement within a band); (3) if USD is unavoidable (imported goods where the manufacturer invoices in USD), your procurement policy may need an exception for genuine foreign currency exposure. Legal considerations: (1) the South African Reserve Bank's Exchange Control Regulations regulate cross-border payments — ZAR payments to SADC countries are generally permitted; (2) if you pay in USD, you need exchange control approval for the currency conversion; (3) SARS treats foreign currency contracts as taxable events at the spot rate on transaction date. For SADC expansion: (1) establish local suppliers in Botswana and Namibia where possible — avoids currency and logistics issues; (2) if importing from South Africa, negotiate ZAR pricing with your South African supplier; (3) hedge currency exposure if USD is genuinely required. The supplier's desire for USD reflects their own currency risk — offer a compromise with shared risk rather than accepting full USD exposure.

**Q:** 196 Our store's emergency exit route passes through a tiled corridor. Fire Department inspection flagged that the tiles are "too slippery for emergency evacuation." The tiles meet SANS 10107 for normal use. What is the specific requirement for emergency egress slip resistance?
**A:** Emergency egress routes have specific slip resistance requirements beyond general flooring standards. SANS 10400 Part T (Fire Protection) and SANS 10114 (Interior lighting) address escape routes. For slip resistance in emergency egress: (1) SANS 10107:2018 recommends PTV ≥ 45 (wet) for escape routes — this is higher than the ≥ 36 for normal commercial areas; (2) the route must remain safe even when wet (sprinkler activation, fire hose use); (3) the route must not have level changes >6mm without bevelled edges; (4) the route must be clearly marked (photoluminescent strip or contrasting colour). Your tiles may meet SANS 10107 for normal use but fail the higher requirement for escape routes. Solutions: (1) apply anti-slip treatment to the escape route section; (2) install anti-slip tread strips at the escape route; (3) replace the escape route section with R11 or R12 rated tiles; (4) ensure the escape route is included in the annual slip resistance audit. The Fire Department's concern is valid — wet tiles during a fire emergency are extremely hazardous. Document your remediation and request re-inspection. Municipal fire bylaws may have additional requirements.

**Q:** 197 Our store's subfloor is a steel mezzanine deck (not concrete). The tiling contractor has never installed on steel and wants to pour a concrete topping first. The structural engineer says the steel deck cannot support the additional weight of a concrete topping plus tiles. What is the solution for tiling on steel substrates?
**A:** Tiling directly on steel requires a specialised system: (1) LIGHTWEIGHT SCREED — use a calcium sulphate or cement-based lightweight screed (minimum 15mm bonded) specifically designed for steel decks — these weigh 1,200-1,500 kg/m³ vs. 2,400 kg/m³ for normal concrete; (2) PLYWOOD SUBSTRATE — install marine-grade plywood (minimum 18mm) over the steel deck, then tile over the plywood using a flexible adhesive — this is lighter but requires the plywood to be securely fixed to prevent movement; (3) DIRECT STEEL ADHESION — some specialist epoxy adhesives can bond directly to prepared steel, but this is rare and requires expert application; (4) ACCESS FLOORING SYSTEM — install a raised access floor system with steel or aluminium panels, then tile the panels. The structural engineer's concern about weight is valid — steel mezzanines are designed for specific live loads. A typical 50mm concrete topping adds 120kg/m² — if the deck was not designed for this, overloading is a real risk. The lightweight screed option is usually the best compromise — engage a flooring specialist with steel deck experience. SANS 10109 does not specifically address steel substrates — this requires specialist design input. Ensure the steel deck has adequate deflection limits (L/360 minimum) and vibration damping for tiled finishes.

**Q:** 198 Our store has been built on dolomite land (Gauteng west rand area). A sinkhole has developed 50 metres from the store. The landlord has commissioned a geotechnical investigation but work continues in the store. Should we be concerned about the tile installation?
**A:** Dolomite land in Gauteng is well-known for sinkhole and subsidence risk (Centurion,parts of Randburg, Westonaria, Carletonville). A sinkhole 50 metres away is a serious concern: (1) dolomite instability can propagate — a sinkhole 50m away may indicate broader geological instability; (2) ongoing ground movement will stress any rigid floor finish, including tiles — you may see cracking, lipping, or hollow sounds; (3) the geotechnical investigation should include a Zone of Influence assessment — how far from the sinkhole is the ground potentially affected? (4) SANS 1936 (development of dolomite land) provides guidelines for construction on dolomite — your building should have been assessed during design. Recommendations: (1) do not install expensive finishes until the geotechnical investigation is complete and the building is certified as safe; (2) if the investigation identifies ongoing subsidence risk, consider flexible flooring (vinyl, carpet) rather than rigid tiles; (3) if tiles are installed, use a decoupling membrane to isolate the tile layer from subfloor movement; (4) ensure the building has appropriate foundations for dolomite (compensated foundations, raft foundations, or piles); (5) check that the building has sinkhole insurance — standard commercial policies often exclude sinkhole damage on known dolomite land. The Gauteng Department of Agriculture and Rural Development regulates dolomite risk management. Do not ignore sinkhole warnings — they can develop rapidly and catastrophically.

**Q:** 199 Our corporate procurement policy requires 30-day payment terms. Our tiling SME supplier has requested 7-day payment terms, claiming cash flow pressure from their own suppliers. Our B-BBEE strategy depends on this supplier. Can we legally shorten payment terms for a specific supplier, and does this affect our B-BBEE scorecard?
**A:** Yes, you can legally shorten payment terms for a specific supplier — there is no law mandating 30-day terms. In fact, shortening payment terms benefits the supplier and enhances your B-BBEE position. B-BBEE impact: (1) under Statement 400 (Enterprise and Supplier Development), early payment can qualify as a "supply chain development" contribution if structured through a formal supplier finance programme; (2) paying SMEs within 7 days instead of 30 significantly improves their cash flow and may count toward your ESD recognition; (3) the B-BBEE Codes encourage support for black-owned SMEs — accelerated payment is a recognised form of support. Legal considerations: (1) ensure the shortened terms are documented in a contract amendment; (2) ensure your accounts payable system can process within 7 days; (3) check if your corporate treasury policy allows exceptions to standard terms; (4) ensure the supplier provides valid tax invoices to claim input VAT within the prescribed period. The only caution: if the 7-day payment is so short that it creates administrative burden or you miss payments and incur penalties, it may strain the relationship. Consider 14-day terms as a compromise. The Prompt Payment Act (not yet fully enacted) encourages payment within 30 days — paying earlier is always permissible and encouraged.

**Q:** 200 Our store's tile floor has a hidden safe installed beneath a removable tile section (custom security feature). The landlord's maintenance contractor, unaware of the safe, grouted the tile section in place during a routine maintenance visit. We need to access the safe urgently but cannot remove the tiles without breaking them. Who is liable for the damage?
**A:** This is a fascinating factual scenario combining property law, contractor negligence, and security design. Liability analysis: (1) the LANDLORD'S CONTRACTOR is primarily liable — they performed unauthorised work (grouting a security feature that was not part of their maintenance scope) without checking or consulting; (2) the LANDLORD may be vicariously liable for their contractor's negligence; (3) YOU may share liability if the security feature was not documented in the building's maintenance manual or if there was no visible indication that the tile section was removable. Resolution: (1) the contractor must pay for tile removal and replacement to access the safe — this is not "maintenance" but unauthorised alteration; (2) if the contractor refuses, claim against the landlord (their contractor, their responsibility); (3) if you need urgent access, engage a specialist to cut the tiles carefully — document the process and claim costs. Prevention: (1) any hidden or removable features must be documented in the building maintenance file; (2) tile sections covering access points should be marked with subtle indicators or different grout colour; (3) maintenance briefings should highlight all non-standard features; (4) for future: use a magnetic or mechanical latch system for removable tile sections rather than dry-lay (unglued) installation. The contractor's insurer (public liability / professional indemnity) should respond to this claim — it was an avoidable error caused by failure to check the scope of work. If the tiles were custom or irreplaceable, the claim value may be significant.
