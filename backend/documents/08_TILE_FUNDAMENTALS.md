# Tile Fundamentals

## Purpose

Use this file when customers ask basic tile questions.

## Ceramic vs Porcelain

### Simple Answer

Porcelain and ceramic are both tile products, but porcelain is usually denser and has lower water absorption than standard ceramic.

Porcelain is commonly used for floors and higher-wear applications. Ceramic is often used for walls and lighter-duty areas, depending on the product.

### Safe Tori Answer

> Porcelain is generally denser and less absorbent than standard ceramic, so it is often used for floors and higher-wear areas. Ceramic can be excellent for walls and some floors if the product is rated for that use. Always check the specific product before buying.

## Porcelain Water Absorption

General industry definition:

- Porcelain is commonly treated as impervious tile with water absorption of 0.5% or less.
- Do not claim a specific tile is porcelain unless product data confirms it.

## Glazed vs Unglazed

Glazed tiles have a surface layer that provides colour, pattern and protection.

Unglazed or full-body products may have colour or composition through the tile body, depending on the product.

Safe answer:

> Glazed tiles have a surface finish on top. Unglazed or full-body products may be more consistent through the tile body, but the exact structure depends on the product. Check the product data sheet for the specific tile.

## Rectified vs Non-Rectified

Rectified tiles have mechanically finished edges. They can often be installed with narrower grout joints if the tile, substrate and installer allow.

Non-rectified tiles have more natural manufacturing tolerance and usually require wider grout joints.

Safe answer:

> Rectified tiles have straighter cut edges, which can create a cleaner modern look. They still need grout joints and a flat surface. The installer should confirm the suitable joint width.

## Polished, Matte and Structured

### Polished

Reflective surface. Can look premium but may show marks, scratches, water spots or slip risk depending on application.

### Matte

Lower reflection. Practical for many floors and bathrooms.

### Structured / Textured

More surface texture. Often considered for outdoor or wet-prone areas, but must still be confirmed for application.

Safe answer:

> Finish choice affects look, cleaning and slip risk. Do not choose only by appearance; confirm the tile is suitable for the area.

## V-Rating / Shade Variation

V-rating describes how much visual variation may appear between tiles.

- V1: Uniform appearance.
- V2: Slight variation.
- V3: Moderate variation.
- V4: Substantial/random variation.

Safe answer:

> A V-rating tells you how much the tiles may vary in colour, pattern or texture. V3 and V4 tiles should be viewed across several pieces and blended from multiple boxes during installation.

## Batch, Shade and Calibre

### Batch / Shade

Tiles from different production batches may vary in shade or tone.

### Calibre

Calibre relates to manufacturing size grouping.

Safe answer:

> Before installation, check product code, shade, batch, calibre and quantity. If labels are mixed or missing, stop and contact Tiletoria before fixing the tiles.

## Tile Faces

Tile faces are different printed designs within the same range.

More faces can make stone-look, marble-look, cement-look or wood-look tiles look more natural.

Safe answer:

> A tile range with several faces has more pattern variation. The installer should open and blend tiles from multiple boxes to avoid repeated patterns in one area.

## Grout Joints

Tiles need grout joints.

Never say tiles can be installed with no joint.

Safe answer:

> Tiles should not be installed hard against each other. Grout joints allow for tile tolerance, alignment and finishing. The correct width depends on the tile, edge type, size, substrate and installation method.

## Lippage

Lippage is when one tile edge sits higher than the tile next to it.

Risk increases with:

- Large format tiles.
- Uneven screeds.
- Poor adhesive coverage.
- Incorrect trowel technique.
- Excessive offset on rectangular tiles.
- No levelling system where needed.

Safe answer:

> Large format tiles need a flatter substrate and careful installation. A levelling clip system can help control lippage, but it cannot fix a bad substrate.

## Indoor vs Outdoor

Outdoor tiles must be selected with the full system in mind:

- Tile suitability.
- Surface texture.
- Slope.
- Drainage.
- Adhesive.
- Grout.
- Movement joints.
- Cleaning and algae maintenance.

Safe answer:

> Outdoor use is not only about the tile surface. Drainage, slope, adhesive, grout and movement joints matter.

## Wall Tile on Floor

Do not approve wall tile on floor unless product data confirms floor suitability.

Safe answer:

> Wall tiles should not be used on floors unless the product is specifically rated for floor use. Floor traffic can damage unsuitable wall tiles.

## Floor Tile on Wall

Floor tiles may be used on walls if weight, substrate, adhesive and fixing method are suitable.

Safe answer:

> Some floor tiles can be used on walls, but large or heavy tiles need the correct substrate, adhesive and installer confirmation.
