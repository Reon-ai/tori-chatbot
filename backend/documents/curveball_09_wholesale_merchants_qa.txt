# PERSONA 9: SMALLER FLOORING MERCHANTS (WHOLESALE) — CURVEBALL Q&A

---

## EXCLUSIVE DISTRIBUTION RIGHTS & TERRITORIAL PROTECTION

**Q:** 1. I stock a ceramic range from a supplier who now gave exclusivity to a new CTM branch 800 metres from me. My supply agreement never mentioned radius protection. Do I have any legal recourse under the CPA?
**A:** If your written supply agreement does not contain a radius or territorial exclusivity clause, you unfortunately have no contractual claim. The CPA does not create territorial protection where none was agreed. Going forward, negotiate an exclusive distribution rider specifying a minimum radius (e.g., 5 km for urban, 25 km for peri-urban) and tie it to your MOQ commitment. Have it registered as a restraint of trade only if it meets the reasonableness test under South African common law — it must protect a legitimate proprietary interest and be no wider than necessary.

**Q:** 2. My supplier wants me to sign a new agreement giving me exclusivity for Limpopo but demands I take 3,000 m² per quarter. That is 40% more than my current turnover. Can I challenge this under the Competition Act as an abuse of dominance?
**A:** An exclusivity arrangement tied to an excessive MOQ can potentially be assessed under section 8 of the Competition Act if the supplier holds a dominant position in the relevant market (typically >35% market share). However, exclusivity agreements between non-dominant parties are generally permissible. Rather than a Competition Commission complaint, negotiate a ramp-up period — e.g., 1,500 m² in Q1, scaling to 3,000 m² by Q4 — with a clawback clause if you miss target through no fault of your own. Document your market size in Limpopo to justify a lower threshold.

**Q:** 3. A competitor merchant claims his "verbal exclusivity" from 2019 for a imported porcelain brand overrides my signed 2023 written distributorship. He has WhatsApp messages but no contract. Which agreement prevails?
**A:** Under the Alienation of Land Act and general contract principles, distributorship agreements need not be in writing to be valid, but exclusivity terms must be clear and certain. A vague WhatsApp exchange promising "you're my guy in Gauteng" is unlikely to constitute a binding exclusivity clause. Your signed written agreement with explicit territorial terms will almost certainly prevail. Advise the competitor that oral exclusivity claims are extremely difficult to prove and recommend he formalise any arrangement going forward.

**Q:** 4. I am the sole stockist of a Turkish tile brand in the Eastern Cape. The manufacturer now sells directly to a large Port Elizabeth developer via their website at prices below my landed cost. Is this parallel importation lawful?
**A:** If the manufacturer is selling directly into your territory — bypassing you as the authorised distributor — this constitutes a breach of your distribution agreement if it contains exclusivity and/or prohibition-on-direct-sales clauses. Under South African law, parallel importation of genuine goods (grey imports) is generally lawful unless restricted by contract or the Consumer Protection Act. Check if your agreement contains a "no direct sales" clause. If yes, issue a lawyer's letter demanding cessation and claiming damages. If your agreement is silent, this is a harsh lesson: always include anti-circumvention provisions.

**Q:** 5. My supplier granted me "non-exclusive" distribution rights but advertises my region on their website showing "find a stockist" with only my competitor listed. They are effectively steering customers away from me. Is this a CPA issue?
**A:** This is arguably misleading conduct under section 41 of the CPA — if the supplier's conduct creates the impression that your competitor is the only or preferred stockist. Write to the supplier demanding equal listing treatment, with a reasonable time limit. If they refuse, lodge a complaint with the National Consumer Commission. Simultaneously, build your own direct marketing presence (Google Business Profile, local SEO) so you are not dependent on their website. Consider whether this breach is material enough to justify termination of your supply agreement.

## CONSIGNMENT STOCK & RISK ALLOCATION

**Q:** 6. I want a supplier to place R800,000 of stock on consignment in my warehouse — they own it until sold. But their consignment agreement makes me liable for theft, fire, and water damage. Is this fair under the CPA?
**A:** This is a standard risk allocation in consignment arrangements but the CPA may regulate it. Under section 48, a term that makes the consumer (you) liable for damage not caused by your negligence may be an unfair contract term. Negotiate: (a) liability only for damage caused by your negligence or wilful misconduct; (b) require the supplier to carry their own goods-in-transit or stock-throughput insurance; (c) include a cap on your liability (e.g., 10% of consignment value). Get your insurer to confirm coverage — your commercial policy may not extend to goods you do not own.

**Q:** 7. A consignment stock tile range has been in my showroom for 14 months with minimal sales. The supplier now demands I buy it or pay storage fees. Our agreement said nothing about time limits. Must I comply?
**A:** If the consignment agreement is silent on duration and rotation requirements, the supplier cannot unilaterally impose time limits or storage fees. However, they can terminate the consignment arrangement with reasonable notice (typically 30-60 days) and demand return of their stock. You should negotiate a written consignment agreement upfront specifying: maximum consignment period (e.g., 12 months), agreed rotation date, what happens on expiry (return at supplier's cost vs. conversion to sale at discounted price). Without written terms, you are both exposed.

**Q:** 8. Consignment stock was stolen in a warehouse break-in. My security system was off because of load shedding and the inverter failed. The supplier's agreement says I am strictly liable regardless of cause. Can load shedding be a defence?
**A:** Strict liability means you are liable regardless of fault — but load shedding as a defence depends on your agreement's force majeure clause. Eskom load shedding is increasingly considered a foreseeable event in South Africa, making it harder to claim force majeure unless the specific stage (e.g., Stage 6) was unforeseeable. Your best argument is that the supplier's strict liability clause is an unfair contract term under CPA section 48. Going forward, install a generator or solar backup for your alarm system — courts expect businesses to take reasonable precautions against known risks. Claim on your commercial insurance if you have stock coverage that extends to consignment goods.

**Q:** 9. I sold consignment stock to a customer but the customer hasn't paid me yet. The supplier wants payment NOW because the stock left my warehouse. Do I owe before I have been paid?
**A:** This depends entirely on your consignment agreement terms. Standard consignment arrangements trigger payment obligation at point of sale (to the end customer) or at a defined event (e.g., 30 days after delivery to customer, whichever is earlier). If your cash flow depends on customer payment first, negotiate "pay-when-paid" or "pay-if-paid" terms in your consignment agreement. Be aware that "pay-if-paid" clauses are generally unenforceable in construction contracts under the MBA/NEC standard forms, but consignment of goods is different — it is governed by your specific agreement. Get a commercial lawyer to draft protective terms.

**Q:** 10. My supplier's consignment agreement requires me to segregate their stock physically and mark it as "property of [supplier]" in my accounting system. This is administratively burdensome. Can I refuse?
**A:** No — these requirements exist to protect the supplier's ownership rights under the Insolvency Act and the concept of "recognition of ownership" (RoO). If you become insolvent, properly segregated and documented consignment stock does not form part of your insolvent estate. Without segregation and marking, the stock becomes your property on insolvency and the supplier ranks as an unsecured creditor. This is non-negotiable from the supplier's perspective. Implement proper stock management software that can handle consignment tracking — it protects both parties.

## WHITE-LABEL / PRIVATE LABEL

**Q:** 11. I want to import tiles from China and sell them under my own private label brand. Do I need NRCS (National Regulator for Compulsory Specifications) approval for every SKU, or can I batch-approve by factory?
**A:** Ceramic and porcelain tiles fall under the compulsory specification VC9006, regulated by the NRCS. You need a Letter of Authority (LOA) for each tile classification — but this is typically per factory/product line, not per individual SKU. The LOA covers the manufacturing facility's compliance with SANS 1449. You must also ensure SANS 10107 compliance for slip resistance classification. Engage an NRCS-accredited testing laboratory for initial type testing. Budget R15,000-R25,000 per product line for testing and LOA application. Your private label branding does not create a separate approval requirement if the underlying product is already approved.

**Q:** 12. My private label tile has my brand printed on the box but the factory code on the back of the tile itself. A customer is demanding to know the "real manufacturer" claiming CPA right to information. Must I disclose?
**A:** Under section 23 of the CPA, consumers have the right to disclosure of the business name and address of the supplier (you) and, for imported goods, the country of origin. There is no specific CPA requirement to disclose the OEM manufacturer's identity. However, if the customer claims a product defect and initiates legal action, you may need to disclose the manufacturer for third-party proceedings. Your box should comply with SANS labelling requirements: country of origin, your SA business details, SANS classification, and batch number. The factory code on the tile is for your traceability — it does not create a disclosure obligation.

**Q:** 13. I want to private-label porcelain tiles from a Spanish factory. What is the realistic minimum order quantity (MOQ) for factory-direct private label packaging?
**A:** For Spanish and Italian porcelain manufacturers, private label MOQs typically start at one full 20-foot container (approximately 800-1,000 m² depending on tile format and thickness). Some factories require a minimum of 2-3 containers per design for custom box printing. Custom private label packaging (your brand on the box, insert cards, your barcode) adds 4-6 weeks to production lead time and costs approximately EUR0.50-1.20 per square metre extra. Very few European factories will private label for less than one container — the setup costs for printing plates and line changeover are not economical below that threshold.

**Q:** 14. If my private label tile fails and causes property damage, am I liable as the "manufacturer" under the CPA, or can I pass liability to the OEM factory in China?
**A:** Under section 61 of the CPA, strict product liability attaches to the "producer," which includes the manufacturer, importer, and anyone who puts their own brand on goods produced by another. As the private label brand owner, YOU are the "producer" for CPA liability purposes and bear strict liability to the consumer. You can seek recourse against the Chinese manufacturer via your supply contract (indemnity clause), but enforcement against a Chinese entity is extremely difficult. Protect yourself: (a) product liability insurance with minimum R5 million cover; (b) stringent QC inspections before shipment (hire a third-party inspector like SGS or Bureau Veritas in China); (c) contractual indemnity from the manufacturer with an escrow or letter of credit holdback.

**Q:** 15. A competitor is selling what looks identical to my private label tile but under their brand at 20% less. I suspect the same Chinese factory is double-dealing. How can I prove it and what can I do?
**A:** This is a common problem. Obtain samples of their tile and have both tiles independently tested (XRF analysis, water absorption, dimensional accuracy). If the technical specifications match within manufacturing tolerance, you have circumstantial evidence of the same source. Check if your supply agreement with the Chinese factory contains exclusivity for the South African market — Chinese factories often ignore verbal exclusivity promises. Your legal remedies: (a) breach of contract claim against the factory (enforceability issues in Chinese courts); (b) if the competitor is making misleading representations about origin or quality, a complaint to the Advertising Regulatory Board; (c) focus on differentiating through service, warranty, and local stockholding rather than trying to enforce exclusivity across borders.

## CONTAINER-DIRECT ORDERING & IMPORTING

**Q:** 16. I want to import directly from an Italian tile factory and skip the local agent who adds 35% margin. Can the local agent stop me if I deal factory-direct?
**A:** The local agent can only stop you if they hold an exclusive distributorship agreement with the Italian factory that covers your territory. Contact the factory directly and ask: (a) do they have an exclusive agent/distributor for South Africa? (b) what are their terms for direct accounts? (c) can they quote FOB (Free On Board) Italian port? If an exclusive agreement exists, the factory may be contractually bound not to sell to you directly — though many factories will quietly sell direct if your volume is significant. Be prepared to handle your own importing: freight forwarding, marine insurance, customs clearance, VAT (15% on CIF value + duty), and NRCS compliance.

**Q:** 17. What is the all-in landed cost difference between buying from a local distributor vs. importing a 20-foot container direct from Spain, considering current rand volatility?
**A:** As a rule of thumb, local distributor pricing typically reflects: factory ex-works price + 25-40% distributor margin + local logistics + stockholding cost. Container-direct savings can be 15-25% on a like-for-like product IF you achieve sufficient volume. All-in landed cost components for a Spain container: (a) FOB price per m²; (b) ocean freight (currently $1,200-$2,500 per 20ft container depending on carrier and port); (c) marine insurance (0.15-0.3% of CIF); (d) customs duty on ceramic tiles (typically 0-8% depending on HS code and EU origin — check ITAC tariff); (e) VAT at 15% on (CIF + duty); (f) clearing agent fees (R3,500-R6,000); (g) inland transport from Durban/Cape Town port to your warehouse; (h) financing cost for 90-120 days capital tied up. Factor in 8-12% currency depreciation risk over a 3-month order cycle — this can erase your margin entirely.

**Q:** 18. I ordered a container direct from Turkey. The tiles arrived with the wrong shade — batch A54 instead of A53. The factory says "shade variation is normal in ceramics." My customer rejected the batch. Who bears the cost?
**A:** Check your purchase order for the shade/batch specification. If you specified shade A53 and received A54, this is a breach of contract. Under Incoterms (if FOB or CIF), risk transfers at the port of shipment, but the seller remains liable for conformity of goods. Document everything: photographs, your PO showing shade A53, the packing list showing A54, your customer's rejection letter. Your remedies: (a) reject the goods and claim refund (if shipment was under letter of credit, invoke the LC terms); (b) accept at a reduced price; (c) if the factory refuses, you will need to pursue international arbitration if your contract has an arbitration clause — otherwise you are looking at costly cross-border litigation. Always specify shade tolerance and require pre-shipment sample approval in your PO.

**Q:** 19. I want to import a full 40-foot high-cube container of porcelain tiles from India. How much can it hold and what is the weight limit I need to worry about for road transport from Durban?
**A:** A 40-foot HC container has internal volume of approximately 76 cubic metres. Porcelain tiles (10mm thick, 600x600mm) weigh approximately 18-20 kg per box (1.44 m²). You can fit approximately 1,100-1,200 boxes (1,600-1,700 m²) before hitting the road weight limit. The maximum gross weight for a 40ft container is 30,480 kg (including container tare of ~3,700 kg), giving you a payload of ~26,700 kg. At 20 kg/box, your maximum is ~1,330 boxes. Road transport from Durban to Gauteng on an interlink can handle this. If delivering to the Western Cape, check axle weight restrictions on the N2. Always obtain a weightbridge certificate before the container leaves the port.

**Q:** 20. The rand dropped 12% between my placing the order and the factory invoice being due 60 days later. My forward exchange contract only covered 8%. Can I legally cancel the order citing "commercial impossibility"?
**A:** Commercial impossibility (also called supervening impossibility of performance) requires that performance has become objectively impossible, not merely more expensive or less profitable. A currency depreciation, even 12%, does not render performance impossible — it makes it more costly. South African courts have consistently held that currency fluctuations are a normal commercial risk. Unless your contract contains a "material adverse change" or hardship clause, you cannot cancel. Your options: (a) negotiate with the factory for partial discount; (b) draw on an existing credit facility to cover the shortfall; (c) if the factory agrees, convert to a later delivery date hoping for rand recovery. Going forward, cover 100% of foreign currency exposure with forward exchange contracts or currency options.

## CLEARANCE & DISCONTINUED STOCK

**Q:** 21. My supplier offered me discontinued stock at 45% discount but with a "no returns, no warranty" clause. If a customer later claims these tiles are defective, am I still liable under the CPA despite the "no warranty" term?
**A:** Yes. Under section 56 of the CPA, all goods sold to consumers carry an implied 6-month warranty of quality regardless of any disclaimer. A "no warranty" clause on clearance stock is an unfair contract term and is void under section 48. However, your supplier's discount offer may reflect this risk — they are effectively transferring CPA liability to you. Protect yourself: (a) sell discontinued stock with full disclosure to the customer ("end-of-line, sold as-is, no batch continuity for future purchases"); (b) inspect the stock thoroughly before accepting; (c) negotiate a back-to-back warranty from the supplier (e.g., 12 months latent defect coverage); (d) keep your product liability insurance current.

**Q:** 22. I bought 5,000 m² of clearance porcelain at 50% off. The batch numbers show it is 4 years old. Is there a shelf life for uninstalled porcelain tiles?
**A:** Technically, porcelain tiles have no shelf life — they are fired at over 1,200°C and are chemically stable indefinitely if stored correctly (dry, protected from impact). However, adhesive recommendations change over time — the adhesive system specified 4 years ago may no longer be available. More critically, older stock may not meet current SANS revisions if the standard has been updated. Check the batch date against current SANS 1449 and whether NRCS LOA requirements have changed. The real risk is not tile degradation but batch matching: if you cannot supply matching tiles later, customers will be dissatisfied. Sell old batch stock with a clear advisory: "limited stock, no continuity guaranteed."

**Q:** 23. A supplier cleared out a range to me at 40% off but the same range is still on their website at full price. I planned to sell at a modest markup but now online customers see the "full RRP" and expect that quality. Can I be accused of passing off or misrepresentation if I sell clearance stock as current range?
**A:** You can sell the tiles as the current range if they are genuine, unaltered product. The issue is pricing representation: if you advertise "was R450/m², now R270/m²" and the supplier's website still shows R450/m², that is truthful. However, if the range has been officially discontinued and you represent it as current stock without disclosing it is end-of-line, that could be misleading under section 41 of the CPA. Best practice: sell as "while stocks last" or "limited clearance" — honest disclosure builds trust. You are not passing off if you are selling genuine branded goods (first sale doctrine applies in South Africa).

**Q:** 24. My supplier has a "first in, first out" (FIFO) policy and refuses to sell me clearance stock until I have rotated existing stock of the same range. Can they enforce this?
**A:** This depends on your supply agreement terms. A FIFO clause is standard in supplier-merchant agreements to prevent the merchant from cherry-picking clearance stock while holding regular-priced stock. If your signed agreement contains a FIFO or stock rotation clause, the supplier can enforce it. If no such clause exists, they cannot unilaterally impose it. However, consider the relationship dimension: abusing clearance offers while your regular stock sits unsold will damage your supplier relationship. Negotiate a balanced approach: commit to moving X% of existing stock within Y months in exchange for clearance allocation.

**Q:** 25. I purchased end-of-line mosaic tiles at clearance prices. When my tiler installed them, the sheet backing adhesive had degraded and the mosaics fell off the mesh. The supplier says "clearance stock, no warranty." What are my rights?
**A:** Mosaic mesh backing can degrade over time, especially if stored in humid conditions. Under section 56 of the CPA, the tiles must be reasonably suitable for their purpose. If the degradation occurred due to supplier storage conditions (before you took delivery), the supplier remains liable despite "clearance" status — the CPA warranty is non-waivable. However, if the degradation occurred in YOUR warehouse due to poor storage, liability shifts to you. Document: (a) delivery date and storage conditions; (b) manufacture date from batch code; (c) photographic evidence. If the tiles were delivered to you already degraded, demand replacement or credit. If you stored them poorly for 18 months before sale, the liability is yours.

## FINANCING & STOCK LOANS

**Q:** 26. My supplier offered me a R2 million stock loan at prime + 3% over 12 months, secured by a general notarial bond over my warehouse stock. What are the risks of signing a general notarial bond?
**A:** A general notarial bond (GNB) under the Security by Means of Movable Property Act gives the supplier a security interest over all your movable stock without taking possession. The risks: (a) the bond may prevent you from obtaining other stock finance (your bank may refuse a second bond); (b) on default, the supplier can apply for a court order to attach your stock — ALL stock, not just their brand; (c) the GNB must be registered at the Deeds Office to be effective against third parties (cost: R1,500-R3,000); (d) your other creditors may be prejudiced. Negotiate: (a) a specific notarial bond limited to the supplier's stock only; (b) a maximum bond amount rather than unlimited; (c) no personal suretyship if possible; (d) a right to substitute stock (sell slow lines, replace with fast lines).

**Q:** 27. A tile manufacturer is offering me 90 days interest-free stock financing but requires me to cede my debtor's book as security. Is this normal and what does it mean practically?
**A:** Debtor book cession (cession of trade receivables) is increasingly common in supplier financing arrangements but carries significant implications: (a) you must notify your customers that their debt has been ceded — this can damage relationships; (b) payments from ceded debtors must go into a controlled bank account; (c) if a ceded debtor pays you directly by mistake, you may be in breach; (d) the cession may trigger a default under your existing bank overdraft facility (check your loan covenants). Negotiate alternatives: (a) limited cession of specific invoices rather than entire book; (b) a pledge of stock instead; (c) personal suretyship for a capped amount. Get your accountant and lawyer to review the cession agreement before signing.

**Q:** 28. I took stock financing from a supplier and the interest rate is linked to prime. Prime has gone up 2% since I signed. My agreement has no cap on interest rate increases. Can I challenge this under the National Credit Act?
**A:** The National Credit Act (NCA) applies to credit agreements below a prescribed threshold (currently R0 — all credit agreements fall under NCA if the credit provider is in the business of providing credit). If your supplier is providing stock financing as a regular part of their business, the NCA applies and the interest rate is capped. For commercial credit agreements (juristic persons with asset value or turnover above R1 million), the NCA does not apply — your agreement is governed by common law. If the NCA does apply, the maximum interest rate for commercial credit is Repo x 2.2 + 5%. If you are a small business (below R1 million threshold), check if the NCA applies and calculate if prime + 3% exceeds the NCA cap. If the NCA does not apply, you have no statutory protection against interest rate increases — negotiate a cap in future.

**Q:** 29. A supplier is offering me "supplier credit" of R500,000 but the fine print says they can convert this to a cash-on-delivery (COD) arrangement with 7 days' notice if my payment history shows any late payment. Is this enforceable?
**A:** This is a standard credit supplier protection clause and is generally enforceable under South African contract law — provided the 7-day notice period is reasonable for you to arrange alternative finance. Seven days is arguably tight for a R500,000 facility; courts have held that termination notice must be reasonable in the circumstances. Challenge it: negotiate for 30 days' notice of credit withdrawal, or a grace period of one late payment per quarter before conversion to COD. Also check if this arrangement triggers the National Credit Act — if so, the supplier must follow NCA termination procedures which are more protective. Document all correspondence if they invoke this clause.

**Q:** 30. My bank refuses to extend my stock finance facility because my debtors book is concentrated among three large contractors who represent 60% of my turnover. The supplier wants me to pay cash. What financing alternatives exist for a smaller tile merchant?
**A:** Concentration risk is a real problem for smaller merchants. Alternatives: (a) invoice discounting with a specialist funder (Lulalend, Merchant Capital, Grovest) — they advance 70-85% of invoice value within 24-48 hours; (b) trade finance from an import specialist (Tradeflow, Investec Trade and Working Capital Solutions) — they pay your foreign supplier and you repay over 90-120 days; (c) asset-based lending against your property (if you own your premises); (d) BEE supplier development funding — if you have a Level 1-4 BEE partner, they may have access to enterprise development grants; (e) joining a buying group (e.g., Independent Buying Group in building materials) for collective bargaining power and shared credit facilities. Diversify your customer base to reduce concentration risk going forward.

## COMPETITOR OPENS NEARBY

**Q:** 31. Italtile is opening a full-range store 300 metres from my shop in Menlyn. My supplier also supplies Italtile and I am certain they will get better pricing due to volume. Do I have any rights as a smaller stockist?
**A:** Volume-based pricing differentiation is lawful under the Competition Act unless it constitutes price discrimination that substantially prevents or lessens competition (section 9). Large chains like Italtile negotiate significant volume rebates and centralised distribution efficiencies. As a smaller merchant, your strategy should be: (a) differentiate on service — contractors want immediate stock availability, not a weekend wait; (b) specialise in niches (e.g., commercial-grade porcelain, specialised mosaics) that the chain does not stock; (c) build deep contractor relationships with credit terms and delivery flexibility; (d) negotiate a "most favoured customer" clause in your supplier agreement ensuring you receive the same net pricing as any other stockist for equivalent volumes. Realistically, matching Italtile on price is a race to the bottom you will lose.

**Q:** 32. CTM opened nearby and is advertising my key porcelain range at what appears to be below cost. Can I report them for predatory pricing under the Competition Act?
**A:** Predatory pricing (selling below cost to eliminate competition) is prohibited under section 8 of the Competition Act, but proving it requires evidence that: (a) CTM is pricing below average avoidable cost or average variable cost; (b) they have a reasonable prospect of recouping losses through future monopoly pricing; (c) their behaviour substantially prevents or lessens competition. Large retailers often have supplier rebates and marketing contributions that reduce their net cost — what looks like below-cost selling may actually be profitable. Before lodging a Competition Commission complaint (which can take 2-3 years), gather evidence: obtain written quotes, estimate their landed cost, document the duration of the low pricing. A more practical response: focus on where CTM is weak — custom orders, technical advice, out-of-hours service.

**Q:** 33. A competitor merchant 2 km away is selling the same imported range as me but at prices 15% lower because they are importing container-direct while I buy through the local agent. Should I confront the local agent about their double-standard pricing?
**A:** The local agent is likely selling to the competitor at the same landed cost but the competitor's lower retail price reflects their decision to accept lower margin, not preferential pricing. Alternatively, the competitor may have achieved volume-based tier pricing. Before confronting the agent, anonymously obtain a quote from the competitor to confirm their actual selling price. Then approach the agent diplomatically: "I am losing business to a stockist who is undercutting me significantly on your range. Can we review my pricing tier?" Frame it as a mutual business risk — if you stop stocking their range, they lose a sales channel. Consider whether container-direct importing is viable for your volume — if you can achieve one container per year, the savings may justify bypassing the agent.

**Q:** 34. Both Italtile and CTM have opened within 1 km of me in the past 18 months. My turnover has dropped 35%. Can I claim "passing off" or unfair competition because customers confuse my independent store with theirs?
**A:** Passing off requires proof of: (a) goodwill in your business name/trading style; (b) misrepresentation by the competitor causing confusion; (c) damage to your goodwill. Italtile and CTM have distinctive branding that is unlikely to be confused with an independent merchant's store. However, if a competitor has adopted a name, colour scheme, or layout deliberately similar to yours, you may have a passing off claim. Unfair competition is not a standalone cause of action in South African law — you need a specific legal basis (CPA, Competition Act, trademark infringement). Your practical response: rebrand with a strong independent identity, invest in local SEO, and differentiate through specialisation rather than competing head-on with chains on price and range.

**Q:** 35. My landlord is also the developer of the shopping centre. He has given Italtile a 12-month rent-free period to anchor the centre but refuses me any rental concession despite my 8-year tenancy. Is this discriminatory treatment challengeable?
**A:** Under the Consumer Protection Act, tenants of commercial premises have certain protections against unfair contract terms, but landlords are generally free to offer different deals to different tenants — this is not unlawful discrimination under the Equality Act unless based on a prohibited ground (race, gender, disability). Anchor tenant incentives (rent-free periods, contribution to fit-out) are standard commercial practice in retail leasing. Your lease may contain a "most favoured tenant" clause — check this. If not, negotiate: request a rent reduction tied to a performance metric (e.g., if your turnover drops >20% due to Italtile opening), or negotiate a shorter lease term with a break clause. If the landlord refuses, consider relocating to a location with better tenant mix for independent merchants.

## E-COMMERCE & ONLINE SELLING

**Q:** 36. My supplier discovered I am selling their tiles on my website at prices 10% below my showroom price and has threatened to terminate my distributorship citing a breach of their "MAP" (Minimum Advertised Price) policy. Is MAP enforcement legal in South Africa?
**A:** Minimum Advertised Price (MAP) policies are a form of resale price maintenance. Under section 5 of the Competition Act, minimum resale price maintenance is a per se prohibited restrictive horizontal practice. However, the Competition Tribunal has recognised that MAP policies that merely restrict ADVERTISED prices (not actual selling prices) may be treated differently from hard RPM. This is a grey area in South African competition law. Practically: (a) check if your supply agreement actually contains a MAP clause — if not, the supplier cannot unilaterally impose it; (b) if the clause exists, its enforceability is uncertain; (c) the more serious risk is termination of your distributorship — the supplier can terminate for any lawful reason or no reason (if your contract allows termination on notice). Negotiate: remove online pricing entirely and use "contact us for pricing" or "get a quote" instead.

**Q:** 37. I want to sell tiles on Takealot's marketplace. Their commission is 12-15% and they handle delivery. My supplier says I cannot sell their brand on third-party platforms. Can they legally restrict this?
**A:** Yes — your supplier can restrict authorised sales channels in your distributorship agreement. Under South African contract law, a supplier can specify where and how their brand is sold. If your agreement contains a channel restriction ("authorised sales channels: physical showroom only"), selling on Takealot is a breach. If no such restriction exists, the supplier cannot retroactively impose it without your consent. However, consider the strategic dimension: Takealot returns are notoriously high for building materials (customers order wrong quantities, wrong colours), and the 12-15% commission plus your margin plus supplier price may make the product uncompetitive. Many tile suppliers also have concerns about brand positioning on discount platforms.

**Q:** 38. A customer bought tiles from my online store, collected from my warehouse, and now wants to return them under the CPA "cooling off" provision for online purchases. But the CPA 5-day cooling-off only applies to direct marketing. Does it apply to my online store?
**A:** Section 16 of the CPA gives consumers a 5-business-day cooling-off right for transactions resulting from direct marketing. If the customer found your online store through organic search and purchased without any direct marketing (no email, SMS, or phone call from you initiating the transaction), the cooling-off right does NOT apply. However, section 44 gives consumers a general right to return defective or unsafe goods, and section 55 requires goods to be fit for purpose. Your online terms should clearly state that purchases are not direct marketing transactions and the cooling-off period does not apply. Still, offer a reasonable returns policy for unopened stock — it builds customer confidence.

**Q:** 39. I have an e-commerce website but the supplier's product images are copyrighted. Can I use their photos on my site, or must I take my own?
**A:** Product photographs provided by the supplier are typically licensed to authorised distributors for marketing purposes. Check your distributorship agreement for a marketing licence or image use clause. If silent, request written permission to use their images on your e-commerce platform — most suppliers will grant this as it drives sales. If you are NOT an authorised distributor, using their copyrighted images without permission constitutes copyright infringement under the Copyright Act. Taking your own photos is always safest but expensive for a full range. A practical solution: use the supplier images where permitted, and for clearance/private label stock, take your own photos. Watermark supplier images with your own branding to prevent competitors from copying them.

**Q:** 40. A competitor merchant copied my entire website layout, product descriptions, and pricing structure. They even used my Google reviews screenshots on their site. What can I do?
**A:** Multiple potential claims: (a) copyright infringement — your original product descriptions and website content are protected by copyright; (b) passing off — if their site creates confusion with yours; (c) unlawful competition — if they are free-riding on your marketing investment; (d) Google review screenshots used without permission may violate Google's terms of service and constitute misleading marketing under the CPA. Send a cease-and-desist letter from an attorney demanding removal within 7 days. If they refuse, you can lodge a complaint with the Advertising Regulatory Board for misleading advertising and consider litigation for copyright infringement and passing off. Document everything with screenshots (timestamped via Wayback Machine). Budget R15,000-R40,000 for legal action.

## SOCIAL MEDIA MARKETING

**Q:** 41. My supplier promised co-op marketing funds (1% of purchases) but has only paid out R2,000 of the R18,000 I am owed over 3 years. They say I didn't submit claims "in the prescribed format." Can I enforce this?
**A:** Co-operative marketing contributions are typically governed by a Marketing Development Fund (MDF) agreement or clause in your supply agreement. Check: (a) is the contribution automatic or claim-based? (b) what is the claim format and deadline? (c) is there a time limit on claims (e.g., "claims must be submitted within 90 days of quarter end")? If you missed claim deadlines, the supplier may be within their rights to refuse payment — this is common with MDF funds. However, if there was no clear claim process defined, you can argue the supplier's refusal is unfair. Going forward: (a) get the MDF terms in writing; (b) submit claims quarterly with proper documentation (invoices, screenshots, analytics); (c) request automatic credit notes instead of reimbursement claims.

**Q:** 42. I hired a local Instagram influencer to promote my tile business. She visited my showroom and posted negative content claiming our prices were "ridiculous" compared to CTM. Can I sue her for defamation?
**A:** If her statement that your prices are "ridiculous" is a statement of opinion based on disclosed facts (she compared specific prices), it is likely fair comment and protected. If she made false statements of fact (e.g., "these tiles are fake imports" when they are genuine), that could be defamatory. However, suing an influencer is expensive and generates further negative publicity. Your better course: (a) respond professionally in the comments explaining your value proposition; (b) if the post contains demonstrably false facts, request a correction via the influencer's management or the platform; (c) for future influencer partnerships, use written agreements specifying content approval rights, factual accuracy requirements, and non-disparagement clauses. Consider this a lesson in influencer vetting.

**Q:** 43. My supplier shared a Facebook post of a stunning installation using their tiles — but the installation was done by my competitor merchant who is also their stockist. The post implies my competitor is the "preferred" installer. Can I demand equal social media exposure?
**A:** Unless your supply agreement contains a clause guaranteeing equal marketing exposure (highly unlikely), the supplier has no obligation to feature you equally on their social media. However, this is a relationship issue: raise it with your supplier relationship manager. Frame it constructively: "I noticed a competitor was featured — I have some excellent installation photos I would love to share for your social media." Build a portfolio of professional installation photography and proactively share it. Many suppliers welcome quality content. Also build your own social media following — your owned channels are more valuable than anything the supplier does. Consider whether your competitor earned that feature through better engagement or relationship management.

**Q:** 44. I want to run Facebook ads targeting my competitor's followers. Is this allowed under South African advertising law?
**A:** Targeting competitor followers on social media is not unlawful per se — it is standard digital marketing practice. However, you must not: (a) use the competitor's trademark in your ad copy without permission (trademark infringement); (b) make false comparative claims ("we are 30% cheaper than Competitor X" unless demonstrably true and current); (c) use the competitor's branded content or images without permission. Your ads should focus on your own value proposition. The Advertising Regulatory Board Code of Advertising Practice requires honesty and substantiation of claims. Retargeting users who visited a competitor's website is legal but may be blocked by some platforms' policies — use interest-based and lookalike audience targeting instead.

**Q:** 45. A contractor posted a video on TikTok of a failed tile installation using tiles purchased from me. The video went viral with 200,000 views and comments blaming my tiles. The actual cause was the tiler's poor workmanship. What should I do?
**A:** This is a reputation management crisis. Do NOT engage in the comments section with accusations against the tiler — this will escalate. Instead: (a) contact the contractor directly and politely, requesting a correction or follow-up video explaining the actual cause; (b) offer to send a technical representative to assess the installation and provide a professional report; (c) if the contractor refuses, post a calm, factual response on your own channels explaining tile installation requirements without naming the contractor; (d) engage a reputation management service to push positive content up in search results; (e) consider legal action for defamation only if the contractor made objectively false statements of fact with intent to damage your business — but litigation is slow and public. Prevention: encourage satisfied customers to post positive reviews and installation videos.

## STAFF TRAINING & CPD

**Q:** 46. My showroom staff need product knowledge training but my supplier says I must pay R3,500 per person for their training course. Is there any government or SETA funding available for this training?
**A:** Yes — the Construction Education and Training Authority (CETA) and the Fibre Processing and Manufacturing SETA (FP&M SETA) both offer learnerships and skills programmes relevant to the building materials sector. If you are levy-paying (your payroll exceeds R500,000 annually), you can claim Mandatory Grant funding (20% of your skills levy) by submitting a Workplace Skills Plan (WSP) and Annual Training Report (ATR) by 30 April each year. For non-levy payers, discretionary grants may be available. Additionally, the supplier should provide product training as part of their merchant support — negotiate this as a condition of your distributorship, especially if you are achieving your sales targets. Document training attendance for BEE Skills Development points if applicable.

**Q:** 47. A customer slipped and fell on tiles in my showroom and is suing me. They claim my staff should have warned them the tiles were slippery when wet. Does my staff training on slip resistance actually reduce my legal liability?
**A:** Yes — demonstrating that your staff received proper training on slip resistance ratings (R9-R13 under SANS 10107/DIN 51130) and that they proactively advise customers on appropriate tile selection for wet areas creates a strong defence. Under the Occupational Health and Safety Act, you owe a duty of care to visitors to your premises. If you can show: (a) staff were trained to explain slip ratings; (b) the customer was advised of the slip rating; (c) signage was present; (d) the customer signed an acknowledgement — your liability is significantly reduced. However, for showroom floors themselves, ensure they meet minimum R10 slip resistance (wet) to prevent accidents on your own premises.

**Q:** 48. I sent my staff for a CPD-accredited course on tile installation techniques. Can I use this training to justify higher pricing to customers, and does it give me any marketing advantage?
**A:** CPD-accredited training demonstrates professional competence and can be a powerful marketing differentiator — especially when competing against mass-market retailers whose staff lack technical depth. You can market this as: "Our showroom consultants are CPD-accredited in tile specification and installation." However, be careful not to overstate the training: CPD courses provide general knowledge, not the specific expertise of a qualified tiler or architect. Do not claim your staff are "certified tile installers" unless they hold an artisan qualification (Red Seal or equivalent). The real marketing value is trust: customers are more likely to buy from consultants who can explain technical differences between porcelain grades, adhesive systems, and grouting options.

**Q:** 49. My supplier's training manual is 8 years old and references adhesive products that are no longer available. If my staff follow this outdated training and specify the wrong adhesive, who is liable for a failed installation?
**A:** You are. As the employer, you are responsible for ensuring your staff have current, accurate product knowledge. Relying on an 8-year-old training manual is negligent. The supplier may share some liability if they provided the outdated material knowing it was obsolete, but your primary duty is to your customer. Update your training materials: request current technical datasheets from all your adhesive suppliers (Mapei, Weber, TAL, Tile Tucker), compile an internal specification guide, and review it quarterly. Subscribe to product update notifications from your key suppliers. If a supplier's training is consistently outdated, raise it at management level — it reflects poorly on their brand and puts their entire dealer network at risk.

**Q:** 50. Can I claim a tax deduction for staff training expenses, including travel and accommodation for an out-of-town training course?
**A:** Yes. Under section 11(a) of the Income Tax Act, expenditure incurred in the production of income is deductible if it is not of a capital nature. Staff training expenses — including course fees, travel, accommodation, and meals — are generally fully deductible as operational expenses. If the training leads to a formal qualification or long-term skill (e.g., a diploma in construction management), SARS may argue it is capital in nature and disallow the deduction — though this is rare for short courses. Keep all invoices and proof of attendance. Skills Development Levy payments are also deductible. If you receive a SETA grant for the training, the grant is taxable income but the training expense remains deductible.

## POS DISPLAYS & MERCHANDISING

**Q:** 51. My supplier provided display stands on loan but now wants them back after I decided to stop stocking their brand. The stands are bolted to my floor and integrated into my showroom layout. Do I have to remove them?
**A:** Check your display stand agreement (often buried in the supply terms). Standard supplier display agreements state that stands remain the supplier's property and must be returned on termination, in good condition, at the merchant's cost. If they are bolted to the floor, they may constitute fixtures (permanently attached to the property) and ownership may have transferred to you or your landlord under the doctrine of fixtures. However, most suppliers include a "retention of ownership" clause overriding this. Practical approach: negotiate — offer to purchase the stands at written-down value, or request the supplier arrange removal at their cost. If you refuse to return them, the supplier can claim the replacement value and potentially offset it against any credit owed to you.

**Q:** 52. A supplier's sample boards are taking up 15% of my showroom wall space but represent only 3% of my sales. I want to return them but the supplier says they are on permanent loan and cannot be returned without 12 months' notice. Is this reasonable?
**A:** This depends on your written agreement. If the supplier provided sample boards under a "permanent display" clause with a notice period, it is enforceable. However, 12 months is arguably excessive — 3 months is more standard. Negotiate: give written notice of your intent to delist the brand or reduce display space, propose a 3-month wind-down, and offer to retain only the top-selling lines. If the supplier refuses, you must honour the agreement or risk termination of your supply terms. Going forward, negotiate display terms separately from supply terms: specify maximum display space, right to return slow-moving boards, and shorter notice periods. Track display space ROI — each square metre of wall space should generate proportional revenue.

**Q:** 53. My supplier promised a "shop-in-shop" concept with branded display units and lighting but has delivered nothing in 18 months despite repeated promises. Can I withhold payment or claim damages?
**A:** Unless your supply agreement specifically binds the supplier to deliver the shop-in-shop by a specific date, verbal promises are difficult to enforce. Check: (a) is there a written shop-in-shop agreement with milestones? (b) were the promises made in emails or formal proposals? (c) did you incur costs in anticipation (e.g., prepared floor space, electrical points)? If you have documented promises and incurred costs, you may have a claim for promissory estoppel or breach of collateral agreement. However, withholding payment for stock already delivered is a breach of your supply agreement and exposes you to termination and legal action. Better approach: write a formal letter of demand with a reasonable deadline, then escalate to the supplier's managing director. If unresolved, consider whether the relationship is worth maintaining.

**Q:** 54. A supplier's POS display includes a digital screen that plays promotional videos. The screen stopped working after 6 months. The supplier says warranty was only 3 months. Who pays for repair?
**A:** Under the CPA, goods supplied to a consumer (you, as the merchant, are a consumer of the supplier's display equipment) carry an implied 6-month warranty of quality for defects that existed at delivery. If the screen failure is due to a manufacturing defect (not physical damage or power surge), the supplier must repair or replace within 6 months of delivery. After 6 months, the implied CPA warranty expires but you may still have contractual warranty rights if the supplier offered a longer period. Negotiate: electronic equipment should carry at least a 12-month warranty. Going forward, specify warranty terms for all POS equipment in your display agreement. Consider whether it is worth repairing — a replacement 24-inch commercial display costs R2,500-R4,000.

**Q:** 55. I moved my showroom to a new location. A supplier refuses to relocate their branded display unit, saying relocation is my cost. But the unit was provided free originally. Can they charge me for the move?
**A:** This depends entirely on your display agreement terms. Most supplier-provided POS agreements state: "delivery and initial installation at supplier's cost; relocation at merchant's cost." If your agreement is silent, you bear the cost as the party requesting the move — the supplier has no obligation to pay for your relocation. However, you can negotiate: if you are a significant stockist, the supplier may contribute to relocation as a goodwill gesture. Alternatively, offer to return the unit and request a new one for the new location — some suppliers will provide new displays for relocated or expanded showrooms. Document everything: photograph the unit before and after the move to prove it was not damaged during relocation.

## RETURNS POLICY & SLOW-MOVING STOCK

**Q:** 56. My supplier allows returns of slow-moving stock within 12 months but charges a 25% restocking fee. Is this restocking fee enforceable under the CPA?
**A:** The CPA regulates terms between supplier and CONSUMER, not B2B transactions between supplier and merchant. Your supplier-merchant relationship is governed by contract. A 25% restocking fee is commercially standard for slow-moving stock returns — it covers the supplier's handling, inspection, and remarketing costs. It is enforceable if it is in your written agreement. If no restocking fee was agreed, the supplier cannot unilaterally impose one. Negotiate: a tiered restocking fee (15% if returned within 6 months, 25% after 12 months) or a maximum annual return allowance (e.g., 10% of purchases). The key is getting slow-moving stock return terms in writing before you place large orders.

**Q:** 57. I have R400,000 of tiles that have been in stock for 2 years. My supplier has discontinued the range and refuses returns. Is there any way to recover value?
**A:** Discontinued stock with no return rights is your loss unless you can find alternative disposal channels: (a) clearance sales at heavy discount (expect 40-60% markdown); (b) export to neighbouring countries (Botswana, Namibia) where the range may still have demand; (c) donate to a registered PBO (Public Benefit Organisation) for a section 18A tax deduction — the deduction is limited to cost price, not selling price; (d) job lot auctions — expect 10-20 cents in the rand; (e) repurpose as feature walls, outdoor cladding, or creative installations where shade matching is less critical. Going forward, negotiate a right of return for discontinued lines and set internal stock age limits (e.g., automatic markdown at 9 months, clearance at 12 months).

**Q:** 58. A customer returned unopened tiles after 4 months. My supplier's returns policy says "returns within 30 days only." The customer is threatening CPA action. Who is correct?
**A:** The CPA gives consumers the right to return goods that are defective or unsafe within 6 months (section 56). However, the CPA does not give consumers an automatic right to return non-defective goods simply because they changed their mind — unless the transaction was a result of direct marketing (5-day cooling off). For non-defective returns, your own returns policy applies, provided it is fair and communicated at point of sale. A 30-day return policy for non-defective goods is reasonable and enforceable. However, if the tiles were defective (shading issues, dimensional inaccuracy, cracking), the 6-month CPA warranty overrides your 30-day policy. Assess the actual reason for return before deciding.

**Q:** 59. A batch of tiles I sold to a contractor was later found to have a factory glaze defect. I gave the contractor a credit. My supplier says the defect claim period has expired (6 months from delivery to me). Can I force them to reimburse me?
**A:** This depends on your supply agreement's defect claim clause. Standard supplier terms require notification of defects within 30-90 days of delivery to you. However, under the CPA, latent defects (defects not discoverable on reasonable inspection at delivery) can be claimed within 6 months by the end consumer, and this liability flows up the chain. Your position: (a) if the defect was not discoverable on reasonable inspection when you received the stock, argue it is a latent defect; (b) if the supplier's 6-month limitation is shorter than the CPA's 6-month warranty, the CPA prevails for consumer sales; (c) check if the supplier offers a "dealer protection" policy covering post-sale defects. Going forward: negotiate a 12-month back-to-back warranty from your suppliers and consider product liability insurance.

**Q:** 60. My supplier accepts returns but issues credit notes that can only be used against future purchases, not refunded in cash. I need the cash for operational expenses. Is this legal?
**A:** This is a standard supplier practice — credit notes against future purchases rather than cash refunds. It is legal under South African contract law if your supply agreement specifies this. The rationale is that suppliers want to maintain the trading relationship, not fund your cash flow. However, you can negotiate: (a) a cash refund option with an administrative fee (e.g., 5%); (b) credit note conversion to cash after 90 days if not utilised; (c) a maximum credit note balance beyond which cash refunds are automatic. If the supplier refuses and you genuinely need cash flow, consider selling the credit note to a trade credit broker (at a discount) or using it to settle other obligations with the same supplier if they allow cross-utilisation.

## DAMAGED IN TRANSIT

**Q:** 61. A container arrived from Spain with visible seal tampering. On opening, 30% of pallets showed water damage from what appears to be a leak in the vessel's hold. The shipping line says damage occurred before loading. The factory says it was seaworthy. Who is responsible?
**A:** Under CIF (Cost Insurance Freight) Incoterms, risk transfers to you at the port of loading in Spain. However, the seller is responsible for procuring marine insurance covering the transit. File a claim with the marine insurance underwriter immediately — they will investigate and subrogate against the shipping line if the carrier was at fault. Also: (a) photograph everything before moving stock; (b) get a surveyor's report (most marine policies require this for claims above a threshold); (c) notify the shipping line in writing within 3 days of delivery (standard maritime time limits); (d) check the Bill of Lading for any carrier disclaimers. If insurance does not cover the full loss, you may have a claim against the carrier under the Carriage of Goods by Sea Act, but maritime claims are complex and time-barred after one year.

**Q:** 62. My supplier delivered tiles on a Friday afternoon. I signed the delivery note "unchecked" because I was busy. On Monday, I discovered 15% breakage. The supplier says "signed delivery note, no claim." Am I stuck?
**A:** Signing "unchecked" or "not examined" on a delivery note does NOT waive your right to claim for transit damage. Under South African law, a delivery note signature acknowledges receipt of the goods in the quantities stated, not their condition. Notify the supplier in writing within a reasonable time (ideally within 48 hours of discovery, but up to 7 days is generally acceptable for concealed damage). Provide photographic evidence of the damage and the packaging condition. However, if you signed the delivery note without ANY qualification and the packaging showed no external damage, the supplier may argue the damage occurred after delivery (in your handling). Going forward: always inspect deliveries before signing, or note "unchecked — subject to inspection" on the delivery note.

**Q:** 63. A supplier's truck was hijacked en route to my warehouse. The tiles were insured by the supplier but their insurer is refusing to pay because the driver stopped at an unauthorised location. The supplier wants me to pay for the replacement stock. Do I owe them?
**A:** Under standard supply terms, risk transfers at the point of delivery to you. If the hijacking occurred before delivery (truck still en route), the supplier bears the loss unless your agreement specifies otherwise. The supplier's insurance dispute with their insurer is not your problem — their failure to insure adequately or their driver's negligence does not shift liability to you. Refuse to pay and reference your delivery terms. If the supplier persists, request: (a) proof that delivery was completed (signed delivery note); (b) a police case number for the hijacking; (c) their insurance policy terms. If the supplier cannot deliver, you may need to source elsewhere and claim damages for non-delivery — but only if the supplier was in breach of a firm delivery commitment.

**Q:** 64. I received a container where the inner plastic wrapping was intact but the tiles inside had hairline cracks — consistent with compression damage during transit. The freight forwarder says "packaging intact, no claim." What is my recourse?
**A:** "Packaging intact" is not a valid defence if the goods inside are damaged due to improper packaging, overstacking, or rough handling. Hairline cracks in tiles despite intact inner wrapping suggest: (a) inadequate packaging design for the tile thickness; (b) overstacking of pallets in the container; (c) shock during loading/unloading. Your claim: (a) under marine cargo insurance (file a claim with photos, packing list, and surveyor report); (b) against the carrier for negligent handling (if you can prove it); (c) against the supplier for inadequate packaging (if the packaging was insufficient for the transport method). Obtain a third-party inspection report from a cargo surveyor — most insurers require this. Cost: R2,500-R5,000, but it is essential for a successful claim.

**Q:** 65. Tiles delivered by a courier company were left at my gate because nobody was present. They were stolen overnight. The delivery note says "left at premises — signature not required." Who bears the loss?
**A:** If the supplier arranged delivery and the courier was their agent, risk has not transferred to you until delivery to your designated delivery point with your acknowledgement. "Left at the gate" is not proper delivery to a business premises — the courier had a duty to obtain a signature or leave the goods in a secure location with your explicit authorisation. Demand replacement from the supplier. If the supplier refuses, you have a claim for non-delivery. However, if YOU specified "leave at gate if unattended" or authorised release without signature, you bear the risk. Going forward: require signed delivery for all stock deliveries; install a receiving bay with CCTV; specify delivery windows when staff are present. For smaller courier deliveries, use a lockable parcel box.

## REST OF AFRICA EXPORT

**Q:** 66. A contractor in Gaborone wants me to supply 8,000 m² of porcelain tiles. What export documentation do I need and how does VAT work on exports from South Africa?
**A:** Exports from South Africa are zero-rated for VAT (0% VAT charged) provided you have the correct documentation. Requirements: (a) export invoice showing zero VAT with statement "supplied in terms of section 11(1)(a) of the VAT Act"; (b) SAD 500 customs declaration processed through Customs at the border; (c) proof of export ( Customs exit stamp or electronic confirmation); (d) transport document (CMR, waybill, or bill of lading). The Botswana importer pays Botswana customs duty (typically 0-20% depending on HS code and SADC origin rules) and 12% VAT in Botswana. If the tiles qualify as SADC origin (manufactured in a SADC country), present a SADC Certificate of Origin for preferential duty. Use a customs broker experienced in SADC trade — the Beitbridge or Skilpadshek border procedures can be complex.

**Q:** 67. I sold tiles to a customer in Mozambique who paid 50% deposit in USD cash and will pay the balance on delivery in Pula. Is this legal under South African exchange control regulations?
**A:** South African exchange control regulations (administered by the South African Reserve Bank via Authorised Dealers) require that all foreign currency receipts be surrendered to an Authorised Dealer (your bank) and converted to rand unless you hold a Foreign Currency Account (FCA). Receiving USD cash is problematic: (a) cash USD transactions above $10,000 must be reported to SARB; (b) you cannot hold foreign cash indefinitely — it must be declared and converted; (c) accepting payment in Pula is an import of foreign currency — same surrender requirement. Your options: (a) open a Business Foreign Currency Account and deposit foreign currency receipts there (permitted for exporters); (b) require payment in ZAR via electronic transfer to your SA bank account — let the customer handle currency conversion; (c) use a trade finance house to handle the cross-border payment. Consult your bank's forex desk before proceeding.

**Q:** 68. A Namibian developer wants me to supply tiles on 90-day credit terms. How do I protect myself against non-payment when the contract is governed by South African law but the customer is in Namibia?
**A:** Cross-border credit sales are high-risk. Protections: (a) credit insurance through a specialist like Coface or Euler Hermes — they cover export credit risk for a premium of 0.5-2% of invoice value; (b) documentary collection via your bank — the bank holds documents (bill of lading, invoice) until payment is received; (c) letter of credit from a Namibian bank — the safest but most expensive option (1-2% LC fee); (d) personal suretyship from the Namibian company's directors, enforceable in SA if they have SA assets; (e) retention of title clause in your contract — you retain ownership until payment, and register this on the Namibia Securities Registry if applicable. For enforcement, a South African court judgment can be registered in Namibia under the reciprocal enforcement of civil judgments regime. Budget R30,000-R80,000 for cross-border debt recovery.

**Q:** 69. I want to export clearance stock to Zambia. The tiles are branded with my supplier's name. Does the supplier's trademark rights extend to Zambia, and can they stop me?
**A:** Trademark rights are territorial. If your supplier has not registered their trademark in Zambia, they cannot enforce it there. However, international brands typically register in key African markets. Check the Zambian Patents and Companies Registration Agency (PACRA) trademark database. If the mark is registered, you need the supplier's written authorisation to export to Zambia — otherwise it is trademark infringement. If unregistered, you can export under the first sale doctrine (exhaustion of rights). However, consider the relationship: exporting clearance stock to Africa may undermine your supplier's planned expansion into that market. They may terminate your SA distributorship if they discover unauthorised exports. Best practice: request written export authorisation — most suppliers will grant it for clearance stock.

**Q:** 70. A Zimbabwean customer wants to pay me in ZiG (Zimbabwe Gold). Can I accept this currency and how do I account for it in my South African tax return?
**A:** The ZiG is a relatively new currency and its convertibility and stability are uncertain. Under South African exchange control, you must convert foreign currency receipts to rand through an Authorised Dealer. The ZiG is not freely convertible on international markets, making this difficult. Practical considerations: (a) your SA bank may not accept ZiG deposits; (b) the exchange rate is volatile and official vs. parallel rates differ significantly; (c) SARS requires you to declare foreign income at the spot exchange rate on the date of receipt. Your options: (a) require payment in USD via a recognised correspondent bank; (b) use a regional payment platform like EcoCash or Mukuru (verify regulatory compliance); (c) structure the transaction as a CIF delivery to the SA border, with the Zimbabwean customer arranging their own cross-border transport. Accepting ZiG directly is not recommended for South African VAT and exchange control compliance.

## CURRENCY HEDGING & PRICING STABILITY

**Q:** 71. My European supplier quotes in euros but settles in USD (their preference). My bank only offers ZAR/EUR forward contracts, not ZAR/USD. Can I force the supplier to invoice in EUR?
**A:** No — the supplier can choose their invoicing currency. If they invoice in USD, you need ZAR/USD hedging. Your options: (a) request your bank to provide ZAR/USD forwards — all major South African banks (Standard Bank, FNB, Nedbank, ABSA) offer this; (b) use a non-deliverable forward (NDF) if there are regulatory constraints; (c) negotiate with the supplier to invoice in EUR — some will accommodate if you commit to volume, but they are not obligated; (d) accept USD invoicing and hedge via a synthetic forward using EUR/USD and ZAR/EUR cross-rates (complex, requires treasury expertise). The USD invoicing may actually help if your exports to Africa are USD-denominated — natural hedge.

**Q:** 72. My supplier offers "price stability" — a fixed ZAR price for 6 months if I commit to a forecast. But if the rand strengthens, I am locked into a higher price. Can I negotiate a collar or band instead?
**A:** Yes — a currency band or collar is a legitimate negotiation point. Propose: (a) a floor and ceiling (e.g., if EUR/ZAR moves more than 5% from the base rate, the price is renegotiated); (b) monthly price reviews based on a reference exchange rate (Reuters spot rate on the 1st of each month); (c) a cost pass-through formula with a time lag (e.g., 60-day rolling average). Most suppliers will resist true price stability (it transfers all currency risk to them) but may accept a sharing arrangement. Document the formula clearly in your supply agreement. Also consider: if the rand strengthens, your competitors buying on spot are getting cheaper stock — a 6-month fixed price could make you uncompetitive.

**Q:** 73. I hedged my EUR exposure at R19.50 but the rand strengthened to R18.80. My forward contract is now showing a mark-to-market loss. The bank is calling for margin. Can I walk away from the forward contract?
**A:** No — a forward exchange contract is a binding obligation. If you walk away, the bank will close out the position and claim the realised loss from you, plus administrative fees. Margin calls on forward contracts are standard when the market moves against you — the bank requires collateral to cover potential default. Your options: (a) meet the margin call to maintain the hedge; (b) negotiate with the bank to restructure the forward (extend tenor, partial unwind) — this may crystallise a loss; (c) if you genuinely cannot meet the margin, negotiate a payment plan — banks prefer this to litigation. The lesson: only hedge confirmed foreign currency exposures (firm orders), and ensure you have liquidity to meet margin calls. Consider currency options instead of forwards — you pay a premium but have no margin call obligation.

**Q:** 74. My Chinese supplier quotes in USD but my customer in Botswana pays in Pula, which I convert to ZAR. I am exposed to USD/ZAR, ZAR/BWP, and potentially EUR/USD (supplier's conversion). How do I manage this triple currency risk?
**A:** This is a classic multi-currency exposure. Strategies: (a) quote your Botswana customer in USD — transfer the currency risk to them; (b) use natural hedging — match USD payables to the supplier with USD receivables from Botswana/other export customers; (c) open a USD Foreign Currency Account and hold USD received from exports to settle supplier payments without converting through ZAR; (d) if natural hedging is insufficient, hedge the residual net USD exposure with forward contracts; (e) for the ZAR/BWP component, the volatility is lower (Pula is pegged to a basket including the rand), so the risk is smaller. The key is reducing the number of conversion steps — ideally: customer pays USD → you hold USD → pay supplier in USD, with ZAR only for your local costs and profit.

**Q:** 75. I am a cash buyer with no foreign currency exposure because I only buy from local distributors. But my main local distributor just told me all imports will now be priced in USD due to rand volatility. Do I now have currency risk as a domestic buyer?
**A:** Yes — your distributor is transferring their currency risk to you by quoting in USD or pegging prices to a USD/ZAR reference rate. Your options: (a) negotiate to remain on ZAR pricing, accepting that your prices may adjust monthly or quarterly with a published exchange rate formula; (b) if forced to USD pricing, pay the USD amount via your bank (you will need an FCA or your bank will convert ZAR to USD at spot); (c) source from a different distributor who still quotes in ZAR; (d) consider importing direct — if you are taking USD exposure anyway, you might as well capture the distributor margin. The distributor's shift to USD pricing reflects the rand's volatility — it is a sign of stress in their business. Monitor their creditworthiness.

## INSURANCE FOR STOCK

**Q:** 76. The KZN floods destroyed R1.2 million of my stock. My insurer says my policy has a "flood exclusion" for stock stored at ground level. I have been paying premiums for 5 years and never noticed this exclusion. Can I challenge this?
**A:** Under the Policyholder Protection Rules (PPR), insurers must draw exclusions to your attention in plain language before policy inception. If the flood exclusion was buried in fine print and not specifically highlighted, you may have a claim under the PPR or a complaint to the Ombudsman for Short-Term Insurance. However, if the policy clearly stated the exclusion and you signed acknowledging it, the exclusion is enforceable. Your immediate action: (a) lodge a formal complaint with the insurer's complaints department; (b) if rejected, escalate to the Ombudsman (free service, resolution within 6 months); (c) simultaneously claim under the Disaster Management Act relief if government assistance was declared for the KZN floods; (d) check if your landlord's building insurance covers any portion. Going forward: specifically request flood coverage — it is available in KZN but at higher premium. Consider relocating high-value stock above ground level.

**Q:** 77. My stock throughput insurance policy requires "intruder detection system linked to armed response" but my alarm monitoring company went into liquidation 3 months ago and I haven't replaced them. During a break-in, R600,000 of stock was stolen. Will my claim be rejected?
**A:** Yes — almost certainly. Stock throughput and commercial policies contain warranty conditions (not merely conditions precedent to liability). Breach of a security warranty gives the insurer grounds to repudiate the claim entirely, even if the lack of monitoring did not cause the loss. Your only potential defence: if the insurer was aware (or should have been aware) that monitoring had lapsed and continued to accept premiums without notifying you of the breach, they may be estopped from relying on the warranty. Document: (a) when the monitoring company liquidated; (b) any correspondence with the insurer; (c) whether premium deductions continued unchanged. If the insurer repudiates, approach the Ombudsman. The lesson: never let security warranties lapse — even temporarily. Replace the monitoring company immediately when the previous one fails.

**Q:** 78. I store stock in a rented warehouse. The lease says the landlord is not responsible for my goods. My stock insurance policy requires me to warrant that "all fire exits are kept clear at all times." The landlord occasionally stores building materials in front of one fire exit. If there is a fire and my stock is destroyed, can the insurer repudiate based on my breach of the fire exit warranty?
**A:** Yes — and this is a common problem for tenants. The warranty is your obligation regardless of who controls the fire exit. If the landlord blocks the fire exit, you are in breach of your insurance policy. Your remedies: (a) notify the landlord in writing of the fire exit obstruction and copy your insurer — this creates a record; (b) if the landlord refuses to clear it, notify the local fire department — they can issue a compliance notice; (c) request your insurer note the landlord's actions on your policy file; (d) consider whether this gives you grounds to terminate your lease (material breach by landlord). The best protection: do not sign warehouse leases that allow the landlord to compromise fire safety. If unavoidable, obtain written confirmation from the landlord that fire exits will remain clear, and ensure your stock policy contains a "landlord's acts" exception to the warranty.

**Q:** 79. My commercial insurance broker recommended a "first loss" policy with a limit of R3 million because my average stock holding is R2.5 million. But I took delivery of a large order and stock peaked at R4.2 million when the warehouse burned down. How much will the insurer pay?
**A:** Under a first loss policy, the insurer pays up to the policy limit (R3 million) regardless of the actual value at risk, provided you declared the full value at risk honestly at inception. If you declared R2.5 million as your maximum and stock exceeded this without notification, the insurer may apply underinsurance (average) — paying (declared value / actual value) x loss. So: (R2.5m / R4.2m) x R4.2m = R2.5m, capped at your R3m limit = R2.5m payout. You would be R1.7m out of pocket. First loss policies are dangerous for businesses with fluctuating stock. Better option: an annual declaration policy where you declare actual values monthly and premium adjusts, or a policy with automatic seasonal increase cover (typically 25% above average for specified periods). Always notify your insurer when stock levels will exceed declared values.

**Q:** 80. My insurer has refused to renew my stock policy after three theft claims in two years. They say I am "uninsurable." What options do I have?
**A:** After multiple claims, you enter the "excess and surplus lines" market. Options: (a) the South African Special Risks Insurance Association (SASRIA) — covers riot, strike, and terrorism but not theft; (b) specialist high-risk insurers like Lloyds of London syndicates operating in South Africa (accessed through a broker); (c) self-insurance — set aside reserves for stock loss (only viable if you have strong cash flow); (d) improve your risk profile and reapply: install CCTV with remote monitoring, armed response, access control, security guards, perimeter fencing, stock cage for high-value items. After 12-18 months of improved security, standard insurers may reconsider. Meanwhile, negotiate with your existing insurer for renewal with higher excess (e.g., R50,000 per claim), increased premium, and enhanced security requirements rather than outright refusal.

## STOCK ROTATION & BATCH MANAGEMENT

**Q:** 81. A large commercial client specified "all tiles from the same batch" for a 5,000 m² project. My supplier delivered batch X01 for the first 2,000 m² and batch X02 for the balance. The shade difference is visible. The client is withholding R800,000 payment. Who is liable?
**A:** If you specified "single batch" on your purchase order and the supplier delivered mixed batches, the supplier is in breach and liable for the cost of replacement and any consequential losses (re-tiling labour, delay penalties). However, if your PO did not specify single batch, and you merely told the client you would supply single batch without securing this from the supplier, the liability is yours. Tile shade variation between batches is normal in ceramics — SANS 1449 allows defined colour variation. Your immediate action: (a) inspect both batches under controlled lighting to quantify the difference; (b) if within SANS tolerance, explain this to the client with a technical report; (c) if outside tolerance, claim from the supplier. Going forward: for large commercial projects, always specify batch control in your PO and confirm batch availability before quoting.

**Q:** 82. I have two batches of the same white porcelain tile — batch A from 2021 and batch B from 2023. They look identical to the naked eye but a fussy architect is insisting on a laboratory shade match report. Is this my cost or the client's?
**A:** Laboratory shade matching (using a spectrophotometer to Delta E values) costs R1,500-R3,000 per comparison and is not standard practice for merchant stock unless specified in the contract. If the client (or their architect) is insisting on this level of precision beyond normal commercial tolerances, they should bear the cost — provided your product meets SANS 1449 shade classification. State your position: "Our tiles comply with SANS 1449 shade tolerance of [specify]. Laboratory shade matching beyond this standard is available at the client's cost." If the architect's specification requires tighter tolerances than SANS, you should have priced this premium requirement into your original quote. For future projects with architects, clarify shade tolerance requirements at quotation stage.

**Q:** 83. My warehouse management system doesn't track batch numbers — only SKUs. A customer needs 200 m² matching batch 47B from a job 8 months ago. I have no idea what batch I sold them. How do I handle this?
**A:** Without batch tracking, you cannot guarantee a match. Your options: (a) inspect your current stock and provide the closest available batch, disclosing that exact batch matching cannot be guaranteed; (b) contact the manufacturer with the original order date — they may be able to identify the batch shipped; (c) offer to replace the entire area with current batch at a negotiated price. The lesson: implement batch tracking in your inventory system — it is essential for tile merchants. Even a simple spreadsheet logging batch numbers against customer orders is better than nothing. Modern WMS systems (like SAP Business One, Sage, or even cloud solutions like Zoho Inventory) support batch tracking at low cost. The cost of a proper system is far less than one lost customer or replacement job.

**Q:** 84. A customer returned unopened boxes from batch X15 that they bought 6 months ago and now wants to exchange for batch X18 (current stock) because they are completing a second phase of their project. The batches have a visible shade difference. Should I accept the return?
**A:** If the boxes are unopened, undamaged, and within your returns policy period, you should accept the return as a commercial goodwill gesture — but issue a credit note at the current selling price (which may be lower if prices have dropped). Do NOT allow exchange batch-for-batch unless you can absorb the shade difference risk. Explain to the customer that batch X15 and X18 will have visible variation — they must accept this or replace the entire first-phase installation. For the second phase, recommend they purchase enough from batch X18 to complete the job plus 10% wastage. Going forward: advise all customers doing phased projects to purchase full quantity plus wastage upfront, with storage at your warehouse if needed (charge a small holding fee).

**Q:** 85. My supplier's new batch of a popular marble-look porcelain has a noticeable glaze difference from the previous batch. They say it is "within manufacturing tolerance" and refuse a credit. My customer has rejected the new batch. What is my position?
**A:** Obtain the supplier's technical data sheet showing the manufacturing tolerance for this product. Compare the actual variation against the published tolerance and against SANS 1449 requirements. If the variation exceeds the supplier's own specification or the SANS standard, you have a clear claim for replacement or credit. If within specification, your customer is being unreasonable — but you still have a commercial problem. Options: (a) offer the customer a discount to accept the variation; (b) source the original batch from another merchant; (c) offer a full upgrade to a premium range at your cost. Your claim against the supplier depends on what you specified in your PO — if you did not specify batch consistency, the supplier has delivered compliant goods and you bear the customer relationship cost.

## SEASONAL BUYING & CASH FLOW

**Q:** 86. I need to stock up before the December/January building shutdown but my cash flow is tight. Is it better to use supplier credit at prime + 4% or discount my debtor's book through invoice factoring?
**A:** Compare the all-in costs: supplier credit at prime + 4% (currently approx. 12.25%) is annualised. If you hold the stock for 3 months, the effective cost is ~3.06%. Invoice factoring typically costs 2-4% per month (24-48% annualised) plus an administration fee of 0.5-1%. Factoring is significantly more expensive but gives immediate cash flow. A better alternative: negotiate extended payment terms with your supplier (90 days instead of 30) for the pre-December stock-up, or request a stock loan with interest capitalised until March. If your contractors are reliable payers, offer them a 2.5% settlement discount for payment within 7 days — this accelerates cash inflow at lower cost than factoring. The optimal strategy usually combines extended supplier terms with selective early payment discounts from customers.

**Q:** 87. Every February my business has a cash crunch because contractors who owe me from December jobs pay late or not at all. How can I smooth this seasonal pattern?
**A:** February cash crunches are endemic in the building industry. Strategies: (a) implement strict December credit limits — do not extend credit beyond what you can afford to not receive until March; (b) require deposits or staged payments for December jobs; (c) build a cash reserve during September-November (your strongest months) equal to 2-3 months of operating expenses; (d) negotiate with suppliers for a payment holiday or reduced payments in January-February; (e) offer contractors a settlement discount for January payment — even 5% discount is cheaper than overdraft interest; (f) diversify your customer base beyond seasonal residential contractors into commercial maintenance and insurance repairs, which are year-round. Consider invoice discounting for your December invoices as a safety net.

**Q:** 88. My supplier runs a "pre-Christmas stock-up special" with 15% discount for orders placed and paid by 30 November, but delivery only in February. I have to fund the stock for 3 months before I can sell it. Is the 15% discount worth the carrying cost?
**A:** Calculate your cost of carry: if you fund the purchase through overdraft at prime + 2% (say 10.25% annual), 3 months' carrying cost is ~2.56%. If you use supplier credit at prime + 4% (12.25%), 3 months is ~3.06%. The 15% discount exceeds your carrying cost in both scenarios, making it financially attractive IF you sell the stock. However: (a) you are locked into those specific products — if market preferences shift by February, you are stuck with unwanted stock; (b) the supplier's cash flow benefit (they get your money 3 months early) is significant — negotiate for 20% discount or earlier delivery; (c) ensure the 15% is off your normal buying price, not an inflated "special" price. Only commit to stock you are confident will sell within 60 days of delivery.

**Q:** 89. During winter (May-August), my turnover drops 40% but my fixed costs remain the same. Should I retrench staff or reduce stock levels to match the seasonal dip?
**A:** Retrenchment under the Labour Relations Act requires a fair reason (operational requirements) and fair procedure (consultation, selection criteria, severance pay = 1 week per year of service). It is a permanent solution to a temporary problem. Better strategies for winter: (a) reduce stock to core ranges and negotiate sale-or-return for seasonal items; (b) cross-train staff for marketing, warehouse reorganisation, and customer outreach during quiet periods; (c) target indoor winter renovation projects (bathroom renovations, kitchen upgrades); (d) offer winter specials to stimulate demand; (e) use quiet periods for staff training and showroom refurbishment; (f) negotiate reduced winter operating hours with staff consent rather than retrenchment. If you must reduce headcount, consider fixed-term contracts for seasonal workers rather than permanent employees.

**Q:** 90. My accountant says I should use just-in-time (JIT) ordering to reduce stock holding, but my contractor customers expect immediate availability. How do I balance cash flow efficiency with customer service?
**A:** True JIT is impractical for tile merchants because contractors plan jobs on Monday and expect delivery Tuesday — they will not wait 8-12 weeks for a container from Italy. A hybrid approach: (a) hold 2-3 months' cover of fast-moving SKUs (your top 20% of lines generating 80% of revenue); (b) use supplier back-to-back ordering for slow-moving and specialised lines — your supplier holds the stock and delivers within 5-7 days of your order; (c) negotiate "stock on the water" visibility — know what containers are arriving and when; (d) use consignment stock for new ranges where demand is uncertain; (e) implement ABC analysis: A-items = weekly review, B-items = monthly review, C-items = quarterly review or made-to-order. The carrying cost of A-item stock (fast movers) is offset by higher margin and customer retention. C-items are where you save cash.

## GOVERNMENT CONTRACTS & TENDERING

**Q:** 91. I want to tender for tile supply to a municipality. They require CSD (Central Supplier Database) registration, tax clearance, and a BEE certificate. I am a white male sole proprietor — what BEE level can I achieve?
**A:** As a white male sole proprietor with no black ownership, your automatic BEE status is non-compliant (Level 8 or non-compliant) under the generic scorecard. However, as an Exempted Micro Enterprise (EME — turnover below R10 million) or Qualifying Small Enterprise (QSE — turnover R10m-R50m), you qualify for automatic BEE levels based on annual turnover: EMEs with 100% black ownership = Level 1; EMEs with >51% black ownership = Level 2; EMEs without black ownership = Level 4 (turnover-based). If your turnover is below R10 million, you automatically get Level 4 as an EME, which is acceptable for many municipal tenders (minimum requirement is often Level 4-6). Above R10 million, you must undergo a formal BEE verification. Consider a BEE partnership or employee ownership scheme to improve your level.

**Q:** 92. A provincial housing department tender specifies SABS-approved tiles but the SABS mark is no longer mandatory for ceramic tiles — NRCS LOA replaced it. Do I still need SABS certification?
**A:** The SABS mark scheme for ceramic tiles was replaced by the NRCS compulsory specification (VC9006). NRCS Letter of Authority (LOA) is the current mandatory requirement. If a government tender still references "SABS-approved," this is outdated terminology. You should: (a) submit your NRCS LOA as proof of compliance; (b) include a technical clarification in your tender response explaining that NRCS LOA superseded SABS marking; (c) contact the tender official before closing date to confirm NRCS LOA is acceptable — get this in writing. If the tender is rigidly interpreted, you risk disqualification for not having SABS certification (which no longer exists for this product). Challenge outdated specifications through the tender clarification process.

**Q:** 93. I won a government tender for R3.2 million of tile supply to a municipality. They want 90-day payment terms. Can I negotiate shorter terms or charge interest for late payment?
**A:** Government payment terms are typically 30 days under the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA), but in practice 60-90 days is common. Charging interest on late payment is permitted under the Prescribed Rate of Interest Act (currently 10.5% per annum). However, actually collecting interest from a government department is bureaucratically difficult. Better strategies: (a) negotiate 30-day terms at tender stage — this is your best leverage; (b) use the Office of the Chief Procurement Officer (OCPO) complaints mechanism if payment exceeds 30 days; (c) consider supply chain financing — some banks offer government invoice discounting at preferential rates because government debt is considered low-risk; (d) factor the delayed payment into your tender pricing — add 1.5-2% for the carrying cost of 90-day receivables. Never rely on government payment timing for operational cash flow.

**Q:** 94. A municipal tender requires "CIDB grading" for the supply of tiles. CIDB registration is for contractors doing construction work, not for suppliers. Is this requirement valid?
**A:** The Construction Industry Development Board (CIDB) registers contractors for construction works, not suppliers of materials. A tender requiring CIDB registration for pure supply of goods is an invalid requirement — it conflates construction works with goods supply. You should: (a) lodge a tender objection before closing date citing the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act (PPPFA) and CIDB Act; (b) request the municipality confirm that CIDB registration is not required for goods-only supply; (c) if the tender is published on the eTender portal, use the query function. Some municipalities incorrectly bundle goods and services tenders under construction CIDB requirements. If your tender includes installation (works), then CIDB registration IS required — at minimum a GB (General Building) grade corresponding to the tender value.

**Q:** 95. I submitted a tender 3 minutes after the closing time due to a slow internet connection. The tender box was electronic (eTender portal) and automatically rejected my submission. Is there any recourse?
**A:** Unfortunately, very limited. Electronic tender portals have strict time cutoffs with no flexibility — 3 minutes late is as disqualifying as 3 days late. Your potential arguments: (a) if the portal was inaccessible due to the municipality's server issues (not your internet), lodge a complaint with supporting evidence (screenshots, ISP logs); (b) if you submitted a "ghost" copy before closing (email to tender official), argue equitable relief — but this rarely succeeds; (c) if the late submission was due to a disability requiring reasonable accommodation, argue unfair discrimination. Prevention: submit at least 2 hours before closing time. Do not rely on last-minute uploads. For critical tenders, have a backup internet connection (mobile data) and submit from a location with reliable connectivity.

## SHOPFITTING & STORE OPENING SUPPORT

**Q:** 96. My supplier promised R150,000 shopfitting contribution for a new store but has only paid R50,000, claiming the balance is contingent on me achieving R1 million sales in the first year. I am projected to hit R800,000. Can I enforce the full contribution?
**A:** This depends on your shopfitting agreement. If the R150,000 was unconditional ("we will contribute R150,000 towards your store fit-out"), the sales target cannot be retroactively imposed. If the agreement stated R150,000 "subject to achievement of R1 million first-year sales," the condition is enforceable. Check: (a) was the sales target clearly communicated before you committed to the store? (b) is it in writing? (c) if you were induced by the promise of R150,000 and would not have opened the store without it, you may have a claim for misrepresentation. Negotiate: propose a tiered contribution (R100,000 base + R50,000 if you reach R1m) or an extension of the performance period to 18 months. Going forward: never commit to store opening costs based on verbal supplier promises.

**Q:** 97. I am opening a new store in a mall and the landlord requires shopfitting plans to be approved by their architect at my cost (R25,000). My supplier promised shopfitting support but says mall approval costs are my responsibility. Who is right?
**A:** Mall landlord approval costs are typically the tenant's responsibility unless the supplier explicitly agreed to cover them. Check your shopfitting support agreement for inclusions and exclusions. Standard supplier shopfitting contributions cover: display units, sample boards, signage, lighting. They typically exclude: landlord professional fees, building plan approval, fire department compliance, tenant installation (plumbing, electrics, flooring). Negotiate with the supplier: if they want your store in that mall, they should contribute to the landlord approval cost as part of their "new store opening package." Alternatively, negotiate with the landlord: some landlords waive professional fees for tenants bringing desirable brands. Budget R50,000-R100,000 for all shopfitting-related professional fees beyond the supplier's direct contribution.

**Q:** 98. My shopfitting agreement with a supplier says they own the display fixtures and can remove them if I stop stocking their brand within 3 years. I want to delist them after 18 months due to poor sales. Can they really come and remove fixtures from my leased premises?
**A:** Yes — if your shopfitting agreement contains a retention of title clause and specifies removal rights, this is enforceable. The fixtures remain the supplier's property. However, they cannot enter your premises without your consent — they would need to apply for a court order for specific performance or damages. Practically: negotiate a buyout of the fixtures (written down over 3 years, so 50% of value at 18 months) or agree to a handover date for removal that does not disrupt your business. If the fixtures are bolted or integrated, removal may damage your landlord's premises — check your lease for alterations consent. The landlord may require the fixtures to remain or be made good. This three-way complexity (you, supplier, landlord) is why shopfitting agreements need legal review before signing.

**Q:** 99. A supplier offered a "new store design package" including a store layout designed by their architect. The design looks identical to three other stores in the same province. Can I customise it, or am I locked into their template?
**A:** Unless your shopfitting agreement specifies that the design must be implemented exactly as provided, you can customise. However, the supplier may have contributed to the design cost on the basis of brand consistency — they want their stores to look recognisably similar. Negotiate customisation: (a) maintain the supplier's branded elements (colour scheme, logo placement, display system); (b) customise layout for your specific space and customer flow; (c) add your own touches that differentiate from other stores. If the supplier funded the design, they may require approval of modifications. Get any customisation approval in writing. If you paid for the design yourself, you have full control. Consider whether brand consistency actually helps — customers may prefer a store that does not look like a cookie-cutter franchise.

**Q:** 100. My landlord requires a R200,000 deposit for shopfitting alterations to the mall unit. My supplier's contribution is R100,000. Can I use the supplier's contribution for the landlord deposit, or must it go directly to shopfitting?
**A:** This depends on your supplier agreement terms. Most supplier shopfitting contributions are earmarked for specific shopfitting items (displays, signage) and cannot be used for landlord deposits or professional fees. However, everything is negotiable. If your total shopfitting budget is constrained by the landlord deposit, present this to the supplier: "I need R200,000 for the landlord deposit before I can spend anything on your displays. Can we reallocate your contribution?" Some suppliers will agree if they are keen to secure the location. Alternatively: (a) negotiate a reduced deposit with the landlord (offer a bank guarantee instead of cash); (b) finance the deposit through a short-term facility and use the supplier contribution for actual shopfitting; (c) consider whether the landlord's deposit requirement is reasonable — the Retail Leases Act does not regulate commercial deposits, but market practice is 2-3 months' rent, not a percentage of shopfitting cost.

## DELIVERY & LOGISTICS

**Q:** 101. My own delivery truck was involved in an accident and the tiles being delivered were damaged. My driver's licence had expired 2 weeks prior. Will my goods-in-transit insurance pay out?
**A:** Almost certainly not. Goods-in-transit policies contain a warranty that the driver holds a valid licence appropriate for the vehicle class. Driving with an expired licence is a criminal offence and voids your insurance coverage. The insurer will repudiate the claim on the basis of breach of warranty. Your options: (a) claim from the other driver if they were at fault (through the RAF or their insurance); (b) absorb the loss; (c) if the accident was caused by a road defect, claim from the relevant road authority (very difficult). The lesson: implement a strict driver licence monitoring system with monthly checks. Use a fleet management app that alerts you 30 days before licence expiry. The cost of a system like this (R500/month) is trivial compared to one uninsured loss.

**Q:** 102. A contractor ordered tiles for delivery on Friday. My truck broke down and delivery happened on Monday. The contractor's tiler stood idle over the weekend and is charging the contractor R4,500. The contractor wants me to pay. Am I liable?
**A:** Liability for consequential losses depends on what was agreed: (a) if your delivery terms were "best endeavour" with no guaranteed day, you are not liable for the delay unless it was negligent (e.g., poor vehicle maintenance); (b) if you contractually committed to Friday delivery, you are in breach and liable for foreseeable consequential losses — a tiler standing idle is arguably foreseeable; (c) if the contractor did not mitigate (e.g., could have sourced tiles elsewhere for the weekend), your liability is reduced. Limit your exposure: specify delivery windows rather than specific days; include a limitation of liability clause in your terms and conditions (cap at invoice value or a fixed amount); maintain a backup delivery arrangement (rental truck, courier service) for breakdowns. For this incident, negotiate a goodwill gesture (R1,000-R2,000 credit) rather than admitting full liability.

**Q:** 103. I charge a flat R350 delivery fee within a 30km radius. A customer in a remote area (65km away) wants the same fee. My actual fuel cost is R580 for that trip. Can I refuse delivery or charge a higher fee?
**A:** You can charge a distance-based delivery fee provided it is clearly communicated before the customer places the order. The CPA does not regulate delivery fees specifically, but section 41 prohibits misleading pricing. If you advertise "delivery from R350," the "from" qualifier allows higher fees for remote areas. Your delivery policy should state: "Delivery within 30km: R350. Delivery 31-50km: R450. Delivery 51-80km: R600." Display this prominently on your website and quote sheets. If you previously offered the customer flat-rate delivery without distance qualification, you may be estopped from charging more for that specific transaction. Going forward, use Google Maps API to calculate exact distance and charge accordingly.

**Q:** 104. My delivery driver accepted a cash payment from a customer on delivery without authorisation. He then pocketed the cash and resigned the next day. The customer has a receipt he signed. Do I have to honour the "payment" or can I demand the customer pays again?
**A:** This is a difficult situation. If the driver was your employee acting within the apparent scope of his authority (delivering goods and collecting payment), the customer has a valid defence — they dealt with your agent. Your remedy is against the dishonest employee: (a) lay criminal charges of theft/fraud; (b) claim damages in the Labour Court or civil court (though recovery from a departing employee is unlikely); (c) claim under your fidelity guarantee insurance if you have employee dishonesty cover. The customer is not at fault if they reasonably believed the driver was authorised to collect payment. Going forward: (a) prohibit drivers from collecting cash — payment must be made before delivery or via EFT/card; (b) if cash on delivery is necessary, use a two-person delivery team or GPS-tracked cash bags; (c) implement immediate notification to the office when cash is collected.

**Q:** 105. I outsource delivery to a third-party logistics company. Their driver damaged a customer's gate while reversing into the driveway. The customer is claiming R12,000 from me. Is my logistics provider liable instead?
**A:** Your customer contracted with YOU for delivery, not with the logistics provider. Under the doctrine of vicarious liability, you are responsible for the negligence of your agents, including independent contractors acting on your behalf. However, you have a right of recourse against the logistics provider if their driver was negligent. Check your service agreement with the logistics provider: (a) do they carry public liability insurance? (b) does their contract include an indemnity for damage caused by their drivers? (c) is there a limitation of liability clause? Pay the customer to preserve the relationship, then claim from your logistics provider or their insurer. Going forward: require your logistics provider to produce proof of public liability insurance (minimum R5 million) and name you as an additional insured.

## INSTALLATION SERVICES

**Q:** 106. I started offering installation as an added service using a network of independent tilers. A customer's tiles cracked 3 months after installation. The tiler says the tiles were defective; the customer blames the tiler. I sold the tiles AND arranged the tiler. Am I liable for both?
**A:** Yes — you are potentially liable under both the CPA (for defective tiles, section 56) and for the tiler's defective workmanship (as the introducer/arranger). Your exposure: (a) for the tiles, claim against your supplier under the CPA warranty; (b) for the workmanship, your recourse is against the tiler — but the customer will sue you as the contracting party. Protect yourself: (a) use written sub-contractor agreements with tilers that include warranty of workmanship (12 months), insurance requirements, and indemnity; (b) require tilers to carry their own public liability and defective workmanship insurance; (c) have the customer sign a separate contract with the tiler, making clear the tiler is an independent contractor, not your employee; (d) take out "workmanship guarantee" insurance as an add-on to your product liability policy. Never guarantee installation quality unless you employ the tilers directly and supervise them.

**Q:** 107. A customer is insisting I provide a 10-year warranty on the installation because I am a "professional tile merchant." I only arranged the tiler. What warranty period is reasonable?
**A:** SANS 10107 (the tiling code of practice) recommends a 5-year warranty period for tiling installations in standard conditions. For wet areas (showers, pools), a 2-3 year warranty is more common due to higher failure risk. A 10-year warranty is excessive unless you are providing a premium guaranteed installation service with specific performance specifications. Your response: "Our installation partners provide a 5-year workmanship warranty against installation defects, backed by their insurance. Tile material warranty is covered by the manufacturer's 10-year warranty against manufacturing defects." Do not confuse material warranty with installation warranty. Get the customer to sign an installation warranty schedule specifying exactly what is covered (lip page, hollow spots, grout failure) and what is excluded (structural movement, subfloor failure, maintenance issues).

**Q:** 108. A tiler in my contractor network fell off a ladder while installing tiles at a customer's home and is claiming compensation from me under COIDA. But he is an independent contractor, not my employee. Does COIDA apply?
**A:** The Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act (COIDA) applies to EMPLOYEES, not independent contractors. If the tiler is genuinely an independent contractor (provides own tools, sets own hours, invoices you for services, works for other merchants too), COIDA does not apply and he cannot claim from the Compensation Commissioner. However, he may sue you in delict for negligence if you controlled the work environment (e.g., provided the ladder, specified unsafe working methods). Ensure your contractor agreements clearly establish independent contractor status. Require all contractors to: (a) have their own COIDA registration or personal accident insurance; (b) provide proof of insurance before commencing work; (c) sign an acknowledgement of independent contractor status. If a contractor works exclusively for you and you control their methods, SARS and the Labour Court may reclassify them as employees regardless of the contract label.

**Q:** 109. A wealthy customer wants me to install tiles in their wine cellar. The temperature is 12°C constant and humidity is 85%. I have never done a wine cellar. What technical considerations apply?
**A:** Wine cellars present specific challenges: (a) humidity at 85% requires tiles with water absorption <0.5% ( porcelain, not ceramic) to prevent moisture ingress; (b) the substrate must be moisture-resistant (tanked with a bituminous coating or cementitious waterproofing); (c) adhesive must be suitable for high-humidity environments — standard cement-based adhesives may not cure properly; use an epoxy or specialised flexible adhesive; (d) grout must be epoxy or fungicide-resistant to prevent mould growth; (e) expansion joints are critical due to temperature differentials between the cellar and adjacent rooms; (f) slip resistance (R10-R11) is important given potential condensation. SANS 10107 does not specifically address wine cellars, so follow manufacturer technical datasheets for the specific products. Consider engaging a specialist waterproofer. Charge a premium for this niche work — it requires expertise beyond standard tiling.

**Q:** 110. I arranged a tiler for a customer through my contractor network. The tiler stole jewellery from the customer's home. The customer is blaming me for "vetting" the tiler inadequately. Am I liable?
**A:** Potentially, yes. If you presented the tiler as part of your "trusted contractor network" or gave any impression of vetting or endorsement, the customer can argue you assumed a duty of care. Even without explicit endorsement, arranging the introduction creates a degree of responsibility. Your defences: (a) the tiler is an independent contractor, not your employee (but this weakens if you marketed him as "your" tiler); (b) you performed reasonable vetting (criminal record check, reference checks); (c) the theft was unforeseeable. Practical protection: (a) never describe contractors as "our tilers" — use "independent contractors we can introduce"; (b) require contractors to sign a code of conduct; (c) require contractors to carry fidelity guarantee insurance; (d) advise customers to secure valuables during installation; (e) consider taking out contingent liability insurance for acts of contractors you recommend. The customer's recourse is primarily against the tiler (criminal charges, civil claim) — but they will pursue you as the deeper pocket.

## AFTER-HOURS & EMERGENCY SERVICE

**Q:** 111. A contractor called me at 6:30 PM on a Friday demanding 150 m² of porcelain tiles for a job starting Saturday morning — a hotel lobby that must be completed before Monday check-in. He is my biggest customer. Should I open the warehouse?
**A:** This is a commercial judgment call, not a legal question. Consider: (a) the revenue from this sale vs. the cost of after-hours opening (staff overtime, security, your personal time); (b) whether this sets a precedent — if you open once, the contractor will expect it every time; (c) the risk of mistakes and accidents when rushing in low light; (d) your insurance — does it cover staff working outside normal hours? Many successful tile merchants offer a premium after-hours service for key accounts: "Emergency stock collection available Friday 6-8 PM at 1.5x normal price, Saturday mornings by appointment." This monetises the service rather than giving it away. Set clear boundaries: minimum order value (e.g., R50,000), 24 hours' notice where possible, and charge a call-out fee. Train a deputy who can handle emergency openings so you are not personally on call.

**Q:** 112. I opened my warehouse on a Sunday for a contractor emergency. While loading, the contractor's labourer injured his back lifting a box. He is now claiming from me because the injury happened on my premises. Am I liable even on a Sunday?
**A:** Under the Occupational Health and Safety Act, you owe a duty of care to ALL persons on your premises, including visitors and contractors' employees, regardless of the day. Liability depends on whether you were negligent: (a) did you provide safe loading equipment (trolley, pallet jack)? (b) was the contractor's labourer following reasonable safety practices? (c) did you supervise or direct the loading method? Your best defence is that the injured person was not your employee and you did not control their work methods — the contractor (his employer) bears primary responsibility under COIDA and common law. However, if your premises were unsafe (poor lighting, slippery floor, no loading bay), you share liability. Check your public liability insurance covers after-hours visitors. Going forward: require all after-hours visitors to sign an induction acknowledging they follow their employer's safety procedures and enter at their own risk — this is not a waiver but creates evidence of awareness.

**Q:** 113. I provide my cell phone number to key contractors for "emergencies only." One contractor now calls me at 10 PM three times a week for non-urgent questions. Can I bill him for after-hours consultation time?
**A:** You can implement a policy of after-hours call charges, but it must be communicated clearly in advance — you cannot retroactively bill for calls already made. Best practice: send a polite message to all contractors: "To ensure work-life balance and quality service during business hours, calls after 7 PM and on weekends will be charged at R350 per 15-minute increment, invoiced monthly. For non-urgent queries, please WhatsApp or email for a response by 9 AM the next business day." Most contractors will respect this if communicated professionally. Alternatively, use a separate business phone that you switch off after hours, routing emergencies to a voicemail with callback commitment within 1 hour for genuine emergencies (defined as: stock needed for next-day job, quality issues stopping work, safety concerns). The real issue is boundary-setting, not billing.

**Q:** 114. During load shedding Stage 4, a contractor arrived to collect an emergency order but my gate motor and security system were inoperative. I refused entry due to security concerns and the contractor lost a day's work. He wants compensation. Am I obliged to facilitate emergency collections during load shedding?
**A:** No — you are not obliged to compromise your security. Load shedding is a foreseeable national condition, and both you and the contractor should plan around it. Your premises insurance and security warranties likely require functional security systems. However, as a service provider, you should plan for load shedding: (a) install a solar backup system for gate motor, alarm, and essential lighting; (b) maintain a manual override for the gate; (c) keep a generator for the yard lights; (d) communicate your load shedding schedule to key contractors so they can plan collections during power availability. If you have repeatedly failed to accommodate emergency needs and competitors have backup power, you will lose customers. The cost of a basic solar backup (R80,000-R150,000) is less than the lifetime value of a major contractor account.

**Q:** 115. A contractor has asked me to keep my warehouse open until 8 PM on weekdays to accommodate his team that only finishes their day job at 6 PM. What are the legal implications of extending trading hours for staff?
**A:** Extended hours trigger several legal considerations: (a) Basic Conditions of Employment Act — hours beyond 45 per week require overtime pay (1.5x normal rate) or time off in lieu; (b) if your staff work after 8 PM, you must provide transport home if public transport is unsafe (especially for female staff); (c) your commercial insurance may have restrictions on hours of operation — check your policy; (d) municipal by-laws may regulate trading hours for your zoning — check with your municipality; (e) security arrangements must be extended. Financial viability: calculate whether the additional revenue from the extended hours covers the additional staff costs, security, and utilities. For many tile merchants, extending hours is not profitable — consider instead a "late collection by appointment" service with a surcharge, available 2-3 evenings per week with minimum notice.

## PRODUCT RECALLS

**Q:** 116. My supplier recalled a porcelain tile range due to excessive lead content in the glaze. I have already sold 3,000 m² to various customers. What is my legal obligation to notify these customers?
**A:** Under section 60 of the CPA, if goods pose a safety risk, the supplier (which includes you as the distributor) must recall the goods and notify consumers. Your obligations: (a) immediately stop selling the recalled range; (b) contact all customers who purchased the tiles using your sales records — phone, email, and registered mail for high-value customers; (c) provide the supplier's recall notice or your own notice explaining the risk and the remedy (replacement, refund, or safe disposal); (d) maintain records of all notifications sent and responses received; (e) report to the National Consumer Commission if the recall was safety-related and the supplier has not done so. If you fail to notify customers and someone is harmed by the recalled product, your liability increases significantly. The supplier should bear the cost of recall logistics — claim this from them.

**Q:** 117. A tile adhesive manufacturer recalled a product for inadequate bond strength. I have 200 bags in stock that I bought 8 months ago. The supplier says the recall only covers stock purchased within the last 3 months. Is this limitation valid?
**A:** A product recall should cover ALL affected product regardless of purchase date — if the defect is inherent to the product, all batches are potentially affected. However, suppliers sometimes limit recalls for commercial reasons. Your position: (a) demand the batch numbers affected and check if your stock matches; (b) if your batch is not included in the recall, request a written statement from the manufacturer that your batch is safe; (c) if the manufacturer refuses to accept your stock for recall credit, you face a dilemma — sell it with disclosure of the recall risk, or write it off. If you sell recalled product knowing it is defective, you expose yourself to CPA liability. Safest course: withhold the stock from sale and negotiate with the manufacturer — threaten to report them to the NCC if they do not accept full responsibility. Document all correspondence.

**Q:** 118. A recalled tile range was installed in a major commercial project. The replacement tiles are a different batch with visible shade variation. The developer is demanding the entire installation be replaced for consistency. Who pays — me, my supplier, or the manufacturer?
**A:** The developer's demand for full replacement (including non-defective tiles) for aesthetic consistency is a consequential loss issue. Under the CPA, the consumer is entitled to replacement of the defective goods — not necessarily undamaged goods installed alongside them. However, for a high-profile commercial project, the developer may have a legitimate claim if the shade mismatch damages the project value. Liability chain: (a) the manufacturer is primarily liable for the defective product; (b) you are liable to the developer as the direct supplier; (c) you have a right of recourse against the manufacturer. Negotiate: (a) the manufacturer covers replacement of recalled tiles plus a contribution to replacement of adjacent tiles; (b) your product liability insurance may cover this if you have consequential loss coverage; (c) the developer's building insurance may cover the remedial work. This is why large commercial jobs require specific defect and rectification insurance.

**Q:** 119. I received a product recall notice from a supplier via email but it went to my spam folder and I missed it. I continued selling the recalled tiles for 3 weeks. A customer was injured by a cracked tile from the recalled batch. Am I liable despite not receiving the notice?
**A:** You are liable to the customer under the CPA regardless of whether you received the recall notice — the consumer's claim is against you as the direct supplier. Your defence against the customer is limited. However, you may have a claim against the supplier for negligent notification — if they sent the recall notice only by email without following up (phone call, registered mail, courier), a court may find their notification inadequate. Standard practice for safety recalls requires multiple notification channels. Your immediate action: (a) attend to the injured customer (medical expenses, replacement, apology); (b) document your spam folder showing the missed email; (c) notify your product liability insurer immediately; (d) write to the supplier claiming indemnity due to inadequate notification. Going forward: whitelist all supplier domains and maintain an emergency contact protocol for product safety issues.

**Q:** 120. A supplier recalled a range and offered replacement with a "comparable" alternative. My customers reject the alternative because it is 15% more expensive and a different aesthetic. Must my customers accept the substitute, or can they demand a full cash refund?
**A:** Under section 56 of the CPA, if goods are defective or subject to a safety recall, the consumer can choose between repair, replacement, OR refund. The supplier cannot force a substitute product if the consumer prefers a refund. However, for refunds on goods purchased some time ago, the supplier may offer credit rather than cash — this is negotiable. Your role as merchant: (a) pass the customer's preference to the supplier; (b) if the supplier offers replacement only, explain to the customer that a cash refund may take longer to process; (c) do not leave the customer in limbo while you argue with the supplier — offer an immediate store credit and claim from the supplier later. The CPA protects the consumer's right to choose their remedy. A supplier who refuses a lawful refund is in breach and the customer can escalate to the National Consumer Commission.

## OVERSTOCKED ITEMS & TRANSFERS

**Q:** 121. I overstocked a large-format tile that is not selling. My supplier has a branch in Durban and suggests I transfer it there at my transport cost. Should I do this or sell at clearance locally?
**A:** Compare the economics: (a) cost of transfer to Durban (R80-R120 per box for inter-city freight) vs. clearance markdown locally (40-50% discount); (b) whether Durban has demand for this specific range; (c) whether the transfer credit equals your landed cost or is discounted; (d) time value of money — how long will the stock sit in Durban before selling? In most cases, a deep local clearance is better than inter-branch transfer because: (a) you recover cash faster; (b) no additional logistics cost and damage risk; (c) clearance sales attract new customers; (d) you free up warehouse space for faster-moving stock. Only transfer if: (a) the receiving branch has confirmed demand and will pay you full cost; (b) the transport cost is less than 15% of stock value; (c) the stock is current range that will not be discontinued before it sells in Durban.

**Q:** 122. I have R600,000 of dead stock across 15 SKUs that have not sold in 18 months. My accountant says I must write it down for tax purposes. What is the correct tax treatment of obsolete stock?
**A:** For income tax purposes, stock is valued at the lower of cost or net realisable value (NRV). If your dead stock has an NRV below cost (which it does if it has not sold in 18 months), you can write it down to NRV and claim the write-down as a deduction in the current year. To support the write-down: (a) document efforts to sell (price reductions, clearance offers, contact with other merchants); (b) obtain independent valuations or quotes from job lot buyers; (c) for stock with zero realisable value (discontinued, damaged, obsolete), write it down to a nominal amount (R1) and physically scrap or donate it. SARS may query large write-downs — keep supporting documentation for 5 years. If you later sell the written-down stock for more than its written-down value, the recovery is taxable income in the year of sale.

**Q:** 123. My competitor offered to buy my overstocked tiles at 30% below my cost. If I sell to them, will my supplier terminate my distributorship for selling to an unauthorised party?
**A:** Check your supply agreement for restrictions on resale. Most supplier agreements do not prohibit you from selling to other merchants — they prohibit selling below MAP or into exclusive territories of other distributors. If your agreement is silent on inter-merchant sales, you can sell to your competitor. However, consider: (a) will the competitor undercut you in your own market with your own stock? (b) does your supplier view this as damaging the brand? (c) would you be better off job-lotting through an independent auctioneer rather than strengthening a competitor? If you do sell to the competitor, ensure the transaction is confidential and the stock is removed from your premises before your supplier's rep visits. Alternatively, ask your supplier if they will facilitate a transfer to another authorised stockist — this is the cleanest route.

**Q:** 124. I have 3 years of purchase history showing I consistently over-order certain categories. Can I negotiate a "sale or return" arrangement with my supplier for my weakest categories, and what terms are typical?
**A:** Sale or return (SOR) is common in book publishing and fashion but rare in tiles due to the weight, fragility, and batch-control issues. However, you can negotiate: (a) limited SOR on new range introductions (e.g., 6-month evaluation period, return at 80% of cost if not sold); (b) SOR on display stock only (the stock in your showroom, not warehouse); (c) a right to return up to 10% of annual purchases across all lines, with a 15% restocking fee. Suppliers are more likely to agree if: (a) you are a significant volume account; (b) you share your sales data transparently; (c) you commit to increased purchases of their core ranges in exchange. Frame it as risk-sharing: "I want to range more of your products but need a safety net on untested lines." Get any SOR agreement in writing with clear time limits and condition requirements.

**Q:** 125. I donated obsolete tiles to a rural school building project. Can I claim the full cost price as a tax deduction, and do I need a section 18A receipt?
**A:** For income tax purposes, a donation of trading stock is treated as a disposal at market value — you are deemed to have sold the stock and must include the market value in your gross income. This is not tax-efficient. However, if you donate to a registered PBO (Public Benefit Organisation) with section 18A approval, you can claim a tax deduction limited to 10% of your taxable income. The deduction is based on the lower of cost or market value — not full cost if the stock is obsolete. You MUST obtain a valid section 18A receipt from the PBO with their registration number, your details, description of donation, and date. Without this receipt, SARS will disallow the deduction. Note: section 18A deductions are for donations to approved PBOs only — donating to an unregistered community project does not qualify.

## CREDIT CONTROL & BAD DEBTS

**Q:** 126. A contractor owes me R180,000 and has gone into business rescue under section 128 of the Companies Act. I supplied the tiles 45 days ago and have not been paid. Will I recover anything?
**A:** Business rescue places a moratorium on all legal proceedings against the company. As an unsecured creditor, you rank below secured creditors, employees, and post-commencement finance. Typical recovery for unsecured creditors in business rescue is 0-20 cents in the rand. Your actions: (a) submit a claim to the business rescue practitioner (BRP) within the claims period; (b) if the tiles were supplied under retention of title and you registered a security interest, you may have a claim to specific goods — but this is difficult if the tiles have already been installed; (c) if you have personal suretyships from the directors, pursue them personally (they are not protected by the business rescue moratorium); (d) check if the contractor was insolvent when they ordered from you — if so, you may have a claim for reckless trading against the directors. Going forward: credit-check contractors regularly, require personal suretyships for accounts above R50,000, and monitor payment patterns for early warning signs.

**Q:** 127. I have been using a debt collection attorney for contractor bad debts. He charges 15% commission on recovery plus R2,500 upfront fee. A new "no win no fee" collection agency offers 25% commission but no upfront fee. Which is better?
**A:** It depends on your recovery rate and debt profile. Calculate: (a) if your attorney recovers 60% of debts at 15% + R2,500 upfront, your net recovery is 60% x 85% minus upfront fees; (b) if the agency recovers 40% (typical for "no win no fee" agencies that are less incentivised to pursue difficult cases) at 25%, your net is 40% x 75%. Run the numbers on your historical data. Also consider: (a) attorney-collected debts can be enforced through court judgments and sheriffs; (b) collection agencies are limited to phone calls and letters unless they partner with attorneys; (c) the upfront fee often correlates with quality — no upfront fee agencies may take only easy cases. For debts under R50,000, the Small Claims Court (no attorney needed, free) is often more effective than either option. For debts above R50,000, an attorney with building industry experience is usually worth the cost.

**Q:** 128. A customer paid me with a cheque that bounced. I have a "no cheque" policy displayed at my counter but my staff accepted it. Can I still claim the amount plus bank charges and a penalty fee?
**A:** A "no cheque" sign creates a policy but does not prevent the formation of a contract if your employee accepted the cheque — the employee's actions bind you. You can claim: (a) the original debt amount; (b) bank charges for the bounced cheque (R150-R250); (c) a reasonable administration fee for the inconvenience (R250-R500, if communicated in your terms); (d) interest at the Prescribed Rate (10.5% per annum) from the date the cheque should have cleared. You cannot claim a penalty unless it was agreed in your terms and conditions. Criminal proceedings for fraud are possible if the customer knew the cheque would bounce (requires proof of intent), but civil recovery is more practical. For bounced cheques, the Payments Association of South Africa (PASA) allows you to report the account holder, which may result in their cheque facility being withdrawn.

**Q:** 129. I want to blacklist a contractor with the credit bureaus for non-payment of R95,000. What is the legal process, and can they sue me for defamation if the debt is disputed?
**A:** Under the National Credit Act, you can only list a consumer with a credit bureau if: (a) you are a registered credit provider (if you extend credit regularly, you may need to register with the NCR); (b) the listing is accurate and not outdated; (c) you have followed the required pre-listing process (written notice of intent to list, 20 business days to respond). For a business debt (contractor is a company), the NCA does not apply and you can list with commercial credit bureaus (e.g., Experian Business, CIPC enforcement). Defamation risk: if the debt is genuinely disputed on reasonable grounds (not just refusal to pay), listing could be defamatory. Ensure: (a) the debt is undisputed or you have a court judgment; (b) you have documented all payment demands; (c) the listing amount is accurate. If the contractor is listed and sues for defamation, truth and public interest are your defences.

**Q:** 130. A long-standing contractor customer has asked me to increase his credit limit from R150,000 to R500,000 for a major hotel project. His last financial statements show negative equity. Should I grant the increase?
**A:** Negative equity means the contractor's liabilities exceed assets — technically insolvent. Extending credit to an insolvent company is reckless and may expose you to claims under section 22 of the Companies Act (reckless trading) if you knew or should have known of the insolvency. Your options: (a) decline the increase but offer COD terms with a settlement discount; (b) require personal suretyship from the directors (with proof of their personal assets); (c) require the hotel developer to provide a payment guarantee or direct payment arrangement; (d) offer supply against architect's certificates or progress payments tied to completed milestones; (e) require credit insurance for the increased limit. If you do extend credit, perfect a security interest via a notarial bond over the contractor's assets or a cession of the hotel contract proceeds. Do NOT rely on historical relationship — negative equity is a red flag that overrides past payment history.

## EMPLOYEE THEFT & SECURITY

**Q:** 131. My warehouse manager stole R400,000 of stock over 18 months by creating false delivery notes. I only discovered it during stock take. My fidelity insurance policy has a R10,000 excess per claim and an annual aggregate limit of R200,000. Can I make two separate claims to get around the aggregate limit?
**A:** No — an aggregate limit caps the insurer's total liability for the policy period regardless of the number of claims. Splitting a single loss into multiple claims is fraud. Your recovery is limited to R200,000 minus the excess. However: (a) check if the policy has a "discovery period" — fidelity policies often cover losses discovered during the policy period even if the theft occurred earlier; (b) the R400,000 theft spanning 18 months may have crossed two policy periods — if so, you may have two aggregate limits (one per period); (c) pursue the warehouse manager criminally (lay charges with SAPS) and civilly (attachment order against any assets); (d) if the manager was colluding with a delivery driver or customer, they may also be liable. Going forward: implement cycle counting (monthly partial stock counts) to detect discrepancies early, and segregation of duties — no single person should control stock receipt, dispatch, and record-keeping.

**Q:** 132. A customer reported that my sales consultant offered to sell tiles "cash on the side" at 30% below my price, bypassing the till. I confronted the employee who denied it. The customer is now demanding the discounted price. How do I handle this?
**A:** This is a serious disciplinary and reputational issue. Your actions: (a) investigate thoroughly — interview the employee, review CCTV, check if stock was removed without documentation; (b) if confirmed, institute disciplinary proceedings for theft/gross misconduct; (c) do NOT honour the customer's "discounted" price — it was not an authorised offer and honouring it encourages collusion; (d) explain to the customer that the employee acted without authority and has been disciplined; (e) offer a small goodwill discount (5-10%) for bringing the matter to your attention; (f) if the employee sold company stock and pocketed the cash, lay criminal charges. Prevention: (a) all sales must go through the POS system; (b) random spot-checks of quotes vs. actual sales; (c) CCTV at the sales counter; (d) culture of ethics — employees should know that side deals result in immediate termination and criminal prosecution. This is theft by false pretences.

**Q:** 133. My security guard caught a casual labourer loading tiles into his personal vehicle at 6 AM before the official start of work. The labourer claims he was "borrowing" them and would return them after a weekend side job. Is this theft or borrowing?
**A:** This is theft under the Common Law — taking property without the owner's consent with intent to deprive the owner permanently (or temporarily, which is sufficient for theft in South Africa). The labourer had no authority to remove stock and "borrowing" company stock for personal use is not a defence. Your actions: (a) lay criminal charges with SAPS; (b) dismiss the labourer for gross misconduct after a disciplinary hearing (even if the stock is recovered, unauthorised removal is a dismissible offence); (c) claim the value of the tiles from the labourer via civil action (though recovery is unlikely); (d) review your access control — how did he get keys or access at 6 AM? Prevention: (a) no employee access to stock outside business hours without manager authorisation; (b) sign-out register for all stock movements; (c) CCTV with motion detection in the yard; (d) clear policy in employment contracts: "Removal of company property without written authorisation constitutes theft and will result in immediate dismissal and criminal prosecution."

**Q:** 134. I installed GPS trackers on all delivery vehicles after a driver diverted a load to sell to a competitor. The drivers' union says this is an invasion of privacy and a violation of POPIA. Are they right?
**A:** No — GPS tracking of company vehicles during working hours is a legitimate business interest and does not violate POPIA provided: (a) the drivers are informed that tracking is in place (transparency principle); (b) tracking is limited to business hours and company vehicles; (c) a clear policy explains the purpose (route optimisation, theft prevention, driver safety); (d) data is used only for legitimate business purposes, not to monitor personal activities during breaks. POPIA requires that personal information be collected for a specific, explicitly defined, lawful purpose — vehicle tracking during working hours meets this. Tracking outside working hours or personal vehicles would require consent. The union's objection is likely a bargaining position — address it through a collective agreement that codifies the tracking policy and protects driver rights. Do not track personal devices or personal vehicles without explicit consent.

**Q:** 135. My annual stock take shows a shrinkage rate of 4.2% (R420,000 on R10m turnover). Industry average is 1.5%. My insurer says this is "expected loss" not covered by insurance. How do I reduce shrinkage to insurable levels?
**A:** A 4.2% shrinkage rate is unsustainable and indicates systemic control failures. Your shrinkage comprises: theft (internal and external), administrative errors (incorrect receiving, dispatch mistakes), damage, and supplier short-shipping. Immediate actions: (a) install CCTV with 90-day retention covering all entry/exit points and high-value stock areas; (b) implement cycle counting — count 10% of SKUs weekly so every item is counted every 10 weeks; (c) segregate duties — receiving, storage, dispatch, and recording should be done by different people; (d) require dual authorisation for all stock adjustments; (e) weigh-check containers on receipt (compare actual weight to declared weight); (f) install an access control system with swipe cards logging all warehouse entries; (g) conduct unannounced spot checks. Once shrinkage drops below 2% for 6 consecutive months, renegotiate with your insurer for coverage. Some insurers offer premium reductions for verified shrinkage below 1%.

## FLOODING, THEFT & NATURAL DISASTER

**Q:** 136. During the April 2022 KZN floods, my warehouse was inundated. R2.3 million of stock was destroyed. My insurer paid R1.8 million after applying underinsurance (average). My landlord's building insurance did not cover my stock. Can I claim the R500,000 shortfall from the municipality for inadequate stormwater drainage?
**A:** Claims against municipalities for flood damage are theoretically possible under the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act if you can prove: (a) the municipality had a legal duty to maintain stormwater infrastructure; (b) they negligently failed to do so; (c) this failure caused or materially contributed to your loss; (d) the loss was foreseeable. In practice, these claims are extremely difficult: (a) the 2022 KZN floods were an unprecedented natural disaster — the municipality will argue vis major (act of God); (b) proving specific negligence in stormwater maintenance requires expert engineering evidence; (c) municipal claims must be lodged within 6 months of the event. Budget R100,000-R300,000 for legal costs with uncertain prospects. Your better recovery path: (a) SARS disaster relief if declared; (b) claim the R500,000 as a casualty loss for tax purposes (write off the uninsured portion against income); (c) UIF Temporary Employer/Employee Relief Scheme (TERS) if staff were unable to work.

**Q:** 137. My warehouse was burgled and R800,000 of stock stolen. SAPS investigated but no arrests. My insurer is demanding the SAPS case number and investigation report before paying. SAPS says the report is "not available." How do I get my claim paid?
**A:** Insurers routinely require a SAPS case number as proof that the theft was reported. They are not entitled to the full investigation report — that is SAPS property and subject to confidentiality. Your approach: (a) provide the SAPS case number and the stamped acknowledgement (CAS number) you received when reporting; (b) if the insurer insists on the investigation report, request a letter from the SAPS station commander confirming the case is under investigation and the report is not yet available; (c) if the insurer still refuses, escalate to the Ombudsman for Short-Term Insurance — they consistently rule that a CAS number is sufficient proof of reporting; (d) for substantial claims, the insurer may appoint their own investigator (loss adjuster) — cooperate fully. The insurer has 30 days to accept or reject a claim once all reasonable documentation is provided. If they exceed this without justification, interest accrues on the claim amount.

**Q:** 138. After a warehouse fire, my insurer appointed a loss adjuster who is taking 4 months to finalise the claim. I need cash to restock and keep the business running. Can I compel the insurer to make an interim payment?
**A:** Under the Policyholder Protection Rules (PPR), the insurer must decide on your claim within 30 days of receiving all required documentation. For large or complex claims, this may extend to 60-90 days with written explanation. A 4-month delay is unreasonable. Your remedies: (a) write to the insurer's claims manager demanding an interim payment (citing PPR Rule 6.1) — for undisputed portions of the claim, interim payments should be made; (b) if the delay is causing business failure, threaten to escalate to the Ombudsman and the Prudential Authority; (c) obtain bridging finance from your bank against the expected insurance payout (the bank will require the insurer's acknowledgement of the claim); (d) formally escalate to the Ombudsman — they can award interest on delayed payments at the prescribed rate plus costs. Document every communication — the Ombudsman favours policyholders where insurers delay without justification.

**Q:** 139. My neighbour's tree fell on my warehouse during a storm and damaged the roof, allowing rain to destroy R300,000 of stock. My insurer says this is "neighbour's liability" and I must claim from their insurance. But the neighbour says it was an act of God. Who is correct?
**A:** Both arguments have merit. If the tree fell solely due to an unforeseeable storm (no prior rot or disease), it is vis major and your neighbour is not negligent — your insurer should cover it under your own policy. If the tree was visibly diseased, dead, or unstable and the neighbour knew or should have known, they are negligent and liable for the damage. Your action plan: (a) claim from your own insurer immediately — they have a duty to indemnify you and then subrogate against the neighbour if negligence is proven; (b) if your insurer repudiates claiming "third party liability," challenge this — storm damage to your building is an insured peril regardless of the tree's origin; (c) photograph the tree stump for evidence of rot; (d) obtain a tree surgeon's report if possible. Do not accept your insurer's attempt to push you onto the neighbour's insurance — that is subrogation, your insurer's job, not yours.

**Q:** 140. After the KZN floods, I had to close my business for 6 weeks for clean-up and restocking. My business interruption insurance has a 4-week waiting period. I can only claim 2 weeks. But the real loss is the permanent customers who went to competitors. Can I claim for loss of future profits?
**A:** Standard business interruption insurance covers loss of gross profit during the indemnity period (typically 12 months) starting after the waiting period. It does NOT cover loss of future profits or market share beyond the indemnity period. Your 2 weeks of coverage will be calculated as: (expected turnover - actual turnover) x gross profit margin, adjusted for trends and seasonal variations. The permanent customer loss is uninsured — this is a business risk. To protect against this: (a) some insurers offer extended indemnity periods (24-36 months) at higher premium; (b) "customer loss" extensions are available as add-ons but are expensive and have specific conditions; (c) the best protection is business continuity planning: pre-arranged alternative supply sources, cloud-based systems, and a recovery plan that gets you operational within days, not weeks. The 6-week closure suggests inadequate disaster preparedness — use this as a catalyst for investment in resilience.

## PRICE MATCHING & COMPETITIVE PRICING

**Q:** 141. A customer showed me a WhatsApp message from a competitor offering identical tiles at 18% below my price. The competitor is selling below cost to clear stock. Must I match the price to keep the customer?
**A:** No — you are not obliged to match any competitor's price. Price matching is a commercial strategy, not a legal requirement. Consider: (a) can you afford the margin reduction on this specific job? (b) is the customer price-sensitive or relationship-driven? (c) will matching set a precedent for this customer? (d) is the competitor's offer genuine or a loss leader on a small quantity? Your options: (a) match partially (e.g., 10% discount, not 18%); (b) offer value-adds instead of price reduction (free delivery, extended payment terms, installation advice); (c) explain your value proposition (stock availability, technical support, warranty handling); (d) let the customer go if they are purely price-driven — they are not profitable long-term. If the competitor is a major chain pricing below cost consistently, consider a Competition Commission complaint for predatory pricing — but only if you have documented evidence of sustained below-cost selling.

**Q:** 142. A contractor claims CTM quoted him a price for my supplier's tiles that is lower than my cost price from the same supplier. How is this possible and is the supplier giving CTM preferential pricing?
**A:** Several possibilities: (a) CTM achieved a volume-based tier price that you do not qualify for; (b) CTM is buying a different grade or specification with the same face design (B-grade vs. first grade); (c) CTM negotiated a centralised distribution deal with significant rebate structure not available to independents; (d) CTM is selling at a loss to win the contractor's overall business (cross-subsidising); (e) the contractor is bluffing or comparing different products. Your response: do not automatically assume supplier favouritism. Ask the contractor for the written CTM quote with full product specifications and batch details. If it genuinely is the identical product at below your cost, approach your supplier for a pricing review citing competitive pressure. Request a "most favoured customer" clause for future protection. If the supplier refuses, evaluate whether the range is viable for you or whether you should focus on ranges where you have competitive positioning.

**Q:** 143. I want to run a "we will beat any written quote by 5%" promotion. What are the legal risks of this advertising claim?
**A:** This is a comparative pricing claim regulated by the Advertising Regulatory Board Code. Risks: (a) the claim must be genuine — you must actually beat any written quote by 5% when presented, with reasonable terms and conditions; (b) you must specify exclusions (e.g., clearance stock, imported specials, online-only prices, quotes from outside your trading area); (c) the competitor quote must be for an identical product (same brand, specification, grade, batch where relevant); (d) you must honour the claim promptly — failure to do so is misleading advertising; (e) you cannot require the customer to purchase from you to get the price match (that is conditional selling). Your terms and conditions must be clear and displayed prominently. Keep records of all price matches honoured — the Advertising Regulatory Board may request evidence if a competitor complains. The biggest risk is attracting price-shoppers who have no loyalty.

**Q:** 144. A supplier offers me a "price protection" scheme: if they reduce the list price of a range within 6 months of my purchase, they refund the difference. Is this common and are there hidden catches?
**A:** Price protection schemes are offered by some suppliers to encourage stock commitment but are not universal. Common catches: (a) the refund may be in the form of credit notes, not cash; (b) the protection applies only to list price reductions, not promotional or clearance pricing; (c) you must claim within a short window (e.g., 30 days of the price reduction); (d) the protection may be void if you have not paid for the original stock; (e) volume rebates or special deal pricing may be excluded from the protection calculation. Evaluate: price protection is valuable if you are buying ahead of a known price increase, but less valuable if the supplier rarely reduces list prices (they usually introduce new SKUs at lower prices instead). Get the terms in writing and model the scenarios before relying on it.

**Q:** 145. A competitor is advertising "factory prices to the public" and claiming to sell at the same price as wholesalers like me. Their prices are indeed at my cost level. How can they do this and is it false advertising?
**A:** "Factory prices" is a marketing term, not a legal standard. It typically means "low prices comparable to what you would pay at a factory outlet" — not literally the ex-factory price. It is not false advertising unless they explicitly claim to be selling at the manufacturer's ex-works price when they are not. They can achieve low prices by: (a) importing direct with lower overheads; (b) operating on razor-thin margins to build market share; (c) selling B-grade or factory seconds as first grade; (d) clearing stock with no warranty support. Your response: do not compete on price — emphasise the services and guarantees that justify your pricing: technical advice, stock availability, returns policy, CPA warranty handling, and established reputation. Some customers will always choose the cheapest option — they are not your target market. Focus on quality-conscious customers and commercial clients who need reliability.

## TRADE SHOWS & EXPO DEALS

**Q:** 146. At the Tile Africa Expo, a supplier offered me "show-only" pricing with 25% discount if I sign the order at the expo. I signed but now realise the "discounted" price is still 10% above what I could negotiate normally. Can I cancel the order?
**A:** Under the CPA, if the transaction was concluded at a trade show or expo, it may be considered a transaction "resulting from direct marketing" if the supplier actively solicited you at the show — giving you a 5-day cooling-off right. However, B2B transactions at trade shows are generally not covered by the direct marketing cooling-off because both parties are acting in the course of business. Your cancellation right depends on your order terms: (a) if the order contains a cancellation clause, follow it; (b) if the supplier made a fraudulent misrepresentation (knowingly inflated the "normal" price to create an artificial discount), you can rescind for fraud; (c) if it was merely aggressive salesmanship, you are bound. Your practical recourse: negotiate — most suppliers prefer to maintain the relationship rather than enforce a disputed expo order. Threaten to publicise the misleading "discount" within the industry if they refuse to negotiate.

**Q:** 147. I signed a large expo order under the influence of alcohol at the supplier's evening networking event. The next morning I realised I had committed to R1.2 million of stock I cannot afford. Is the contract voidable?
**A:** A contract entered into while intoxicated MAY be voidable if you can prove: (a) you were so intoxicated that you lacked contractual capacity (did not understand the nature of the transaction); (b) the supplier knew or should have known of your intoxication; (c) you repudiate promptly upon sobering up (next morning is prompt); (d) the supplier has not relied on the contract to their detriment (e.g., placed factory orders). This is a difficult defence — courts are sceptical of "I was drunk" claims because adults are expected to control their alcohol consumption. Your better approach: contact the supplier immediately, explain the situation honestly, and negotiate a reduction or cancellation. Most suppliers have encountered this and will work with you if you act quickly and in good faith. If the supplier insists on enforcement, consult a lawyer — but expect to pay something (restocking fee, administration cost) for the cancellation.

**Q:** 148. A supplier at a trade show offered me a "exclusive expo range" not available to non-attendees. I took 5,000 m². Two months later, I discovered the same range being sold by another merchant who did not attend the expo. Was I misled?
**A:** If the supplier represented the range as "exclusive to expo attendees" and you relied on this exclusivity in making your purchase decision, this could be misleading conduct under section 41 of the CPA and the Advertising Code. However: (a) "exclusive expo range" may mean "first available at the expo" not "permanently exclusive to attendees"; (b) if the other merchant bought the range after the expo from regular distribution, the supplier may not have breached any promise; (c) your remedy depends on what was expressly promised — check your expo order and any marketing materials. If there was a clear exclusivity promise that was breached, demand: (a) a volume rebate to compensate for lost exclusivity premium; (b) genuine exclusivity on the next range; (c) or return the unsold stock at full credit. Document the supplier's expo claims with photographs of their stand signage.

**Q:** 149. I attended a trade show in Milan and signed a purchase order with an Italian manufacturer. Back in South Africa, I want to cancel because the rand has weakened 15%. The manufacturer says the order is binding and will enforce it in Italy. What are my options?
**A:** An order signed at a trade show is binding unless it contains a cancellation clause. Your options: (a) check the Incoterms and payment terms — if no deposit has been paid and no letter of credit opened, the manufacturer's leverage is reduced (but they can still sue for damages); (b) negotiate a cancellation fee (typically 15-30% of order value for factory-direct orders); (c) if the manufacturer misrepresented any material fact at the show, you may have a claim for misrepresentation; (d) check if your order is subject to the UN Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG) — both Italy and South Africa are signatories, which provides uniform rules for international sales including remedies for breach; (e) the rand weakening is not grounds for cancellation — it is a normal commercial risk. If you proceed, hedge your currency exposure immediately. If you cancel, expect to pay a negotiated exit fee rather than the full order value.

**Q:** 150. At a trade show, a supplier's sales rep verbally promised "permanent free delivery" as part of an expo deal. The written order confirmation excludes delivery. Which prevails — the verbal promise or the written terms?
**A:** Under the parol evidence rule, when parties have reduced their agreement to writing, extrinsic (verbal) evidence is generally inadmissible to contradict the written terms. However, exceptions apply: (a) if the verbal promise was a term that both parties intended to include but was omitted by mistake (rectification); (b) if the written terms are ambiguous, extrinsic evidence can clarify; (c) if there was a collateral contract (separate from the main written agreement). In practice, proving a verbal promise against written terms is very difficult. Your approach: (a) contact the sales rep and ask them to confirm the delivery promise in writing (email follow-up); (b) if they confirm, the supplier is bound; (c) if they deny it, you have a difficult evidentiary problem. The lesson: always confirm verbal promises in writing before signing, or ensure they are included in the written order. For expo deals, take photographs of any promotional signage that includes the verbal promise.

## B-BBEE REQUIREMENTS

**Q:** 151. A major developer client informed me that I must achieve BEE Level 4 by next year or they will remove me from their approved supplier list. I am a white-owned business with R18 million turnover. Is there a realistic path to Level 4?
**A:** Yes. As a QSE (Qualifying Small Enterprise, turnover R10m-R50m), you are measured on all 5 BEE elements but with relaxed thresholds compared to large enterprises. To achieve Level 4 as a white-owned QSE: (a) Ownership (25 points) — you score 0 on black ownership, but QSEs need not score points in every element; (b) Management Control (15 points) — appoint black senior managers and board members if possible; (c) Skills Development (25 points) — train black employees (including learnerships and apprenticeships); (d) Enterprise and Supplier Development (30 points) — procure from black-owned suppliers, offer payment terms to black-owned businesses; (e) Socio-Economic Development (5 points) — donate to qualifying beneficiaries. With strong performance in Skills Development, ESD, and SED, you can achieve Level 4 despite zero ownership points. Engage a BEE consultant — the cost (R8,000-R15,000) is recoverable through improved tender success.

**Q:** 152. A supplier offered me a "BEE partnership" where I effectively lend my BEE certificate to their business for a fee, but I have no real involvement. Is this fronting and what are the penalties?
**A:** This is classic fronting — a criminal offence under the B-BBEE Amendment Act 46 of 2013. Penalties include: (a) fines of up to 10% of annual turnover; (b) imprisonment of up to 10 years; (c) cancellation of contracts; (d) debarment from doing business with government for up to 10 years; (e) listing on a register of fronting practitioners. The B-BBEE Commission actively investigates fronting tip-offs. A genuine BEE partnership requires: real ownership transfer (not nominee holding), actual participation in management and control, real economic interest in profits and losses, and genuine operational involvement. If a supplier approaches you with a "lend your certificate" arrangement, report it to the B-BBEE Commission and decline immediately. The reputational and legal risk far exceeds any fee offered.

**Q:** 153. I am a white-owned tile merchant. To improve my BEE score, I am considering selling 51% of my business to a black partner. What are the risks of BEE ownership transactions that I should consider?
**A:** BEE ownership transactions carry significant risks if not properly structured: (a) funding — if the black partner cannot self-fund, the deal typically involves vendor financing (you lend them the money to buy your shares) — if the business underperforms, you may not recover the loan; (b) control — a 51% partner has voting control — ensure your shareholders' agreement protects your operational role and includes a "golden share" or veto on key decisions; (c) net value — the BEE Codes require the black partner to pay off the acquisition debt within a reasonable period (typically 7-10 years) — if they cannot, your BEE points may not be recognised; (d) exit — what happens if the relationship fails? Include buy-back provisions and dispute resolution mechanisms. Engage a specialist BEE ownership structuring lawyer — the cost of proper structuring (R30,000-R80,000) is minor compared to the value at risk.

**Q:** 154. My black business partner in a tile merchant venture wants to exit after 3 years and sell his shares back to me. Will I lose my BEE Level 2 status immediately?
**A:** BEE status is based on a verified scorecard at a specific measurement date. If your partner exits, your ownership points drop and your overall level may decrease. However: (a) if the transaction is structured as a "graduated sale" where ownership transfers incrementally, the BEE Codes may recognise partial ownership for a transition period; (b) if your partner sells to another black shareholder, your ownership points are preserved; (c) if you have built sufficient points in other elements (Skills Development, ESD, Management Control), you may maintain your overall level despite reduced ownership points. Plan the exit: (a) begin the sale process 12 months before your next BEE verification; (b) find a replacement black shareholder; (c) or invest heavily in Skills Development and ESD to compensate for ownership point loss. Do not let the exit happen without a BEE transition plan.

**Q:** 155. A government tender requires a BEE joint venture between a tile supplier and an installation contractor. The tender is worth R8 million. What BEE structure maximises our scoring?
**A:** For a joint venture (JV) tender, BEE recognition depends on the JV agreement structure: (a) if the JV is an unincorporated consortium, each party's BEE level contributes proportionally based on their work share; (b) if the JV is a incorporated vehicle, it is measured as a separate entity. For maximum BEE scoring: (a) ensure the JV entity is at least 51% black-owned (this qualifies for enhanced preference points under the 80/20 preference point system for tenders below R50 million); (b) allocate work share to maximise the black-owned partner's contribution; (c) ensure the black partner has genuine operational responsibility, not just a passive share; (d) both parties must have valid BEE certificates; (e) the JV agreement must specify BEE obligations and consequences of non-performance. The 80/20 system gives significant advantage to Level 1-4 BEE entities — a 51% black-owned JV can qualify for maximum preference points. Have the JV agreement reviewed by a BEE specialist before tender submission.

## LEGAL DISPUTES & PRODUCT LIABILITY

**Q:** 156. A customer slipped on tiles I sold them (polished porcelain in their entrance hall) and fractured their hip. They are suing me for R2.5 million. The tiles were correctly installed and met SANS standards. Am I liable?
**A:** Under section 61 of the CPA, producers (including distributors) face strict liability for harm caused by unsafe goods. However, polished porcelain is inherently slippery when wet — this is a known characteristic, not a defect, provided the tiles were correctly classified under SANS 10107 (slip resistance). Your defences: (a) the tiles were not unsafe — they performed as expected for their classification; (b) the customer was warned of the slip risk (in writing, at point of sale); (c) the customer chose polished porcelain for an entrance hall despite advice against it; (d) the harm was caused by the customer's failure to use entrance mats or clean up water promptly, not by the tiles. You need: (a) a slip resistance test report (R2,000-R4,000) confirming the tile's R-rating; (b) your sales documentation showing the customer was advised; (c) product liability insurance — this is exactly what it is for. Notify your insurer immediately.

**Q:** 157. A contractor installed tiles I supplied using a different adhesive than I recommended. The installation failed and the homeowner is suing both of us. Can I be held liable for the contractor's choice of adhesive?
**A:** Your liability depends on your role and what you represented: (a) if you merely supplied tiles with no installation involvement, you are not liable for the contractor's adhesive choice — the homeowner's claim against you will fail unless the tiles themselves were defective; (b) if you recommended or supplied the adhesive, you share liability for its suitability; (c) if you arranged the contractor (even as an introduction), you may have assumed a duty of care for their work. Your defence: the failed installation was caused by the contractor's deviation from manufacturer specifications, not by your product. However, the homeowner will sue both parties and let the court apportion liability. Ensure your sales documentation includes a disclaimer: "Tile installation should be performed by a qualified tiler following adhesive manufacturer specifications. The tile merchant is not responsible for installation method or materials selected by the installer."

**Q:** 158. A customer bought tiles from me, installed them, and 2 years later claims the tiles are "defective" because the colour has faded in the sun. Porcelain does not fade. Can they still claim under the CPA?
**A:** The CPA 6-month implied warranty (section 56) applies to defects that existed at the time of sale. A claim 2 years later is outside this period unless the customer can prove: (a) a latent defect that was not discoverable within 6 months; (b) an express warranty (e.g., "10-year colour warranty") that extends the protection period; (c) the tiles were unsafe (not applicable here). Fading in porcelain tiles after 2 years of sun exposure is virtually impossible — porcelain is UV-stable. Your response: (a) request photographic evidence of the "fading"; (b) offer an independent assessment by a tiling inspector (at the customer's cost if no defect is found); (c) explain that porcelain tiles are inert and do not fade — any colour change is due to surface soiling, grout discolouration, or cleaning chemical damage; (d) provide a cleaning and maintenance guide. If the customer persists, their claim is outside the CPA warranty period and lacks technical merit. Do not offer a goodwill replacement — it validates a false claim.

**Q:** 159. A developer is threatening to sue me for R5 million because tiles I supplied for a apartment complex allegedly caused water ingress into units below. The tiles were installed on balconies. My supplier says the tiles are not at fault. I am caught in the middle. What do I do?
**A:** This is a classic product liability chain dispute. Your immediate actions: (a) notify both your product liability insurer AND your supplier in writing within 24 hours — most policies have notification time limits; (b) do not admit liability to the developer — say "we are investigating and will respond through our legal representatives"; (c) engage an independent tiling inspector to assess the cause of failure — likely causes: incorrect fall to drain, missing or failed waterproofing membrane, incorrect adhesive/grout, cracked tiles due to substrate movement, or missing expansion joints. The tiles themselves are rarely the cause of water ingress on balconies; (d) if the inspection shows non-tile-related failure, share the report with the developer and deny liability; (e) if the tiles contributed (e.g., excessive water absorption for the application), claim against your supplier. The developer will likely sue all parties (you, the tiler, the architect, the waterproofing contractor) and let the court apportion liability. Your insurance should cover defence costs and any settlement.

**Q:** 160. A customer is threatening to lay criminal charges against me for "fraud" because I sold them tiles that were advertised as "Italian" but manufactured in Turkey under licence from an Italian company. Have I committed fraud?
**A:** Fraud requires intentional misrepresentation with intent to deceive and cause prejudice. If you genuinely believed the tiles were Italian-made and the packaging/branding did not mislead you, there is no fraud — at most, an innocent misrepresentation. However, if you KNEW the tiles were Turkish-made and represented them as Italian to command a premium price, this could be fraud. Your defence: (a) the tiles are branded by an Italian company — they are "Italian design" even if manufactured in Turkey; (b) this is common in the tile industry — many "Italian" brands manufacture in Turkey, India, and China; (c) you did not specifically state "made in Italy" — the customer assumed. Going forward: be precise in your origin claims. Use "Italian-designed" or "Italian brand" rather than "Italian" if manufacturing is offshore. If the Italian company owns the Turkish factory, "Italian-owned" is accurate. Train your sales staff on correct origin terminology to avoid these disputes.

## COVENANT RESTRICTIONS & LEASE ISSUES

**Q:** 161. My mall lease contains a "tenant mix" clause giving the landlord the right to object to my product range if it competes with an existing tenant. I want to add engineered wood flooring, but the landlord says the flooring specialist tenant has exclusivity. Can I challenge this?
**A:** Exclusivity clauses in mall leases are common and generally enforceable if clearly worded. Check: (a) does the flooring specialist's lease actually contain an exclusivity clause for "wood flooring"? (b) does the exclusivity cover "engineered wood" specifically or just "solid wood flooring"? (c) is there a radius restriction? If the exclusivity is genuine and your lease acknowledges the landlord's right to enforce tenant mix, you cannot add engineered wood without landlord consent. Negotiate: (a) request the landlord obtain a waiver from the flooring tenant (unlikely); (b) differentiate your offering — perhaps laminate or vinyl flooring is not covered by the exclusivity; (c) offer to relocate to a different position in the centre where the exclusivity does not apply; (d) if the exclusivity was not disclosed when you signed your lease, argue it was a material non-disclosure and request rent reduction or right to terminate. Going forward: read existing tenant exclusivities before signing any mall lease.

**Q:** 162. My warehouse lease says I cannot store "hazardous materials." I need to stock tile adhesive containing cement (which contains trace chromium VI). Is cement classified as hazardous, and can my landlord evict me?
**A:** Cement is classified as hazardous under the Globally Harmonised System (GHS) due to its alkaline nature and potential for skin/eye irritation. Chromium VI is a known carcinogen and regulated under the Hazardous Chemical Substances Regulations. However, bagged cement and cement-based adhesives in sealed packaging are generally considered "consumer products" rather than bulk hazardous materials. Your position: (a) tile adhesive in sealed bags is not "hazardous material storage" in the sense the lease intends (bulk chemicals, flammables, toxics); (b) cement is widely used in construction and its storage in a warehouse is standard practice; (c) if the landlord objects, offer to store adhesives in a designated area with spill containment and MSDS sheets available. If the landlord is inflexible, obtain a written opinion from an occupational hygienist confirming that sealed-bag adhesive storage does not constitute hazardous material storage under the HCS Regulations. Eviction for this would be unreasonable and challengeable.

**Q:** 163. My lease requires me to obtain "written landlord consent" for any alterations to the premises. I need to install a racking system for tile storage that bolts to the floor. Is this an "alteration" requiring consent?
**A:** Yes — bolted racking is a fixture (permanently attached to the property) and constitutes an alteration under virtually all commercial leases. Consequences of proceeding without consent: (a) breach of lease, potentially leading to termination; (b) the landlord can require you to remove the racking and make good at your cost; (c) on lease termination, the landlord may claim the racking as their property (doctrine of fixtures). Your approach: (a) submit detailed racking plans to the landlord with structural engineer sign-off; (b) offer to remove and make good at the end of the lease; (c) negotiate that the racking remains as a lease improvement (benefiting the landlord) with a rent adjustment; (d) obtain written consent before any installation. Some landlords welcome racking installation because it improves the premises for future warehouse tenants — frame it as a value-add.

**Q:** 164. My lease has 3 years remaining but the landlord has sold the property. The new owner wants to develop the site into residential apartments and has given me 6 months' notice to vacate. Can they do this?
**A:** No — the new owner inherits the existing lease under the "huur gaat voor koop" principle (lease prevails over sale). They cannot terminate before the lease expiry unless: (a) your lease contains a demolition/renovation clause allowing early termination for redevelopment; (b) you agree to a negotiated exit (compensation for moving costs, loss of profit, and potentially a "key money" payment); (c) they offer to relocate you to alternative premises of equivalent quality. If none of these apply, you can remain until lease expiry. The new owner may offer a financial incentive for early departure — negotiate hard: (a) moving costs (R50,000-R150,000); (b) loss of profits for the unexpired term (get a valuation); (c) fit-out cost compensation for improvements you made; (d) signage and marketing cost to announce the move. Do not accept the first offer — redevelopment pressure creates leverage for you.

**Q:** 165. My warehouse lease includes a "gross turnover rent" clause — I pay base rent plus 2% of turnover above a threshold. Do I include tile installation revenue (which I outsource) in my turnover calculation?
**A:** This depends on your lease's definition of "gross turnover." Standard definitions include "all revenue derived from the business conducted on the premises" — which could include installation revenue if the installation is arranged from your premises. However, if you merely introduce the contractor and the installation contract is between the customer and the contractor (with you taking a commission or referral fee), only your commission/fee is your revenue, not the full installation value. Check: (a) how do you invoice the customer — for tiles only, or tiles plus installation? (b) does the installation money pass through your account? (c) what does your lease say about "turnover" — is it defined as money received by you, or value of all transactions? Ambiguity favours the tenant in lease interpretation — if the definition is unclear, you can argue installation revenue is not your turnover. Get a legal opinion if the amount in dispute is significant.

## FIRE DEPARTMENT COMPLIANCE

**Q:** 166. My warehouse stores tiles on racking 5 metres high. The fire department inspected and says I need a "storage licence" because the racking exceeds 4 metres. Is this correct and what does it cost?
**A:** Under municipal fire by-laws (varies by municipality), high-rack storage may require a fire safety certificate or storage permit when exceeding specified heights. The 4-metre threshold is common. Requirements typically include: (a) sprinkler system designed for high-rack storage ( Ordinary Hazard Group III or High Hazard, depending on racking configuration); (b) fire detection and alarm system; (c) compartmentalisation (fire-rated walls every certain area); (d) adequate fire exits and emergency lighting; (e) flue spaces in racking to allow water penetration; (f) fire department access around the building. Costs: sprinkler installation for a 2,000 m² warehouse with 5m racking: R400,000-R800,000; fire certificate application: R5,000-R15,000 depending on municipality; annual inspection fees: R2,000-R5,000. Non-compliance can result in closure of the premises. Engage a fire engineer for a compliance assessment — they can often design a solution that meets requirements at lower cost than the fire department's initial demands.

**Q:** 167. The fire department says my tile warehouse needs a fire hydrant within 45 metres of the building. The nearest municipal hydrant is 120 metres away. They want me to install a private fire hydrant at my cost. Is this my responsibility?
**A:** Municipal by-laws typically require the property owner to ensure adequate firefighting water supply. If the municipal hydrant is beyond the effective distance, you may need to install: (a) a private fire hydrant connected to the municipal main (requires municipal consent and connection fee); (b) a fire water tank (minimum 20,000-45,000 litres depending on floor area and hazard classification); (c) a booster pump to achieve required pressure. The cost is yours as the property owner (or tenant, depending on lease terms). Budget: private hydrant connection R30,000-R60,000; fire water tank with pump R80,000-R150,000. Check your lease — if the landlord is responsible for building compliance, they should bear this cost. If you are responsible, factor it into your rent negotiation at lease renewal. This is a life safety requirement — non-compliance can result in criminal prosecution under the Fire Services Act.

**Q:** 168. I want to use part of my warehouse for adhesive and grout mixing (creating a small "wet area"). The fire department says this changes my occupancy classification from "storage" to "factory" with significantly higher fire safety requirements. Is this correct?
**A:** Yes — changing use from storage to manufacturing/processing changes your occupancy classification under SANS 10400-T (fire protection) and potentially SANS 10400-D (public safety). "Factory" occupancy typically requires: (a) higher fire resistance rating for structural elements; (b) more stringent exit requirements (travel distances to exits, exit width); (c) enhanced ventilation (especially for dust-generating processes); (d) potentially a change of use approval from the municipality. If the mixing area is incidental (less than 10% of floor area) and uses non-flammable materials (cement-based adhesives), you may negotiate a mixed occupancy classification with the building control officer. However, if solvents or flammable additives are involved, the fire safety requirements escalate significantly. Get a fire engineer's report before proceeding with any manufacturing activity in a storage facility.

**Q:** 169. My fire sprinkler system activated accidentally and water-damaged R200,000 of stock. The sprinkler maintenance company says the activation was due to a manufacturing defect in the head. My insurer says the damage is not covered because it resulted from "mechanical breakdown" not an insured peril. Is this right?
**A:** Check your policy wording carefully. Standard commercial policies cover "accidental damage" including sprinkler leakage. Some policies exclude damage caused by "mechanical or electrical breakdown" of equipment — but sprinkler heads are passive devices, not mechanical equipment. Your argument: (a) the sprinkler activation was an accidental discharge, which is a standard insured peril; (b) the maintenance company may be liable for negligence if they recently serviced the system; (c) the sprinkler manufacturer may be liable for the defective head under product liability. Your recovery paths: (a) claim from your insurer under accidental damage; (b) if insurer repudiates, escalate to the Ombudsman; (c) claim from the maintenance company's professional indemnity insurance; (d) claim from the sprinkler manufacturer. Do not accept the first repudiation — sprinkler discharge is one of the most common commercial property claims and is generally covered. Document: the head that failed, maintenance records, and the water damage extent.

**Q:** 170. The fire department has ordered me to clear a 1.5-metre aisle between all racking rows for fire access. This reduces my storage capacity by 20%. Can I claim compensation from the municipality for loss of storage income?
**A:** No — fire safety requirements are imposed in the public interest and the municipality is immune from claims for loss resulting from compliance with safety regulations (unless the requirement is ultra vires or applied discriminatorily). The 1.5-metre aisle requirement is standard in SANS 10400-T and international fire codes — it allows firefighters to access and suppress fires within racking systems. Your options: (a) comply — the fire department can close your premises for non-compliance; (b) install an in-rack sprinkler system, which may allow narrower aisles (consult a fire engineer); (c) reconfigure racking to optimise the remaining space — consider narrower racking with deeper bays; (d) rent additional off-site storage to compensate for the lost capacity. Factor fire compliance costs into your pricing — it is a cost of doing business. The fire department's order is not negotiable without professional engineering intervention.

## ENVIRONMENTAL COMPLIANCE

**Q:** 171. I generate approximately 8 tonnes of tile offcuts and broken tiles per year. The waste collector I use dumps them illegally in a field in Soweto. I discovered this when I saw my branded boxes in a news report about illegal dumping. Am I liable?
**A:** Yes — under the National Environmental Management Act (NEMA) and the Waste Act, the generator of waste is responsible for its lawful disposal from cradle to grave. Illegal dumping by your waste contractor does not absolve you — you have a duty of care to ensure lawful disposal. Penalties: (a) fines up to R10 million or imprisonment up to 10 years for serious offences; (b) cleanup costs; (c) reputational damage. Your immediate actions: (a) engage a reputable licensed waste management contractor with a Waste Management Licence; (b) request waste manifest documents for every collection (proof of lawful disposal at a licensed landfill); (c) if the illegal dumping is traced to you, cooperate with authorities and fund the cleanup — this may reduce penalties; (d) lay criminal charges against the rogue contractor. Going forward: only use contractors registered on the South African Waste Information Centre (SAWIC) database. Request quarterly waste disposal certificates. The cost of lawful disposal (R500-R1,500 per tonne) is far less than the liability for illegal dumping.

**Q:** 172. My tile cutting operation generates silica dust. An inspector from the Department of Employment and Labour visited and issued a compliance notice requiring dust extraction and health surveillance for workers. What must I do and what will it cost?
**A:** Silica dust is a regulated hazardous chemical substance under the Hazardous Chemical Substances Regulations (HCS). Your obligations: (a) install local exhaust ventilation (LEV) at cutting stations — budget R15,000-R40,000 per station; (b) conduct air monitoring to ensure respirable silica levels are below the Occupational Exposure Limit (OEL) of 0.1 mg/m³; (c) implement a health surveillance programme — baseline and annual medical examinations (chest X-ray, lung function test) for exposed workers — R800-R1,500 per worker per year; (d) provide respiratory protective equipment (RPE) as a last resort after engineering controls; (e) train workers on silica hazards; (f) maintain a health and safety file. Non-compliance fines: R50,000-R500,000 per violation. The inspector can issue a prohibition notice stopping work until compliance. Engage an occupational hygienist for an assessment and remediation plan — their report (R8,000-R15,000) demonstrates good faith and may reduce penalties.

**Q:** 173. I want to recycle my tile offcuts into aggregate for concrete production. Do I need a waste management licence for this, and are tile offcuts classified as general waste or hazardous waste?
**A:** Tile offcuts (ceramic and porcelain) are generally classified as General Waste (GW) under the Waste Classification and Management Regulations, not hazardous waste — provided they do not contain lead-based glazes or other contaminants. However, processing waste for recycling may trigger licensing requirements: (a) if you are merely separating and storing offcuts for collection by a recycler, no licence is needed; (b) if you are crushing/processing the tiles on-site to produce aggregate, you may need a Waste Management Licence under Category C (waste storage and treatment) or an exemption from the DEFF; (c) if annual waste processed exceeds specified thresholds, a licence is definitely required. Contact your provincial Department of Environment for guidance — many provinces have streamlined processes for low-risk waste recycling. Alternatively, partner with an existing licensed recycler who collects your offcuts and processes them at their facility — this avoids licensing requirements for you.

**Q:** 174. A customer returned tiles and claimed they were "environmentally unsafe" because the packaging stated they were "tested on animals." This is impossible for tiles. But the customer threatened to report me to the Green Scorpions ( environmental enforcement). How do I handle this?
**A:** This appears to be a confused or vexatious customer. Ceramic tiles are inorganic mineral products — no animal testing is involved in tile manufacturing. The "Green Scorpions" (the Environmental Management Inspectorate) deal with serious environmental crimes — they do not respond to consumer complaints about product labelling. Your response: (a) calmly explain that ceramic tiles are mineral-based and no animal testing is conducted; (b) offer to show the product's environmental certifications (if any) such as ISO 14001, Ecolabel, or LEED compliance; (c) if the customer persists, ask for their concern in writing — vexatious complainants rarely follow through; (d) do not offer a refund for a non-existent issue — it encourages frivolous complaints. If the customer reports you to any authority, cooperate fully — a quick explanation will resolve the matter. Consider whether this customer is genuinely confused or attempting to extort a refund through threats.

**Q:** 175. My landlord is demanding I remove all asbestos-containing material (ACMs) from the warehouse roof before my lease ends. The asbestos was there before I moved in. Who is responsible for removal and what does it cost?
**A:** Asbestos removal is regulated under the Asbestos Abatement Regulations 2020. Responsibility depends on your lease: (a) if the lease requires you to return the premises in the same condition as received (fair wear and tear excepted), you are not responsible for pre-existing asbestos; (b) if you disturbed the asbestos during your tenancy (e.g., drilled into roof sheets, damaged them), you may be liable for remediation; (c) the landlord as property owner has ultimate responsibility for asbestos management. Asbestos roof removal for a 1,000 m² warehouse: R250,000-R500,000 including licensed removal, disposal at a licensed hazardous waste facility, air monitoring, and clearance certificate. The landlord cannot force you to pay for pre-existing asbestos unless your lease specifically requires it. If there is a dispute, the Department of Employment and Labour can issue a compliance notice to the property owner (the landlord). Do not attempt DIY removal — it is a criminal offence.

## ONLINE MARKETPLACE SELLING

**Q:** 176. I want to sell tiles on the Builders Marketplace platform. They require me to commit to 48-hour dispatch and 14-day free returns. My tiles are bulky and returns cost R350 per box. How do I make this economically viable?
**A:** Marketplace selling of tiles is challenging due to logistics costs. Strategies: (a) limit marketplace offerings to your fastest-moving, highest-margin SKUs — do not list the full range; (b) build the return cost into your pricing — add R200-R300 per box to cover expected return rate (typically 8-15% for building materials due to customer ordering errors); (c) use detailed product descriptions, high-resolution images, and a "order samples first" option to reduce wrong-purchase returns; (d) negotiate with the marketplace for category-specific terms — Builders may accept 72-hour dispatch and customer-paid returns for bulky items; (e) offer "click and collect" from your warehouse as the primary fulfilment method, with delivery as a paid option; (f) set minimum order values (e.g., R2,000) to make the logistics cost bearable. Calculate your total cost of selling on the platform: commission (10-15%) + payment processing (2-3%) + packaging (R50/box) + delivery (R150-350 depending on distance) + returns allocation (8-15% of revenue) = total cost of 25-40%. Your marketplace pricing must reflect this.

**Q:** 177. A customer bought tiles from me on Takealot and gave a 1-star review stating "tiles arrived broken, merchant refused refund." The tiles were intact when dispatched — the customer dropped them during unloading. Can I have the review removed?
**A:** Takealot's review policy allows merchants to flag reviews for removal if they are: factually incorrect, contain abusive language, or violate platform rules. However, Takealot generally does not remove negative reviews unless they breach specific policies. Your options: (a) respond professionally to the review: "We are sorry to hear about the damage. Our dispatch records show the tiles were inspected and securely packaged. We have offered to arrange a damage assessment — please contact us directly"; (b) flag the review to Takealot as "disputed factual claim" — provide your dispatch photos and packaging records; (c) if the review is defamatory (makes objectively false statements that damage your reputation), consult a lawyer about a takedown letter — but this is expensive and slow; (d) proactively encourage satisfied customers to leave positive reviews to dilute the negative one. The most effective response is a professional, factual reply that demonstrates to future customers that you handle issues responsibly.

**Q:** 178. Builders Marketplace requires all product images to have a white background and no branding watermarks. My supplier's brand images have their logo embedded. If I remove the logo, am I infringing their copyright or trademark?
**A:** If the supplier has provided you with authorised product images for marketing purposes, removing their logo to comply with marketplace requirements is generally permissible — you are adapting the image for a specific sales channel, not claiming ownership of the brand. However: (a) check your distributorship agreement for image modification rights; (b) do not replace the supplier's logo with your own — that would be passing off; (c) if the logo is integral to the product design (e.g., embossed on the tile face), it cannot be removed as it represents the actual product. Best practice: request "marketplace-ready" images from the supplier without watermarks. Most suppliers have these for digital commerce. If they do not, take your own product photographs — this also differentiates your listing from competitors using the same supplier images.

**Q:** 179. I sold tiles through an online marketplace and the customer paid via credit card. Three months later, the customer initiated a chargeback claiming "item not as described." The tiles were exactly as described. How do I dispute the chargeback?
**A:** Chargebacks are a significant risk for online merchants. Your dispute process: (a) respond to the marketplace/payment processor within the specified timeframe (usually 7-10 days); (b) provide evidence: product description page screenshots, product specifications, photos of the actual tiles dispatched, delivery confirmation with signature, and any customer correspondence; (c) if you have a signed delivery note and the customer did not report issues within 7 days of delivery, argue they accepted the goods; (d) under CPA section 55, the customer must prove the goods are not reasonably suitable for the purpose — a vague "not as described" claim requires substantiation. Chargeback win rates for merchants are low (20-30%) because card schemes tend to favour cardholders. Prevention: (a) detailed product descriptions with measurements, material specifications, and care instructions; (b) require customer acknowledgement of product details before checkout; (c) photograph every order before dispatch; (d) use signed delivery confirmation. Build a chargeback reserve of 2-3% of online revenue.

**Q:** 180. An online marketplace customer ordered 50 boxes of tiles, received them, and then claimed they "never ordered them" — suggesting fraud on their account. The marketplace refunded them and debited my account. The tiles were not returned. What can I do?
**A:** This is "friendly fraud" — increasingly common in e-commerce. Your options: (a) dispute the refund through the marketplace's seller protection programme — provide delivery confirmation, GPS tracking, and signature; (b) if the marketplace debited your account without a valid return, this may breach their seller terms — escalate to their merchant support team and threaten to delist; (c) the customer has been unjustly enriched — send a formal letter of demand for the tile value plus costs; (d) if the value exceeds R20,000, consider litigation in the Magistrate's Court; (e) if the customer used a stolen credit card, the liability typically falls on the card issuer, not the merchant — but marketplaces often pass this risk to sellers. Prevention: (a) verify billing and delivery addresses match for high-value orders; (b) require ID verification for orders above a threshold; (c) use a fraud detection service (e.g., Stripe Radar, PayPal fraud filters); (d) photograph the delivery with the customer's premises visible as evidence. For marketplace sellers, the risk of fraud losses is a cost of doing business — factor it into your pricing.

## CROSS-CUTTING & EDGE CASES

**Q:** 181. A traditional council in rural Eastern Cape wants me to supply tiles for a community hall but insists on paying in instalments from community fundraising. There is no written contract. How do I protect myself?
**A:** Trading with traditional councils in former homeland areas requires special care: (a) the council may not have legal personality to contract — check if they are registered as a juristic person or if the Tribal Authority Act gives them contracting capacity; (b) demand a resolution from the council authorising the purchase and specifying payment terms; (c) require payment of at least 70% upfront before delivery — community projects are notorious for funding shortfalls; (d) obtain personal suretyships from senior councillors or the traditional leader (Induna) — this gives you recourse against individuals with assets; (e) register a notarial bond over the tiles until full payment (retention of title); (f) deliver in phases against payment — do not release the full order until fully paid. Document everything in writing, even if the council is uncomfortable with formal contracts — explain that your insurer and SARS require documentation. Be respectful of cultural protocols while protecting your business interests.

**Q:** 182. A customer wants to use swimming pool tiles (glazed ceramic) for their kitchen splashback. I warned them the tiles are not rated for food preparation areas due to the glaze composition. They insist and will sign a waiver. If something goes wrong, does the waiver protect me?
**A:** A signed waiver does not override the CPA. Under section 48, any term that purports to waive the consumer's rights under the Act is void. Even with a signed waiver, you remain liable if: (a) the tiles cause harm due to an unsafe characteristic; (b) you should have known the intended use was dangerous; (c) you failed to provide adequate safety information. Your best protection: (a) refuse the sale if you genuinely believe the product is unsafe for the intended use; (b) if you proceed, provide a written warning specifying the risk and recommending an appropriate alternative; (c) ensure the tiles carry the correct SANS classification and labelling; (d) maintain product liability insurance. A waiver may be considered in mitigation of damages (reducing the amount you pay) but will not eliminate liability. If the customer becomes insistent, consider whether the sale is worth the risk.

**Q:** 183. My business partner and I are splitting our tile merchant business. He is taking the installation division; I am keeping the supply division. How do we handle existing stock that is jointly owned and existing customer contracts?
**A:** This is a complex unbundling requiring: (a) a formal partnership dissolution agreement drafted by a commercial attorney; (b) stock valuation by an independent assessor — each party takes stock proportionate to their ownership or buys out the other's share; (c) customer contract allocation — assign contracts to the relevant division with customer consent (unilateral assignment without consent may breach the contracts); (d) supplier agreement renegotiation — suppliers may need to approve the split and enter new agreements with each entity; (e) BEE implications — if your combined BEE status was based on shared ownership, each new entity must be independently rated; (f) tax clearance and VAT registration for the new entity; (g) employee allocation — follow section 197 of the Labour Relations Act if the division is a transfer of a going concern (employees transfer with their terms and conditions). Budget R50,000-R150,000 for legal, accounting, and valuation costs. Do not attempt an informal split — it creates disputes for years.

**Q:** 184. A customer paid a R50,000 deposit for tiles, then died before delivery. Their executor is demanding the deposit back but the tiles were already ordered from my supplier (non-refundable). What is my legal position?
**A:** When a customer dies, their contract with you does not automatically terminate — it becomes an obligation of their estate. The executor can choose to: (a) honour the contract and take delivery (paying the balance); (b) cancel the contract, in which case standard cancellation terms apply. If your terms state deposits are non-refundable once orders are placed with the supplier, this is enforceable against the estate — provided the term is fair and was communicated. However, under the CPA, the estate may argue the term is unfair. Your practical approach: (a) offer to cancel and refund the deposit less your actual costs (supplier cancellation fee, administration); (b) if the tiles are generic and resellable, offer a full refund once you resell them; (c) if the tiles are custom-ordered and non-returnable, retain the deposit with a clear breakdown of your costs. Be empathetic in communication — the executor is dealing with bereavement — but protect your commercial position.

**Q:** 185. I want to offer a loyalty programme where contractors earn points redeemable for merchandise (tools, braai equipment) rather than discounts. Are there tax implications for me and the contractors?
**A:** Loyalty programmes have tax implications: (a) for you, the cost of loyalty rewards is tax-deductible as a marketing expense; (b) for the contractor (if a registered business), the value of rewards received may be considered taxable fringe benefits if they exceed R5,000 per annum — but this is rarely enforced for small business loyalty programmes; (c) if the loyalty points are linked to purchase volume, they are treated as a trade discount for VAT purposes — you must account for VAT on the full selling price, not the net price after points value; (d) if rewards are given as prizes in a competition/lottery, they may be subject to 15% LOTTO tax (unlikely to apply to standard loyalty programmes). Structuring: keep rewards below R5,000 per contractor per year; issue annual statements showing points earned and redeemed; record the cost as marketing expense. Consult your accountant on the specific VAT treatment — SARS views loyalty rewards as part of the overall transaction price for VAT purposes.

**Q:** 186. A competitor has registered a business name almost identical to mine ("Tile Master" vs my "Tile Masters") and is operating in the same city. They are confusing my customers. What legal action can I take?
**A:** You have multiple potential claims: (a) passing off — if you can prove goodwill in your name, confusion, and damage; (b) unlawful competition — if they are deliberately free-riding on your reputation; (c) if you have a registered trademark for "Tile Masters," trademark infringement (stronger claim than passing off); (d) if the name was registered at CIPC after yours, you can object to the registration under the Companies Act (objection within 12 months of your becoming aware). Immediate actions: (a) send a cease-and-desist letter from an attorney; (b) if they continue, apply for an interim interdict to stop them using the name — this is urgent and can be obtained within days if confusion is proven; (c) lodge a complaint with CIPC to deregister or amend their name; (d) simultaneously, strengthen your own branding to reduce confusion (distinctive logo, tagline, colour scheme). Trademark registration is critical — if you have not registered your trading name, do so immediately through a trademark attorney (R4,000-R8,000 per class).

**Q:** 187. My 18-year-old son works in the business during school holidays. I pay him a casual wage. A labour inspector says I must register him for UIF and deduct PAYE. Is this correct for holiday employment?
**A:** Under South African labour law: (a) any person working more than 24 hours per month must be registered for UIF — this applies to casual and holiday workers; (b) PAYE applies if the employee earns above the tax threshold (currently R91,250 per annum for individuals under 65) — casual holiday earnings typically do not exceed this; (c) if your son is a genuine employee (works under your direction, uses your equipment, paid a wage), employment law applies regardless of family relationship; (d) however, if he is genuinely assisting as a family member without a formal employment relationship, the inspector may be overreaching — but this is a grey area. Safest approach: register him for UIF (employer contribution only, 1% of wage) and do not deduct PAYE if he is below the threshold. The UIF registration cost is minimal and avoids penalties. Keep accurate timesheets and pay slips to demonstrate compliance.

**Q:** 188. A customer wants me to supply tiles for a prison renovation project. The Department of Correctional Services requires that all suppliers and their employees pass a security clearance. None of my staff have criminal records, but the clearance process takes 8 weeks. Can I charge the customer for the delay?
**A:** Government security clearances are outside your control and are a known requirement for correctional facility contracts. Your contract with the customer should specify: (a) delivery is subject to security clearance being obtained; (b) the timeline for clearance is an estimate based on the Department's processing time; (c) you are not liable for delays caused by the clearance process. If you did not include these terms in your quotation, you cannot retrospectively charge for delay — but you can negotiate an extension of time with the customer. For future government security work: (a) build clearance processing time into your project schedule; (b) apply for clearances as early as possible; (c) consider which staff members are likely to pass clearance and assign them to sensitive projects; (d) include a "security clearance contingency" in your pricing to cover the administrative cost and timeline risk.

**Q:** 189. I discovered that my warehouse supervisor has been running a competing tile supply business from my premises after hours, using my phones and computer. He has taken my customer list. Can I claim damages for theft of confidential information?
**A:** Yes — multiple claims: (a) breach of employment contract (fiduciary duty, conflict of interest); (b) theft of confidential information (your customer list is a trade secret); (c) unfair competition (using your resources for his business); (d) possibly computer-related fraud if he accessed your systems without authorisation. Your actions: (a) suspend him pending disciplinary investigation; (b) preserve digital evidence (emails, phone logs, system access records) before he can delete it; (c) commission a forensic IT audit to document the extent of data theft and resource misuse; (d) institute disciplinary proceedings for gross misconduct — dismissal is appropriate; (e) lay criminal charges for theft and fraud; (f) sue for damages — quantify the loss (diverted customers, misuse of resources, devaluation of customer list). An urgent interdict can stop him from using the stolen information while the case proceeds. Budget R50,000-R150,000 for legal and forensic costs.

**Q:** 190. A customer bought tiles from me and paid in cash. They now want a VAT invoice to claim input tax. But I recorded the sale as a cash transaction without their details. Can I issue a retrospective VAT invoice?
**A:** Yes, you can issue a tax invoice retrospectively provided: (a) the original transaction was a taxable supply; (b) you have a valid tax invoice containing all required particulars (your VAT number, customer's VAT number if over R5,000, date, description, amount, VAT charged or tax rate); (c) you include the transaction in the correct VAT period (or adjust in the current period if the original period is closed). If you did not charge VAT at the time (e.g., you treated it as a zero-rated or exempt sale in error), you must correct this. Retrospective tax invoices are permitted under SARS VAT rules — issue it now with the original transaction date and a note "retrospective tax invoice issued [date]." Ensure the customer's VAT number is on the invoice if the supply exceeds R5,000. Do not issue a VAT invoice for a sale where no VAT was actually charged — this constitutes tax fraud.

**Q:** 191. My supplier has been placed under business rescue. I have paid a R300,000 deposit for tiles that have not been delivered. The business rescue practitioner says I am an unsecured creditor and will get "cents in the rand." Can I claim my tiles specifically (they are in the supplier's warehouse) or am I stuck in the creditor queue?
**A:** Under business rescue (section 128 of the Companies Act), a moratorium applies to all claims against the company. However: (a) if your contract contained a valid retention of title clause and the tiles are identifiable (specific batch, your order number, segregated), you can claim your specific goods — they do not form part of the rescue practitioner's estate; (b) if you registered a security interest (notarial bond or PPSA security) over the stock, you are a secured creditor with priority; (c) if you have neither retention of title nor registered security, you are an unsecured creditor and must wait for the business rescue plan. Act immediately: (a) identify your specific stock in the warehouse (visit with the practitioner's permission); (b) provide your purchase order and proof of payment; (c) if stock is identifiable, demand its release — the practitioner cannot withhold your property. If the practitioner refuses, apply to court for an order compelling release of your property. Time is critical — stock may be sold to fund the rescue.

**Q:** 192. I want to retire and sell my tile merchant business. A competitor wants to buy my customer list and stock but not my premises or staff. What are my obligations to my employees under section 197 of the Labour Relations Act?
**A:** Section 197 applies when a business (or part of it) is transferred as a going concern. If the competitor is buying only stock and customer list (not the business itself), section 197 does not apply — the employees remain your responsibility. You must: (a) follow fair retrenchment procedures if you are closing the business (consultation, selection criteria, severance pay = 1 week per year of service, notice pay); (b) if the competitor is taking over your premises and continuing a similar business, they may be obliged to take on your employees on the same terms — consult a labour lawyer on whether this constitutes a transfer of a going concern; (c) inform employees of the sale process as soon as practicable — secrecy breeds distrust and potential litigation; (d) consider negotiating with the competitor to take key staff as a condition of sale — this may increase the sale price but reduces your retrenchment costs. The customer list is a valuable asset — ensure it is properly protected with confidentiality clauses in the sale agreement.

**Q:** 193. A customer claims the tiles I supplied contain radioactive material (they read about "radioactive granite" online) and are unsafe for their home. They want a full refund and compensation for "emotional distress." How do I respond?
**A:** Ceramic and porcelain tiles are made from clay, silica, and feldspar — materials with negligible radioactivity. "Radioactive tiles" is an internet myth unless the tiles contain specific mineral additives (rare in standard ceramics). Your response: (a) request the specific basis for their concern — what article, what brand, what test results?; (b) provide the tile's technical data sheet showing composition — standard ceramics contain no radioactive materials; (c) if the customer persists, offer an independent laboratory test for radioactivity (gamma spectroscopy, R5,000-R10,000) — at their cost if the test shows no radioactivity; (d) emotional distress compensation is not recoverable under the CPA for unfounded fears — actual harm must be proven; (e) do not refund without evidence — it validates a baseless claim and sets a precedent. If the tiles are porcelain (fired at 1,200°C+), they are chemically and radiologically inert. This is a customer education issue, not a product safety issue.

**Q:** 194. My biggest supplier has been acquired by a larger international group. They have sent a new distribution agreement that replaces my existing terms with unfavourable conditions (shorter payment terms, higher MOQ, no returns). Can they force me to sign?
**A:** No — your existing agreement remains valid until it expires or is lawfully terminated. The new owner inherits all existing contracts (cession of contracts applies on share sale, not asset sale). They cannot unilaterally impose new terms mid-contract. Your options: (a) refuse to sign the new agreement and continue on existing terms until expiry; (b) negotiate the new terms — the new owner may have flexibility they are not initially showing; (c) if they threaten to terminate your existing agreement, check the termination clause — most distributorship agreements require notice (3-12 months); (d) if they refuse to supply under existing terms, this is breach of contract — you can claim damages and apply for an urgent interdict to force supply while the dispute is resolved. However, consider the relationship: fighting a new owner rarely ends well. Assess whether the new terms are genuinely unworkable or merely less favourable, and whether alternative suppliers exist.

**Q:** 195. A heritage architect has specified reclaimed Victorian-era tiles for a renovation in the Bo-Kaap. I have sourced replicas from Portugal but the heritage authority insists on genuine period tiles. Can I challenge this as a restraint of trade?
**A:** Heritage authorities in the Western Cape (Heritage Western Cape) and Cape Town have significant power to regulate alterations to heritage-protected buildings and precincts. The Bo-Kaap is a Protected Area under the National Heritage Resources Act. If HWC has issued a heritage permit condition requiring genuine period materials, this is not a restraint of trade — it is a lawful exercise of heritage protection powers. Your options: (a) source genuine reclaimed Victorian tiles from salvage yards, demolition sites, or UK salvage specialists (expensive but authentic); (b) engage a heritage consultant to argue that high-quality replicas meet the conservation objective — some heritage officers accept replicas if they are visually and materially indistinguishable; (c) obtain a written motivation from the architect supporting the replica option; (d) if the heritage authority is unreasonable, appeal to the Provincial Heritage Resources Authority. Do not install replicas without heritage approval — the building owner can be ordered to remove non-compliant work at their cost.

**Q:** 196. I want to sponsor a local school rugby team's kit in exchange for branding on their jerseys and at the school. The school says I must provide a BEE certificate to sponsor them. Is this required for a private sponsorship?
**A:** No — a private sponsorship is a commercial transaction, not a government procurement process. The school cannot require a BEE certificate unless: (a) the sponsorship is funded by government money channelled through the school; (b) the school is a public school and the sponsorship agreement is subject to PFMA or provincial procurement rules (unusual for kit sponsorships); (c) the school has its own policy requiring BEE credentials for all suppliers. As a private business, you can sponsor whoever you choose without BEE documentation. However, for your own BEE scorecard, the sponsorship may qualify for Socio-Economic Development (SED) points if the school is a registered PBO with section 18A status — so it is in your interest to obtain a section 18A receipt regardless. If the school insists on BEE and it is a genuine condition, provide your BEE certificate if you have one, or explain that BEE is not applicable to private sponsorships.

**Q:** 197. A contractor paid me with an EFT that cleared. Two weeks later, his bank reversed the payment claiming "unauthorised transaction." I had already dispatched the tiles. The bank says I must resolve it with the customer. What is my legal position?
**A:** This is a difficult situation. When an EFT clears into your account, you are entitled to rely on that payment — the bank's subsequent reversal is between the bank and their customer (the contractor), not your problem. Your position: (a) the contractor authorised the payment (he gave you the instruction or made the transfer himself) — the "unauthorised" claim is likely a dispute between the contractor and his bank, not genuine fraud; (b) you are a bona fide recipient for value — you gave consideration (tiles) in exchange for the payment; (c) write to the bank stating you are a bona fide third party and the reversal has unjustly enriched their customer at your expense; (d) demand payment from the contractor — the reversed payment does not extinguish his debt; (e) if the contractor refuses, sue him for the outstanding amount — the fact that his bank reversed the payment is his problem, not yours. Prevention: for large payments, wait 7-10 days before dispatching goods if the customer relationship is new; for established customers, EFT reliance is standard business practice and the reversal risk is low.

**Q:** 198. My commercial lease allows the landlord to increase rent by "CPI + 2%" annually. CPI has been high (5-6%) and the compounded increases over 5 years have made the rent unaffordable. Is there any limit on rent escalation in commercial leases?
**A:** Unlike residential leases (which have limited rent control under the Rental Housing Act), commercial leases are entirely governed by contract. A "CPI + 2%" escalation clause is enforceable regardless of how high the compounded increase becomes. Your options: (a) negotiate a rent review or cap with the landlord — if market rents have not kept pace with your escalation, the landlord may prefer a reduced rent to a vacant premises; (b) exercise a break clause if your lease has one; (c) assign the lease to another tenant if permitted; (d) if the escalation was not clearly explained when you signed, argue the landlord had a common law duty of fair dealing — but this is weak; (e) if the landlord has breached any lease term, you may have grounds for termination. Going forward: negotiate a cap on annual escalation (e.g., maximum 8% regardless of CPI) and a rent review clause at year 3 or 5 allowing market-rate adjustment.

**Q:** 199. A customer bought expensive marble-look porcelain tiles from me and installed them. After the first winter, efflorescence (white powder) appeared on the surface. They blame the tiles. My tiler says the subfloor was not properly cured. The customer is threatening to name and shame me on Facebook with photos. What should I do?
**A:** Efflorescence is a soluble salt deposit that migrates from the cement screed or adhesive through the tile to the surface. It is almost never caused by the tile itself — it indicates moisture migration from an inadequately cured subfloor or insufficient damp-proofing. Your response strategy: (a) get a qualified tiling inspector to assess the cause — the cost (R3,000-R5,000) is worth it for the evidence; (b) if the inspection confirms subfloor/adhesive issues, share the report with the customer and explain that efflorescence is an installation/substrate issue, not a tile defect; (c) offer a solution: professional cleaning to remove the efflorescence, and if it recurs, re-tiling with proper damp-proofing — but this is at the customer's cost unless you or your tiler were responsible for the subfloor preparation; (d) regarding the Facebook threat: if the customer posts false statements of fact ("these tiles are defective" when the cause is installation), that is defamatory — send a lawyer's letter warning of legal action if defamatory posts are published; (e) do not engage in a public social media argument — respond once, factually, and then take the dispute offline. Prevention: always provide installation guidelines specifying subfloor curing requirements (minimum 6 weeks for cement screeds, 1-3% moisture content).

**Q:** 200. I am 62 years old and want to hand over my tile merchant business to my daughter over the next 5 years. She has been working in the business for 10 years. What is the most tax-efficient way to structure this succession, and what are the pitfalls?
**A:** Tax-efficient succession planning options: (a) gradual share transfer — gift or sell shares annually within the donations tax threshold (R100,000 per annum tax-free) or at a discounted value — capital gains tax applies on the growth in value; (b) family trust — transfer shares to a discretionary trust, with your daughter as beneficiary and potential trustee — this provides asset protection and estate planning benefits but triggers CGT and transfer duty considerations; (c) employee share ownership — your daughter buys shares over time using dividends or salary, potentially qualifying for BEE ownership points; (d) section 42 asset-for-share rollover — transfer business assets to a company in exchange for shares (tax-deferred if structured correctly). Pitfalls: (a) donations tax at 20% on values above R100,000 per annum; (b) estate duty at 20% on the value remaining in your estate; (c) CGT on deemed disposal at death; (d) family disputes — document everything in a shareholders' agreement with clear roles, dispute resolution, and exit mechanisms; (e) SARS scrutiny of related-party transactions — ensure valuations are at arm's length with independent valuation. Engage a tax attorney and estate planner — the fees (R30,000-R80,000) will save significant tax and prevent family conflict. Start now — 5 years allows for phased implementation and annual tax planning.
