# Persona 7: Interior Designers — Q&A Knowledge Base

## Category 1: Showroom Experience & Inspiration

**Q1:** What is Spec Lab, and where is it located?
**A:** Spec Lab is Tiletoria's dedicated professional showroom for architects, interior designers, and property developers. It's located at Spec Lab Menlyn, and offers a curated environment where you can browse imported collections, work in private consultation spaces, and draw inspiration from styled room vignettes.

**Q2:** Can I just walk into Spec Lab, or do I need an appointment?

**Q3:** What makes the Spec Lab different from a regular tile showroom?

## Category 2: Trade Account & Pricing

**Q59:** What discount do interior designers receive on Tiletoria products?
**A:** Registered interior designers receive a 15-25% trade discount depending on annual volume and Design Circle tier. Silver members start at 15%, Gold at 20%, and Platinum at 25% on most collections.

**Q60:** How do I set up a trade account with Tiletoria?
**A:** Visit Spec Lab or any Tiletoria branch with your CIFID practice registration, business certificate, and proof of address. We'll complete the application in 30 minutes and activate your account within 24 hours.

**Q61:** Is there a minimum annual spend to maintain my trade account?
**A:** There's no minimum to maintain your account, but your Design Circle tier — which determines your discount level and benefits — is reviewed annually based on spend. Silver requires no minimum, Gold R150,000+, and Platinum R400,000+.

**Q62:** Can my trade discount be applied to contractor-mediated purchases?
**A:** Yes. If you specify Tiletoria products and your contractor procures them, we can structure the transaction so your trade discount is protected. Your liaison will coordinate the paper trail confidentially.

**Q63:** How is my markup protected when the contractor buys directly?
**A:** We understand that your creative fee and product markup are part of your practice revenue. Your liaison manages procurement so the contractor receives trade-appropriate pricing while your margin remains confidential and protected.

**Q64:** What payment terms are available for design practices?
**A:** Standard terms are payment on order for new accounts. After six months of trading history, qualifying practices can apply for 30-day account terms. Platinum members may negotiate 60-day terms on confirmed projects.

**Q65:** Can I pay for samples, or are they free?
**A:** Full-size sample loans are free for registered designers — no deposit, two-week loan period. If you wish to keep samples permanently for your library, they're available at trade cost plus VAT.

**Q66:** Does the trade discount apply to sanware and accessories too?
**A:** Yes, your trade discount applies across the full Tiletoria range including our premium tapware range, our tapware range, our tapware range, and our tapware range sanware, as well as grout, trim, and accessories. It's a complete bathroom and tiling specification under one account.

**Q67:** Can I get a pro forma invoice for client budget approval before ordering?
**A:** Absolutely. Your liaison will prepare a detailed pro forma with full product descriptions, quantities, trade pricing, and estimated delivery. You can present this directly to your client or fold it into your own fee proposal.

**Q68:** Is pricing negotiable for very large hospitality or commercial projects?
**A:** For projects exceeding R500,000 in tile value, our commercial team can negotiate project-specific pricing. This is separate from your standard Design Circle tier and reflects genuine volume commitment.

**Q69:** Can I structure pricing so my client sees retail rates while I receive trade rates?
**A:** Yes, this is standard practice. We can provide retail-list documentation for client presentations while your trade account processes at the discounted rate. Your liaison manages this discreetly.

**Q70:** What happens to my discount if I don't meet the annual tier threshold?
**A:** Your tier is reviewed annually. If you don't meet the threshold, you'll move to the appropriate tier for your actual spend. We don't penalise — we adjust. Many designers fluctuate between Gold and Silver based on project cycles.

**Q71:** Can I have multiple trade accounts if I have separate residential and commercial practices?
**A:** We recommend a single account per legal entity for clean administration. Your Design Circle tier aggregates all spend, which typically works in your favour. Your liaison can tag projects by type for internal reporting.

**Q72:** Do you offer project-specific credit facilities?
**A:** For confirmed commercial projects with signed contracts and payment guarantees, we can establish project-specific credit facilities. This is arranged through our commercial division, not your standard trade account.

**Q73:** Can my bookkeeper or PA manage the account administration?
**A:** Yes, you can nominate authorised contacts on your account. They can place orders, query deliveries, and manage payments, but pricing and discount tiers remain confidential to you as the principal.

**Q74:** How quickly can I get a quote for a full project specification?
**A:** For projects with completed schedules of quantities, we return detailed quotes within 24 hours. For complex hospitality specifications requiring value engineering, allow 48-72 hours for our commercial team.

**Q75:** Is there a difference in discount between Italian imports and local ranges?
**A:** Your Design Circle discount applies uniformly across our range. However, Italian and Spanish imports have higher base prices, so the absolute Rand value of your discount is greater on imported collections.

**Q76:** Can I get early access to sale pricing before it's public?
**A:** Gold and Platinum Design Circle members receive 72-hour advance notice of any promotional pricing. You can secure sale-priced stock for pending projects before the public announcement.

**Q77:** What documentation do I receive for my tax records?
**A:** All invoices are VAT-compliant with full product descriptions. We can provide monthly statements, project-specific summaries, and annual spend reports for your accountant. Everything is SARS-compliant.

## Category 6: Aesthetic Selection Support

**Q236:** Can you help me choose between warm and cool tones for my project?

**Q237:** What's the difference between matte and polished tiles in terms of look and maintenance?
**A:** Polished tiles reflect light, making spaces feel larger and more luxurious, but they show water spots and scratches more readily. Matte tiles absorb light, creating a softer, more intimate atmosphere and hiding imperfections. For bathrooms, matte floor with polished wall is a classic combination.

**Q238:** When should I specify rectified edges versus pressed edges?
**A:** Specify rectified edges when you want minimal grout joints (2mm) and a seamless, contemporary aesthetic. Rectified is essential for large-format tiles and modern minimalism. Pressed edges suit traditional settings where slightly wider joints (3-5mm) are acceptable and the slight cushion feels appropriate.

**Q239:** Can you advise on the best tile format for a small bathroom?
**A:** Counterintuitively, large-format tiles (600x600mm or 600x1200mm) often work best in small bathrooms — fewer grout joints create visual calm and the space feels larger. Avoid tiny mosaics on floors; they create busy grout grids. A feature mosaic on one wall adds interest without overwhelming.

**Q240:** What laying patterns do you recommend beyond basic grid?
**A:** Herringbone adds dynamism to rectangular formats, running bond (brick pattern) softens rigid spaces, chevron creates directional energy, and basket-weave brings traditional elegance. For large-format rectified tiles, stack bond (perfect grid) is the contemporary standard. Your liaison can show you examples of each.

**Q241:** How do I choose between a bold feature tile and a subtle texture?

**Q353:** What's the difference between cement-based grout and epoxy-based grout?
**A:** Cement-based grout is traditional, easier to apply, and suitable for most installations. Epoxy grout is virtually stain-proof, chemical-resistant, and ideal for commercial kitchens, showers, and stain-prone areas. It's harder to apply and costs more, but the performance is unmatched.

**Q354:** Do you recommend epoxy-based grout for all bathroom floors?
**A:** Epoxy grout is excellent for shower floors and wet areas where mould and staining are concerns. For general bathroom floors, high-quality cement grout with a grout sealer is usually sufficient and more economical. We recommend epoxy for the shower and cement grout for the main bathroom floor.

**Q357:** What's the best way to finish a tile edge where it meets a different material?

**Q359:** What's the standard grout joint width for rectified tiles?
**A:** Rectified tiles are designed for 2mm grout joints — the minimum practical width that accommodates slight size variation and allows for grout application. Pressed-edge tiles typically use 3-5mm joints. Never go below 2mm, even with rectified tiles; the joint is structurally necessary.

**Q360:** Can I get coloured grout in epoxy formulation?

**Q362:** How do I choose between a pencil trim and a metal profile for my edges?
**A:** Pencil trim (ceramic or stone, typically 15-20mm wide) provides a traditional, integrated look — the edge reads as part of the tile installation. ## Category 17: Technical Basics for Designers

**Q613:** What does PEI rating mean, and why should I care about it?
**A:** PEI (Porcelain Enamel Institute) rating measures a tile's resistance to abrasion and wear. It ranges from PEI 0 (wall-only, no foot traffic) to PEI 5 (heavy commercial traffic). For residential interiors, PEI 3 is suitable for normal residential floors, PEI 4 for heavy residential and light commercial. Always match the PEI rating to the traffic level of the space. We label PEI ratings for all our floor tiles and our consultants can guide you to the right specification.

**Q614:** What is a slip rating, and when do I need to specify it?
**A:** Slip ratings (R9 to R13) measure a tile's slip resistance under wet conditions. R9 is suitable for dry interior floors; R10 for general residential wet areas; R11 for commercial kitchens, ramps, and external areas; R12-R13 for high-risk wet environments like swimming pool surrounds and industrial wet areas. For bathrooms, we recommend R10 minimum. For commercial projects, building control officers will require documented slip ratings. We provide slip rating data for all our floor tiles.

**Q615:** What's the difference between ceramic and porcelain tiles technically?
**A:** Porcelain tiles are made from finer clay, fired at higher temperatures (above 1,200°C), and are denser and less porous than ceramic tiles. The key technical difference is water absorption: porcelain absorbs less than 0.5% water, making it frost-resistant and suitable for outdoor use, wet areas, and underfloor heating. Ceramic tiles typically absorb 3-10% water and are best limited to walls and dry interior floors. For professional specifications, porcelain is the safer default.

**Q616:** What are expansion joints, and when are they required?
**A:** Expansion joints are gaps in the tiled surface that allow for thermal movement, structural settlement, and material expansion. They're required at: perimeters where tiles meet walls or columns; over structural movement joints in the substrate; at changes in substrate material; at doorways between rooms; and at regular intervals in large floor areas (typically every 8-10 metres). Expansion joints must be filled with flexible sealant, not grout. Failure to include them is a leading cause of cracked tiles.

**Q617:** What's the difference between cement-based grout and epoxy-based grout?
**A:** cement-based grout is the traditional option — easier to apply, more forgiving, and available in a wide colour range, but porous and prone to staining. Epoxy grout is resin-based, virtually stain-proof, chemically resistant, and far more durable, but more expensive and requiring skilled application. For kitchens, bathrooms, and commercial spaces, we recommend epoxy-based grout. For general residential floors, quality cement grout with an additive is usually sufficient. Our technical team can advise on the best choice for your project.

**Q618:** What does "rectified" mean, and what size grout joint should I specify?
**A:** Rectified tiles are mechanically ground to ensure all tiles in a batch are exactly the same size, allowing for tight, consistent grout joints. For rectified tiles, specify 1.5mm to 2mm joints. For non-rectified tiles, joints should be 3mm or wider to accommodate natural size variation. Tighter joints create a more seamless, contemporary look but require a flatter substrate and more skilled installation.

**Q619:** What is "calibre" in tile terminology, and why does it matter?
**A:** Calibre refers to the dimensional category of a tile batch. Even rectified tiles have slight variations between production runs, so tiles of the same nominal size are grouped into calibre categories. Always ensure all tiles for a single installation area are from the same calibre — mixing calibres will result in visible size variation and uneven joints. We manage calibre consistency within each order, but if you're combining stock from different orders, always verify the calibre markings.

**Q620:** What's the difference between through-body and glazed porcelain?
**A:** Through-body porcelain has colour and pattern running through the entire tile body, so if a corner chips or an edge is cut, the exposed surface matches the face. Glazed porcelain has a white or beige biscuit body with a printed decorative layer on the surface only — chips expose the base colour. Through-body is more expensive but essential for high-traffic areas, commercial projects, and any installation where durability and appearance under wear are critical.

**Q621:** What is "lapato" or "semi-polished" finish?
**A:** Lapato (also called semi-polished or honed polished) is a finish that sits between matte and fully polished. The tile surface undergoes a light polishing process that removes the matte texture without achieving full gloss. The result catches light softly, creates subtle luminosity, and feels refined without the high-maintenance reflectivity of a full polish. Lapato is increasingly popular in residential living spaces and boutique hospitality for its understated luxury.

**Q622:** What does "digital glaze variation" or V-rating mean?

**A:** Polished stone has a glossy, reflective surface that maximises colour depth — it's stunning but shows scratches and etching readily. Honed stone has a smooth, matte finish that feels velvety and hides wear much better — our most recommended finish for residential floors. Brushed (or leathered) stone has a textured, tactile surface created by brushing the stone with abrasive — it offers excellent slip resistance and a rustic, organic character. Each finish transforms the same stone into a different design statement.

**Q624:** What is "water absorption" and why does it matter?

**Q625:** What's the difference between "full-bodied" and "double-charged" porcelain?
**A:** Full-bodied (or through-body) porcelain has consistent colour and pattern throughout the entire tile body. Double-charged porcelain is created by feeding a layer of coloured powder into the press before the main body powder, creating a surface layer that's typically 3-4mm deep. Double-charged offers a cost-effective middle ground — better than surface-glazed for wear resistance, though not as consistent through the full depth as true through-body. It's popular for commercial flooring.

**Q626:** What does "modular" mean in tile terminology?
**A:** A modular collection includes multiple tile sizes that share the same design and are proportionally scaled to work together visually. For example, a modular system might include 600×600mm, 600×300mm, 300×300mm, and 75×300mm border tiles, all from the same design family. This allows designers to create dynamic layouts — field tiles in the large format, borders in the small format — while maintaining perfect visual cohesion.

**Q627:** What's a "tile trim" or "profiles" and when are they used?
**A:** Tile trims (also called profiles or edge trims) are metal, PVC, or matching tile strips that protect and finish exposed tile edges. They're used at: wall corners and external corners; transitions between different floor materials; perimeter edges where tiles meet a different surface; and step nosing for safety. Schluter and similar systems offer trims in various metals, finishes, and profiles that can complement or contrast with your tile selection. We stock a comprehensive range.

**Q628:** What is "substrate preparation" and why is it critical?
**A:** Substrate preparation is everything that happens to the floor or wall surface before tiles are installed. It includes levelling (screeding), cleaning, priming, and ensuring adequate strength and stability. Poor substrate preparation is the leading cause of tile installation failures — cracked tiles, hollow drummy sounds, and debonding. We strongly recommend that your specification includes a requirement for the substrate to meet SANS 10107 standards before tiling commences.

**Q629:** What's the difference between flexible and rigid adhesive?
**A:** Flexible adhesive (also called polymer-modified or S1/S2 adhesive) contains additives that allow it to absorb movement and vibration without cracking. It's required for: wooden or plywood substrates; underfloor heating systems; exterior applications; and areas subject to structural movement. Rigid adhesive is suitable only for stable, non-moving substrates like concrete slabs in dry interior areas. For most professional specifications, flexible adhesive is the safer default.

**Q630:** What is "tanking" or waterproofing in tile installations?
**A:** Tanking is the process of creating a waterproof barrier beneath tiled surfaces in wet areas — showers, bathrooms, balconies, and rooftops. It involves applying a liquid waterproofing membrane (typically two coats) to the walls and floor before tiling, with reinforcement at joints and corners. SANS 10400-L requires waterproofing in all wet areas, and proper tanking is your insurance against water damage, mould, and structural failure. Never skip it.

**Q631:** How thick should tile adhesive bed be?
**A:** For standard wall and floor tiles, adhesive bed thickness ranges from 3mm to 6mm using the thin-bed method, which is suitable for tiles up to approximately 600×600mm on flat, well-prepared substrates. For larger format tiles (900×900mm and above) and uneven substrates, the thick-bed method (10-20mm) may be required. Your tiler should use a notched trowel of appropriate size for the tile format — larger tiles require larger notches for full adhesive coverage.

**Q632:** What's the maximum tile size that can be used on walls?
**A:** Technically, very large tiles can be installed on walls using the correct adhesive and techniques. However, the weight of the tile and the strength of the substrate are limiting factors. On a standard plastered masonry wall, 600×600mm is typically the practical maximum without additional support. For larger wall tiles — 1200×600mm and beyond — the wall substrate needs to be solid, flat, and able to support the weight. Special adhesives and mechanical fixing aids may be required.

**Q633:** What does "nominal size" versus "actual size" mean for tiles?
**A:** Nominal size is the stated dimension (e.g., 600×600mm), while actual size is the precise measured dimension of the tile (e.g., 598×598mm for a rectified tile, or 595×595mm for a pressed tile). The difference accounts for manufacturing tolerances and the grout joint. When calculating quantities, always use the nominal size for area coverage, but be aware that the actual tile will be slightly smaller to accommodate the grout gap.

**Q634:** What's the difference between "pressed" and "rectified" edges?
**A:** Pressed tiles are shaped in a mould and have a natural, slightly rounded edge with some size variation between tiles — they require wider grout joints (3mm+). Rectified tiles have their edges mechanically ground after firing to achieve precise, consistent dimensions and sharp 90-degree edges — allowing for tight grout joints (1.5-2mm) and a seamless appearance. Rectified tiles cost more but deliver a significantly more refined result.

**Q635:** What are "movement joints" versus "structural joints"?
**A:** Structural joints are gaps in the building's structure that allow for major movement — they're designed by the engineer and must be maintained through the tile installation. Movement joints (also called perimeter joints or intermediate joints) are gaps in the tiled surface itself that accommodate thermal expansion, contraction, and minor substrate movement. Both must be filled with flexible sealant, never rigid grout. The tiler must identify and respect both types of joints.

**Q636:** What is "point load" and why might it matter for my tile specification?

**Q637:** What's the difference between "tiling" and "cladding"?
**A:** Tiling refers to installing tiles on floors and walls using adhesive and grout, with full bedding coverage. Cladding refers to fixing larger-format tiles, stone panels, or porcelain slabs to walls using mechanical fixing systems, particularly for exterior facades and large interior feature walls. Cladding requires different engineering considerations, including wind load calculations for exterior applications. Our technical team can advise if your project involves cladding rather than conventional tiling.

**Q638:** What is "tiling over existing tiles" and is it advisable?
**A:** Tiling over existing tiles is possible but not generally recommended for professional specifications. While it saves time and demolition cost, it adds weight to the structure, reduces ceiling height/door clearances, and the success depends entirely on the bond between the existing tiles and the substrate — which you can't verify. If the existing tiles are well-bonded and the additional height is acceptable, use a flexible adhesive and ensure the surface is clean and primed. For high-end projects, proper removal and substrate preparation is always the better route.

**Q639:** What's the difference between "wet areas" and "dry areas" in building regulations?
**A:** Wet areas are defined in SANS 10400-L as spaces subject to regular water exposure — showers, bathrooms, laundries, kitchens, balconies, and rooftops. These areas require waterproofing (tanking), appropriate slip-resistant flooring, and moisture-resistant substrate preparation. Dry areas are all other interior spaces. The distinction matters because building inspectors will check for compliance with wet area regulations, and failure to meet these standards can invalidate warranties and insurance claims.

**Q640:** What does "green screed" versus "dry screed" mean?
**A:** A green screed is a fresh cement screed that is still curing — typically less than 28 days old. Tiles should never be installed on a green screed because the moisture released during curing will prevent proper adhesive bonding and can cause efflorescence (white salt deposits) on the tile surface. A dry screed has cured for at least 28 days and has a moisture content below the threshold specified by the adhesive manufacturer. Always verify screed maturity before tiling.

**Q641:** What's "efflorescence" and how do I prevent it?
**A:** Efflorescence is the white, powdery deposit that appears on tile surfaces and grout lines when moisture draws soluble salts from the cement-based substrate or adhesive to the surface. It's unsightly and difficult to remove permanently. Prevention measures include: ensuring the substrate is fully dry before tiling; using low-alkali adhesive; installing a damp-proof membrane where rising damp is a concern; and ensuring adequate ventilation in the tiled area. If efflorescence appears, it can be treated with specialist cleaners, but prevention is far preferable.

**Q642:** What is "tile lippage" and how is it controlled?
**A:** Lippage is the height difference between adjacent tiles — when one edge sits higher than its neighbour, creating a trip hazard and an unsightly surface. Lippage is controlled by: ensuring a flat substrate; using the correct adhesive bed thickness; employing tile levelling systems (clips and wedges); and skilled trowel technique. For large-format tiles, levelling systems are essential. The acceptable lippage limit is typically 1mm for tiles up to 300mm length and 2mm for larger tiles.

**Q643:** What does "tile adhesive open time" mean?
**A:** Open time is the period after spreading adhesive during which tiles can be placed and adjusted before a skin forms on the adhesive surface. If tiles are placed after the open time has expired, the adhesive won't bond properly, leading to hollow sounds and potential debonding. Open time varies by adhesive type and ambient conditions — hot, dry, or windy conditions reduce open time significantly. Experienced tilers work in manageable sections and check the adhesive surface before placing each tile.

**Q644:** What's the difference between "bonded" and "unbonded" screeds?
**A:** A bonded screed is applied directly onto a concrete slab with bonding agent, typically at 15-40mm thickness. It's used when the existing slab is sound but needs levelling. An unbonded screed is separated from the substrate by a damp-proof membrane, typically at 50mm+ thickness, and is used over concrete slabs with damp issues or over suspended floors. A floating screed sits on insulation and is used with underfloor heating. Each type has specific requirements for tile installation.

**Q645:** What are tile levelling systems, and should I specify them?
**A:** Tile levelling systems are mechanical aids — typically plastic clips and wedges — that hold adjacent tiles at the same height during adhesive curing, preventing lippage. They're essential for large-format tiles (600×600mm and above) and strongly recommended for rectified tiles with tight joints. The clips are left in place, the wedges are removed after the adhesive sets, and the clips are either hidden by grout or snapped off at the base. We recommend specifying levelling systems for any precision installation.

**Q646:** What's "back-buttering" and when is it necessary?

**Q647:** What does "curing time" mean for tile adhesive?
**A:** Curing time is the period required for tile adhesive to fully harden and develop its design strength. Standard cement-based adhesives typically require 24 hours before grouting and 28 days before full traffic load. Rapid-setting adhesives cure faster (groutable in 3-4 hours) but are more expensive. During curing, the tiled area must be protected from traffic, water, and extreme temperatures. Your project programme should include adequate curing time before handover.

**Q648:** What's the difference between "unglazed" and "glazed" porcelain?
**A:** Glazed porcelain has a glass-like coating applied to the surface, which provides colour, pattern, and stain resistance. The glaze can replicate, wood, concrete, or fabric textures with remarkable realism. Unglazed porcelain has no surface coating — the colour and pattern are in the tile body itself. Unglazed tiles are more slip-resistant (ideal for commercial and outdoor use) but can stain if not sealed. Glazed tiles offer greater design flexibility and are easier to clean.

**Q649:** What does "ISO 13006 classification" mean for tiles?
**A:** ISO 13006 is the international standard for ceramic tiles. It classifies tiles by their manufacturing method (pressed or extruded), water absorption (Groups AI to BIII), and intended use (wall or floor). The classification determines the tile's suitability for different applications. All tiles we stock conform to ISO 13006, and our technical datasheets include the relevant classification codes. This is particularly useful when specifying for commercial projects that require documented compliance.

**Q651:** What is "frost resistance" and do I need it in South Africa?
**A:** Frost resistance is a tile's ability to withstand freeze-thaw cycles without cracking. While South Africa doesn't have extreme winters, frost can occur in high-altitude areas (Johannesburg highveld, Drakensberg, parts of the Karoo) and is a consideration for outdoor installations in these regions. All porcelain tiles with water absorption below 0.5% are inherently frost-resistant. If you're specifying outdoor tiles for frost-prone areas, confirm frost resistance with your consultant.

**Q652:** What does " Shade variation" mean on a tile box?
**A:** Shade variation (often printed as a batch or shade number on the tile box) indicates that the tiles in that box come from a single production batch with consistent colour. Tiles from different shade numbers may have visible colour differences. For large installations, always order tiles from the same shade batch. For very large areas, we recommend ordering all tiles at once and blending tiles from multiple boxes during installation to ensure even colour distribution. Your tiler should be briefed on this before starting.

**Q653:** What's the difference between "floor tile" and "wall tile" ratings?
**A:** Floor tiles must pass strength, abrasion, and slip-resistance tests. They can also be used on walls. Wall tiles are not tested for these properties and should never be used on floors — they're thinner, softer, and lack the structural integrity to withstand foot traffic. Always check the tile's rating: if it's rated for floors (indicated by a foot symbol on the packaging or datasheet), it's safe for both floor and wall. If it's wall-only, keep it on the wall.

**Q656:** What is "tile warpage" and how does it affect installation?
**A:** Warpage is a slight curvature or bow in a tile, which is a normal manufacturing characteristic, particularly in large-format tiles. When installed, warpage can cause lippage if not managed correctly. Skilled tilers lay warped tiles with the concave side down and use levelling systems to ensure adjacent tiles sit flat. The warpage tolerance is specified in manufacturing standards — rectified tiles have tighter warpage tolerances than pressed tiles. We only stock tiles within acceptable warpage limits.

**Q657:** What's the difference between "grout" and "silicone sealant"?
**A:** Grout is a cement-based or epoxy-based material used to fill the joints between tiles. It's rigid once cured and is suitable for the main tiled surface. Silicone sealant is a flexible material used in expansion joints, perimeter gaps, and junctions between different materials (e.g., tile to bath, tile to window frame). Silicone accommodates movement without cracking, while grout does not. Both are necessary in a proper installation — grout for tile joints, silicone for movement joints.

**Q658:** What does "full coverage" mean in tile installation?
**A:** Full coverage means the adhesive completely covers the back of the tile with no voids or hollow spots. It's essential for structural integrity, sound reduction, and waterproofing in wet areas. Industry standards require minimum 80% coverage for dry areas and 100% for wet areas and exterior applications. Back-buttering large tiles and using the correct trowel size are the best ways to ensure full coverage. You can check coverage by occasionally removing a tile during installation.

**Q659:** What is "anti-fracture membrane" and when should I specify it?
**A:** An anti-fracture membrane (also called an uncoupling membrane) is a specialised underlayment that separates the tile installation from the substrate, preventing cracks in the substrate from transferring through to the tile surface. Specify it when: tiling over substrates with existing fine cracks; installing over timber or plywood; working with underfloor heating; or in any situation where substrate movement is anticipated. Schluter-DITRA is the most recognised brand, and we can supply it.

**Q660:** What's the "bedding" method for tile installation?
**A:** Bedding refers to the method of applying adhesive beneath the tile. Thin-bed bedding (3-6mm) is the standard for most tile installations using a notched trowel. Thick-bed bedding (10-20mm+ or even 40mm) is used for large-format tiles, uneven substrates, or exterior installations where greater adhesive depth is needed to achieve a flat, level surface. The bedding method should be specified based on tile size, substrate condition, and application.

**Q661:** What does "tile break strength" mean?
**A:** Break strength measures the force required to break a tile, expressed in newtons (N). Higher break strength indicates a stronger, more durable tile. Porcelain tiles typically have break strengths of 1,300N or higher, while ceramic tiles may be 600-900N. For commercial floors, heavy-traffic residential areas, and any installation where durability is critical, specify tiles with higher break strength ratings. We include break strength data in our technical specifications.

**Q662:** What's the difference between "Class 1" and "Class 5" tile adhesive?
**A:** Adhesive classification is manufacturer-specific, but generally Class 1 refers to standard cement-based adhesive suitable for interior walls and light-traffic floors on stable substrates. Higher classifications (Class 2, Class 3, S1, S2) indicate progressively enhanced properties — improved flexibility, bond strength, and resistance to moisture, heat, and chemicals. For professional specifications, we typically recommend S1-rated flexible adhesive as the minimum standard.

**Q663:** What is "tile layout planning" and why should I do it before installation?
**A:** Tile layout planning is the process of determining exactly how tiles will be arranged before any adhesive is spread. It involves measuring the room, accounting for fixed elements (doors, windows, fixtures), and deciding where cuts will fall. Proper planning ensures: balanced cuts (not tiny slivers at edges); centre-line symmetry where appropriate; efficient tile usage with minimal waste; and a harmonious visual result. We provide layout planning assistance for complex installations.

**Q666:** What's the difference between "single-fired" and "double-fired" tiles?
**A:** Single-fired (monocottura) tiles are shaped, glazed, and fired once at high temperature — this is the modern standard for most porcelain tiles, producing a strong, dense body with well-fused glaze. Double-fired (bicottura) tiles are fired once as a biscuit, then glazed and fired again at a lower temperature — traditionally used for decorative wall tiles and some ceramic tiles. For floor applications, single-fired porcelain is the superior and more common choice.

Suspended ceiling installation refers to hanging ceiling tiles from a metal framework, which is more common in commercial buildings with services running above the ceiling. For wall and floor tiles in residential and commercial interiors, direct sticking is the standard method. **Q672:** What's the "working time" of tile adhesive?
**A:** Working time (also called pot life) is the period after mixing during which adhesive remains workable and can be spread. Standard cement adhesive has a working time of approximately 2-3 hours. Rapid-set adhesive may have 30-60 minutes. Epoxy adhesive typically has 45-60 minutes. Hot, dry conditions reduce working time, so tilers mix smaller batches in summer. **Q674:** What's the difference between "ceramic glaze" and "porcelain glaze"?
**A:** Ceramic glazes are applied to the surface of ceramic tiles and fired at lower temperatures (typically 1,000-1,100°C). Porcelain glazes are applied to porcelain biscuit and fired at higher temperatures (1,200-1,400°C), resulting in a harder, more durable, more vitrified glaze that's more resistant to abrasion, chemicals, and staining. **Q676:** What's a "tile schedule" and should I create one for my projects?

**Q677:** What does "field tile" versus "feature tile" mean?

Feature tiles are the accent pieces that create visual interest — patterned tiles, textured tiles, mosaics, or bold-coloured tiles used in specific areas like splashbacks, shower niches, or entrance floors. The relationship between field and feature tiles is one of the fundamental principles of tile design. We curate our collections to ensure compatible field and feature pairings.

**Q678:** What's the difference between "bullnose" and "pencil" tiles?
**A:** A bullnose tile has a rounded, finished edge designed to cap the exposed edge of a tiled surface — ideal for wall edges, window reveals, and step fronts. A pencil tile (or liner tile) is a narrow decorative strip, typically 20-30mm wide, used as an accent border within a tiled surface. Both serve to finish and detail a tile installation. While traditional, they're less commonly used in contemporary design where metal profiles and rectified edges have become preferred.

**Q679:** What does "modular layout" mean in tile design?
**A:** A modular layout uses multiple tile sizes from the same collection arranged in a predetermined pattern — for example, a French pattern using four sizes (large, medium, small, and accent) in a repeating layout that mimics the random arrangement of traditional stone floors. Modular layouts create visual complexity and old-world character while maintaining the practical benefits of modern porcelain. Many of our stone-effect collections offer modular layout options.

**A:** Rooftop areas require comprehensive waterproofing before any surface finish is applied. The system typically includes: a structural concrete slab; slope-to-drain screed (minimum 1:80 fall); primer; liquid waterproofing membrane (two coats, 1.5-2mm total thickness); reinforcement at joints and penetrations; protection board; and then the tile or paver installation. For rooftop pavers, a pedestal system allows drainage and access to the waterproofing layer. SANS 10400-L governs these requirements, and building control will inspect. Always use a certified waterproofing applicator.

**Q794:** How do I specify tiles that will perform well in a gym or fitness centre?
**A:** Gym floors need high PEI ratings (5), excellent impact resistance, anti-slip properties (R10-R11), and resistance to rubber marking from gym equipment. Rubber marks from weights and machines can be difficult to remove from porous or textured surfaces — specify a smooth, dense glaze that's easy to clean. Locker room and shower areas need R11 slip rating and full waterproofing. Large-format tiles in the workout area reduce grout joints where sweat and dirt accumulate.

**Q795:** What's the best approach for specifying tiles in a multi-unit residential development?
**A:** Multi-unit developments balance design quality, durability, cost efficiency, and maintenance simplicity. We recommend: selecting 2-3 tile types maximum across the entire development (bulk pricing benefits, simplified procurement); choosing proven, hard-wearing porcelain with high PEI ratings; specifying medium-format tiles (easier installation, less wastage); using the same tile throughout each unit type for consistency; and selecting grout colours that hide dirt between maintenance cycles. Our commercial team can structure project pricing for volume developments.

**Q796:** How do I handle the acoustic and thermal properties of tiles in a recording studio or music room?
**A:** Tiles are unsuitable for recording studio interiors due to their high reflectivity and sound transmission. However, they work well in ancillary spaces — reception areas, bathrooms, kitchenettes. If tiles must be used in acoustically sensitive areas, specify the most textured, irregular surface available to scatter sound waves, and ensure the substrate includes acoustic insulation. For the recording room itself, recommend acoustic wall panels and timber floors instead. We can advise on appropriate products for the non-recording spaces.

**Q799:** What should I know about specifying tiles for an industrial or manufacturing facility?

**Q968:** What's the most enduring design principle that applies to tile specification?
**A:** Proportion. The relationship between tile size, room size, joint width, pattern scale, and the human body has been understood since the ancient Greeks. Tiles that are too small for a room feel busy and dated; tiles that are too large for a space feel heavy and imposing. The grout joint should be proportional to the tile size.
