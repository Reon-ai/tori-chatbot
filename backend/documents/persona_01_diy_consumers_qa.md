**A:** Many of our ranges are modular, meaning the same design is available in multiple sizes (e.g., 600x600mm, 600x1200mm, and 300x600mm from the same range). This allows you to use the same tile on floors and walls in different sizes for a cohesive look.

**Q81:** Does Tiletoria sell subways tiles?
**A:** Yes, subway/metro tiles are a core part of our range, available in classic 75x300mm and 100x300mm formats, as well as larger 100x400mm and bevelled-edge versions. Colours range from classic white to pastels, bold colours, and glossy or matte finishes.

**Q82:** What is a rectified edge tile, and do you sell them?
**A:** Rectified edge tiles are mechanically trimmed after firing to achieve precise, uniform dimensions with perfectly straight edges. This allows for very narrow grout joints (1-2mm). We sell many rectified tile ranges, particularly in our larger format and imported collections.

**Q83:** Do you sell tiles with a matte finish?
**A:** Yes, matte finish tiles are widely available across our ranges. They're increasingly popular as they don't show water spots or scratches as easily as polished tiles, and they offer a more contemporary, understated aesthetic.

**Q84:** What's the difference between polished, lappato, and matte finishes?
**A:** Polished tiles have a high-gloss, mirror-like finish achieved by mechanical polishing. Lappato (or semi-polished) has a satin sheen with some areas polished and others matte, creating visual depth. Matte tiles have a flat, non-reflective surface. We stock all three finishes across our ranges.

**Q85:** Does Tiletoria have patterned or decorative tiles?
**A:** Yes, we stock encaustic-look patterned tiles, Moroccan-inspired designs, geometric patterns, and floral motifs. These are popular for feature areas, splashbacks, and entrance halls. Patterned 200x200mm and 300x300mm floor tiles are particularly popular for heritage-style homes.

**Q86:** Can I get large format tiles (600x1200mm or bigger) as a DIY consumer?
**A:** Yes, large format tiles are available to all customers. However, tiles 600x1200mm and larger require more skill to install due to weight and the need for perfectly flat substrates. We recommend professional installation for tiles 750x1500mm and above, though experienced DIYers can handle 600x1200mm with proper preparation.

**Q87:** Do you stock white body wall tiles?
**A:** Yes, we stock traditional white-body ceramic wall tiles, particularly for kitchens and bathrooms. These are lighter and easier to cut than porcelain tiles but are suitable for wall applications only.

**Q88:** What's the difference between floor tiles and wall tiles?
**A:** Floor tiles are manufactured to be thicker, harder, and more resistant to wear and impact. They have higher PEI ratings and anti-slip properties. Wall tiles are typically lighter, thinner, and may have more delicate glazes. Floor tiles can be used on walls, but wall tiles should not be used on floors (except very light-duty areas).

**Q89:** Does Tiletoria have any exclusive tile designs I can't get elsewhere?
**A:** Yes, through our relationships with international manufacturers, we carry exclusive designs and ranges that aren't available at other South African retailers. our exclusive mosaic range range and certain imported collections are exclusive to Tiletoria.

**Q90:** Do you sell black tiles?
**A:** Yes, we stock black tiles in various formats — matte black subway tiles for bathrooms, polished black porcelain floor tiles, black-look tiles with white veining, and black mosaic options.

**Q91:** Can I get tiles that look like screed or raw concrete?
**A:** Yes, industrial-style tiles that replicate raw screed, concrete, and plaster finishes are available. These typically have a matte or rough texture and come in grey tones from light to dark charcoal.

**Q92:** What are the thinnest tiles you sell?

**Q103:** What's the difference between glazed porcelain and full-body porcelain?
**A:** Glazed porcelain has a decorative glaze layer on top of a porcelain body. If chipped, the lighter body colour may show. Full-body (or through-body) porcelain has consistent colour throughout the entire tile thickness, so chips and scratches are less visible. Full-body is ideal for high-traffic areas.

## Category 4: Tile Types Explained

**Q166:** What's the difference between porcelain and ceramic tiles?
**A:** Porcelain tiles are made from finer, denser clay and fired at higher temperatures (1200°C+) than ceramic tiles. This makes them harder, less porous (water absorption below 0.5%), more durable, and suitable for both floors and walls. Ceramic tiles are softer, more porous (3-10% water absorption), lighter, and typically used for walls and light-duty floors. For DIY projects, porcelain is the safer all-round choice.

**Q167:** How do I know if a tile is porcelain or ceramic?
**A:** All Tiletoria tiles are clearly labelled as either porcelain or ceramic. Porcelain tiles will typically say "porcelain" or "gres porcellanato" and are denser and heavier than ceramic tiles of the same size. If in doubt, ask a showroom consultant — we always clearly indicate the tile type.

**Q168:** Should I use porcelain or ceramic for my bathroom floor?
**A:** Porcelain is recommended for bathroom floors because it's denser, less porous (critical in wet areas), and more slip-resistant. Ceramic tiles can be used on bathroom walls but should generally be avoided on bathroom floors unless specifically rated for floor use.

**Q169:** Can I use ceramic tiles on my kitchen floor?
**A:** Light-duty ceramic floor tiles can be used in small kitchens with low foot traffic, but porcelain is strongly recommended for kitchen floors due to the risk of dropped utensils, water spills, and higher foot traffic. If you choose ceramic, ensure it has a PEI rating of at least 3.

**Q170:** What does PEI rating mean?
**A:** PEI (Porcelain Enamel Institute) rating measures a tile's resistance to abrasion and wear. PEI 1: wall use only. PEI 2: wall and very light traffic floors (bedrooms). PEI 3: residential floors (kitchens, bathrooms, living areas). PEI 4: heavy residential and light commercial. PEI 5: heavy commercial. For most DIY home projects, PEI 3 or 4 is ideal.

**Q171:** What does "R rating" mean for slip resistance?
**A:** The R rating (R9-R13) measures slip resistance on inclined surfaces. R9: dry indoor areas only (bedrooms, living rooms). R10: general indoor use including kitchens. R11: bathrooms, outdoor areas with shelter. R12: outdoor areas, wet rooms, commercial kitchens. R13: industrial wet areas. For residential bathrooms, we recommend R10 or R11. For outdoor patios, R11 or R12.

**Q172:** What's the difference between rectified and pressed (non-rectified) edge tiles?
**A:** Rectified tiles are mechanically cut or ground after firing to achieve precise, uniform dimensions with perfectly straight edges. This allows for very narrow grout joints (1-2mm). Pressed (non-rectified) tiles have slight size variations from the pressing process and require wider grout joints (3-5mm) to accommodate these variations. Rectified tiles give a more seamless, modern look.

**Q173:** What's the difference between glazed and unglazed tiles?
**A:** Glazed tiles have a liquid glass coating applied before firing, which creates the colour and pattern on the surface. The glaze makes them stain-resistant and easy to clean, but if chipped, the body colour underneath may show. Unglazed (full-body) tiles have no surface coating — the colour runs through the entire tile. They're more durable for high-traffic areas but may require sealing if porous.

**Q174:** What are full-body (through-body) porcelain tiles?
**A:** Full-body porcelain tiles have the same colour and composition throughout the entire thickness, not just a surface layer. If chipped or worn, the damage is far less visible. These are the most durable tiles available and are ideal for high-traffic floors, commercial spaces, and areas where long-term durability is critical.

**Q175:** What is a "double-loaded" or "double-charged" porcelain tile?
**A:** Double-charged porcelain tiles are made by pressing two layers of clay together — a base body layer and a thinner decorative surface layer containing the colour and pattern. The pattern penetrates deeper than a standard glazed tile, making them more resistant to wear. They're commonly used for commercial and high-traffic residential floors with stone-look patterns.

**Q176:** What does "digital glaze" or "inkjet printed" mean?
**A:** Modern tiles often use inkjet printing technology to apply high-definition designs onto the tile surface. This allows for incredibly realistic reproductions of natural materials like, wood, and stone, with high variation between tiles. Most wood-look and stone-look tiles use this technology.

**Q177:** What's the difference between polished, honed, and lappato finishes?
**A:** Polished tiles have a high-gloss, mirror-like finish created by mechanical polishing. Honed tiles have a smooth, satin-matte finish with no gloss. Lappato (semi-polished) tiles have a partial polish that creates a glossy-matte mix with visual depth and texture. Polished shows scratches more easily; honed and lappato are more forgiving.

**Q178:** What are "structured" or "textured" tiles?
**A:** Structured tiles have a physical, tactile texture on the surface — this could replicate wood grain, stone texture, fabric weave, or 3D geometric patterns. The texture improves slip resistance and adds visual and tactile interest. They're popular for feature walls and contemporary floor designs.

**Q179:** What is a "nanocoated" or "nano polished" tile?
**A:** Nano-polished tiles have a microscopic layer of liquid glass (nanoparticles) applied to the surface during manufacturing. This creates an extremely smooth, glossy finish that's highly stain-resistant and easy to clean. The nano coating fills microscopic pores that could otherwise trap dirt.

**Q180:** What does "vitrified" mean?
**A:** Vitrified means the tile has been fired at such a high temperature that it becomes glass-like, with extremely low porosity (below 0.5%). All porcelain tiles are vitrified. "Fully vitrified" tiles are impervious to water and extremely hard-wearing.

**Q181:** What's the difference between homogeneous and heterogeneous tiles?
**A:** Homogeneous tiles (also called full-body or through-body) have uniform composition and colour throughout. Heterogeneous tiles have different layers — typically a porcelain body with a glazed or printed decorative surface. Homogeneous tiles are more durable but offer fewer design options.

**Q182:** What are "stone plastic composite" (SPC) tiles?
**A:** SPC is a type of rigid core LVT (Luxury Vinyl Tile) flooring made from a composite of porcelain tile and plastic. It's extremely stable, waterproof, and durable. SPC flooring is sold under our vinyl/LVT category and is popular for DIY installations due to its click-lock system.

**Q183:** What does "AC rating" mean for laminate flooring?
**A:** AC (Abrasion Criteria) rating measures laminate flooring's resistance to wear. AC1: moderate residential (bedrooms). AC2: general residential (living rooms, dining rooms). AC3: heavy residential/light commercial. AC4: commercial. AC5: heavy commercial. For home DIY projects, we recommend AC3 (8mm) or AC4 (12mm). our laminate flooring and our laminate flooring ranges include AC3-AC5 options.

**Q184:** What does "waterproof laminate" mean?
**A:** Traditional laminate flooring is moisture-resistant but not waterproof — prolonged water exposure can cause swelling at the edges. Waterproof laminate has a specially treated core and joint system that resists water penetration for extended periods. This makes it suitable for kitchens, bathrooms, and laundry rooms where traditional laminate might fail.

**Q185:** What's the difference between vinyl flooring and LVT?
**A:** Traditional vinyl flooring typically comes in sheets or flexible tiles. LVT (Luxury Vinyl Tile) is a premium evolution of vinyl, manufactured in rigid plank or tile formats with realistic photographic layers and protective wear layers. LVT is thicker, more durable, and more realistic than standard vinyl. Our LVT ranges are particularly popular for DIY installations.

**Q186:** What is WPC flooring?
**A:** WPC (Wood Plastic Composite) is a type of LVT with a core made from wood fibre and plastic polymers. It's softer and warmer underfoot than SPC, with better sound insulation. WPC is comfortable for residential use but slightly less dent-resistant than SPC.

**Q187:** What does "pressed powder" or "dust-pressed" mean?
**A:** Dust-pressed refers to the manufacturing method where finely ground clay powder is compressed in a mould before firing. This creates a denser, more uniform tile body compared to the older extrusion method. All modern porcelain and most ceramic tiles are dust-pressed.

**Q188:** What are "monoporosa" or "monocottura" tiles?
**A:** Monocottura (single-fired) tiles are shaped and glazed before undergoing a single firing process. This results in a hard, durable tile suitable for floors. Monoporosa is a variation with a more porous body and richer glaze colours, typically used for decorative wall tiles.

**Q189:** What's the difference between a "tile" and a "paver"?

**Q190:** What does "COF" or "coefficient of friction" mean?
**A:** COF measures the frictional resistance between the tile surface and a foot or object. Higher COF = better slip resistance. Dry COF of 0.5+ and wet COF of 0.4+ are generally considered safe for residential floors. Tiles with textured or matte surfaces typically have higher COF values.

**A:** Field tiles are the main, plain tiles used to cover the majority of a surface. Feature tiles are decorative accent tiles (patterned, textured, mosaic, or coloured) used to create visual interest on specific walls or areas. **Q193:** What are "bullnose" tiles?
**A:** Bullnose tiles have one or more rounded edges, used to finish exposed tile edges neatly. They're commonly used on window sills, stair nosing, countertop edges, and wall corners. Where matching bullnose isn't available, trim profiles can be used instead.

## Category 6: Installation Guidance

**Q256:** Can I install tiles myself as a DIYer, or should I hire a professional?

**Q257:** What tools do I need to tile a bathroom floor myself?
**A:** Essential tools include: a tile cutter (manual or electric), tile nipper, notched trowel (size depends on tile), rubber grout float, spirit level, measuring tape, pencil, tile spacers, bucket for mixing adhesive, mixing paddle, sponge, and tile file/sanding stone. A wet saw is recommended for porcelain and large format tiles.

**Q258:** How much does a basic tiling tool set cost?
**A:** A basic DIY tiling tool kit costs approximately R1,500-R3,000. This includes a manual tile cutter (R400-R800), trowels (R80-R150 each), grout float (R100-R200), spirit level (R200-R500), tile spacers (R30-R80), mixing paddle (R150-R300), and buckets/sponges. A wet tile saw costs R2,000-R5,000 to buy or R300-R500/day to hire.

**Q259:** Where can I hire a wet tile saw?
**A:** Wet tile saws can be hired from most major tool hire companies in South Africa (like Coastal Hire, Talisman, or local hire shops). Typical hire rates are R300-R500 per day or R1,000-R1,500 per week. Tell them you're cutting porcelain tiles as this determines the blade type.

**Q260:** How do I prepare my subfloor before tiling?
**A:** The subfloor must be clean, dry, sound, and level. Remove all loose material, paint, and old adhesive. Fill cracks and holes with appropriate filler. Check for level — variations should not exceed 3mm over a 2m straight edge. Concrete floors should be at least 28 days old. Prime porous surfaces with a suitable primer before tiling.

**Q261:** What is screeding, and do I need it?
**A:** Screeding is applying a layer of cement-based levelling compound (screed) over the structural floor to create a flat, level surface for tiling. You need screeding if your floor is uneven, has significant height variations, or if you're installing underfloor heating pipes. A professional screed typically costs R80-R150/m².

**Q262:** How long does screed need to dry before tiling?
**A:** Traditional sand and cement screed needs at least 28 days to dry before tiling. Rapid-drying screeds (like calcium sulphate or cement-based rapid screeds) can be tiled over in 24 hours to 7 days depending on the product. Always follow the manufacturer's drying time guidelines.

**Q263:** Can I tile over existing tiles?
**A:** Yes, provided the existing tiles are firmly bonded, not loose or hollow-sounding, and the surface is clean. Roughen the surface with coarse sandpaper, apply a suitable primer, then use a flexible tile adhesive. Avoid tiling over more than one layer of existing tiles. Do not tile over tiles in wet areas — strip to substrate and waterproof properly.

**Q264:** Can I tile directly onto plywood or wooden floors?
**A:** Tiling directly onto plywood is not recommended without proper preparation. You need to install a tile backer board or cementitious tile underlay over the plywood first, using a flexible adhesive. Wood expands and contracts with moisture, so an uncoupling membrane is also recommended to prevent tile cracking.

**Q265:** What type of adhesive should I use for porcelain floor tiles?
**A:** Use a flexible, cement-based tile adhesive (C2 class or higher) for porcelain floor tiles. Porcelain is dense and non-porous, so standard adhesive may not bond as well. For large format tiles (600x600mm+), use a high-bed (thick-bed) adhesive or adhesive specifically formulated for large tiles.

**Q266:** What size notched trowel should I use?
**A:** For tiles up to 300x300mm, use a 6-8mm notched trowel. For 400x400mm to 600x600mm tiles, use a 10mm notched trowel. For large format tiles (600x1200mm+), use a 12mm notched trowel. The goal is full adhesive coverage — check by lifting a tile after setting to ensure at least 80% coverage.

**Q267:** How do I mix tile adhesive?
**A:** Add the adhesive powder to clean, cool water (not the other way around) in a clean bucket. Use a mixing paddle on a slow-speed drill to mix to a smooth, toothpaste-like consistency. Let it slake (rest) for 5-10 minutes, then remix before use. Never add extra water after mixing — this weakens the adhesive.

**Q268:** How long does tile adhesive take to set?
**A:** Standard adhesive sets in 24 hours. "Open time" (the time you have to place the tile after spreading adhesive) is typically 20-30 minutes. Rapid-set adhesive can be grouted in 3-6 hours. Always check the manufacturer's instructions on the bag. Don't walk on tiles for at least 24 hours after installation.

**Q269:** What does "back-buttering" mean?
**A:** Back-buttering means spreading a thin layer of adhesive on the back of the tile in addition to the adhesive on the floor/wall. This is essential for large format tiles (600x600mm+), porcelain tiles (which are dense and less absorbent), and any tile that needs extra bonding. It ensures full adhesive coverage and eliminates voids.

**Q270:** How do I lay large format tiles (600x1200mm) without lippage?
**A:** Use a tile levelling system (clips and wedges) to ensure perfectly flat tiles with no lippage (height differences between adjacent tiles). These systems hold tiles at the same level while adhesive sets. Back-butter each tile, use a 12mm notched trowel, and check flatness with a straight edge as you work.

**Q271:** What is a tile levelling system, and do I need one?
**A:** A tile levelling system uses plastic clips and wedges (or caps) to hold adjacent tiles at exactly the same height while adhesive cures. They're essential for large format tiles and highly recommended for rectified tiles with narrow joints. They produce professional-looking, lippage-free results. They're reusable and affordable (R200-R400 for enough clips for 20-30m²).

**Q272:** How long should I wait before grouting?
**A:** Wait at least 24 hours after laying tiles before grouting. For rapid-set adhesive, 6 hours may be sufficient. The adhesive must be fully set before you apply grout. In cold or humid conditions, wait longer — up to 48 hours.

**Q273:** How do I mix grout?
**A:** Mix grout powder with clean water to a smooth, lump-free consistency (like thick peanut butter). Let it slake for 5-10 minutes, then remix. Work in small sections, applying grout with a rubber float at a 45° angle to the joints. Remove excess grout immediately with the float.

**Q274:** How do I choose a grout colour?
**A:** For a seamless look where grout lines blend in, choose a grout colour close to the tile colour. For a contrasting grid effect, choose a significantly different colour (e.g., dark grey grout with white subway tiles). Consider that darker grout hides dirt better in floor applications. We offer a range of grout colours to match popular tile shades.

**Q275:** What's the difference between sanded and unsanded grout?
**A:** Sanded grout contains fine sand particles and is used for wider joints (3mm+). It's stronger and less likely to crack. Unsanded (fine) grout is used for narrow joints (under 3mm) and with soft stones that could be scratched by sand particles. For most DIY floor projects with 3-5mm joints, sanded grout is appropriate.

**Q276:** What's epoxy-based grout, and should I use it?
**A:** Epoxy grout is a two-part resin-based grout that's extremely durable, stain-proof, and waterproof. It's ideal for kitchens, bathrooms, and areas exposed to harsh cleaning. However, it's more expensive (R300-R500 per kit vs R60-R100 for cementitious grout), harder to apply, and sets quickly, making it challenging for beginners. Many DIYers use epoxy only in critical areas like shower floors.

**Q277:** How long does grout take to dry?
**A:** Cementitious grout is dry to the touch in 24 hours and fully cured in 72 hours. Avoid getting grout wet for the first 48 hours. Epoxy grout cures faster — typically 24 hours for full cure. During the curing period, keep the area well-ventilated.

**Q278:** Do I need to seal my grout?
**A:** Cementitious grout is porous and should be sealed in wet areas (showers, bathrooms) and kitchens to prevent staining and mould growth. Use a penetrating grout sealer applied 72 hours after grouting. Epoxy grout doesn't require sealing. Some premium pre-mixed grouts also contain sealant.

**Q279:** How do I cut tiles?
**A:** For straight cuts on ceramic tiles, a manual score-and-snap cutter works well. For porcelain tiles and all cut-outs (around pipes, power points), a wet saw (electric tile cutter with water-cooled blade) is essential. Small curved cuts can be made with tile nippers. For complex cuts, consider hiring a professional.

**Q280:** How do I tile around a toilet pipe?
**A:** Measure the pipe diameter and position on the tile. Drill a hole slightly larger than the pipe diameter using a diamond hole saw or tile drill bit. If the pipe is at the edge of a tile, cut the tile in half and notch out the semi-circle for the pipe. A tile nipper can help refine the cut.

**Q281:** How do I tile around electrical outlets and switches?
**A:** Turn off the power at the DB board. Remove the outlet cover plate. Measure and mark the cut-out position on the tile. Cut the tile using a wet saw or angle grinder with a diamond blade, making relief cuts and removing the section. Extend the electrical box with a spacer if needed so the cover plate sits flush over the tile.

**Q283:** How do I ensure my tiles are level?
**A:** Use a spirit level frequently as you work, checking both individual tiles and rows. Work from a known level reference point. For floors, use a long straight edge (1.5m or 2m level) to check across multiple tiles. A tile levelling system is the best insurance against lippage for beginners.

**Q284:** Should I start tiling from the centre or from a corner?
**A:** For floors, start from the centre of the room and work outward toward the walls. This gives balanced cuts on all sides. Find the centre by snapping chalk lines between opposite wall midpoints. For walls, start from a visible horizontal reference line (like the top of a bath or a level line at eye height) and work up and down from there.

**Q285:** How do I lay out tiles before starting the installation?
**A:** Dry-lay your tiles first — place them without adhesive to check the layout, pattern, and where cuts will fall. Adjust the starting position to avoid narrow sliver cuts at edges. Aim for cuts at the perimeter to be at least half a tile width. For walls, mark a level starting line using a spirit level and chalk line.

**Q286:** Can I tile over painted walls?
**A:** No, paint must be removed or the wall must be properly prepared. Gloss paint will prevent adhesive from bonding. Remove loose paint, sand glossy surfaces thoroughly, and apply a bonding primer or tile backer board. In bathrooms, it's best to remove all paint and tile onto proper substrate or tile backer board.

**Q287:** What is tile primer, and do I need it?
**A:** Tile primer is a liquid preparation applied to porous substrates (plaster, concrete, screed) before tiling. It prevents the substrate from drawing moisture out of the adhesive too quickly (which weakens the bond), reduces dust, and improves adhesion. Priming is essential and should never be skipped.

**Q288:** How do I remove old tiles before retiling?
**A:** Remove old tiles using a hammer and chisel, floor scraper, or power chisel. Wear safety goggles, heavy gloves, and a dust mask. Remove all old adhesive back to a sound substrate. Patch any damage to the substrate before priming and tiling. Be careful not to damage plumbing or electrical services.

**Q289:** What is an uncoupling membrane, and do I need one?
**A:** An uncoupling membrane (like Ditra) is a polyethylene sheet with a grid structure that separates the tile layer from the substrate. It allows for minor movement between the substrate and tiles, preventing cracks. It's recommended over plywood, concrete with minor cracking, and areas subject to thermal movement. It's highly recommended for DIY projects over questionable substrates.

**Q290:** Can I tile in a bathroom without removing the toilet and basin?
**A:** It's possible but not recommended. Tiling around fixtures creates more cuts, potential weak points, and a less professional finish. Removing the toilet and basin (and capping plumbing temporarily) allows for a cleaner installation. If you must tile around them, use a tile nipper for small, careful cuts.

**Q291:** How do I create movement/expansion joints in my tile installation?
**A:** Expansion joints are essential in large floor areas — typically every 8-10m in each direction, at doorways, and where tiles meet different materials. Use a flexible silicone sealant in these joints rather than hard grout. Your trowel should leave the joint area clear of adhesive to allow for movement.

**Q292:** What's the most common mistake DIY tilers make?
**A:** The top mistakes are: inadequate subfloor preparation (uneven surfaces), mixing adhesive too wet (weakens bond), not back-buttering large tiles, working too slowly so adhesive skins over before tiles are placed, not using spacers consistently, and walking on tiles too soon. Take your time and follow manufacturer guidelines.

**Q293:** How long before I can walk on newly laid floor tiles?
**A:** Wait at least 24 hours before walking on newly laid tiles. Avoid heavy furniture and foot traffic for 48 hours. Wait 72 hours before wet cleaning or exposing to significant water. The adhesive continues to gain strength over 28 days.

**Q294:** How do I clean tiles after grouting?
**A:** About 20-30 minutes after applying grout (while it's still slightly damp), wipe the tile surface with a damp sponge in diagonal strokes across the joints. Rinse the sponge frequently. Don't use too much water — this can wash grout out of the joints. Once fully dry (24 hours), buff off any haze with a dry cloth.

**Q299:** What safety equipment do I need when tiling?

Work in a well-ventilated area.

**Q300:** How do I mix adhesive and grout quantities for a small repair job?
**A:** Mix small batches — you can always mix more. For adhesive, start with 2-3 litres of water and add powder gradually while mixing. For grout, mix about 1kg at a time. Mix only what you can use within the working time (typically 1-2 hours). Discard any mixed material that has started to set.

**Q301:** What's the difference between wall tile adhesive and floor tile adhesive?
**A:** Wall tile adhesive is typically a standard-set adhesive with good grab (tackiness) to hold tiles on vertical surfaces. Floor tile adhesive is usually a flexible, higher-strength adhesive that can withstand foot traffic and subfloor movement. Many modern adhesives are suitable for both walls and floors — check the packaging.

**Q302:** Can I use tile adhesive instead of plaster to patch walls?
**A:** Tile adhesive can be used for small patching and filling (up to 5mm), but it's not a substitute for proper wall preparation. For larger repairs, use appropriate plaster or patching compound, let it dry completely, prime if porous, then tile over it.

**Q303:** How do I prevent tiles from sliding down the wall while adhesive sets?
**A:** Use an adhesive with good initial grab/tack designed for wall tiles. Apply adhesive with the correct notched trowel, press tiles firmly into place, and use the appropriate size tile spacers. For large wall tiles, temporary support brackets or a tile levelling system can help. Don't tile walls in very hot or windy conditions.

**Q304:** Should I tile the floor or walls first in a bathroom?
**A:** Professional tilers typically tile walls first (starting from the top down or using a ledger board), then the floor. This prevents floor tiles from being damaged during wall tiling and allows the floor to be tiled after the wall tiles are set, ensuring clean junctions. Some tilers prefer floor-first — both methods work.

**Q305:** How do I handle the joint between floor and wall tiles?
**A:** The junction between floor and wall tiles should be filled with flexible silicone sealant, not grout. This allows for movement between the floor and wall and prevents cracking. Use a silicone colour that matches your grout for a seamless appearance. Apply masking tape on both sides for a neat bead.

**Q306:** What's the correct way to remove and replace a cracked tile?
**A:** Drill holes in the cracked tile with a masonry bit, then chip out the tile carefully with a hammer and chisel, working from the centre outward. Scrape out old adhesive, clean the area, apply new adhesive, set the new tile, let it set 24 hours, then grout and seal if needed. Be careful not to damage adjacent tiles.

**Q307:** Can I tile a bathroom in winter?
**A:** Yes, but cold and damp conditions slow adhesive and grout curing. Ensure the room is well-ventilated and ideally heated to at least 10°C during installation and for 48 hours after. Don't force-dry with heaters — let adhesive cure naturally. In very cold weather, consider using rapid-set products.

**Q308:** How do I avoid air pockets (voids) under tiles?
**A:** Voids occur when adhesive isn't fully distributed under the tile. Use the correct notched trowel and comb the adhesive in straight, parallel lines (not swirls). Press and slightly twist each tile into place. Back-butter large tiles. Periodically lift a tile to check coverage — you should see adhesive covering at least 80% of the tile back.

**Q309:** What should I do if I run out of adhesive mid-way through a project?
**A:** You can mix more adhesive and continue — the new batch bonds to the previous application. However, don't spread more adhesive than you can cover with tiles within the open time (typically 20-30 minutes). If adhesive has started to skin over, scrape it off and reapply fresh adhesive.

**Q310:** How do I know if my subfloor is concrete or screed?
**A:** Concrete is the structural slab — it's typically grey, hard, and may have a steel trowelled finish. Screed is a thinner layer (25-50mm) applied over the concrete slab to create a smooth, level surface for finishing. If your floor has been levelled with a cement layer on top of the slab, that's screed. Both can be tiled over if sound and properly prepared.

## Category 7: LVT & Alternative Flooring

**Q311:** What is LVT flooring?
**A:** LVT (Luxury Vinyl Tile) is a premium vinyl flooring product manufactured in rigid tile or plank formats. It consists of multiple layers: a protective wear layer, a printed design layer (photographic image of wood, stone, or tile), a rigid core (SPC or WPC), and a backing layer. LVT is waterproof, durable, and increasingly popular for DIY installations.

**Q312:** What's the difference between SPC and WPC flooring?
**A:** SPC (Stone Plastic Composite) has a rigid porcelain tile-polymer core that's extremely stable and dent-resistant. WPC (Wood Plastic Composite) has a softer, more flexible core made from wood fibres and plastic, offering better sound insulation and a warmer feel underfoot. SPC is better for high-traffic and commercial areas; WPC is more comfortable for residential use.

**Q313:** Does Tiletoria sell click-system LVT?
**A:** Yes, we stock click-system LVT flooring that locks together without glue, making it ideal for DIY installation. The planks or tiles simply click together using a tongue-and-groove locking mechanism, floating over the subfloor without adhesive.

**Q314:** How much does LVT flooring cost at Tiletoria?
**A:** Our LVT ranges typically cost R250-R450/m² depending on the thickness, wear layer, and design quality. This is comparable to mid-range porcelain tiles but with the added benefit of easier, faster installation.

**Q315:** Is LVT suitable for bathrooms and kitchens?
**A:** Yes, SPC-based LVT is 100% waterproof and ideal for bathrooms, kitchens, and laundry rooms. The rigid core doesn't swell when exposed to water, and the click-lock joints are designed to resist moisture penetration. Always install a suitable underlay and follow manufacturer's guidelines for wet areas.

**Q316:** Can I install LVT myself, or do I need a professional?
**A:** Click-system LVT is one of the most DIY-friendly flooring options available. If you're comfortable with basic measuring, cutting with a utility knife, and following instructions, you can install LVT yourself. It's significantly easier than tiling — no adhesive mixing, no grouting, and you can walk on it immediately.

**Q317:** What tools do I need to install click LVT?
**A:** You'll need: a tape measure, utility knife (for cutting planks), a straight edge or T-square, a tapping block, a pull bar, spacers (for expansion gaps), a pencil, and safety goggles. A jigsaw or mitre saw is helpful for cutting around door frames and complex shapes. Total tool cost: R500-R2,000.

**Q318:** How do I prepare my subfloor for LVT installation?
**A:** The subfloor must be clean, dry, and level — variations should not exceed 3mm over a 2m span. Sweep and vacuum thoroughly. Remove any protruding nails or staples. LVT can be installed over concrete, existing tiles (if level and sound), plywood, and most other hard surfaces. A self-levelling compound may be needed for uneven floors.

**Q319:** Can I install LVT over existing tiles?
**A:** Yes, provided the existing tiles are firmly bonded, not cracked or loose, and the surface is level. Fill any wide grout joints with a levelling compound first (joints over 3mm can telegraph through). Clean the tile surface thoroughly. An underlay can help smooth minor irregularities.

**Q320:** Do I need underlay for LVT flooring?
**A:** Many LVT products have a pre-attached underlay/foam backing, in which case no additional underlay is needed. If your LVT doesn't have attached underlay, a thin LVT-specific underlay (1-2mm) is recommended for sound insulation, moisture protection, and comfort. Check your product specifications.

**Q321:** What is an expansion gap, and why do I need it for LVT?
**A:** An expansion gap is a 5-10mm space left around the perimeter of the room (at walls, door frames, and fixed objects) that allows the LVT to expand and contract with temperature changes. Without this gap, the floor can buckle or lift. The gap is covered by skirting boards or Scotia trim after installation.

**Q322:** How do I cut LVT planks?
**A:** Score the top surface of the plank with a sharp utility knife and straight edge, then snap the plank along the score line. For lengthwise cuts, score deeply and bend back to snap. For complex shapes (around door frames), use a jigsaw with a fine-tooth blade. Always wear safety goggles.

**Q323:** Can I use LVT with underfloor heating?
**A:** Yes, most LVT is compatible with underfloor heating systems. SPC has better thermal conductivity than WPC. Always check the manufacturer's maximum temperature rating (typically 27°C surface temperature) and follow the heating system's installation guidelines.

**Q324:** How long does LVT installation take compared to tiling?
**A:** LVT installation is significantly faster than tiling. An experienced DIYer can install 15-20m² of click LVT in a day. The equivalent area in tiles would take 2-3 days including adhesive setting and grouting. LVT rooms are usable immediately — no curing time required.

**Q325:** How durable is LVT compared to porcelain tiles?
**A:** Quality LVT with a thick wear layer (0.5mm+) is highly durable for residential use, resisting scratches, stains, and wear. However, porcelain tiles are ultimately more durable — they're harder to scratch, won't dent under heavy furniture, and have an indefinite lifespan. LVT typically lasts 10-25 years depending on quality and traffic.

**Q326:** What does "wear layer" mean for LVT?
**A:** The wear layer is the transparent top coating that protects the printed design layer. It's measured in millimetres (0.3mm to 0.7mm). Thicker wear layers offer better protection against scratches and wear. For residential use, 0.3-0.5mm is adequate. For high-traffic areas, choose 0.5mm+.

**Q327:** Is LVT slippery when wet?
**A:** Most LVT has a textured surface that provides reasonable slip resistance (similar to R9-R10 rating). However, like any hard flooring, it can be slippery when very wet. Rinse wet LVT floors promptly. Some ranges have enhanced anti-slip textures for bathrooms.

**Q328:** Can LVT be used outdoors?
**A:** Standard LVT is not designed for outdoor use — UV exposure can cause fading and temperature extremes can affect the core. For outdoor areas, we recommend 20mm porcelain pavers or outdoor-rated tiles.

**Q329:** Does Tiletoria sell laminate flooring?
**A:** Yes, Tiletoria sells laminate flooring through our exclusive ranges: our laminate flooring and our laminate flooring. These include AC3, AC4, and AC5 rated options in 8.3mm and 12mm thicknesses, complete with matching skirtings and border options.

**Q330:** What's the difference between laminate and LVT?
**A:** Laminate has a fibreboard (HDF) core with a printed decorative layer and melamine wear layer. LVT has a vinyl/plastic core. Laminate is generally harder and more scratch-resistant but vulnerable to moisture (swells when wet). LVT is waterproof and more stable in damp conditions. LVT is better for bathrooms and kitchens; laminate works well in dry living areas and bedrooms.

**Q331:** How much does laminate flooring cost at Tiletoria?
**A:** our laminate flooring and our laminate flooring laminate ranges cost approximately R180-R350/m² depending on the thickness, AC rating, and design. Entry-level 8mm AC3 boards are at the lower end, while 12mm AC5 premium designs are at the higher end.

**Q332:** What is AC rating, and which one do I need?
**A:** AC (Abrasion Criteria) rating measures laminate's resistance to wear: AC1-2 for light residential, AC3 for heavy residential (living rooms, hallways), AC4 for commercial/light commercial, AC5 for heavy commercial. For most homes, AC3 is sufficient. Choose AC4 for very busy homes with pets and children.

**Q333:** Can I install laminate flooring in a bathroom?
**A:** Traditional laminate is not recommended for bathrooms as moisture can cause the HDF core to swell and warp. However, we do stock water-resistant and waterproof laminate options that are suitable for bathrooms and kitchens. Check the product specifications or ask our staff for bathroom-suitable laminate.

**Q334:** How do I install laminate flooring?
**A:** Laminate is installed as a floating floor — planks click together over an underlay without adhesive. The process is similar to click LVT: prepare the subfloor, lay underlay, install planks clicking together, maintain an expansion gap around the perimeter, and finish with skirting or Scotia trim. Most DIYers can install laminate successfully.

**Q335:** Does laminate flooring need underlay?
**A:** Yes, laminate always requires underlay (unlike some LVT with built-in underlay). Underlay provides moisture protection, sound insulation, and cushioning. Underlay costs approximately R30-R60/m² and is essential for warranty validity.

**Q336:** Can I install laminate over existing tiles?
**A:** Yes, if the tiles are level, firmly bonded, and not cracked. Fill wide grout joints with levelling compound first. Lay a moisture barrier underlay before installing the laminate. Ensure the total height (tile + underlay + laminate) doesn't cause issues with door clearances.

**Q337:** How long does laminate flooring last?
**A:** Quality laminate flooring (AC3-AC4) typically lasts 15-25 years in residential use. Higher AC ratings, proper installation, and regular maintenance (sweeping, damp mopping, protective pads under furniture) extend lifespan. Laminate can't be refinished like real wood — worn sections need replacement.

**Q338:** Does Tiletoria sell engineered wood flooring?
**A:** Yes, we offer engineered wood flooring options including bamboo flooring. Engineered wood has a real wood veneer over a plywood or composite core, making it more dimensionally stable than solid wood and suitable for areas where solid wood might warp.

**Q339:** What's the difference between engineered wood and laminate?
**A:** Engineered wood has a genuine wood veneer surface (can be sanded and refinished 1-3 times), while laminate has a printed photograph of wood under a melamine layer. Engineered wood is more expensive (R400-R800/m²) but offers the authentic look and feel of real wood. Laminate is more affordable and more scratch-resistant.

**Q340:** Does Tiletoria offer bamboo flooring?
**A:** Yes, bamboo flooring is available through our engineered wood/natural flooring range. Bamboo is a sustainable, rapidly renewable resource that's harder than many hardwoods. It's available in strand-woven (extremely hard) and traditional horizontal/vertical grain styles.

**Q341:** Can I install engineered wood flooring myself?
**A:** Engineered wood can be installed by experienced DIYers using floating (click-lock), glue-down, or nail-down methods. Floating installation is most DIY-friendly. However, engineered wood is more sensitive to subfloor preparation and moisture than LVT or laminate, so proper acclimation and installation are critical.

**Q342:** How do I clean and maintain LVT flooring?
**A:** Sweep or vacuum regularly to remove grit that can scratch the surface. Clean with a damp (not wet) mop using a pH-neutral LVT cleaner. Avoid steam mops, abrasive cleaners, and wax-based polishes. Wipe up spills promptly. Use felt pads under furniture legs.

**Q343:** Can LVT or laminate be damaged by pet claws?
**A:** Large or untrimmed pet claws can scratch any flooring, including LVT and laminate. Choose products with thicker wear layers (0.5mm+) for homes with pets. Keep pet claws trimmed, clean paws after outdoor play, and place mats at entrances. LVT is generally more pet-resistant than laminate as it won't swell from pet accidents.

**Q344:** What happens if water gets under my click LVT flooring?
**A:** If water penetrates the joints and gets under the flooring, lift the affected planks immediately and dry the subfloor thoroughly. SPC core planks won't be damaged by the water itself but mould can grow if moisture is trapped. Allow the subfloor to dry completely before reinstalling. Consider replacing the underlay if saturated.

**Q345:** Can I install LVT on stairs?
**A:** Yes, LVT can be installed on stairs using the plank format. Stairs require full-spread adhesive installation (not floating click method) for safety. Use stair-nose profiles for the leading edge of each step. This is an advanced DIY project — consider professional installation for stairs.

**Q346:** Does L flooring feel cold underfoot?
**A:** LVT is warmer underfoot than tiles or concrete because it's an insulating material. WPC is slightly warmer than SPC. LVT is compatible with underfloor heating, which makes it even more comfortable. It's a good choice for bedrooms and living areas where warmth underfoot is desired.

**Q347:** How do I repair a damaged LVT plank?
**A:** For click-system LVT, individual planks can be replaced by unclicking planks from the nearest wall edge back to the damaged plank, replacing it, and re-clicking all planks back into place. This is a major advantage over sheet vinyl or glued flooring. Keep spare planks from your original installation for repairs.

**Q348:** Can I use rugs and mats on LVT flooring?
**A:** Yes, but avoid rubber-backed rugs and mats as the rubber can react with the vinyl and cause discolouration or staining. Use rugs with a felt or synthetic non-slip backing designed for use on vinyl floors.

**Q349:** What thickness of LVT should I choose?
**A:** LVT thickness ranges from 4mm to 8mm+. Thicker planks (5.5mm+) feel more solid underfoot, offer better sound insulation, and hide minor subfloor imperfections. For residential use, 4-5.5mm is adequate. For commercial or high-traffic areas, choose 5.5mm+ with a 0.5mm+ wear layer.

**Q350:** Does LVT fade in sunlight?
**A:** Quality LVT has UV-resistant coatings, but prolonged direct sunlight can cause gradual fading over many years. Use curtains or blinds in rooms with intense sun exposure, especially south- and west-facing rooms in South Africa. Rotate rugs and furniture periodically to ensure even exposure.

**Q351:** How does the cost of LVT compare to having tiles installed professionally?
**A:** LVT material costs (R250-R450/m²) are similar to mid-range tiles (R200-R400/m²). However, because LVT is DIY-friendly, you save R150-R300/m² in professional installation labour. A 40m² home could save R6,000-R12,000 by choosing DIY LVT over professionally installed tiles.

**Q352:** Can I install LVT in a conservatory or sunroom?
**A:** Yes, but be aware that conservatories experience significant temperature swings. Ensure adequate expansion gaps are maintained and use a high-quality LVT designed for temperature variation. SPC is more dimensionally stable than WPC in temperature extremes. Never install LVT in areas that will exceed 50°C floor temperature.

**Q353:** What are the skirting options for LVT and laminate floors?
**A:** Tiletoria offers matching skirtings for our laminate ranges. For LVT, you can use Scotia trim (quadrant beading) that covers the expansion gap while sitting against existing skirting boards, or remove existing skirtings and reinstall them over the flooring. Matching skirting heights are available.

**Q354:** Can LVT be laid in any direction, or does it matter?
**A:** You can lay LVT in any direction, but the orientation affects the room's perceived proportions. Laying planks along the longest wall makes the room appear longer. Laying toward a light source (window) can make seams less visible. In narrow hallways, laying lengthwise is usually best.

**Q355:** How do I transition between LVT and a different flooring type?
**A:** Use transition profiles (T-bars, reducer strips, or end caps) at doorways where LVT meets a different flooring material at a different height. These profiles are available in aluminium and wood-effect finishes. For same-height transitions, a T-bar profile works. For different heights, a reducer ramp profile bridges the difference.

## Category 8: Bathroom-Specific Questions

**Q356:** What tiles are best for bathroom floors?
**A:** For bathroom floors, we recommend matte or textured porcelain tiles with an R10 or R11 slip rating. Popular choices include 300x300mm, 400x400mm, or 600x600mm anti-slip floor tiles. Avoid polished tiles on bathroom floors — they're slippery when wet. Wood-look porcelain with a textured surface is also excellent for bathrooms.

**Q357:** Can I use the same tile on my bathroom floor and walls?
**A:** Yes, many porcelain ranges offer both floor and wall versions. Floor tiles can always be used on walls, creating a seamless, spa-like look. Using the same tile in different sizes (e.g., 600x600mm floor, 300x600mm wall) is a popular contemporary approach.

**Q358:** What size tiles work best for bathroom walls?
**A:** 300x600mm and 400x800mm are the most popular bathroom wall tile sizes. Larger tiles mean fewer grout lines (easier cleaning, more seamless look). Subway tiles (75x300mm, 100x300mm) are classic for a timeless look. Mosaic feature strips add visual interest.

**Q359:** Should I tile my bathroom walls all the way to the ceiling or stop at standard height?
**A:** Tiling to the ceiling creates a more luxurious, finished look and protects walls from moisture in steamy bathrooms. Standard tiling height is 2.1m (just above shower height). Budget-conscious DIYers often tile the shower area to the ceiling and other walls to 2.1m, with painted plaster above.

**Q360:** What tiles should I use in a shower (inside the shower cubicle)?
**A:** Shower walls should use porcelain or ceramic wall tiles — porcelain is preferred for its water resistance. Shower floors need small-format tiles (mosaics or 100x100mm) to accommodate the slope to the drain. Choose R11 or R12 slip-rated tiles for shower floors.

**Q361:** Do I need to waterproof my bathroom before tiling?
**A:** Yes, waterproofing is essential and required by the NHBRC and SANS 10400 building standards. All wet areas (shower floors, shower walls up to 1.8m, bath surrounds, bathroom floors) must be waterproofed before tiling. Use a liquid-applied waterproofing membrane system. This is critical — water damage from inadequate waterproofing is extremely costly to repair.

**Q362:** Can I tile over existing bathroom tiles?
**A:** We don't recommend tiling over existing tiles in wet areas like showers. The risk of water penetrating to the old layer is too high. For dry areas of the bathroom, it may be possible if the existing tiles are sound, but proper preparation with a bonding primer is essential. For best results in bathrooms, strip to substrate and start fresh.

**Q363:** What's the best tile for a feature wall in a bathroom?
**A:** Feature walls are a great way to add personality. Popular choices include: 3D textured wall tiles-look porcelain with dramatic veining, mosaic sheets, patterned/encaustic-look tiles, and zellige-look tiles with their characteristic glossy, uneven surface. Use the feature tile on one wall (typically behind the vanity or opposite the door) with coordinating plain tiles elsewhere.

**Q364:** How do I choose a slip-resistant tile for my bathroom floor?
**A:** Look for tiles with an R10 or R11 slip rating — this information is on the product label or our staff can advise. Visually, textured or matte surfaces provide better grip than polished surfaces. Test the slip resistance in the showroom — the tile should feel "grippy" under a wet shoe. All our bathroom floor tiles have appropriate slip ratings clearly indicated.

**Q365:** Can I use wood-look tiles in a bathroom?
**A:** Absolutely — wood-look porcelain tiles are extremely popular for bathrooms because they offer the warmth of wood with complete water resistance. Choose a textured finish (not polished) for the floor, and use the same or coordinating wood-look tiles on walls for a spa-like feel.

**Q366:** What colour tiles make a small bathroom look bigger?
**A:** Light colours (white, cream, light grey) reflect more light and make small bathrooms feel larger and brighter. Using large format tiles with matching grout minimises visual breaks. Glossy finishes on walls reflect light, while keeping the floor slightly darker creates depth. Avoid busy patterns in small spaces.

**Q367:** How do I tile around a bath?
**A:** Remove the bath panel if possible for easier access. Tile the wall behind and around the bath first, then tile the floor. The joint between the bath rim and the wall tiles must be sealed with silicone sealant (not grout) to allow for movement and prevent water ingress. Consider tiling the bath panel itself for a built-in look.

**Q368:** What is the best way to tile a shower floor?
**A:** Shower floors must slope 1:100 toward the drain. Use small tiles (50x50mm mosaics or 100x100mm) that can follow the slope. Ensure the floor is properly waterproofed before tiling. Use a waterproof, flexible adhesive and grout. If you're unsure about creating the slope or waterproofing, hire a professional for this critical area.

**Q369:** Should I use epoxy-based grout in my shower?
**A:** Epoxy grout is highly recommended for shower floors and recommended for shower walls. It's completely waterproof, stain-proof, and mould-resistant — essential in a constantly wet environment. It's more expensive and harder to apply than cementitious grout, but the long-term benefits in a shower are worth it. Consider hiring a professional for epoxy-based grouting if you're not confident.

**Q370:** How do I prevent mould in my bathroom tiles?
**A:** Use epoxy-based grout or ensure cementitious grout is properly sealed. Ensure adequate ventilation (an extractor fan is essential). Wipe down tiles and grout after showering. Clean grout lines regularly with a mould-preventing cleaner. Choose tiles with smooth, glazed surfaces that don't harbour moisture. Avoid persistent dampness — fix any leaks immediately.

**Q371:** Can I tile my bathroom ceiling?
**A:** Tiling a bathroom ceiling is possible but unusual. It requires specialised adhesives designed for overhead application and professional installation for safety. Most homeowners opt for a moisture-resistant painted ceiling or PVC ceiling panels in bathrooms.

**Q372:** What's the best layout for bathroom wall tiles?
**A:** The most common layout is a straight stack (tiles aligned in a grid) or brick bond/staggered (each row offset by half a tile). Brick bond is more forgiving of slightly uneven walls. For a contemporary look, vertical stacking (portrait orientation) makes ceilings feel higher. Subway tiles in a herringbone pattern create a premium feature effect.

**Q373:** How do I choose between a built-in shower and a tiled shower tray?
**A:** Built-in tiled showers offer unlimited design flexibility and a seamless look but require proper waterproofing and sloping. Acrylic shower trays are quicker to install, guaranteed waterproof, and often more affordable, but offer less design flexibility. Many DIYers use acrylic trays for ease and tile the surrounding walls.

**Q374:** What trim should I use at the edges of bathroom wall tiles?
**A:** For exposed edges (around windows, niche shelves, external corners), use matching bullnose tiles where available, or aluminium/PVC trim profiles. Trim profiles come in various colours — white, silver, chrome, black — to match your fixtures and tiles. They protect the tile edges and create a neat, professional finish.

**Q375:** Can I use large format tiles (600x1200mm) in a small bathroom?
**A:** Yes, large format tiles work beautifully in small bathrooms. Fewer grout lines create a more seamless, spacious appearance. However, large tiles require very flat walls (which may need extensive preparation) and are heavier to handle. A tile levelling system is essential. Professional installation is recommended for large format tiles in small, confined bathrooms.

**Q376:** How do I create a recessed shower niche?
**A:** A shower niche is a recessed shelf in the shower wall for toiletries. Plan the niche position and size before waterproofing. Build a recessed box between studs, waterproof the entire area including the niche interior, then tile the niche to match or contrast with the surrounding walls. This is an intermediate-to-advanced DIY project.

**Q377:** What tiles work best behind a freestanding bath?
**A:** Freestanding baths are a feature in themselves — choose tiles that complement rather than compete. Large format-look or concrete-look tiles create a sophisticated backdrop. Subway tiles in a contrasting colour (like navy with a white bath) add character. Consider a feature tile arrangement visible from the bathroom door.

**Q378:** Should I tile behind my bathroom vanity or around it?
**A:** Tiling the entire wall behind the vanity (before installing the vanity) is recommended. This protects the wall from water splashes and makes future vanity replacement easier without needing to retile. If tiling around an existing vanity, remove it first if possible for a cleaner installation.

**Q379:** What's the minimum grout joint width for bathroom tiles?
**A:** For rectified wall tiles, minimum 1.5mm. For pressed wall tiles, 2-3mm. For floor tiles, minimum 3mm. Never use zero-joint installation in bathrooms — grout joints are essential for waterproofing and allowing for minor movement. Wider joints (4-5mm) on floors improve slip resistance and are more forgiving for DIY installation.

**Q380:** Can I use mosaic tiles on a bathroom floor?
**A:** Yes, mosaic tiles (especially 50x50mm or smaller on mesh sheets) are excellent for bathroom floors. The numerous grout joints provide excellent slip resistance. They're particularly good for shower floors where the slope to the drain is important. However, more grout joints mean more cleaning — use epoxy-based grout for easier maintenance.

**A:** Powder rooms are small spaces where you can be bold — a striking feature wall, patterned floor tiles, or dramatic dark colours work well because the small scale prevents the design from overwhelming. **Q384:** Can I tile my bathroom in stages (floor first, walls later)?
**A:** Yes, many DIYers tile the floor first, then return to do the walls. Protect the floor with hardboard or cardboard while tiling walls. Alternatively, tile walls first (professionals' preference) and the floor last. Either approach works — the key is protecting finished work from damage.

Apply masking tape 5mm from the joint on both sides, apply the silicone bead, smooth with a wet finger, and remove tape immediately for a perfect finish.

**Q386:** How do I choose between a matte and glossy tile for my bathroom walls?
**A:** Glossy tiles reflect light (making small bathrooms feel brighter), are easy to wipe clean, and have a traditional, clean aesthetic. Matte tiles offer a contemporary, understated look and don't show water spots. Many South African homeowners choose matte for floors and glossy or satin for walls — or matte throughout for a modern spa aesthetic.

The heating mat is installed on the subfloor before tiling, and tiles are laid over it with flexible adhesive. Underfloor heating in bathrooms is increasingly popular in South Africa for comfort during winter mornings.

**Q396:** Should I tile behind my stove/cooktop?
**A:** Yes, definitely. The area behind the stove receives the most grease and food splatter. Use a hard-wearing, easy-to-clean tile — glossy or glass tiles wipe clean easily. A full-height splashback behind the stove (to the extractor hood) looks great and protects the wall. Patterned or feature tiles work well in this zone.

**Q397:** Can I tile my kitchen countertops?
**A:** Yes, porcelain tiles are excellent for countertops. Use large format tiles (600x1200mm or 750x1500mm) to minimise grout joints. The grout joints should be sealed to prevent staining. Alternatively, 12mm or 20mm porcelain slabs (sold as "sintered stone" or "compact surfaces") offer a seamless, grout-free countertop solution.

**Q398:** What grout colour should I use for kitchen tiles?
**A:** For floors, darker grout (charcoal, dark grey, brown) hides dirt and stains between cleans. For splashbacks, choose based on your desired look — matching grout creates a seamless appearance, while contrasting grout (white tiles with dark grey grout) emphasises the pattern. Always use grout sealer in kitchen areas.

**Q399:** Are polished tiles okay for kitchen floors?
**A:** Polished tiles can be used in kitchens but they're slippery when wet (from water or oil spills) and show scratches more readily. A lappato (semi-polished) or matte finish is generally more practical for kitchen floors. Polished tiles work well on kitchen walls and splashbacks.

**Q400:** Can I tile over existing kitchen tiles?
**A:** If the existing tiles are firmly bonded, clean, and the kitchen layout isn't changing, tiling over with a flexible adhesive and bonding primer is possible. However, this raises the floor height which may affect appliances, door clearances, and transitions to adjacent rooms. Carefully consider these implications before proceeding.

**Q401:** How do I protect my kitchen tiles from staining?
**A:** Use glazed porcelain tiles (the glaze makes them stain-resistant). Seal cementitious grout with a penetrating grout sealer. Wipe up spills promptly — especially acidic substances like tomato sauce, wine, and citrus juice. Use cutting boards to protect countertop tiles from knife marks.

**Q402:** What's the difference between a splashback tile and a floor tile?
**A:** Splashback tiles are typically wall tiles — thinner, lighter, and often more decorative. They're not designed to withstand foot traffic. Floor tiles can be used as splashbacks (and many homeowners do this for coordination), but never use wall tiles on floors. For kitchen splashbacks, either wall tiles or porcelain floor tiles work well.

**Q403:** How do I tile around kitchen plug sockets?
**A:** Turn off the power at the DB board. Remove the socket faceplate. Extend the socket box with a spacer so it sits flush over the tile. Mark and cut the tile (using a wet saw or angle grinder with diamond blade) to fit around the socket. Refit the faceplate over the tiled surface.

**Q404:** Should my kitchen floor tiles match my cabinetry or countertops?
**A:** Your floor tiles don't need to match exactly but should complement the overall scheme. A common approach: choose a neutral floor (grey, beige, or wood-look) that works with any cabinet colour, then use the splashback for personality and colour. Bring cabinet door or countertop samples to the showroom to compare.

**Q405:** What are the best tiles for a farmhouse/country-style kitchen?
**A:** Patterned encaustic-look floor tiles (200x200mm or 300x300mm), subway tiles with contrasting grout for the splashback, terracotta-look porcelain, and wood-look floor tiles all suit farmhouse aesthetics. Consider a patterned feature area (like a "rug" effect in front of the stove) surrounded by plain tiles.

**Q406:** What's trending for kitchen tiles in South Africa?
**A:** Current kitchen trends include: large format concrete-look and stone-look floor tiles, herringbone-pattern splashbacks, bold patterned tiles behind stoves, matte black fixtures with white or-look tiles, navy and forest green splashback colours, and seamless floor-to-ceiling tiled walls in modern kitchens.

**Q407:** How do I clean grease off kitchen wall tiles?
**A:** Glazed tiles are easy to clean — wipe with warm soapy water or an all-purpose kitchen cleaner. For stubborn grease buildup, use a degreasing cleaner. Avoid abrasive scrubbers that can scratch the glaze. Epoxy grout is highly resistant to grease staining — consider it for kitchen splashbacks.

**Q408:** Can I use underfloor heating under kitchen tiles?
**A:** Yes, electric underfloor heating works excellently under porcelain kitchen floor tiles. It makes winter mornings more comfortable and helps dry the floor quickly after cleaning. Ensure the heating system's total wattage is suitable for the kitchen area and install according to manufacturer guidelines.

**Q409:** How much does it cost to tile a kitchen (floor + splashback)?
**A:** For an average kitchen (12m² floor + 3m splashback at 0.6m high = 1.8m²): mid-range floor tiles at R250/m² = R3,000. Splashback tiles at R300/m² = R540. Add adhesive, grout, and trim (R1,500-R2,000). Total materials: R5,000-R5,500 for a basic kitchen. Premium tiles would be R8,000-R12,000.

**Q410:** Should I use large tiles or small tiles for a compact kitchen?
**A:** Large tiles (600x600mm+) actually make small kitchens feel more spacious because there are fewer grout lines creating visual breaks. However, very large tiles in a tiny kitchen create many difficult cuts at the edges. 400x400mm or 600x600mm is usually the sweet spot for compact kitchens.

**Q411:** What's the best tile finish for a kitchen floor — matte, lappato, or textured?
**A:** Lappato (semi-polished) offers a good balance — easier to clean than matte, less slippery than fully polished, and hides minor scratches. Textured tiles provide the best slip resistance but can be harder to clean (dirt settles in texture). For most residential kitchens, lappato or matte is ideal.

**Q412:** How do I handle the transition between kitchen tiles and lounge carpet/laminate?
**A:** Use a transition profile (also called a cover strip or threshold) at the doorway between different floorings. Choose the profile type based on the height difference — T-bar for same-height floors, reducer ramp for different heights. Available in aluminium, wood-effect, and other finishes.

**Q413:** Can I install kitchen tiles myself, or should I hire a professional?
**A:** Kitchen floor tiling is achievable for a competent DIYer with proper preparation. Kitchen splashbacks are an excellent beginner-to-intermediate DIY project — the area is small, no furniture needs moving, and mistakes are easily rectified. The splashback is often where DIYers cut their teeth before tackling larger projects.

**Q414:** How do I tile behind a freestanding stove?
**A:** Slide the stove out (disconnect gas/electricity first if moving it fully). Tile the wall behind completely, including any areas currently hidden by the stove — this protects the wall and ensures a finished look if you ever change appliance sizes. Leave a small expansion gap around the stove area and seal with silicone.

**Q415:** What tile layout patterns work best in kitchens?
**A:** Straight stack (grid) is the most common and easiest for DIY. Brick bond (running bond) is forgiving of slightly uneven walls and adds subtle interest. For splashbacks, herringbone pattern creates a high-end look. Diagonal layouts make narrow kitchens feel wider but create more wastage.

**Q416:** Are white kitchen floor tiles practical?
**A:** White floor tiles show dirt more readily than darker colours, requiring more frequent cleaning. If you love the white aesthetic, choose a white tile with subtle pattern or veining that disguises minor dirt between cleans. Alternatively, use white on walls/splashback and a light-to-mid grey on the floor.

**Q417:** How do I choose tiles for a kitchen island base?
**A:** Tiling the kitchen island base creates a custom, built-in look. Use the same tile as your splashback for coordination, or a contrasting feature tile. The island base is a great place to experiment with pattern or texture as it's a contained area. Use tile trim on exposed edges for a neat finish.

**Q418:** Can I use mosaic tiles for a kitchen splashback?
**A:** Yes, mosaic splashbacks (especially glass mosaics) are popular in South African kitchens. They're available in sheet format, making installation manageable for DIYers. Glass mosaics are highly stain-resistant and reflect light, making kitchens feel brighter. They're more expensive per m² than standard tiles but the kitchen splashback area is small.

**Q419:** What sealant should I use at the junction between kitchen countertop and wall tiles?
**A:** Use a high-quality, food-safe silicone sealant (neutral cure, not acetoxy cure which can corrode some materials) in a colour matching your grout. This flexible joint accommodates movement between the counter and wall while preventing water ingress behind the tiles.

**Q420:** How do I prevent my kitchen floor tiles from cracking?
**A:** Ensure proper subfloor preparation — the floor must be sound, level, and primed. Use a flexible adhesive. Install movement joints every 8-10m and at doorways. Don't but tiles hard against walls — leave a 5mm expansion gap covered by skirting. Use appropriate adhesive for your tile type and substrate.

**Q498:** Should I choose tiles or LVT for my home?

**Q503:** Should I choose a trendy tile or a classic, timeless design?

**Q510:** Are wood-look tiles better than real wood flooring?

**A:** The warranty covers manufacturing defects such as: abnormal crazing or cracking under normal conditions, significant colour variation within the same batch, dimensional defects outside manufacturing tolerances, and glaze defects present at the time of installation. **Q523:** How long should porcelain tiles last?
**A:** Quality porcelain tiles, properly installed and maintained, should last 30-50 years or more in residential applications. Porcelain is one of the most durable flooring materials available — it's not uncommon for porcelain floors to outlast the building they're installed in.

**Q524:** How long does ceramic wall tile last?
**A:** Ceramic wall tiles should last 20-30 years or more with proper installation. Because walls don't experience foot traffic or impact, wall tiles typically have a very long lifespan. The main failure points are adhesive degradation over decades or water ingress behind tiles in wet areas.

**Q525:** What should I check when my tile delivery arrives to ensure quality?
**A:** Check that: all boxes are from the same batch/lot number, boxes are undamaged and dry, tile dimensions match specifications, the colour and finish match your sample, and the quantity is correct. Open one box from different pallets to check for any visible defects. Report any concerns before installation.

**Q526:** What are acceptable manufacturing variations in tiles?
**A:** All tiles have manufacturing tolerances that are considered normal: slight colour variation between batches (check V-rating), minor size variation in pressed tiles (±1.5%), slight bowing in large format tiles (which is corrected during installation), and minor glaze variation. These are not defects — they're characteristics of the manufacturing process.

**Q527:** What's the difference between a first-grade and second-grade tile?
**A:** First-grade tiles meet all manufacturer specifications for size, flatness, glaze quality, and appearance. Second-grade tiles (also called "class B" or "commercial grade") have visible defects such as size variation, glaze imperfections, or colour inconsistency. Tiletoria sells predominantly first-grade tiles. Any second-grade or factory-shop items are clearly labelled.

**Q528:** How can I tell if a tile is good quality before buying?
**A:** Check the PEI rating (PEI 3+ for floors), water absorption rate (below 0.5% for porcelain), thickness consistency, rectification quality (edges should be straight and sharp), glaze uniformity, and the backing stamp (should show manufacturer details and standards compliance). Our staff can explain these indicators for any tile you're considering.

**Q529:** What does "SANS 1449" mean on tile packaging?
**A:** SANS 1449 is the South African National Standard for ceramic tiles. It specifies requirements for dimensions, physical properties, and quality. Tiles bearing this mark have been tested and certified to meet South African standards. All tiles sold by Tiletoria comply with or exceed SANS 1449.

**Q530:** What is ISO 13006 classification for tiles?
**A:** ISO 13006 is the international standard that classifies ceramic tiles into groups based on manufacturing method and water absorption: Group B I (low water absorption, <3% — includes porcelain), Group B II (3-10%), and Group B III (>10%). Porcelain tiles are Group B I a (below 0.5% absorption). This classification helps you understand where a tile can be used.

**Q531:** Do imported tiles have different quality standards than local tiles?
**A:** All tiles sold in South Africa must meet SANS 1449 regardless of origin. Our imported tiles are sourced from reputable manufacturers who meet international standards (EN, ISO) as well as SANS requirements. We quality-check all imports on arrival. The country of origin doesn't determine quality — the manufacturer's standards do.

**Q532:** What if my tiles develop cracks months after installation?
**A:** Cracks that appear well after installation are almost always due to substrate movement, inadequate expansion joints, or structural issues rather than tile defects. However, contact us with photos and your installation details — we can help diagnose the cause. If it's a genuine manufacturing defect, we'll address it under warranty. If it's an installation issue, we can advise on remediation.

**Q533:** Are polished porcelain tiles prone to scratching?
**A:** Polished porcelain is harder than most materials and won't scratch from normal foot traffic. However, very hard materials (sand, grit, diamond-hard debris) can scratch the polished surface. Regular sweeping/vacuuming to remove grit is the best prevention. Lappato and matte finishes are more forgiving of minor scratches.

**A:** Yes, mosaics require more time and care to install. The mesh-backed sheets must be properly aligned so grout joints line up between sheets. More cutting is needed at edges and around fixtures. More grout is required (more joints per m²). The result is beautiful but budget extra time and consider your skill level before tackling a large mosaic area.

**Q690:** What's the difference between mesh-backed and dot-mounted mosaics?
**A:** Mesh-backed mosaics are attached to a fibreglass mesh sheet, making them easy to handle and install as a unit. Dot-mounted mosaics use small adhesive dots between individual pieces — these allow for closer spacing and more flexible arrangement but take longer to install. Mesh-backed is standard and more DIY-friendly.

**Q691:** Can I use mosaic tiles on a bathroom floor?
**A:** Yes, mosaic tiles are excellent for bathroom floors — particularly shower floors. The numerous small pieces and grout joints provide excellent slip resistance. Small mosaics (23x23mm, 48x48mm) conform well to falls toward drains. Use epoxy-based grout for easier maintenance in wet areas.

**Q692:** What size are individual mosaic pieces?
**A:** Mosaic pieces range from very small (15x15mm, 20x20mm) to medium (48x48mm, 50x50mm) to large (100x100mm). Smaller pieces create more intricate patterns and better slip resistance but require more grout. Larger pieces are quicker to install and have fewer grout lines. The sheet size is typically 300x300mm regardless of piece size.

**Q693:** Do mosaic tiles need to be sealed?

**Q694:** What's the difference between glass mosaics and stone mosaics?
**A:** Glass mosaics offer brilliant colour, translucency, light reflection, and stain resistance. They're ideal for feature walls, splashbacks, and pools. Stone mosaics offer natural warmth, texture, and an organic aesthetic. They're better for floors and areas where slip resistance is important. Mixed glass-and-stone mosaics combine both aesthetics.

**Q695:** Can I cut mosaic sheets?
**A:** Yes, mosaic sheets can be cut with scissors (for the mesh backing) and a tile cutter or wet saw (for the individual pieces). For edge trimming, cut through the mesh with a utility knife and remove excess pieces. For fitting around fixtures, carefully nip individual pieces with tile nippers.

**Q696:** What are 3D mosaics?
**A:** 3D mosaics have dimensional, textured surfaces that create shadow and depth on walls. They come in various patterns: waves, geometric shapes, pebble effects, hexagons, and abstract forms. These are wall tiles only (not for floors) and create stunning feature walls in bathrooms, entrance halls, and behind headboards.

**Q697:** How do I grout mosaic tiles?
**A:** Mosaics are grouted the same way as regular tiles but require more care: use a rubber grout float to work grout into all joints, ensure full joint penetration (mesh-backed mosaics can trap air), clean excess grout promptly before it hardens on tile faces, and use unsanded grout for narrow joints (under 3mm). Epoxy grout is recommended for wet area mosaics.

**Q698:** What's a mosaic feature strip?
**A:** A feature strip (also called an accent strip or listello) is a horizontal band of mosaic or decorative tiles running across a wall, typically at eye level in a shower or behind a vanity. It adds visual interest and breaks up large plain-tiled areas. A strip one or two mosaic sheets high (30-60cm) is a common design element.

**Q699:** Can I create a mosaic pattern myself instead of buying sheets?
**A:** Yes, experienced DIYers can create custom mosaic patterns by laying individual pieces. However, this is extremely time-consuming and requires patience and precision. Pre-designed mosaic sheets from Tiletoria offer professional results much faster. Custom mosaic work is best left to mosaic artists for feature installations.

**Q700:** Do you sell mosaic murals?
**A:** We stock decorative mosaic panels and can advise on suppliers for custom mosaic murals. Pre-designed mosaic murals are available in various themes — floral, geometric, Mediterranean, and abstract. Custom murals can be created from your own design for a truly unique feature wall.

**Q701:** What's the difference between iridescent and glossy glass mosaics?
**A:** Glossy glass mosaics have a shiny, reflective surface that reflects light directly. Iridescent glass mosaics have a special coating that creates rainbow-like colour shifts as viewing angle changes, similar to oil on water. Iridescent mosaics are popular for feature walls and pool interiors where the colour-shifting effect adds drama.

**Q702:** Can mosaic tiles be used in swimming pools?
**A:** Yes, glass and porcelain mosaic tiles are ideal for swimming pools. They're water-resistant, frost-resistant, and designed to withstand pool chemicals. The small pieces allow for curved surfaces and creative designs. Pool mosaics are available in various colours, including iridescent options that shimmer underwater.

**Q703:** How do I clean mosaic tiles?
**A:** Clean mosaics the same way as regular tiles — sweep/vacuum and damp mop with pH-neutral cleaner. Mosaics have more grout lines, so grout cleaning is more important. Use a soft grout brush to clean between mosaic pieces. For glass mosaics, a glass cleaner restores sparkle. Never use abrasive cleaners on glass or polished stone mosaics.

**Q704:** Are metal mosaics just for looks, or are they durable?
**A:** Metal mosaics (stainless steel, aluminium, copper-look) are both decorative and durable. Stainless steel mosaics are corrosion-resistant and suitable for kitchen splashbacks. Subway mosaics (mini subway pieces) are also trending.

**Q706:** My tiles sound hollow when I tap them — is this a problem?
**A:** A hollow sound indicates a void (air pocket) under the tile where adhesive didn't fully contact the tile back. A few hollow tiles in a large area may not be a problem if the tiles are secure. However, multiple hollow tiles or tiles that feel loose indicate poor adhesive coverage and should be removed and reinstalled before they crack or come loose.

**Q707:** Some of my tiles have slightly different shades — is this normal?
**A:** Slight variation is normal and expected, particularly in stone-look and wood-look tiles (check the V-rating on the packaging — V3 and V4 mean significant variation is intentional). However, if tiles from the same batch show extreme, unexpected colour differences, this may be a batch issue. Contact us with photos if you're concerned.

**Q708:** My grout has turned white and powdery — what happened?

**Q709:** My polished porcelain tiles have lost their shine — how do I restore it?
**A:** Loss of shine is usually due to a build-up of cleaning product residue or dirt. Clean thoroughly with a porcelain-specific cleaner or a solution of warm water with a few drops of dish soap. For stubborn build-up, use a commercial porcelain/tile restorer. Never use wax-based products, oil soaps, or abrasive powders on polished porcelain.

Etching is permanent damage to the stone surface from acidic substances and requires professional re-honing. To prevent etching, seal stone regularly and wipe spills immediately.

**Q711:** our laminate flooring tiles are cracking — what could be causing this?
**A:** Cracking is almost always due to substrate issues rather than tile defects. Common causes: inadequate subfloor preparation, missing or inadequate expansion joints, subfloor movement or settlement, tiles installed over structural cracks without an anti-fracture membrane, inadequate adhesive coverage, or heavy impact. We can help diagnose the cause if you share photos and installation details.

**Q712:** Water is seeping through my shower grout — what should I do?
**A:** Seeping water usually indicates failed or missing grout, failed silicone sealant at junctions, or inadequate waterproofing behind the tiles. Remove and replace damaged grout with epoxy-based grout (waterproof). Reseal all silicone joints. If water continues to seep, the waterproofing membrane behind the tiles may have failed — this requires professional remediation, potentially removing and reinstalling the tiles.

**Q713:** My tiles have developed a white haze after grouting — how do I remove it?

**Q714:** Some of my wall tiles are starting to come loose — how do I fix this?
**A:** Remove loose tiles carefully, scrape all old adhesive from both the tile back and the wall, check that the wall surface is sound (not crumbling), apply fresh adhesive (use wall tile adhesive with good grab), press the tile firmly back into place, and support it until the adhesive sets (24 hours). Regrout the joint once the adhesive is fully cured.

**Q715:** My grout is cracking and falling out between tiles — what's wrong?
**A:** Cracked grout indicates movement in the substrate — either the subfloor is flexing, expansion joints are missing, or the wrong adhesive was used. Remove the cracked grout completely, check for loose tiles (indicating adhesive failure), and re-grout with flexible grout. If the problem recurs, the underlying movement issue must be addressed.

**Q716:** There's mould growing on my bathroom grout — how do I get rid of it?
**A:** Clean mould with a mould remover or diluted bleach solution (wear gloves and ventilate). For persistent mould, the grout may need to be removed and replaced with epoxy-based grout (mould-resistant). Address the root cause: improve ventilation (install/run an extractor fan), reseal cementitious grout, and reduce humidity. Mould-resistant silicone at junctions also helps prevent recurrence.

**Q717:** My LVT floor has developed gaps between planks — what happened?
**A:** Gaps in LVT are typically caused by: inadequate expansion gap around the perimeter (the floor is pushing against walls), temperature fluctuations causing expansion and contraction, or subfloor moisture issues. Check that expansion gaps are clear (not blocked by skirting). For small gaps, gentle tapping may close them. Persistent gaps may require partial reinstallation with proper expansion clearance.

**Q718:** My laminate floor is lifting at the edges — why?
**A:** Edge lifting in laminate is usually due to moisture exposure (water has seeped into the joints and swollen the HDF core) or inadequate expansion gaps. If moisture is the cause, the affected boards must be replaced and the moisture source eliminated. For minor lifting due to expansion pressure, remove the skirting and ensure adequate expansion gaps exist.

**Q719:** Tiles are making a cracking sound when I walk on them — is this normal?
**A:** A cracking or popping sound when walking on tiles indicates that the tile is not fully bonded to the substrate — there's a void underneath allowing the tile to flex and make noise. This is not normal and will eventually lead to cracked tiles. The affected tiles should be removed and reinstalled with proper adhesive coverage.

**Q720:** My underfloor heating isn't heating evenly — could the tiles be the problem?
**A:** Tiles are excellent heat conductors and don't cause uneven heating. Uneven heat is usually due to: heating cable spacing inconsistency, a faulty thermostat or sensor, or air gaps under the tiles (which insulate and prevent heat transfer). Check the heating system first. If some tiles sound hollow, that could explain cold spots — address the adhesive voids.

**Q721:** My stone tiles have developed stains — how do I remove them?
**A:** Stain removal depends on the stain type and stone. Oil-based stains: draw out with a poultice (baking soda and water paste, left for 24-48 hours). Organic stains (coffee, wine): hydrogen peroxide poultice. Rust: commercial rust remover for stone. Always test on a spare tile first. If stains persist, professional stone restoration may be needed.

**Q722:** The colour of my tiles looks different now they're installed vs. the sample — why?
**A:** Colour differences can be due to: different lighting ( showroom vs. home), the surrounding colours affecting perception (wall colour, cabinetry), the larger area creating a different visual effect than a small sample, batch variation, or grout colour influence. Also, tiles often appear slightly darker when wet or when viewed at a different angle. If the difference is extreme, contact us.

**Q723:** My tile cutter keeps breaking tiles instead of cutting them cleanly — what's wrong?
**A:** Common causes: blunt or incorrect blade (porcelain requires a diamond blade), cutting too fast, not enough water lubrication (for wet saws), scoring too lightly (for manual cutters), or trying to cut tiles too thick for the cutter. Ensure you're using the right cutter for your tile type — manual cutters work for ceramic; porcelain needs a wet saw.

**Q724:** The adhesive is setting too fast for me to work — what can I do?
**A:** Adhesive sets faster in hot, dry, or windy conditions. To slow setting: mix smaller batches, work in shaded areas, use cooler water for mixing, cover mixed adhesive with a damp cloth between applications, or switch to a standard-set adhesive if you've been using rapid-set. In very hot weather, work early morning or late afternoon.

**Q725:** I ordered too few tiles and my batch is sold out — what are my options?
**A:** Contact us immediately — we can check all warehouses for remaining stock from the same batch. If the exact batch is unavailable, we can check if a new batch is arriving. If you must use a different batch, install the new batch in a less visible area (behind furniture, under cabinets) with the old batch in visible areas. Slight variation is usually acceptable if the tile has a V3 or V4 rating.

**Q726:** My bathroom floor isn't sloping toward the drain — is this a problem?
**A:** A flat bathroom floor (outside the shower area) is fine — it only needs to be level. However, the shower floor MUST slope toward the drain (minimum 1:100 gradient) to prevent standing water. If your shower floor isn't sloping, this is a significant problem that can cause water pooling, mould, and waterproofing failure. This requires professional remediation.

**Q727:** There are chips on the edges of my tiles after cutting — how do I prevent this?
**A:** Chipping during cutting is usually caused by: a blunt blade, cutting too fast, incorrect blade type (use diamond blade for porcelain), not supporting the tile properly during the cut, or poor quality tiles. Use a sharp blade, cut slowly with consistent pressure, support both sides of the cut line, and use masking tape along the cut line to reduce chipping.

**Q728:** My grout lines are uneven widths — how do I fix this?
**A:** Uneven grout lines are a cosmetic issue that's difficult to fix after grouting without removing tiles. For minor variation, it's often not noticeable once the room is furnished. For significant issues, you may need to remove and reinstall the affected tiles. Prevention is key — use tile spacers consistently and check alignment frequently as you work.

**Q729:** Water is pooling on my outdoor patio tiles — what's wrong?
**A:** Water pooling indicates inadequate slope (should be minimum 1:80 away from the house) or dips in the subfloor. This can lead to slipping hazards, algae growth, and potential water ingress. The solution may involve removing tiles, correcting the screed falls, and reinstalling. In some cases, additional drainage points can be added.

**Q730:** My tiles have developed scratches — can they be repaired?

**Q731:** The silicone sealant around my bath is going mouldy — how do I fix it?
**A:** Remove the old silicone completely using a silicone remover tool, clean the area thoroughly with methylated spirits, ensure the area is completely dry, and apply new mould-resistant bathroom silicone. Use masking tape for a neat finish. Ensure adequate bathroom ventilation to prevent recurrence. Quality silicone should last 5-10 years before mould develops.

**Q732:** My tile installation looks uneven with some tiles higher than others — what happened?
**A:** This is called "lippage" — height differences between adjacent tiles. Causes include: uneven subfloor, not using a tile levelling system with large format tiles, inconsistent adhesive bed thickness, or tiles of varying thickness (shouldn't happen with quality tiles). Minor lippage (under 1mm) is normal; significant lippage requires removing and relevelling affected tiles.

**Q733:** The colour of my grout is different from what I expected — why?
**A:** Grout colour can appear different when wet vs. dry, and the surrounding tile colour affects perception (grout looks darker against light tiles, lighter against dark tiles). Cementitious grout also lightens slightly as it fully cures (up to 7 days). Always let grout dry completely before assessing the colour. If truly wrong, the grout can be stained or removed and replaced.

**Q734:** My laminate floor has water damage from a leak — can it be saved?
**A:** If caught quickly, dry the area thoroughly with fans and dehumidifiers — minor surface moisture on AC4/AC5 laminate may not cause permanent damage. However, if water has penetrated the joints and the HDF core has swollen, the affected boards must be replaced. Laminate cannot be "dried out" once the core has expanded. Keep spare planks from installation for repairs.

**Q735:** There are gaps between our laminate flooring tiles and the wall — is this normal?
**A:** Yes, a 5-10mm gap between floor tiles and walls is essential — it's an expansion joint that allows the floor to expand and contract with temperature changes. This gap should be covered by skirting boards and not filled with grout or hard adhesive. If the gap is visible after skirting is installed, the skirting may need adjustment.

**Q736:** My wood-look tiles look too uniform and fake — how can I improve the look?
**A:** To improve the realism of wood-look tiles: choose a range with high variation (V3-V4) so each tile looks different, mix tiles from multiple boxes during installation (creates natural variation), stagger the joints randomly (like real wood flooring, not in a regular pattern), use a narrow grout joint (1.5-2mm for rectified), and choose a grout colour close to the tile colour.

**Q737:** My outdoor tiles have become slippery with algae — how do I clean them?
**A:** Clean algae from outdoor tiles with a stiff brush and a solution of warm water and household bleach (1 part bleach to 10 parts water), or a commercial patio cleaner. Wear protective gear. For persistent algae, a pressure washer on a low setting can help. To prevent regrowth, ensure adequate drainage, improve sunlight exposure where possible, and consider an anti-slip treatment.

**Q738:** The adhesive isn't sticking to my porcelain tiles — what's wrong?
**A:** Porcelain is dense and non-porous, so standard adhesive may not bond well. Solutions: use a high-quality flexible adhesive specifically formulated for porcelain (C2 class or higher), back-butter each tile to ensure full coverage, ensure the tile back is clean (no dust or manufacturing residue), and don't spread more adhesive than you can cover within the open time.

**Q739:** My tiles were installed yesterday and I can already see staining — what happened?

**Q740:** I dropped a heavy pot and cracked a floor tile — how do I replace just one?
**A:** Remove the grout around the damaged tile, drill holes in the tile centre, carefully chip it out with a hammer and chisel, clean the old adhesive from the substrate, apply fresh adhesive, set the new tile level with its neighbours, let it set 24 hours, then grout. This is why keeping spare tiles from your original installation is so important.

**Q741:** My bathroom extractor fan isn't preventing condensation on my tiles — what else can I do?
**A:** In addition to running the extractor fan during and after showers: keep the bathroom door open after showering to allow air circulation, wipe tiles down with a squeegee after showering, ensure the fan is adequate for the room size (calculate m³/hour rating), consider a heated towel rail (reduces humidity), and in extreme cases, a dehumidifier may be needed. Proper ventilation is essential for tile longevity.

**Q742:** The pattern on my patterned tiles isn't aligning properly — how do I fix this?
**A:** Patterned tiles need careful alignment during installation. If patterns are misaligned: check that all tiles are oriented the same way (there's usually a directional arrow on the back), ensure consistent spacing, and verify that tiles are from the same batch. If already installed, minor misalignment may not be noticeable from a distance. Significant issues may require reinstalling the affected area.

**Q743:** our laminate flooring tiles feel cold in winter — is underfloor heating the only solution?
**A:** Underfloor heating is the best solution but not the only one. Alternatives: area rugs in high-traffic zones (bedside, in front of the sofa), slippers with rubber soles, ensuring good insulation in the room above (if applicable), and closing curtains at night to retain heat. Tiles will always feel cooler than carpet or wood — it's part of their character, but underfloor heating transforms the experience.


**Q745:** My LVT floor feels spongy in some areas — what's wrong?
**A:** A spongy or bouncy feel indicates an uneven subfloor beneath the LVT. The locking joints may be flexing over a hollow or raised spot. This can cause joint failure over time. The solution is to lift the affected area, level the subfloor with a self-levelling compound, and reinstall. Proper subfloor preparation is essential for LVT longevity.

**Q746:** There are bubbles under my vinyl flooring — how do I fix them?
**A:** Bubbles in sheet vinyl can sometimes be punctured with a fine needle and the air pressed out, then weighted down for 24 hours. For persistent bubbles, cut a small slit, apply adhesive underneath, press flat, and seal the slit. Bubbles indicate either trapped air during installation, subfloor moisture, or adhesive failure.

**Q747:** My tiles look different colours in different lights — is this normal?
**A:** Yes, this is completely normal. All tiles appear different under warm incandescent light, cool LED light, natural daylight, and evening light. This is especially noticeable with light-coloured and reflective tiles. The variation can be striking — a tile that looks warm beige in morning light may appear cool grey under LED downlights. Always view samples in your home's actual lighting before deciding.

**Q748:** My grout lines are discoloured even though I sealed them — what happened?
**A:** Grout sealer wears off over time, especially in high-traffic and wet areas. If sealed grout is discolouring, the sealer has likely worn away and needs reapplication. Clean the grout thoroughly first, allow it to dry completely, then apply fresh penetrating sealer. In showers, sealer may need reapplication every 12 months.

**Q749:** I've run out of tiles one box short and can't get the same batch — what do I do?
**A:** Use the different-batch tiles in the least visible areas: behind the toilet, under the vanity, inside cupboards, or in corners. Slight colour variation is usually acceptable if the tile has a V3 or V4 variation rating. If the difference is noticeable, consider creating a deliberate pattern or border with the different tiles to make it look intentional.

**Q750:** My shower door or screen won't close properly after tiling — why?
**A:** Tiling raises the floor level, which can affect door clearances and shower screen alignment. Frameless shower screens need precise level installation. The solution may involve: adjusting the door hinges, trimming the door bottom (if wood), reinstalling the shower screen to the new level, or in some cases, replacing the door with one suited to the new floor height. Always account for tile thickness when planning shower installations.

## Category 21: Environmental & Sustainability Questions

**Q751:** Are tiles an eco-friendly flooring choice?
**A:** Yes, tiles are one of the most environmentally friendly flooring options. Porcelain and ceramic tiles are made from natural clay and minerals, contain no VOCs (volatile organic compounds), don't emit harmful chemicals, are extremely durable (lasting decades, reducing replacement waste), and are 100% recyclable. Their long lifespan means less material ends up in landfills compared to shorter-lived flooring options.

**Q752:** Do Tiletoria tiles contain harmful chemicals like VOCs?
**A:** No. Porcelain and ceramic tiles are fired at extremely high temperatures (1,000-1,400°C), which eliminates all organic compounds. Tiles contain no VOCs, no formaldehyde, no PVC, and no phthalates. They're one of the healthiest flooring choices for indoor air quality — recognised by allergy and asthma organisations worldwide.

**Q753:** Are tiles recyclable?
**A:** Yes, ceramic and porcelain tiles are 100% recyclable. They can be crushed and used as aggregate in road construction, concrete production, and drainage systems. However, recycling infrastructure for post-consumer tiles is limited in South Africa. The best environmental benefit of tiles comes from their extreme durability — they rarely need replacement.

**Q754:** Is porcelain more eco-friendly than ceramic?
**A:** Both are eco-friendly. Porcelain requires higher firing temperatures (uses more energy) but lasts significantly longer. Ceramic uses less energy to produce but may need replacing sooner in high-traffic areas. Over a 50-year lifespan, porcelain's durability often makes it the more sustainable choice despite the higher initial production energy.

**Q755:** Does Tiletoria source sustainable products?
**A:** We work with international manufacturers who are increasingly adopting sustainable practices — using recycled materials in production, investing in energy-efficient kilns, and implementing water recycling in manufacturing. Porcelain tiles themselves are an inherently sustainable product due to their natural composition and extreme longevity.


**Q757:** Is LVT flooring eco-friendly?
**A:** LVT is a PVC-based product, which raises environmental concerns around production and end-of-life disposal. However, quality LVT is extremely durable (10-25 years), reducing replacement frequency. Some manufacturers now offer LVT with recycled content and phthalate-free formulations. For maximum sustainability, porcelain tiles remain the better choice, but LVT offers practical advantages for certain applications.

**Q758:** How does tile flooring compare to carpet for allergies?
**A:** Tiles are vastly superior to carpet for allergy sufferers. Carpet traps dust mites, pet dander, pollen, and mould spores, releasing them when walked on. Tiles have no fibres to harbour allergens and can be thoroughly cleaned. Hard flooring with regular cleaning significantly reduces indoor allergen levels — recommended by allergy specialists.

**Q759:** Do tiles help keep a house cool in summer?
**A:** Yes, tiles are naturally cool underfoot and help regulate indoor temperatures in hot climates. They absorb heat during the day and release it slowly at night. In South African summers, tiled floors feel significantly cooler than wood, carpet, or vinyl. Light-coloured tiles reflect more heat than dark tiles — an important consideration for north and west-facing rooms.

**Q760:** Can tiles contribute to green building certification?
**A:** Yes, porcelain and ceramic tiles can contribute points toward green building certifications like GBCSA (Green Building Council South Africa) through: low VOC emissions, recycled content in some products, local manufacturing options (reducing transport emissions), durability (reducing material replacement), and thermal mass properties. Specific credit depends on the certification system and project.

## Category 22: NHBRC & Building Compliance

**Q761:** What is the NHBRC, and does it affect my tiling project?
**A:** The NHBRC (National Home Builders Registration Council) regulates home building standards in South Africa. If you're building a new home enrolled with the NHBRC, your tiling must comply with their standards, particularly for waterproofing in wet areas. For renovations on existing homes, NHBRC compliance isn't mandatory but the standards provide excellent guidance for best practice.

**Q762:** What are the waterproofing requirements for bathrooms?
**A:** SANS 10400 and NHBRC requirements mandate: shower floors must be waterproofed, shower walls must be waterproofed up to 1.8m high, bath walls must be waterproofed up to the shower rose height where there's a shower over the bath, and bathroom floors must have a waterproofing system. This is typically a liquid-applied membrane applied before tiling.

**Q763:** Do I need a certificate for my waterproofing?
**A:** For new builds enrolled with the NHBRC, a waterproofing certificate from a registered waterproofing contractor is required. For DIY renovations on existing homes, a certificate isn't legally required but proper waterproofing is essential. If you sell your home, having documentation of proper waterproofing adds value and buyer confidence.

**Q764:** What is SANS 10400, and how does it relate to tiling?
**A:** SANS 10400 is the South African National Standard for The Application of the National Building Regulations. Part B (Structural Design), Part K (Floors and Stairways), and Part P (Drainage) contain provisions relevant to tiling, particularly regarding floor drainage, falls to waste, and structural adequacy of substrates.

**Q765:** What SANS standard applies to ceramic tiles specifically?
**A:** SANS 1449 is the South African National Standard for ceramic tiles. It specifies requirements for classification, dimensions, physical properties, and quality. All tiles sold in South Africa should comply with SANS 1449. Tiletoria ensures all our products meet or exceed this standard.

**Q766:** Do I need building plan approval to retile my bathroom?
**A:** For cosmetic retiling (replacing tiles without structural changes), building plan approval is generally not required. However, if you're altering drainage, moving walls, or making structural changes, you may need approval from your local municipality. Check with your local building control office if unsure.

**Q767:** Are there specific requirements for tiling around swimming pools?
**A:** Yes, pool tiling must comply with SANS 10107 for glazing and SANS 10400 for pool construction. Pool tiles must be frost-resistant, chemical-resistant (to chlorine/salt), and have appropriate slip resistance for pool surrounds. The pool shell must be properly waterproofed before tiling. Professional installation is strongly recommended.

**Q768:** What documentation should I keep for my tiling project?
**A:** Keep: original tax invoices (warranty proof), product specification sheets, batch numbers, waterproofing certificates (if applicable), photos of the installation process (subfloor, adhesive coverage, waterproofing), and any correspondence with suppliers or installers. This documentation is invaluable for warranty claims, insurance, and future property sale.

**Q769:** Do I need a professional tiler for my project to comply with standards?
**A:** Building regulations don't mandate that tiling must be done by a professional. However, waterproofing in wet areas should be done by a competent person (and certified for NHBRC-enrolled new builds). DIY tiling is permitted, but you're responsible for ensuring compliance with relevant standards. If you're unsure about waterproofing, hire a professional for that critical element.

**Q770:** What are the minimum falls required for bathroom floors?
**A:** Shower floors must have a minimum fall of 1:100 (1cm drop per 1m) toward the floor waste. General bathroom floors should have a minimum fall of 1:200 toward the floor waste. These falls ensure water drains properly and doesn't pool, which could lead to slipping hazards and waterproofing failure.

**Q803:** How much tile adhesive do I need per m²?
**A:** One 20kg bag of standard adhesive covers approximately 4-6m². For large format tiles requiring thicker adhesive beds, coverage drops to 3-4m² per bag. Always round up — it's better to have extra than run short.

**Q804:** How much grout do I need per m²?
**A:** A 5kg bag of grout covers approximately 10-15m² for standard 300x300mm tiles with 3mm joints. Mosaic tiles with many joints need more grout (5-8m² per 5kg). Large format tiles with minimal joints need less (15-20m² per 5kg).

**Q805:** How long before I can walk on new tiles?
**A:** Wait at least 24 hours before walking on newly laid tiles. Avoid heavy traffic and furniture for 48-72 hours. Wait at least 72 hours before exposing to water or wet cleaning.

**Q806:** How long before I can grout after laying tiles?
**A:** Wait a minimum of 24 hours after laying tiles before grouting. In cold or humid conditions, wait 48 hours. The adhesive must be fully set before grouting begins.

**Q807:** How soon can I use my shower after retiling?
**A:** Wait at least 72 hours after grouting before using a retiled shower. The adhesive and grout need time to fully cure. If epoxy-based grout was used, 24 hours may be sufficient — check manufacturer guidelines.

**Q808:** Can I tile over painted concrete?
**A:** No — paint must be removed or the surface properly prepared. Gloss paint prevents adhesive bonding. Remove loose paint, sand glossy surfaces, and apply a bonding primer before tiling. Best practice is to remove all paint.

**Q809:** Can I tile in the rain?
**A:** Outdoor tiling should not be done in rain — water affects adhesive bond and can wash out fresh grout. Indoor tiling can proceed during rain if the area is enclosed and protected. Humid conditions slow curing times.

**Q810:** How do I remove old tile adhesive?
**A:** Scrape off with a floor scraper, hammer and chisel, or a power scraper. For stubborn adhesive, use an angle grinder with a diamond cup wheel (wear safety gear). The surface must be clean and sound before new tiles are installed.

**Q811:** What's the best way to cut porcelain tiles?
**A:** Use a wet saw (electric tile cutter with water-cooled diamond blade) for straight cuts on porcelain. For small cut-outs, use an angle grinder with a diamond blade. Manual score-and-snap cutters work for ceramic but struggle with thick porcelain.

**Q812:** Can I use tile adhesive instead of grout?
**A:** No — adhesive and grout serve completely different purposes. Adhesive bonds tiles to the substrate. Grout fills the joints between tiles, allowing for movement and preventing water penetration. Never use adhesive as grout or vice versa.

**Q819:** Are dark tiles harder to keep clean?
**A:** Dark floor tiles show dust, pet hair, and water marks more readily than mid-tone or light tiles. However, they hide stains better than white tiles. If you have pets or a busy household, mid-tone tiles (warm grey, greige, taupe) offer the best balance — they hide dust and stains reasonably well.

**Q820:** What's the best grout colour for white tiles?
**A:** White grout creates a seamless look but stains easily. Light grey grout is the practical choice — it still looks clean and bright but hides discolouration much better. Dark grey or black grout creates a bold, graphic look (very popular with subway tiles) but shows every imperfection in installation.

**Q821:** Can I change my grout colour after installation?
**A:** Yes, cementitious grout can be stained with grout colourant (available in various colours). Alternatively, grout can be removed with a grout rake and replaced with a new colour. Both methods are labour-intensive but effective. Epoxy grout can't be stained — it must be removed and replaced.

**Q822:** What's the minimum thickness for floor tiles?
**A:** Residential floor tiles should be at least 8mm thick. Standard porcelain floor tiles are 9-11mm. Thinner tiles (7-8mm) are wall tiles and shouldn't be used on floors. Outdoor pavers are typically 20mm thick.

**Q823:** How do I stop my shower floor from being slippery?
**A:** Use mosaic tiles or small-format tiles (more grout joints = more grip), ensure R11+ slip rating, keep the floor clean (soap scum causes slipping), apply an anti-slip treatment if needed, and ensure adequate falls to the drain so water doesn't pool.

**Q824:** Can I use floor tiles on walls?
**A:** Yes — floor tiles can always be used on walls. They're actually better than wall tiles for areas needing durability (kitchen splashbacks behind stoves). The only downside is that floor tiles are heavier and thicker, which may not suit all wall applications.

**Q825:** Can I use wall tiles on floors?
**A:** No — wall tiles are not designed to withstand foot traffic. They're thinner, softer, and have lower abrasion resistance. Using wall tiles on floors will result in rapid wear, cracking, and potential safety hazards. Always use floor-rated tiles for floors.

**Q826:** How do I know if my subfloor is level enough for tiles?
**A:** Place a long straight edge (1.5m or 2m level) on the floor. Gaps between the straight edge and floor should not exceed 3mm over 2m. For large format tiles (600x1200mm+), the tolerance is even tighter — 2mm over 2m. If your floor exceeds these tolerances, it needs levelling.

**Q827:** Can I tile directly onto a concrete slab?
**A:** Yes, concrete is an ideal substrate for tiles provided it's: at least 28 days old, clean and free of loose material, level within tolerance, and properly primed. New concrete must be cured and primed before tiling to prevent moisture issues.

**Q828:** Do I need an expansion joint in my tiled floor?
**A:** Yes — expansion joints are essential in floors larger than 40m² (joints every 8-10m in each direction), at doorways, where tiles meet different materials, and along building structural joints. Without them, tiles can crack or tent due to thermal and structural movement.

**Q830:** How do I get rid of grout haze?

Apply, let sit 5-10 minutes, scrub with a non-scratch pad, and rinse thoroughly. Remove haze within 24 hours of grouting for easiest cleaning.

**Q837:** How long does it take to tile a bathroom?

**Q838:** How long does it take to tile a kitchen floor?
**A:** A professional tiler completes an average kitchen floor (15-20m²) in 2-3 days including preparation. A DIYer should allow 3-5 days. Additional time is needed for adhesive curing (24 hours) and grouting (after 24 hours).

**Q839:** What's the best time of year to tile?
**A:** In South Africa, tiling can be done year-round. Spring and autumn offer the best conditions — moderate temperatures and humidity for adhesive curing. Summer tiling is fine but work in cooler morning hours (adhesive sets fast in heat). Winter tiling is slower (adhesive takes longer to cure) but otherwise fine.

**Q840:** Can I live in my house while retiling?
**A:** Yes, but expect dust, noise, and limited access to rooms being worked on. Plan the project room-by-room so you always have functional bathroom and kitchen facilities. Cover furniture in adjacent rooms to protect from dust.

**Q841:** How do I protect newly tiled floors during construction?
**A:** Cover new tiles with breathable protection (not plastic which traps moisture) such as cardboard, hardboard, or specialist floor protection rolls. Avoid heavy traffic for 72 hours. When moving furniture back, lift — don't drag. Use felt pads under all furniture legs.

**Q842:** Can tiles be recycled in South Africa?
**A:** While dedicated tile recycling facilities are limited, broken and leftover tiles can be: crushed and used as drainage gravel, used for garden pathways and mosaic art projects, donated to schools or community projects, or disposed of at construction waste facilities. The environmental benefit of tiles comes from their longevity, not recyclability.

**Q843:** Are there any tile rebates or government incentives for home improvements?
**A:** There are currently no specific government rebates for tiling in South Africa. Some banks offer home improvement loans with favourable terms. If you're renovating for disability accessibility, certain tax deductions may apply — consult a tax professional.

**Q844:** Can I claim tiling costs on my home insurance?
**A:** Tiling as part of maintenance or renovation is not covered by insurance. However, if tiles are damaged by an insured event (burst geyser, fire, storm damage), your insurance may cover replacement. Keep your tile invoices and photos of the installation for claims purposes.

**Q845:** What's the difference between a "wet area" and "dry area" in tiling terms?
**A:** Wet areas are spaces with direct water exposure: inside showers, bath surrounds, and areas regularly soaked. Dry areas have minimal water contact: living rooms, bedrooms, hallways. Bathrooms are considered "moisture-prone" — not constantly wet but with regular humidity. Different waterproofing and tile specifications apply to each zone.

**Q846:** Can I tile my braai area with the same tiles as my patio?
**A:** Yes, provided the tiles are rated for outdoor use (frost-resistant, UV-stable, R11+ slip rating). Use full-body porcelain for the braai area as it's more resistant to staining from fat and food splatters. Coordinate the look by using the same or complementary tiles.

**Q847:** How do I know if my house's foundation is suitable for heavy tiles?
**A:** Standard residential foundations support porcelain tiles without issue. If you're concerned (older home, suspended timber floor, upper storey with timber structure), consult a structural engineer. Timber floors may need reinforcement before tiling — an uncoupling membrane helps but doesn't replace structural adequacy.

**Q848:** Can I use tiles in a basement or underground room?
**A:** Yes, but basements often have moisture issues. Test for rising damp and water ingress before tiling. Address any moisture problems first (damp-proofing, drainage). Use tiles with low water absorption (porcelain) and install a damp-proof membrane. Ensure adequate ventilation.

**Q849:** What should I do with leftover tiles?
**A:** Store leftover tiles for future repairs — keep them in their original boxes with the batch number noted. If you won't need them, donate to Habitat for Humanity, local schools, or community projects. Some ceramic studios accept tile offcuts for mosaic art projects. Only dispose of broken tiles as construction waste.

**Q850:** Can I paint over tiles instead of replacing them?
**A:** Yes, tiles can be painted with specialised tile paint or epoxy paint as a budget temporary solution. However, painted tiles are not as durable as new tiles — the paint can chip, peel, and wear, especially on floors. It's a cosmetic fix for a few years, not a permanent solution. Proper tile replacement is always the better long-term option.

**Q851:** How do I remove old silicone sealant?
**A:** Cut along both edges of the silicone bead with a sharp utility knife, then peel out the bulk. Remove residue with a silicone remover product or methylated spirits and a plastic scraper. The surface must be completely clean and dry before applying new silicone. A silicone removal tool makes the job easier.

**Q852:** What's the best way to drill a hole in a tile?
**A:** Use a diamond-tipped tile drill bit or hole saw. Mark the hole position with masking tape (prevents the drill from slipping). Start at a low speed with light pressure, using water to cool the bit. For large holes (pipes), use a diamond hole saw. Never use a hammer drill on tiles — the impact will crack them.

**Q853:** Can I use a pressure washer on outdoor tiles?
**A:** Yes, but use a low-to-medium pressure setting and keep the nozzle at least 30cm from the tile surface. High pressure can damage grout joints and force water behind tiles. Use a wide fan nozzle rather than a concentrated jet. Re-seal grout after pressure washing.

**Q855:** What's the difference between a bullnose tile and a trim profile?
**A:** A bullnose tile is a factory-produced tile with one or more rounded edges, used to finish exposed tile edges. A trim profile is an aluminium, PVC, or stainless steel strip installed over the cut edge of a tile. Bullnose tiles match the tile perfectly; trim profiles offer more colour options and work with any tile.

**Q856:** Can I use caulk instead of grout in some areas?
**A:** Flexible silicone sealant (caulk) should be used at all change-of-plane joints (where walls meet floors, corners, around baths and showers) instead of hard grout. This allows for movement and prevents cracking. Caulk should NOT replace grout in the field of the tile installation — use grout between tiles on the same plane.

**Q857:** How do I choose the right silicone colour?
**A:** Match the silicone to your grout colour for a seamless look. Most sealant manufacturers offer a range of colours matching popular grout shades. If an exact match isn't available, choose clear silicone (works with any colour) or white (for white/light grout). We stock common silicone colours.

**Q858:** Can I tile over a floor with underfloor heating?
**A:** Yes, tiles are the recommended flooring for underfloor heating due to their excellent thermal conductivity. Use flexible tile adhesive rated for heated floors. Never turn the heating on until at least 7 days after grouting, and then increase temperature gradually over several days.

**Q859:** How do I know if my bathroom has adequate ventilation?
**A:** A bathroom should have an extractor fan capable of 15 air changes per hour (ACH), or a window that opens for natural ventilation. Signs of inadequate ventilation include: persistent condensation on mirrors and tiles, mould growth, peeling paint on non-tiled surfaces, and musty smells. If you have these issues, upgrade your ventilation before retiling.

**Q860:** What's the best way to store leftover grout and adhesive?
**A:** Store in airtight containers in a cool, dry place. Grout powder can last 6-12 months if kept dry — discard if it becomes hard or lumpy. Adhesive powder stores similarly. Mixed adhesive or grout cannot be stored — discard any unused mixed material. Keep product labels for reference.

**Q861:** Can I use tiles from different boxes during installation?
**A:** Yes, and you should! Mix tiles from 3-4 different boxes as you install. This blends any slight batch variations and creates a more natural, varied appearance — especially important for stone-look and wood-look tiles with intentional variation (V3-V4).

**Q862:** How do I calculate linear metres of trim needed?
**A:** Measure all exposed tile edges that need trim — wall ends, window reveals, external corners, step edges, and floor-to-wall transitions. Add them together and add 10% for wastage and corners. Example: bathroom with two external corners (2m each) + window reveal (3 sides x 0.8m = 2.4m) + wall ends (1.5m) = 7.9m + 10% = 8.7m.

**Q863:** What is a "movement joint" and where do I need them?
**A:** A movement joint is a flexible gap filled with silicone sealant that allows the tiled surface to expand and contract. You need them: at perimeters (where floor meets walls), at doorways, at changes in substrate or backing material, every 8-10m in large floors, over existing structural movement joints, and where tiles meet fixtures.

**Q864:** Can I tile over an existing timber floor?
**A:** Not directly — timber expands, contracts, and flexes, which will crack tiles. Solutions: remove the timber and tile onto the concrete subfloor, or install a tile backer board over the timber with proper fixing, or use an uncoupling membrane. All three approaches require expertise — consult a professional.

**Q865:** How do I handle pipes and conduits when tiling?
**A:** For floor pipes: notch the substrate to recess pipes, then screed over them before tiling. For wall pipes: carefully measure and cut tile holes using a diamond hole saw. For conduit: ensure conduit is properly recessed into the wall before tiling. Never tile directly over raised pipes or conduit.

**Q866:** What's the best way to mark tiles for cutting?
**A:** Use a China marker (grease pencil) or permanent marker for dark tiles. For precise cuts, measure carefully and mark with a carpenter's square or combination square to ensure lines are perfectly straight. Double-check measurements before cutting — measure twice, cut once!

**Q867:** Can I use a circular saw to cut tiles?
**A:** No — standard circular saw blades are not designed for tile and will chip, crack, or shatter the tile. Use a wet tile saw (diamond blade with water cooling) or an angle grinder with a continuous-rim diamond blade. Always wear safety gear when cutting tiles.

**Q869:** What's the difference between "field" tiles and "feature" tiles?
**A:** Field tiles are the main, plain tiles used to cover the majority of a surface — they're typically more affordable and neutral. Feature tiles are decorative accent tiles (patterned, textured, mosaic, bold colour) used to create visual interest on specific areas. Many designs combine both for impact and budget control.

**Q870:** Can I use one tile for my entire house?

**Q871:** How do I create a feature wall without it looking dated in a few years?
**A:** Choose timeless feature elements: natural-look (never dates), subway tiles in creative layouts, textured 3D tiles in neutral colours, or stone mosaics. Avoid overly trendy patterns or colours that may fall out of fashion. A feature wall that complements rather than dominates the space will age better.

**Q872:** What's the most common reason tiling projects go over budget?
**A:** The top budget-busters are: inadequate subfloor preparation (screeding, levelling), discovering hidden problems (damp, structural issues), underestimating wastage (not ordering enough tiles), not accounting for sundries (adhesive, grout, trim, sealant), changing tile choice mid-project, and DIY mistakes requiring professional rectification.

**Q873:** How do I plan a tiling project timeline?
**A:** A typical bathroom retiling timeline: Day 1-2: remove old tiles and prepare substrate. Day 3: waterproof (if needed). Day 4-5: lay tiles. Day 6: let adhesive cure. Day 7: grout. Day 8-9: let grout cure. Day 10: seal grout and silicone joints. Add buffer days for unexpected issues. Don't rush the curing times.

**Q874:** Can I tile in a room with no windows?
**A:** Yes, but ensure adequate artificial lighting and ventilation. Use light-coloured tiles to maximise the available light. Install extractor fans in bathrooms. Use rapid-set adhesive to reduce curing time in poorly ventilated spaces. A dehumidifier can help manage moisture during curing.

**Q875:** How do I choose between a centre drain and a linear drain in my shower?
**A:** Centre drains are standard, affordable, and work with any tile size. Linear drains (long, narrow drains along one wall) allow for large format tiles on shower floors (no need for small tiles to accommodate slope) and create a minimalist, premium look. Linear drains cost more and require precise installation but offer superior aesthetics.

**Q876:** What's the best way to clean textured/anti-slip floor tiles?
**A:** Textured tiles trap more dirt in their surface grooves. Vacuum first to remove loose dirt, then scrub with a stiff brush and pH-neutral cleaner. A steam cleaner (safe for porcelain) helps lift dirt from texture. Rinse thoroughly. Regular maintenance prevents build-up that becomes harder to clean.

**Q907:** How do I calculate the fall for a shower floor?

**Q1000:** What's the single most important piece of advice for a DIY tiler?
**A:** Preparation is everything. Spend 80% of your time on subfloor preparation — making it clean, level, dry, and sound.
