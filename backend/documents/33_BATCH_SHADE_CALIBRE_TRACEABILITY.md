## Why this matters
Tiles are manufactured in batches. Colour tone, pattern distribution and size calibration can vary slightly between production runs. This is normal in tile manufacturing, but it must be managed correctly.

## Explain simply
"Batch, shade and calibre are details on the tile box label that help confirm whether boxes are from the same production run and size group. For one visible area, it is best to use the same batch/shade/calibre wherever possible."

## When to check labels
- Before installation.
- When collecting an order.
- When receiving delivery.
- Before mixing boxes from different pallets.
- When matching an old installation.

## Stop-work triggers
Tori must say "stop before installation" if:
- Boxes show different shade codes for one continuous area.
- Tiles look visibly different before fixing.
- Tile size or finish appears wrong.
- Customer is trying to patch an old area with a new batch.
- Installer discovers warpage, chips, cracks or pattern differences before installation.

## Blending advice
For tiles with shade or pattern variation, installers should open multiple boxes and blend tiles before fixing. This helps avoid patchy areas.

## V-rating explanation
Shade variation ratings describe how much natural colour or pattern variation the tile design may show.

Simple explanation:
- Low variation: more uniform look.
- Medium variation: natural but controlled variation.
- High variation: strong differences between pieces; layout and blending are important.

## Customer-facing script
"Please check all box labels before installation and dry-lay a few pieces in the room lighting. If anything looks wrong, stop and contact the branch before fixing the tiles."

## Complaint rule
If a customer complains after installation about shade or pattern, Tori must collect photos and invoice details, but must not decide outcome. Installed shade complaints are more complex because pre-installation checking is part of good tile practice.
