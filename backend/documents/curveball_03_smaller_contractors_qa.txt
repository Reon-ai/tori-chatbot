# CURVEBALL Q&A — PERSONA 3: SMALLER CONTRACTORS
## 200 Awkward, Strange & Unexpected (But Real) Questions and Answers

---

## 1. LEGAL LIABILITY, DISPUTES & CONSUMER PROTECTION

**Q: 1** I tiled a bathroom 8 months ago using tiles the client supplied herself. The tiles are now showing hairline cracks everywhere. She's threatening to sue me. Under the Consumer Protection Act, am I liable even though she chose and bought the tiles herself?
**A:** Under Section 55 of the CPA, the consumer (your client) has the right to safe and good-quality goods. However, liability depends on what failed: the tile or the installation. If the tiles themselves are defective (manufacturing fault), the retailer or importer — not you — is the "supplier" liable under Section 56. You are liable only for defective workmanship. Demand a formal inspection by the tile supplier's technical representative (SANS 1449 testing if necessary). Document your adhesive mixing ratios, trowel notch size, and whether you used a primer. If the cracks follow a pattern consistent with substrate movement, the supplier has no defence. Do not admit fault until the cause is independently determined.

**Q: 2** I installed tiles on a new house with full NHBRC enrolment. After 18 months, the screed cracked and took the tiles with it. The developer says it's the tiler's fault. The tiler says it's the screed. Does the NHBRC 5-year structural warranty cover the tiles, or just the structure?
**A:** The NHBRC warranty covers "major structural defects" for 5 years and "roof leaks" for 1 year — not cosmetic finishes like tiles. However, Section 10 of the NHBRC Act makes the home builder responsible for all defects. If the screed was inadequately cured (SANS 10400-J requires minimum 28 days for concrete screeds before tiling), the builder is at fault, and you have a valid third-party claim. The NHBRC may not pay for the tiles directly, but if the screed failure is due to builder negligence, the builder remains liable. Document the screed moisture readings, age at installation, and any written instructions you followed.

**Q: 3** I quoted a job as an "all-in" price including materials and labour. The client is now demanding a VAT invoice, but my turnover is below the compulsory VAT threshold of R1 million. Can she force me to register for VAT just because she wants a VAT invoice?
**A:** No. You cannot be compelled to register for VAT unless your 12-month rolling turnover exceeds R1 million (or R50,000 for voluntary registration, which is your choice). If you are not VAT-registered, you may not charge VAT or issue a tax invoice. You should issue a standard invoice clearly marked "VAT not charged — not a VAT vendor." If the client is a VAT-registered business wanting to claim input tax, that is her problem, not yours. If she withholds payment because you won't charge VAT, she is in breach of contract. However, if you are B-BBEE-registered and want to work with government or corporates, voluntary VAT registration may be strategically advisable.

**Q: 4** A client is claiming "emotional distress" because her kitchen tiles have a visible shade variation she says is ruining her mental health. She's threatening to sue for R200,000 in damages. Is emotional distress even a valid claim for tile shade variation under South African law?
**A:** Emotional distress is not a recognised head of damages for defective building work under South African common law or the CPA. Contractual damages are limited to patrimonial loss — actual financial loss caused by the defect, plus the cost of rectification. Under the CPA, a consumer can claim repair, replacement, or refund (Section 56), but not general damages for "emotional distress" unless there is a separate delictual claim involving intentional infliction of emotional shock (which requires psychiatric injury, not inconvenience). Shade variation is governed by SANS 1449 — there are acceptable tolerances. Offer a technical inspection and remind the client that frivolous litigation in the Small Claims Court can be referred to the Magistrate's Court with costs if the claim exceeds the R20,000 jurisdictional limit.

**Q: 5** I subcontracted a tiler who turned out to be an illegal immigrant. Home Affairs raided the site. Am I criminally liable for employing him even though he's an independent subcontractor, not my employee?
**A:** Yes. Section 38 of the Immigration Act 13 of 2002 makes it a criminal offence to "employ" an illegal foreigner, and "employ" is broadly interpreted to include contracting or subcontracting. You face a fine or imprisonment of up to 5 years (Section 49). As the main contractor, you have a legal duty to verify immigration status before engaging any worker — employee or subcontractor. Insist on certified copies of IDs or passports and work permits before any engagement. The defence of "I didn't know" is not valid if you failed to take reasonable steps to verify status. You may also face penalties under the Employment Services Act and labour inspectorate sanctions.

**Q: 6** A client asked me to sign a contract that says I'm liable for ANY defect for 10 years, regardless of cause. I signed it because I needed the job. Is that even enforceable?
**A:** A 10-year liability clause is not per se unenforceable, but it may be challenged under the Consumer Protection Act if the client is a natural person using the service for personal purposes — Section 48 prohibits unfair, unreasonable, or unjust contract terms. A blanket 10-year liability regardless of cause is likely an unfair term because it excludes the supplier's defences (e.g., consumer misuse, latent defects not discoverable, force majeure). The NHBRC only requires 5 years for structural defects. For non-structural tiling, industry standard is 1 year for workmanship. If the client is a company, the CPA does not apply, and the contract may be enforceable unless unconscionable at common law. Always read contracts before signing — if you already signed, seek legal advice on whether to challenge the term.

**Q: 7** My client's husband signed the contract, but she is the registered owner of the house. He's now refusing to pay, saying his wife never authorised him. Is the contract valid?
**A:** Under South African law, if the husband had ostensible authority (appeared to have the owner's authority — e.g., he was present at all meetings, gave instructions, lived on the property), the contract may be valid. However, Section 15 of the Matrimonial Property Act gives each spouse equal power to bind the joint estate in transactions for "household necessaries" — home improvement work typically qualifies. If they are married out of community of property, the husband's authority depends on her mandate. If she ratifies (accepts) the work by using it or making partial payment, she is deemed to have ratified the contract. In practice, always get both spouses to sign or obtain written authorisation from the registered owner before commencing work.

**Q: 8** I tiled a floor for a church. Now the minister says the tiles are "sending demonic vibrations" and he wants me to remove them at my cost. This is clearly irrational. What are my legal obligations?
**A:** A church is not a "consumer" under the CPA in the same way as a natural person — but the minister as an individual engaging your services may be. Regardless, your obligation is to provide work that is fit for purpose and free from defects as defined by objective standards (SANS 1449, SANS 10107). "Demonic vibrations" is not a recognised defect. Offer a technical inspection by an independent tiling assessor. If the tiles are technically sound, you have no legal obligation to remove them. If the minister persists, he must prove a defect on a balance of probabilities in court — a claim based on superstition will fail. Document everything and do not engage in the theological debate — keep it strictly technical.

**Q: 9** I used Sika products on a job. Six months later, the adhesive failed. Sika's technical rep did a site visit and blamed my application technique. I believe the product was stored incorrectly by the supplier before I bought it. Can I sue the supplier, or does Sika's technical report automatically make me liable?
**A:** A manufacturer's technical report is not legally binding — it is evidence that a court or tribunal will weigh alongside other evidence. Under the CPA Section 55, goods must be of good quality and safe. If the adhesive was stored outside the temperature range specified by the manufacturer before you purchased it, the supplier (retailer or wholesaler) is liable for selling compromised goods. You should request batch numbers, delivery dates, and storage records from the supplier. If you can prove the product was faulty at the time of supply, you have a claim against the supplier. The CPA also allows you to hold the manufacturer directly liable (Section 61). Preserve samples of the failed adhesive for independent laboratory testing — this is your strongest evidence.

**Q: 10** A client's dog was injured when it ran onto the site and stepped on a tile shard I'd left on the lawn. She's suing me for R35,000 in vet bills. Am I liable for a trespassing animal?
**A:** Under the actio de pauperie, a dog owner can sue for damage caused by their own animal only if another person intentionally or negligently caused the harm. Here, the dog was the trespasser, but you may still have a duty of care to keep your site safe — Section 8 of the Occupational Health and Safety Act imposes a general duty to maintain a safe environment, and this duty can extend to foreseeable visitors (including pets that commonly roam residential areas). However, the dog owner also has a duty to control her animal. Contributory negligence would likely reduce any award. Your best defence is to show you took reasonable steps to contain debris (skip bins, site fencing) and that the owner's failure to control the dog was the primary cause. Check whether your public liability insurance covers this.

**Q: 11** I completed a tiling job but didn't get the client to sign off because she was "too busy." Three months later, she claims the work is defective and demands a full refund. Can she do this if she never formally accepted or rejected the work?
**A:** Under South African contract law, acceptance can be express or implied. If the client used the tiled area for three months without complaint, she is deemed to have tacitly accepted the work. Section 56 of the CPA requires the consumer to notify the supplier of the defect within 6 months for the implied warranty to apply, but three months of use without objection weakens her position significantly. Demand a written list of specific defects and offer to rectify genuine defects per your workmanship warranty (typically 1 year for tiling). If she cannot identify specific defects and simply wants a refund, she has no legal basis. The absence of a formal sign-off does not negate the fact of tacit acceptance through use.

**Q: 12** I was tiling a rental property. The landlord approved the work, but the tenant is the one living there and is now complaining about dust and noise. The tenant says he'll sue me for "loss of enjoyment." Do I owe the tenant any duty of care, or only the landlord?
**A:** Your contractual relationship is with the landlord, not the tenant. However, under the law of delict, you owe a duty of care to anyone foreseeably affected by your actions, including tenants. A claim for "loss of enjoyment" would require proof of patrimonial loss (financial loss) or actionable interference with use and enjoyment under the neighbour law principle (sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). Reasonable construction noise during working hours is generally acceptable — the National Building Regulations and municipal bylaws regulate construction hours (typically 07:00–18:00 weekdays, 08:00–13:00 Saturdays, no work Sundays/public holidays). If you worked within these hours, the tenant has no claim. Communicate with tenants in advance and try to accommodate reasonable requests — this avoids disputes even if you have no legal obligation.

**Q: 13** I installed tiles in a client's house. Three weeks later, he was arrested for fraud and the Asset Forfeiture Unit seized the property. The state now says I was never paid and refuses to pay because the assets are frozen. How do I get my money?
**A:** This is a complex situation. If you had a builder's lien (retention right or ius retentionis) over the property, you may have had a right to retain possession until paid — but since you've already completed the work and left site, the lien is lost. You may be able to register a claim against the property in the Deeds Office as a "debtor's lien" if the work improved the property value, but this requires a court order. Your claim would rank as an unsecured creditor in the criminal asset forfeiture process. If the property is sold, you file a claim with the AFU or the Master of the High Court. In future, always include a "resolution of owner" clause requiring disclosure of any legal proceedings that may affect the property, and consider requiring a deposit or payment milestones to reduce exposure.

**Q: 14** A client told me her previous tiler used "black magic" and she wants me to perform a "cleansing ritual" before I start tiling, otherwise she won't pay. I agreed verbally because I needed the job. Now she says I didn't cleanse properly and won't pay. Can I sue for the tiling work even though I agreed to this nonsense?
**A:** Yes. Your claim is for the tiling work performed — the "cleansing ritual" is a separate matter. Under South African law, agreements involving superstitious conditions are not necessarily void unless they involve illegality (which cleansing rituals do not). The client's refusal to pay based on an unfulfilled superstitious condition is not a valid defence to payment for services actually rendered. You can sue in the Small Claims Court for the value of the tiling work. The cleansing ritual is not a recognised condition precedent under any law. However, avoid making claims about spiritual matters in your invoices — simply invoice for "preparation and tiling services as agreed."

**Q: 15** I verbally agreed to tile a floor for R12,000. The client now claims we agreed on R8,000. There's no written contract. How does a court decide who's telling the truth?
**A:** In the absence of a written contract, South African courts apply the parol evidence rule exceptions and look at the preponderance of probabilities — which version is more likely true? Factors considered include: market rates for similar work (expert evidence), your quote history with other clients, any text messages or WhatsApp messages that corroborate the price, whether a deposit was paid and in what amount, and the credibility of witnesses. In the Small Claims Court, the commissioner has discretion to determine a fair price if both versions are credible. Always insist on written quotes and acceptance. A WhatsApp message confirming the price is better than nothing. Going forward, use a standard quote template and get written acceptance.

**Q: 16** My client's estate agent sold the house while I was mid-job. The new owner says he never contracted me and won't pay the balance. The original client has disappeared overseas. Who pays me?
**A:** Your contract was with the original owner. The new owner is not automatically liable unless he expressly assumed the debt (novation) or the sale agreement included a clause making the purchaser responsible for improvements in progress. You may have a builder's lien (ius retentionis) over the property if you can prove the work improved the property and you haven't been paid — this attaches to the property and binds subsequent owners. However, you must act quickly: register a caveat against the property in the Deeds Office to preserve your claim, and issue summons against the original owner (who can be served outside South Africa under High Court Rule 4). If the property was transferred, your lien may still attach if it was registered before transfer.

**Q: 17** I tiled a floor for a client who died two weeks later. The executor of the estate says there's no money in the estate and I won't be paid. Is there anything I can do?
**A:** File a claim with the Master of the High Court as a creditor of the deceased estate. You have a limited time (usually 30 days from publication of the liquidation and distribution account, but file immediately) to lodge your claim. If the estate is insolvent, you rank with other unsecured creditors. However, if the estate has assets (the house itself, furniture, vehicles), the executor must sell assets to pay debts — you are entitled to your share proportionally. If the estate was already wound up before you knew of the death, you may be out of time, but the executor has a duty to advertise for creditors. In future, include a clause in your contract that death does not extinguish the debt and require payment within 7 days of invoice.

**Q: 18** I installed tiles on a new development. The developer has gone into business rescue under Chapter 6 of the Companies Act. I am owed R85,000. Do I have any priority as a subcontractor, or am I just another unsecured creditor?
**A:** Under business rescue, all creditor claims are temporarily frozen. As a subcontractor, you rank as a concurrent creditor — not a secured or preferent creditor unless you have a registered builder's lien or retention guarantee. However, if you can prove the work was completed and the property value was enhanced, you may have a case for a builder's lien (ius retentionis) which gives you a real right over the property — this survives business rescue and binds the rescue practitioner. Act immediately: notify the business rescue practitioner in writing of your claim within the time specified in the notice. If no notice is received, notify within the 30-day general period. Failure to notify on time extinguishes your claim against the rescued company.

**Q: 19** A client demanded I remove my company branding from my work vehicle before entering her estate because the estate rules prohibit "advertising." I'm not removing branding from my own vehicle. Can an estate legally ban branded vehicles?
**A:** Estate rules and homeowners' association rules are contractual in nature — they bind owners and, by extension, their invitees (including contractors). If the estate's conduct rules (registered with the Deeds Office under the Sectional Titles Act or HOA constitution) specifically prohibit commercial branding on vehicles within the estate, the rule is enforceable. However, such rules must be reasonable and cannot be applied discriminatorily. If other branded vehicles (delivery trucks, maintenance vans) are allowed, you have a case for selective enforcement. As a practical matter, ask the client to obtain temporary access permission for your branded vehicle, or park outside and transport materials. Do not unilaterally defy estate rules — you could be trespassed and banned, and the client may hold you liable for access-related delays.

**Q: 20** A client is claiming that my tiling caused her WiFi to stop working. She says the "density of the ceramic" is blocking her signal. She's withholding R15,000 in payment. How do I respond?
**A:** Ceramic tiles do not block WiFi signals — WiFi operates at 2.4GHz and 5GHz frequencies, which penetrate ceramic materials with minimal attenuation. Metal mesh in concrete screeds (for crack control) can interfere with signals, but that is a pre-existing condition unrelated to tiling. Offer to have an IT technician inspect the WiFi setup — at the client's cost if no link to your work is found. Her claim has no scientific basis and would fail in any court. Do not allow unsubstantiated claims to justify payment withholding. Issue a formal letter of demand for the outstanding amount and specify that the WiFi claim is not a recognised defect under the CPA or any building standard. If she persists, proceed to the Small Claims Court.

## 2. B-BBEE, CIDB & REGULATORY COMPLIANCE

**Q: 21** I'm a one-man tiling operation with an annual turnover of R450,000. Do I legally NEED a B-BBEE certificate to work on private residential jobs?
**A:** No. B-BBEE certificates are not legally required for private residential work. The B-BBEE Act 53 of 2003 applies to "measured entities" engaging in economic activity, and while all businesses are technically subject to the Act, B-BBEE certificates are only practically required when: (a) tendering for government contracts, (b) tendering for corporate contracts that have B-BBEE procurement policies, or (c) seeking CIDB registration above certain grades. For small contractors with turnover below R10 million, an Exempted Micro-Enterprise (EME) affidavit (sworn before a Commissioner of Oaths) is sufficient and carries automatic Level 4 or better status depending on black ownership. You do not need to pay a verification agency.

**Q: 22** I want to register with the CIDB so I can tender for government tiling jobs. What's the minimum grade I need, and how much does it cost?
**A:** The CIDB (Construction Industry Development Board) uses a grading system from 1 to 9. For general building works (GB), Grade 1 allows tendering for jobs up to R200,000, Grade 2 up to R550,000, Grade 3 up to R1.3 million, and so on. For tiling specifically, you may need a specialist works designation (SW — Specialist Works for tiling, flooring, etc.). Registration requires: proof of business registration (CIPC), tax clearance (SARS), B-BBEE affidavit or certificate, proof of works capability (previous contracts), and financial capacity. The application fee ranges from approximately R450 to R2,500 depending on grade. Annual renewal is required. Note: CIDB registration is mandatory for all government construction contracts above R30,000.

**Q: 23** I'm a white male contractor. My black partner owns 51% of the business but does no actual work. Can SARS or the B-BBEE commissioner challenge our B-BBEE status as a "fronting practice"?
**A:** Yes. Fronting practices are specifically criminalised under the B-BBEE Amendment Act (Section 130A). A fronting arrangement exists where black ownership is purported but the black participant does not: exercise voting rights, participate in management, receive economic benefits commensurate with their ownership, or have any genuine involvement. Penalties include fines, imprisonment of up to 10 years, and cancellation of any contracts obtained through the fronting arrangement. To avoid fronting allegations, your partner must genuinely participate — attend meetings, sign documents, make decisions, and receive profit distributions. Maintain a minute book and financial records proving genuine participation. SARS can also apply the substance-over-form doctrine for tax purposes.

**Q: 24** My turnover is R2 million per year. Do I qualify as an Exempted Micro-Enterprise (EME) for B-BBEE purposes, and what level do I get?
**A:** No. EME status applies to businesses with annual turnover below R10 million. At R2 million, you are an EME (since you are below the R10 million threshold). As an EME with 100% black ownership, you qualify for an automatic Level 1 (135% procurement recognition). With between 51% and 99% black ownership, you get Level 2 (125% recognition). With less than 51% black ownership or no black ownership, you get Level 4 (100% recognition). An EME affidavit sworn before a Commissioner of Oaths is all you need — no verification certificate required. Download the standard EME affidavit template from the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (DTIC) website.

**Q: 25** A municipality rejected my tender because my CIDB grade is only GB3 and the tender required GB5. Can I subcontract part of the work to a GB5 contractor and still tender as the main contractor?
**A:** No. The CIDB regulations prohibit "tendering outside one's grade" — you cannot use a higher-grade subcontractor to circumvent your own grade limitation. The principle is that the main contractor must have the demonstrated capacity to execute the work. However, you can partner with the GB5 contractor as a joint venture (JV) or consortium, provided the JV entity itself registers with the CIDB at the appropriate grade. The CIDB Standard for Joint Ventures sets out the requirements. Alternatively, build your grading capacity by completing smaller projects successfully and applying for grade escalation. Each successfully completed project in your grade contributes to your capability score.

**Q: 26** I registered my CIDB grade using my cousin's construction company as a reference for previous work. Is this fraud?
**A:** Yes. Providing false information to the CIDB constitutes fraud under the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004 and the CIDB Act itself. If discovered, your CIDB registration will be cancelled, you will be blacklisted from future registration for a period of up to 10 years, and you may face criminal prosecution. The CIDB conducts verification checks on references, including site inspections and client confirmations. If your cousin confirms the reference under false pretences, he may also be liable as an accomplice. Build your grade legitimately — even small residential jobs can be documented as "previous works" if you obtain completion certificates or client testimonials.

**Q: 27** I was told I need a "letter of good standing" from the Department of Labour to get CIDB registration. What does this mean, and how do I get it?
**A:** A letter of good standing from the Department of Employment and Labour (DEL) confirms that you are compliant with: (a) the Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act (COIDA) — registered and paid up, and (b) the Unemployment Insurance Act (UIA) — UIF contributions are current. You apply through the DEL website or at a labour centre. You need your COIDA registration number and UIF reference number. If you have no employees, you still need to show registration status (even if nil returns). This letter is required for CIDB registration at all grades because it demonstrates your compliance with labour laws — a key criterion for responsible contracting.

**Q: 28** I'm an unregistered contractor working "cash in hand" for private clients. What's the worst that could happen if SARS audits me?
**A:** Operating outside the tax system carries significant risks. If SARS determines you have been earning taxable income without declaring it, you face: (a) assessment for all undeclared income for the past 5 years (the standard limitation period, extendable to 10 years for fraud), (b) penalties of up to 200% of the tax evaded for intentional tax evasion, (c) interest on outstanding tax at the SARS prescribed rate, and (d) potential criminal prosecution under the Tax Administration Act for tax fraud. If you voluntarily disclose before SARS initiates an audit, the Voluntary Disclosure Programme (VDP) can reduce penalties significantly. The risk increases if your clients claim home improvement deductions and name you in their tax returns.

**Q: 29** A corporate client demands I be 51% black-owned AND 30% black female-owned to get a PO. My business is 51% black-owned but 0% black female-owned. Is this a legal procurement requirement or can I challenge it?
**A:** Under the B-BBEE Codes of Good Practice (Code Series 000, Statement 000), the minimum generic scorecard requires 25% black female ownership for full points, but this is for measured entities — not necessarily for procurement. Private sector procurement policies are set by individual companies and can exceed B-BBEE minimums. If the client is a government entity or state-owned enterprise, their procurement policy must align with the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act (PPPFA) and B-BBEE Act, which do include black female ownership as a specific criterion. You cannot challenge a private company's procurement policy unless it is discriminatory in a way that violates the Employment Equity Act or Constitution. For government work, the 30% black female ownership target is increasingly standard.

**Q: 30** I'm a foreign national with permanent residency. Can I register a construction business and get CIDB registration?
**A:** Yes, permanent residents can register businesses with CIPC and obtain CIDB registration. However, B-BBEE status is based on South African citizenship for black ownership recognition — permanent residency does not confer B-BBEE points for ownership unless you are a black South African or a black foreign national from an African country (some codes recognise African nationals). You must comply with all other CIDB requirements (tax clearance, COIDA, UIF, financial capacity). Your immigration status must be legal and current. Some government tenders may restrict eligibility to South African citizens, so read tender documents carefully.

**Q: 31** My accountant says I should register for VAT voluntarily even though my turnover is R600,000. Is this good advice for a small tiling contractor?
**A:** Voluntary VAT registration can be strategically beneficial if: (a) most of your clients are VAT-registered businesses that can claim input tax, making your prices effectively 15% cheaper to them, (b) you have significant input VAT on materials and equipment purchases that you can claim back, (c) you want to tender for government or corporate work that requires VAT invoices. However, the downsides include: quarterly VAT returns (administrative burden), the risk of SARS penalties for late or incorrect returns, and the fact that your prices become 15% more expensive to non-VAT-registered residential clients. For a small contractor doing mostly residential work for individuals, voluntary VAT registration is usually not advisable.

**Q: 32** I want to use my B-BBEE Level 4 status to subcontract from a large construction company on a government hospital project. The main contractor says I need to be "B-BBEE verified" by an accredited agency, not just an affidavit. Is this correct?
**A:** For public sector contracts above certain thresholds, the main contractor may require SANAS-accredited B-BBEE verification rather than a sworn affidavit. This is because the main contractor must report B-BBEE spend to the CIDB and the relevant government department, and some departments require verification certificates for all subcontractors to ensure auditability. However, if your annual turnover is below R10 million, you are legally an EME and an affidavit should suffice per the B-BBEE Codes. Check the specific contract conditions — if the main contractor's requirement exceeds what is mandated by law, you can challenge it through the CIDB or the relevant tender dispute mechanism. In practice, many main contractors insist on verification for risk management reasons.

**Q: 33** I was de-registered by the CIDB because I failed to renew on time. I have an active tender deadline next week. Can I get emergency reinstatement?
**A:** The CIDB allows late renewal within a grace period (typically 30 days after expiry) with a late renewal penalty. If you are beyond the grace period, you must submit a new application, which takes 21 working days for processing. There is no "emergency reinstatement" — the CIDB does not expedite applications for tender deadlines. This is a hard lesson: diarise your renewal date 60 days in advance. Some contractors set calendar reminders and allocate the renewal fee in their annual budget. If you miss a tender deadline due to lapsed CIDB registration, you cannot claim damages from the CIDB — maintaining registration is your responsibility.

**Q: 34** I'm a sole proprietor, not a company. Can I still get CIDB registration and B-BBEE certification?
**A:** Yes. Sole proprietors can register with the CIDB — you apply using your personal details (ID number) and business name. Your financial capacity will be assessed based on your personal financial statements or bank records. For B-BBEE, as a sole proprietor with turnover below R10 million, you are an EME and can use a sworn affidavit. If your turnover exceeds R10 million, you must be verified as a Qualifying Small Enterprise (QSE) by a SANAS-accredited agency. Note that sole proprietors have unlimited personal liability — if a claim exceeds your business assets, your personal assets (house, car) are at risk. Consider registering a private company (Pty) Ltd for liability protection.

**Q: 35** A client asked me to issue two invoices — one for the actual amount and one for a lower amount so they can claim a lower amount from their insurance. I refused. Can they force me to do this, and what would happen to me if I agreed?
**A:** They cannot force you, and you should absolutely refuse. Issuing a false invoice is fraud under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act (POCA) and common law. Both you and the client would be committing a criminal offence. If discovered, you face criminal prosecution, loss of CIDB registration, potential SARS penalties for false invoicing, and reputational damage. Your professional integrity is worth more than one job. If the client insists, terminate the relationship and report the request to their insurance company if you believe fraud is being attempted against the insurer. Document the request in writing.

**Q: 36** I'm subcontracting from a large builder who deducts 2.5% from every invoice for " Skills Development Levy." I'm a sole proprietor with no employees. Can he legally deduct SDL from my payments?
**A:** SDL is levied under the Skills Development Levies Act 9 of 1999 on employers with an annual payroll exceeding R500,000. If you are a sole proprietor with no employees, you are not an "employer" under the Act and no SDL is payable on your earnings. Your contractor cannot unilaterally deduct SDL from your payments unless this is a specific contractual term you agreed to (which would be unusual). If you have no employees, you should not be paying SDL. Challenge the deduction and request proof of SDL remittance to SARS. If the main contractor is deducting it as a general levy for their own SDL obligation, that is their cost, not yours — they are your customer, not your employer.

**Q: 37** I want to register for the NHBRC so I can build new houses. My turnover is R800,000. Is NHBRC registration compulsory for all new home construction?
**A:** Yes. The Housing Consumers Protection Measures Act 95 of 1998 makes NHBRC registration mandatory for any person who "sells" a new home, which includes building a home for a client. There is no turnover threshold — even a single new home triggers the requirement. Registration involves: completing the NHBRC enrolment form, paying the enrolment fee (based on the value of the home), providing proof of capability (financial, technical, management), and agreeing to the NHBRC code of conduct. Failure to enrol a new home can result in fines, prohibition from building, and criminal prosecution. The NHBRC provides the 5-year structural warranty to the homeowner.

**Q: 38** I'm a tiling subcontractor on a government school project. The main contractor hasn't paid me in 4 months but keeps saying the government hasn't paid him. Do I have a direct claim against the government under the amended Public Finance Management Act?
**A:** Under the Construction Industry Development Board's i-Tender system and the amended PFMA regulations, there are provisions for "paying subcontractors within 30 days" but these apply to the main contractor's obligations, not a direct right of action against the state. Your contractual relationship is with the main contractor, not the government. However, the CIDB Prompt Payment Regulations require main contractors to pay subcontractors within 30 days of receiving payment from the client, or within 30 days of certifying the work — whichever applies. You can file a complaint with the CIDB against the main contractor. If the main contractor is insolvent, you may have a claim against the state for retention money held, but not for the full contract value.

**Q: 39** I discovered that my competitor has CIDB registration but is using it to tender for jobs that are outside his grade. Should I report this, and to whom?
**A:** Yes. Tendering outside one's CIDB grade is a violation of the CIDB Act and can be reported to the CIDB's Fraud and Ethics Hotline or via the CIDB website. The CIDB investigates all reports of grade misrepresentation and can suspend or cancel registration, impose fines, and blacklist contractors. You can also report to the National Treasury's Tender Fraud hotline if the tender involves public funds. Whistleblowers are protected under the Protected Disclosures Act 26 of 2000. Provide as much evidence as possible: tender reference numbers, advertised grade requirements, and proof of the contractor's actual CIDB grade.

**Q: 40** I'm a small contractor who wants to work in the Western Cape but I'm based in Gauteng. Do I need to register separately with any Western Cape provincial body, or is my CIDB national registration sufficient?
**A:** CIDB registration is national and valid across all provinces. However, some municipalities and provincial departments have additional requirements: (a) municipal supplier database registration for local procurement, (b) provincial treasury supplier registration for provincial tenders, (c) Western Cape Construction Procurement Strategy requirements for preferential procurement. You must register on the Western Cape Supplier Portal (westerncape.gov.za) to tender for provincial work. Municipal work requires registration on that municipality's vendor database. These are in addition to, not instead of, CIDB registration.

## 3. LABOUR LAW, UIF, COIDA & CASUAL WORKERS

**Q: 41** I hire casual labourers for 2-3 days at a time when I have big jobs. Do I need to register them for UIF even if they only work a few days a month?
**A:** Yes. Under the Unemployment Insurance Act 63 of 2001, any "employee" who works more than 24 hours per month must be registered for UIF and contributions (1% employer, 1% employee) must be deducted and paid to SARS or the UIF. If your casual labourers work fewer than 24 hours per month, UIF registration is not legally required — but many contractors register them anyway for compliance simplicity. If they work more than 24 hours monthly, failure to register and contribute is an offence under Section 78 of the Act, punishable by a fine or imprisonment. Keep accurate attendance records to prove hours worked.

**Q: 42** One of my casual labourers was injured on site when a tile stack fell on his foot. He's not on my books as a permanent employee — he's paid cash daily. Am I covered by COIDA, or am I personally liable?
**A:** COIDA (Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act 130 of 1993) applies to all "employees," which includes casual, temporary, and part-time workers — even those paid cash daily. If you are registered with the Compensation Commissioner and paying assessments, the injured worker can claim from the Compensation Fund for medical expenses and disability benefits. If you are NOT registered with COIDA, you are personally liable for all damages arising from the workplace injury, and you commit a criminal offence under Section 81. The fact that he was "casual" and paid cash is irrelevant — the employment relationship is determined by substance (control, supervision, integration into operations), not form (payment method or contract label).

**Q: 43** I'm a one-man show. I hire a labourer to mix adhesive and carry tiles. Is he my employee or an independent contractor? What's the difference for SARS and labour law purposes?
**A:** The distinction depends on the "dominant impression test" — factors include: (a) control (do you supervise and direct his work?), (b) integration (is his work part of your business operations?), (c) economic reality (does he bear his own business risk?), (d) ownership of tools (does he use your tools or his own?), (e) exclusivity (does he work only for you?). A labourer who uses your tools, follows your instructions, works only on your sites, and is paid by the day or hour is almost certainly an employee, regardless of what you call him. SARS applies the same test. Misclassification exposes you to backdated PAYE, UIF, SDL, and COIDA liability, plus labour law protections (leave, notice, unfair dismissal claims).

**Q: 44** My labourer has been with me for 6 months, coming in 3-4 days a week. He now says he's a permanent employee and demands paid annual leave. Is he right?
**A:** Under the Basic Conditions of Employment Act (BCEA), an employee is entitled to 21 consecutive days of annual leave (or 1 day for every 17 days worked) after completing 12 months of continuous service. However, "continuous service" is broadly interpreted — if there was a reasonable expectation of ongoing work, the periods link together. More importantly, if he works regularly and has a reasonable expectation of continued employment, he may argue he is a permanent employee under the Labour Relations Act, entitled to all BCEA benefits including sick leave, family responsibility leave, and notice pay. At 6 months, he does not yet qualify for annual leave under the 12-month rule, but he does qualify for sick leave (1 day for every 26 days worked in the first 6-month cycle) and family responsibility leave (3 days per year).

**Q: 45** I fired a casual labourer because he kept arriving drunk. He's now taken me to the CCMA for unfair dismissal. But he's casual — can he even do that?
**A:** Yes. Any employee — permanent, temporary, or casual — who works more than 24 hours per month and has been employed for more than 4 months has the right to refer an unfair dismissal dispute to the CCMA under the Labour Relations Act. Even employees below the 24-hour/4-month thresholds can challenge dismissal if it was automatically unfair (e.g., based on race, gender, pregnancy, or trade union activity). For drunkenness, you must follow a fair procedure: investigate the allegations, give the employee a chance to respond, consider alternatives to dismissal (warning, suspension), and then decide. Summary dismissal for a first offence is generally unfair unless the misconduct is gross (e.g., endangering safety). At the CCMA, you'll need to prove both substantive fairness (the misconduct occurred) and procedural fairness (proper process was followed).

**Q: 46** I don't give payslips to my casual workers because they're paid cash. A labour inspector visited my site and said this is illegal. Is it?
**A:** Yes. Section 33 of the BCEA requires every employer to provide a written payslip to every employee at each pay interval, regardless of payment method or employment status. The payslip must include: employer name and address, employee name and job, period of payment, total money earned, deductions (with reasons), actual amount paid, and UIF contribution details. Failure to provide payslips is an offence punishable by a fine (Schedule 1 of the BCEA — up to R1,500 per employee for a first offence, escalating for repeat offences). The cash payment method is legal, but the payslip requirement is not waived. Implement a simple handwritten or printed payslip system immediately.

**Q: 47** My tiler is an independent contractor who invoices me monthly. Do I need to deduct PAYE from his payments, or is he responsible for his own tax?
**A:** If he is genuinely an independent contractor (uses his own tools, works for multiple clients, controls how and when he works, bears his own financial risk), you do not deduct PAYE. He is responsible for his own tax (provisional tax if applicable) and should invoice you with his tax number. However, if SARS determines he is actually your employee (the "deemed employee" provision under the Fourth Schedule), you become liable for PAYE, UIF, and SDL on all payments made to him. The key is the substance of the relationship. To protect yourself, ensure he provides invoices, uses his own equipment, works for other contractors too, and is registered for tax. Keep copies of his tax clearance and SARS correspondence.

**Q: 48** One of my workers is HIV-positive and is frequently sick. He's missing 2-3 days per month. Can I dismiss him for poor attendance, or is that automatically unfair discrimination?
**A:** Dismissing someone because of their HIV status is automatically unfair under the Labour Relations Act (Section 187) and constitutes unfair discrimination under the Employment Equity Act and the Code of Good Practice on Key Aspects of HIV/AIDS. However, dismissal for incapacity due to genuine poor performance or attendance is possible if you follow the incapacity procedure: (a) investigate the reasons for absence, (b) give the employee a chance to respond, (c) consider reasonable accommodation (e.g., lighter duties, adjusted hours), (d) obtain medical reports with the employee's consent, (e) explore alternatives to dismissal. If, after reasonable accommodation, the employee cannot perform the essential functions of the job, dismissal may be fair — but this is a high threshold. Consult the CCMA code of good practice on dismissal for incapacity. Never dismiss without following this procedure.

**Q: 49** I employ 3 permanent staff. Am I required to have a written employment contract with each of them, or is a verbal agreement enough?
**A:** Section 29 of the BCEA requires every employer to provide written particulars of employment to every employee within the first day of employment if they work more than 24 hours per month. These particulars must include: full name and address of employer, employee's name and occupation, place of work, date of employment, hours and days of work, remuneration and payment frequency, leave entitlements, notice period, and description of any council or sectoral determination. A formal written contract is not legally required (verbal contracts are valid), but the written particulars are mandatory. In practice, a comprehensive written employment contract protects both parties and prevents disputes. There is no cost to drafting a basic contract — Labour Department templates are available online.

**Q: 50** A labourer walked off my site with R4,200 worth of my tools. Can I deduct the value from his final wages?
**A:** No. Section 34 of the BCEA prohibits an employer from making any deduction from an employee's remuneration without the employee's written consent, except for statutory deductions (PAYE, UIF, garnishee orders). Deduction for loss or damage is only permitted if: (a) the loss or damage occurred in the course of employment and was due to the employee's fault, (b) the employer has followed a fair enquiry, (c) the deduction does not exceed 25% of the employee's remuneration, and (d) the employee has given written consent. Unilateral deduction constitutes an unlawful deduction, and the employee can report you to the labour inspector or CCMA. Your remedy is to lay criminal charges (theft) and/or sue for damages in the Magistrate's Court — not to withhold wages.

**Q: 51** I'm a small contractor. Do I need to register for COIDA if I have no employees and only use subcontractors?
**A:** If you genuinely have no employees and all workers are independent subcontractors, COIDA registration is not required for employee coverage — but the Compensation Commissioner may still require registration if you engage "deemed employees." However, your subcontractors should have their own COIDA registration. More importantly, if a subcontractor's employee is injured on your site, the question of who is the "employer" for COIDA purposes can be complex. The safest approach is to: (a) verify each subcontractor's COIDA letter of good standing before engagement, (b) include an indemnity clause in your subcontractor agreement, (c) consider public liability insurance that covers subcontractor-related incidents. If you directly control and supervise the subcontractor's workers, you may be deemed their employer for COIDA purposes.

**Q: 52** My employee was injured on site. I took him to a private hospital because the public hospital was too far. Can I claim these medical costs back from the Compensation Commissioner?
**A:** Under COIDA, the Compensation Commissioner determines which medical practitioner the employee may consult (usually a public hospital or designated doctor). If you unilaterally took him to a private hospital, the Commissioner may not reimburse those costs unless the private treatment was necessary due to an emergency and no public facility was accessible. You must report the injury to the Commissioner within 7 days (Form W.CL.2) and the employee must submit a claim (Form W.CL.1). Authorised medical costs are then paid directly by the Commissioner. Your upfront payment does not guarantee reimbursement. Always report injuries immediately and follow the Commissioner's procedures.

**Q: 53** I want to employ a Zimbabwean national who has a valid Zimbabwe Exemption Permit (ZEP). Do I need to do anything special regarding labour law and UIF?
**A:** Zimbabwean nationals with valid ZEPs (or its successor permits) are legally authorised to work in South Africa. You must: (a) verify the permit is current and valid for the employment period, (b) register them for UIF (they are entitled to UIF benefits), (c) register for COIDA (they are covered), (d) comply with all BCEA requirements. ZEP holders are not "illegal foreigners" under the Immigration Act. However, if their permit expires during employment, you must ensure renewal or terminate employment — continuing to employ someone whose permit has expired becomes a criminal offence. Keep copies of their permit on file.

**Q: 54** A female employee told me she's pregnant and asked for lighter duties. I'm a small contractor — I can't afford to carry someone doing half the work. What are my obligations?
**A:** Under the BCEA and the Code of Good Practice on the Protection of Employees During Pregnancy and After Childbirth, you must reasonably accommodate a pregnant employee's needs. This may include: lighter duties, adjusted hours, or temporary transfer to less physically demanding work. If no suitable alternative work exists, she may be entitled to leave (though maternity leave officially starts 4 weeks before birth). You cannot dismiss her because of pregnancy — this is automatically unfair under Section 187 of the LRA. However, the Code recognises that smaller employers may have limited options for accommodation. Document your efforts to find suitable alternative duties. If accommodation is genuinely impossible, consult the Department of Labour for guidance before taking any adverse action.

**Q: 55** I work with my brother. We split profits 50/50. Is he my employee, my partner, or a self-employed contractor? How does SARS see this?
**A:** SARS looks at substance, not labels. If you make all business decisions, provide all tools and materials, set the work schedule, and your brother simply works under your direction for a share of profits, he is likely your employee or partner in a partnership (not a company). If you are a sole proprietor and he contributes labour for a profit share, this may constitute a partnership under common law — meaning you are jointly liable for all business debts and he is taxed on his share of partnership profits (not as a salaried employee). If there is no partnership agreement and he simply helps out, he may be an employee with PAYE, UIF, and COIDA obligations. The cleanest structure is to formalise: either register a partnership, or employ him formally, or form a Pty Ltd with shareholders' agreement.

**Q: 56** A labour inspector arrived at my site unannounced. Can I refuse to let him in? What powers does he have?
**A:** No, you cannot refuse entry. Section 65 of the BCEA and Section 29 of the OHSA empower labour inspectors to: enter any workplace at any reasonable time without prior notice, inspect records (employment contracts, payslips, UIF records, COIDA registration), interview employees and employers, take photographs and samples, and issue compliance orders. Refusing entry or obstructing an inspector is an offence punishable by a fine or imprisonment. The inspector must produce his or her identity card upon request. Cooperate fully, answer questions honestly, and do not attempt to hide violations — if violations are found, ask for the correction period and comply promptly. Appeals against compliance orders go to the Labour Court.

**Q: 57** I want to give my employee a company phone for work purposes. Can I make him pay for personal calls, and can I take the phone back if he leaves?
**A:** You can require the employee to pay for personal calls provided this is agreed in writing (Section 34 BCEA — written consent for deductions). The phone remains company property and must be returned upon termination of employment — include this in the employment contract or a separate company property agreement. If the employee refuses to return the phone, you can deduct its reasonable value from his final pay (with written consent) or pursue civil recovery. Do not withhold his entire final pay — only the phone's depreciated value, and only with consent or a court order. Best practice: keep an asset register, have employees sign for all company property, and clarify acceptable use policies in writing.

**Q: 58** My employee asked me for a loan of R5,000. If I give it, can I deduct repayments from his wages?
**A:** Yes, but only with his written consent under Section 34 of the BCEA. The deduction for loan repayment must not reduce his remuneration below the minimum wage (if applicable) or below a reasonable living amount. The written consent must specify the amount, frequency, and duration of deductions. A loan agreement should be separate from the employment contract to avoid it being construed as part of remuneration. Do not charge interest unless you are registered as a credit provider under the National Credit Act — informal money lending can trigger NCA compliance requirements. Best practice: formalise the loan with a simple written agreement signed by both parties.

**Q: 59** I subcontracted a tiler who brought his own assistant. The assistant was injured. Who is responsible — me, the tiler, or both of us?
**A:** The primary employer for COIDA purposes is the person who pays the assistant's wages and has direct control over his work — likely the tiler. However, if you exercised significant control over the assistant (gave him instructions, set his hours, provided tools), you may be deemed a "deemed employer" under the OHSA and COIDA. The courts apply a "dual employer" doctrine in some cases. Your protection: (a) verify the tiler's COIDA letter of good standing before engagement, (b) include an indemnity clause in the subcontract, (c) avoid direct supervision of the subcontractor's employees, (d) ensure the subcontractor provides his own PPE. If the assistant sues, both you and the tiler may be joined as respondents.

**Q: 60** I discovered my employee is working for my competitor on weekends using skills I trained him in. Can I stop this? Do I own the skills I trained?
**A:** You cannot own "skills" — once trained, an employee can use those skills anywhere. However, you can restrict competing activities if: (a) the employee's contract includes a restraint of trade clause (limited in time and geographic scope), or (b) the employee is disclosing confidential information (client lists, pricing, techniques). Under common law, an employee owes a duty of good faith — working for a direct competitor using insider knowledge may breach this duty. But moonlighting itself is not unlawful unless it affects performance or involves a conflict of interest. If his weekend work is not affecting his weekday performance and he's not stealing clients, you have limited recourse. Include a reasonable restraint of trade in future employment contracts.

## 4. PAYMENT DISPUTES, BUILDER'S LIEN & SMALL CLAIMS

**Q: 61** I finished a tiling job and the client is refusing to pay the last R18,000, claiming "the finish isn't perfect." She can't specify any actual defects. What's my fastest legal remedy?
**A:** For R18,000 (below the R20,000 Small Claims Court limit), file a claim in the Small Claims Court — no lawyers required, low cost (R100–R200), and typically resolved within 6–8 weeks. Serve a formal Letter of Demand first (required by the Magistrates' Courts Act), giving her 14 days to pay. If she still refuses, file your summons at the Clerk of the Small Claims Court in your jurisdiction. Bring: your quote/contract, proof of completion, photos of the completed work, your invoices, and any correspondence. The commissioner will hear both sides and issue a judgment immediately. If she cannot identify specific defects, her defence will fail. A judgment in your favour is enforceable like any court order.

**Q: 62** I have a builder's lien over a property where I did R95,000 worth of tiling work and wasn't paid. The client has sold the property to a new buyer. Does my lien survive the sale, or do I lose it?
**A:** A builder's lien (ius retentionis) is a real right that attaches to the improved property and binds subsequent purchasers IF it was registered or the purchaser had notice of it. In South Africa, a builder's lien does not need registration to be valid against the original owner, but an unregistered lien is vulnerable to a bona fide purchaser for value without notice. If the sale has already been registered in the Deeds Office and the purchaser had no knowledge of your claim, your lien is likely extinguished — you become an unsecured creditor of the original owner. To prevent this, you should have registered a caveat against the property (a "pending litigation" caveat) at the Deeds Office as soon as payment was withheld. In future, act fast — register a caveat within days of non-payment.

**Q: 63** The client owes me R32,000. Can I take her straight to the Small Claims Court, or is it too much?
**A:** The Small Claims Court has a jurisdictional limit of R20,000. For R32,000, you have three options: (a) abandon the amount above R20,000 and claim only R20,000 in the SCC (you cannot split the claim), (b) issue a summons in the Magistrate's Court (where you can represent yourself for claims under R100,000, or use an attorney), (c) negotiate a settlement for R20,000 to use the SCC route. The Magistrate's Court is more formal but still accessible — you'll need to prepare a summons, serve it via the sheriff, and attend a pre-trial conference. For R32,000, the Magistrate's Court is your proper forum. You can represent yourself (no attorney required for claims under R100,000 in the Regional Court).

**Q: 64** A client paid me with a cheque that bounced. She then claimed her bank made an error. Two months later, I still don't have my money. What can I do?
**A:** A bounced cheque is not a payment — the debt remains outstanding. Write a formal letter of demand including a request for the original amount plus any bank charges you incurred. If she doesn't pay, sue in the Small Claims Court (if under R20,000) or Magistrate's Court. The fact that the cheque bounced is evidence of her inability or unwillingness to pay. You can also consider laying a charge of fraud if you believe she knew the cheque would bounce (Section 26 of the National Payment Systems Act prohibits knowingly drawing cheques without funds). However, criminal proceedings don't get you paid faster — civil action is your primary remedy. Going forward, require EFT payment or cash — avoid cheques from unreliable clients.

**Q: 65** I agreed to a 10% retention on a R85,000 job. The client is holding R8,500 for "12 months just to be safe." The contract didn't specify a retention period. What's the legal position?
**A:** Retention money should be governed by the contract. If no period is specified, the common law implies a "reasonable time" — for tiling work, this is typically the defects liability period specified in the agreement, or 3 months for minor defects. A 12-month retention for tiling work is excessive unless specifically agreed. Demand the release of the retention, giving the client a reasonable opportunity (14 days) to identify any defects. If no defects are reported, the retention must be released. For residential work with no formal contract, a retention clause is unusual — most residential contracts are "pay on completion." If the client refuses, sue for the retention amount. Note: under the JBCC (Joint Building Contracts Committee) agreements, standard retention is 5% with release at practical completion and final completion.

**Q: 66** A client is offering to pay me in cryptocurrency (Bitcoin) because she says her bank account is frozen. Should I accept this?
**A:** While cryptocurrency is not illegal in South Africa, accepting it as payment carries significant risks: (a) SARS treats crypto as an asset, not currency — you must declare the Rand equivalent at the time of receipt as taxable income, (b) the value can fluctuate wildly between receipt and conversion to Rand, (c) if the client's bank account is frozen due to legal proceedings, you may be drawn into those proceedings, (d) there is no chargeback protection if the transaction fails. The SA Reserve Bank has issued warnings about crypto payments. SARB is moving toward regulation but has not yet prohibited crypto transactions. If you accept, document the Rand value at the time of transaction, convert to Rand immediately, and declare it in your tax return. For a small contractor, the administrative complexity and risk likely outweigh any benefit.

**Q: 67** My client went into debt review under the National Credit Act. I'm owed R22,000. Does debt review mean I can't sue her anymore?
**A:** Once a consumer is under debt review, all legal action by credit providers is automatically stayed under Section 88 of the National Credit Act — this includes summonses, judgments, and enforcement. However, the stay only applies if your debt qualifies as a "credit agreement" under the NCA. If your tiling contract was a simple service contract with payment terms (not a credit facility, not a lay-by, not an instalment sale), you may not be a "credit provider" under the NCA and the stay may not apply to you. If the debt review court order has been granted, all creditors (including you) are bound by the restructured payment plan. If no court order has been granted yet, you may be able to apply to the magistrate's court to have your debt excluded from the debt review if it is not an NCA-regulated credit agreement. Consult an attorney — this is complex.

**Q: 68** I was tiling a flat in a sectional title scheme. The body corporate is now claiming I damaged the common property lift and wants me to pay R15,000. But I never used the lift — my labourers carried tiles up the stairs. Do I owe anything?
**A:** If you genuinely did not use the lift and have evidence (witness statements, photos showing tiles carried via stairs), you have no liability. However, the body corporate may claim you caused damage to the stairwell, entrance, or other common areas. Under Section 44 of the Sectional Titles Schemes Management Act, owners (and their invitees, including contractors) are liable for damage caused to common property. If other contractors were working in the building simultaneously, the body corporate must prove you caused the damage. Do not admit liability. Demand specifics: photographs of the damage, repair quotes, and evidence linking the damage to your work. If they cannot prove causation, their claim fails.

**Q: 69** I quoted a job based on a floor plan the architect provided. On site, the actual area was 40% larger. The client says I must honour the quote. Do I have to?
**A:** This depends on the wording of your quote. If your quote stated "as per architect's drawings" and the drawings were incorrect, you have a claim for a variation order — the contract was based on a fundamental misrepresentation (whether innocent or negligent) of the scope. Under the law of contract, if the basis of the contract (the floor area) was materially different from what was represented, you may be entitled to rescind the contract or claim the difference as a quantum meruit (reasonable value of the extra work). However, if your quote was a lump sum for the entire job regardless of area, you may be bound. Always quote per square metre with a clause stating: "Quantities based on provided plans. Variations in actual quantities will be priced at the agreed rate per m²."

**Q: 70** A client is threatening to post negative reviews about me on social media and HelloPeter unless I give her a R10,000 discount. Is this blackmail, and can I stop her?
**A:** This constitutes extortion under common law and potentially criminal intimidation under the Intimidation Act 72 of 1982 — threatening to publish damaging information to extract a financial benefit. If the reviews are false and published, you may also have a civil claim for defamation. Keep all threatening messages (screenshot WhatsApp, save emails) as evidence. Respond in writing stating you will not be extorted and any false reviews will be met with legal action. If she publishes false reviews, you can: (a) report the review platform requesting removal (most platforms have policies against extortion-based reviews), (b) issue a cease-and-desist letter, (c) sue for defamation if actual financial loss results. Do not give the discount — it legitimises the behaviour and encourages repeat demands.

**Q: 71** I'm owed R14,000 by a client who has now emigrated to Australia. Can I still sue him, and from where?
**A:** Yes. You can sue in a South African court and serve the summons outside South Africa under High Court Rule 4 (for the High Court) or Magistrates' Courts Act provisions for service outside the jurisdiction. You need the court's leave to serve outside the Republic, supported by an affidavit explaining why the South African court has jurisdiction (the contract was concluded here, the work was done here, the property is here). Service is effected through the South African diplomatic mission in Australia or via the Hague Service Convention. If you obtain judgment in South Africa, you can enforce it in Australia under reciprocal enforcement agreements. However, the legal costs may exceed R14,000 — consider whether the debt is worth pursuing. If he has assets in South Africa (the property you tiled), pursue those instead.

**Q: 72** A client wants to pay me in instalments over 6 months. I'm desperate for work and agreed verbally. He paid month 1 and 2, then stopped. What's my position?
**A:** A verbal instalment agreement is legally binding if the essential terms (amount, timing, number of instalments) were agreed. His failure to pay subsequent instalments is a breach of contract. You can sue for the full outstanding balance immediately — an instalment agreement typically contains an acceleration clause (if one instalment is missed, the full balance becomes due), or this is implied at common law. Issue a letter of demand for the full balance. If he doesn't pay, proceed to the Small Claims Court (if under R20,000). His partial payment (months 1 and 2) is evidence that an agreement existed and that he acknowledged the debt — this strengthens your case. In future, always put instalment agreements in writing.

**Q: 73** I signed a contract that says "payment within 30 days of invoice." It's been 60 days. Does the contract mean I have to wait indefinitely, or can I take action now?
**A:** The 30-day payment term is a contractual obligation. Once 30 days have passed without payment, the client is in breach of contract. You are not required to wait indefinitely. After the 30-day period expires: (a) issue a letter of demand giving 7 additional days, (b) if still unpaid, charge interest at the agreed rate (or at the SARS prescribed rate if no rate is specified), (c) file in the Small Claims Court or Magistrate's Court. Under the Prescribed Rate of Interest Act, you are entitled to interest on the debt from the date of breach. The National Credit Act may also apply if this is a credit transaction. Do not continue working for a client who has breached payment terms — suspend work until payment is brought current.

**Q: 74** A client's son — who is a lawyer — is demanding I reduce my invoice by 30% or he'll "make my life difficult with regulatory bodies." I did nothing wrong. How do I handle this?
**A:** This is intimidation and potentially extortion. A lawyer making threats to abuse regulatory processes for personal gain can be reported to the Legal Practice Council for professional misconduct. Document the threat in writing (save all messages). Respond professionally: state that your invoice reflects the agreed scope of work, that you stand by the quality of your work, and that you will not be intimidated into reducing a legitimate invoice. If he files a complaint with the CIDB, NHBRC, or SARS, cooperate fully — if you have done nothing wrong, the complaint will be dismissed, and you may have a claim for malicious prosecution or abuse of process. Issue your letter of demand and proceed to court if payment is not forthcoming.

**Q: 75** I worked on a house that was being renovated for resale. The developer says he'll pay me when the house sells. It's been 8 months. Do I have any rights?
**A:** "Pay when paid" clauses are generally unenforceable in South African construction law unless explicitly agreed in writing. You performed the work — you are entitled to payment according to the agreed terms. If no specific terms were agreed, payment is due on completion (or within a reasonable time). The developer's failure to sell the house is his business risk, not yours. Demand payment immediately. If he refuses, you may have a builder's lien over the property if the work improved its value — this could allow you to secure your claim against the property itself. File a caveat at the Deeds Office to protect your claim, then sue. Do not accept "pay when paid" terms without written agreement and a clear time limit.

**Q: 76** A client paid a R5,000 deposit but cancelled the job before I started because she "changed her mind." I've already ordered materials specifically for her job. Can I keep the deposit?
**A:** This depends on your contract terms. If your quote stated the deposit is non-refundable and that cancellation fees apply, you can retain the deposit to cover your losses (materials ordered, administrative costs, lost opportunity). If there was no cancellation clause, you can only retain the deposit to the extent of your actual losses — the cost of the ordered materials (if they cannot be used elsewhere) plus any direct costs incurred. You must refund any excess. Under the CPA, if the client is a consumer, an unreasonably high cancellation fee may be challenged as an unfair term. Calculate your actual loss and offer a refund of the balance. Materials that can be used on other jobs do not count as a loss.

**Q: 77** I'm a sub-contractor. The main contractor went into liquidation owing me R67,000. I heard the client (developer) still holds retention money. Can I claim directly from the developer?
**A:** Possibly. Under the common law of delegation, if the developer holds retention money that was deducted from the main contractor's payments, that money is trust property — it does not form part of the main contractor's insolvent estate. You may have a direct claim against the developer for the retention money if you can prove you performed the work for which retention was held. However, this requires legal action — an interdict or application to the liquidator to release the retention to you. The liquidator will assess all claims. You should also file a claim as a concurrent creditor in the liquidation. If the developer has not yet paid the main contractor's final account, you may have a direct claim against the developer under the "pay-direct" principle. Consult an attorney — this is complex insolvency law.

**Q: 78** A client says she overpaid me by R3,500 because she miscalculated the square metres. She's demanding a refund. I believe my measurements are correct. Must I refund?
**A:** No, not unless she can prove your measurements were incorrect. Your invoice is based on your professional measurement, which is part of the service. If there is a genuine dispute about the area tiled, offer to jointly remeasure with an independent witness present. If your measurement is correct, no refund is due. If her overpayment was genuinely accidental (e.g., she paid R25,000 instead of R21,500 because she added a zero), that is a different matter — payment by mistake may be recoverable under the condictio indebiti. But if she simply disputes your measurement, the burden is on her to prove you overcharged. Do not refund without verifying the claim. If she paid by EFT into your account, the money is presumed to be payment of a debt — she must prove it was paid in error.

**Q: 79** I quoted a job and the client accepted. Before starting, material prices increased by 18% due to a factory strike. Can I increase my price, or am I locked in?
**A:** Under South African contract law, a fixed-price quote is binding even if your costs increase — this is your commercial risk unless the contract contains a price escalation clause or a material adverse change clause. However, you may have two defences: (a) if the strike was unforeseeable when you quoted and makes performance radically more onerous (not just more expensive), the doctrine of supervening impossibility or the principle of "just and equitable" adjustment under the Consumer Protection Act may apply, or (b) if you can prove there was a mutual mistake about the basis of pricing. Neither defence is guaranteed to succeed. In future, include a clause: "Prices based on current material costs. Increases exceeding 10% will be passed on with supporting documentation."

**Q: 80** A client wants to pay me by doing barter — she'll give me her old car worth R20,000 in exchange for tiling work. Are there tax implications?
**A:** Yes. SARS taxes barter transactions at fair market value. The value of the car you receive is taxable income in your hands — you must declare R20,000 as income and pay tax on it (income tax and possibly VAT if you are registered). Similarly, the client must account for the disposal of the car. From a practical perspective, barter also creates complications: (a) you have no cash to pay suppliers or labourers, (b) transfer of ownership requires paperwork (NATIS documents for the vehicle), (c) the car's actual value may differ from the agreed value, (d) if the car has outstanding finance or is stolen, you receive nothing. SARS requires barter transactions to be recorded at fair market value in your books. It is almost always better to insist on cash payment.

## 5. LOAD SHEDDING, WEATHER & FORCE MAJEURE

**Q: 81** Stage 6 load shedding hit during a job. My adhesive skinned over and I had to throw away R4,800 worth of materials. The client says it's not her problem. Who pays?
**A:** Load shedding is a foreseeable risk in South Africa — Eskom has been implementing load shedding since 2007. A "force majeure" defence (supervening impossibility) is unlikely to succeed because load shedding is no longer an unforeseeable event. Unless your contract specifically allocates load shedding risk to the client, the cost typically falls on the contractor as a business risk. However, you can argue: (a) if the client caused the delay that pushed the work into load shedding hours, she may bear some responsibility, (b) if the adhesive failure was due to product defect rather than load shedding, the supplier may be liable. Your best protection going forward: use extended-open-time adhesives designed for hot climates, keep a generator on site for critical mixing and curing periods, and include a load shedding clause in your quotes.

**Q: 82** I included a "force majeure" clause in my contract covering load shedding. The client says load shedding is not force majeure because it's normal in South Africa. Is she right?
**A:** Force majeure clauses are interpreted strictly — they only cover events that make performance objectively impossible, not merely more difficult or expensive. Load shedding, while disruptive, rarely makes tiling impossible (generators exist, work can be rescheduled). South African courts have held that foreseeable events generally do not qualify as force majeure unless the contract explicitly defines them as such. If your contract specifically lists "Eskom load shedding of Stage 4 or higher" as force majeure with defined consequences (time extension, cost sharing), it may be enforceable as a contractual term. But a generic force majeure clause without specific reference to load shedding is unlikely to protect you. Be precise in your contracts: specify exactly what happens during load shedding (delays, generator costs, wasted materials).

**Q: 83** I'm tiling in Durban. A tropical storm flooded the site overnight and the screed I laid the day before is now waterlogged. The client wants me to continue. I say the screed needs to be redone. Who's right?
**A:** You are right. SANS 10400-J requires screeds to achieve specified strength and moisture content before tiling. A waterlogged screed has compromised integrity — tiling over it will result in adhesive failure, efflorescence, and potential tile debonding. Document the flooding with photographs and moisture readings. If the client insists on proceeding against your advice, provide written confirmation that you are proceeding under protest and that the client assumes all risk for future failures. Better yet, refuse to proceed — your professional reputation and potential liability outweigh the client's impatience. The cost of redoing the screed should be borne by the party responsible for site protection. If the client was responsible for securing the building and failed to do so, she bears the cost. If it was your responsibility, it is your cost.

**Q: 84** A KZN hailstorm broke the windows of the house I was tiling. Water damaged the substrate. The client says I should have protected the site. Am I responsible for weather damage to a house I'm working in?
**A:** Your responsibility for site protection depends on your contract. If you agreed to take possession of the site and protect it during construction, you may be liable for damage caused by your failure to secure the property (e.g., not boarding up openings). However, hailstorm damage is an act of God — unless your negligence exacerbated it (e.g., you removed protective covering and didn't replace it), you are not liable for the hail damage itself. The damaged substrate is a variation to your scope — charge for remediation as an extra. Check your contract's risk allocation clause. Standard JBCC agreements place the risk of loss or damage to the works on the contractor only after "practical completion" — before that, the employer carries the risk. If you have no formal contract, argue that weather damage is the owner's risk.

**Q: 85** I was tiling an outdoor patio in Cape Town. Winter rain started and I couldn't complete the grouting. The client says I should have checked the weather forecast and planned better. Is it my fault?
**A:** Checking weather forecasts is part of professional site planning. Cape Town's winter rainfall pattern (May–September) is predictable. Tiling outdoor areas during the rainy season without contingency planning is negligent planning. However, if an unseasonal storm occurred outside the normal rainfall period, that may be a genuine weather delay. Your obligation is to plan for foreseeable weather — if you proceeded with outdoor tiling in July without rain protection, the delay and any resulting damage are your responsibility. If the rain was unforeseeable, it may constitute a justifiable delay entitling you to a time extension. Always check 7-day forecasts before scheduling outdoor work, have tarpaulin or temporary roofing on standby, and include a weather delay clause in your quote.

**Q: 86** Load shedding destroyed my electronic laser level worth R18,000 when the power surge hit when it came back on. Can I claim this from Eskom?
**A:** Claims against Eskom for load-shedding-related damage are theoretically possible under the Eskom Act and the law of delict (negligence), but practically almost impossible to pursue for small amounts. Eskom has sovereign immunity protections and the cost of litigation would exceed your claim. Your remedy is through insurance — check whether your tools and equipment insurance covers power surge damage. If not, use surge protectors on all electronic equipment going forward. Alternatively, you may have a claim against your electricity provider if the surge was caused by their infrastructure failure. For a small contractor, the practical answer is: insure your equipment and use surge protection — do not waste time suing Eskom.

**Q: 87** The client wants me to work through load shedding using my generator. I don't have a generator. She's threatening to withhold payment for delays. Can she do this?
**A:** She can only withhold payment for delays if the contract required you to provide power continuity or if you guaranteed specific completion dates without load shedding exemptions. In the absence of such terms, load shedding delays are at the employer's risk (unless the contract says otherwise). You are not required to own a generator — it is a business investment, not a legal obligation. However, if load shedding causes delay beyond the contractual completion date, the client may have a claim for late completion damages (liquidated damages) if the contract provides for them. Without a liquidated damages clause, her claim is limited to actual proven losses. Do not allow a client to unilaterally impose new requirements (like generator provision) not in the contract.

**Q: 88** I quoted a 5-day job. KZN floods closed the roads and I couldn't access the site for 3 days. The client says I must absorb the delay costs because "floods are normal in KZN." Are they?
**A:** KZN floods are increasingly common but each event's severity varies. If the roads were officially closed by authorities, the delay is due to supervening impossibility — you could not lawfully access the site. This entitles you to a time extension without penalty. However, the cost of the delay (accommodation, idle labour, equipment hire) depends on your contract. If the contract is silent, each party bears its own delay costs — you absorb your costs, the client absorbs theirs. If the client claims floods are "normal," this undermines a force majeure defence but does not negate your right to a time extension for events outside your control. Municipal road closures are official events — request the closure notice from the municipality as evidence.

**Q: 89** I'm tiling in the Karoo where temperatures regularly exceed 40°C. The adhesive is skinning before I can bed the tiles. The supplier says I'm applying it too slowly. I say the product isn't suitable for these conditions. Who's right?
**A:** Both factors may be at play. SANS 10400-J and adhesive manufacturer specifications define application temperature ranges (typically 5°C to 35°C for most cementitious adhesives). Above 35°C, adhesives cure too quickly due to rapid moisture evaporation. The supplier should specify temperature limitations on the technical data sheet. If the product is rated only to 35°C and you're working at 40°C, the product is not suitable — the supplier should recommend a high-temperature or extended-open-time formulation. However, you also have a duty to adjust your technique: smaller sections, dampening the substrate, working in morning/evening hours. If the product is technically rated for the temperature and you're working too slowly, the fault is yours. Always check the TDS before specifying products for extreme climates.

**Q: 90** My generator was stolen from site during load shedding. The security guard the client hired was asleep. Who bears the loss?
**A:** The allocation of risk for theft depends on your contract. If the client provided site security as part of her obligations, and the theft occurred due to the guard's negligence, the client may be liable for your loss — but only if the contract specifically made her responsible for site security and your equipment. If there was no such agreement, tools and equipment are typically the contractor's risk. Your remedy is through insurance (tools-in-transit or all-risk contractor's insurance) or a claim against the security company (if you can prove negligence). The sleeping guard is evidence of negligence — obtain an incident report and consider a civil claim against the security company. Going forward, include a clause: "Contractor's tools and equipment remain contractor's risk unless client provides secure storage facility."

## 6. MATERIAL ISSUES, SUPPLIER DISPUTES & CLIENT-SUPPLIED TILES

**Q: 91** I installed 400m² of tiles from a well-known supplier. After installation, visible shade variation appeared across the floor — some tiles are noticeably darker. The client has rejected the entire installation. The supplier says it's a "batch variation" and not their fault. Who's liable?
**A:** Under SANS 1449, tiles are classified by shade variation rating: V1 (uniform), V2 (slight variation), V3 (moderate variation), and V4 (substantial variation). The box must indicate the variation rating. If the tiles were sold as V1 or V2 and the actual variation exceeds that rating, the supplier is liable under the CPA Section 56 (implied warranty of quality). If the tiles were correctly labelled V3 or V4, and you installed them without mixing batches or randomising the layout, the installation fault may be yours — high-variation tiles must be blended during installation to avoid clustering. Demand the batch numbers and variation rating from the supplier. If the supplier sold mismatched batches as one batch, they are liable. If the variation is within the stated rating, the liability depends on installation technique.

**Q: 92** A client bought tiles from a clearance sale — end-of-range stock at 60% off. I installed them. Six months later, 30% of the tiles have cracked. The supplier says "clearance stock, no warranty." The client blames me. What's my legal position?
**A:** Clearance sale goods are still subject to the CPA Section 56 implied warranty unless the consumer was explicitly informed of the specific defect and agreed to purchase despite it. "No warranty" signage does not override the CPA for defects that were not disclosed at the time of sale. If the tiles have a latent manufacturing defect (e.g., improper firing causing internal stresses), the supplier remains liable. If the tiles were sold as "seconds" with visible defects clearly marked, and the cracking is related to those known defects, the client assumed the risk. Your liability is limited to installation defects — improper adhesive, no expansion joints, substrate movement. Document your installation method (adhesive type, trowel size, expansion joints) and request independent assessment. If installation was correct, the supplier is liable even for clearance stock.

**Q: 93** I bought tiles on my trade account for a job, but the client cancelled. I can't return the tiles because the supplier has a "special order, no returns" policy. Can I use these tiles for my own home renovation without causing problems?
**A:** Using job-purchased materials for personal use is ethically questionable and potentially creates tax and accounting issues. From a legal perspective, once you purchase the tiles, they are your property — you can use them as you wish. However: (a) if you claimed the cost as a business expense and then used them personally, SARS may consider this a taxable fringe benefit or disallow the deduction, (b) if the supplier gave you a trade discount based on business volume and discovers you used stock personally, they may revoke your trade account, (c) if you originally quoted the client for these specific tiles and she paid a deposit, you may have a duty to refund the deposit or use the tiles for her job if she reinstates. The safest approach: return the tiles if possible (negotiate with the supplier), or sell them to another client at cost, or declare personal use in your tax records.

**Q: 94** I specified and supplied imported Italian tiles for a luxury job. Due to the Rand/Euro exchange rate crash, my cost went up 35% between quote and delivery. The client won't pay the increase. Can I cancel the contract?
**A:** Unless your contract contains a price escalation or currency fluctuation clause, you are bound by your quoted price. Exchange rate fluctuations are a normal commercial risk for contractors who import materials. Cancellation would be a breach of contract, exposing you to damages. Your options: (a) absorb the loss and complete the job (commercial decision), (b) negotiate a price increase with the client (voluntary), (c) if the increase makes performance genuinely impossible (not just unprofitable), argue supervening impossibility — but this is a high threshold. Going forward: quote imported materials at "prevailing exchange rate" with a specific rate locked in for acceptance within a limited period, or add a currency fluctuation clause covering movements exceeding a specified percentage.

**Q: 95** The client supplied her own tiles and adhesive. I installed them using my normal technique. The tiles are now debonding. She's blaming my installation. The adhesive she bought was the cheapest available and clearly unsuitable for the tile type. Am I liable?
**A:** Your liability depends on whether you had a duty to advise against the unsuitable adhesive. As a professional tiler, you have a duty of care that includes advising the client if the materials supplied are clearly unsuitable for the application. If you installed the tiles knowing the adhesive was wrong without warning the client, you share liability. If you advised against the adhesive in writing and the client insisted, your liability is minimised — but you may still bear some responsibility for proceeding with known unsuitable materials. The best practice: always provide a written memo specifying required adhesive type and state: "Installation warranty is void if client-supplied materials do not meet these specifications." If you advised against the adhesive, produce the written advice as your defence.

**Q: 96** I used 25kg adhesive bags that had been stored in my open trailer for 3 weeks. Rain got into some bags. I used them anyway because they looked fine. Now the tiles are lifting. Is this my fault or the supplier's?
**A:** This is your fault. Cementitious adhesives are hygroscopic — moisture ingress starts the curing process prematurely. Even if the bags looked fine externally, internal clumping indicates moisture absorption. Manufacturer specifications universally require storage in a dry, covered area. Using water-damaged adhesive constitutes negligence, and any resulting failure is your liability. You cannot claim against the supplier for your failure to store the product correctly. The adhesive technical data sheet (TDS) specifies storage conditions — ignorance is not a defence. Going forward: store all cementitious products in a dry, weatherproof area, and discard any bag that shows signs of moisture exposure. The cost of replacement adhesive is far less than the cost of retiling.

**Q: 97** A supplier sold me tiles as "first grade" but the batch number indicates they are "commercial grade" (second grade with more lenient tolerances). I only discovered this after installation. Do I have a claim against the supplier for the difference in value?
**A:** Yes. Selling commercial grade tiles as first grade is misrepresentation under the CPA Section 41 (false, misleading, or deceptive representations). You have a claim for the difference in value between first grade and commercial grade tiles, plus any consequential damages (removal and replacement costs if the grade difference caused defects). First grade and commercial grade tiles have different tolerances under SANS 1449 — commercial grade allows greater size variation, warping, and surface defects. If these tolerances caused visible defects in the installation, the supplier is liable for all remediation costs. Preserve the batch records, packaging, and your invoice as evidence. Report the supplier to the National Consumer Commission if they refuse to rectify.

**Q: 98** I ordered 200m² of tiles. The supplier delivered 180m² and said the balance is "on back order" with no confirmed delivery date. The client wants me to start immediately. Can I cancel the order and buy from another supplier, charging the original supplier for the price difference?
**A:** If the supplier failed to deliver the full quantity within the agreed timeframe (or a reasonable time if no timeframe was specified), they are in breach of contract. You are entitled to: (a) cancel the entire order and claim damages (the price difference from an alternative supplier), or (b) accept the 180m² and claim damages for the shortfall. Under the CPA Section 47, if the supplier cannot deliver within a reasonable time, you can cancel and receive a full refund. Document the original delivery promise, the shortfall, and your costs of cover (purchasing elsewhere). If the alternative supplier charges more, the original supplier is liable for the difference plus any direct costs (additional delivery charges, delay costs).

**Q: 99** The client wants me to install tiles he bought from a bankrupt supplier's auction. The tiles have no box markings, no batch numbers, and no SANS 1449 compliance label. Should I install them?
**A:** No. SANS 1449 requires tile packaging to bear the manufacturer's mark, grade, size, and batch number. Tiles without these markings may be defective, non-compliant, counterfeit, or recalled stock. Installing non-compliant tiles exposes you to liability under the NBR (National Building Regulations) and the CPA. If the tiles fail, you cannot trace the batch or claim against a manufacturer. Refuse the installation and document your refusal in writing, citing SANS 1449 compliance requirements. If the client insists, require him to sign a waiver acknowledging: "I accept full responsibility for tile quality and release the contractor from all liability for tile failure." Even with a waiver, your professional reputation is at risk — consider whether the job is worth it.

**Q: 100** I used a rapid-set adhesive on a large format tile (600x600mm) job because the client was in a rush. The tiles are now showing lippage and hollow spots. The adhesive manufacturer says their product is not suitable for large format tiles. Am I liable, or is the supplier who sold me the adhesive liable?
**A:** You are primarily liable. As the tiling professional, you have a duty to select the correct adhesive for the tile format and application. Rapid-set adhesives have shorter open times and are generally not recommended for large format tiles because they don't allow sufficient time for proper bedding and adjustment. The supplier's liability depends on whether they knew your application and recommended the wrong product. If you simply bought the adhesive without asking for advice, the supplier has no liability. If you told them you were tiling 600x600mm tiles and they recommended rapid-set, they share liability for negligent advice. Under the CPA, suppliers who give advice have a duty of care to ensure the advice is appropriate. Document what you told the supplier at the time of purchase.

## 7. ASBESTOS, SAFETY & SITE HAZARDS

**Q: 101** I was ripping out old floor tiles in a 1970s building and discovered they contain asbestos. I immediately stopped work. The client is furious and says I'm being dramatic. What are my legal obligations under the Asbestos Regulations?
**A:** You did exactly the right thing. The Asbestos Regulations of 2020 (under the OHSA) are strict: any work that may disturb asbestos-containing materials (ACMs) must cease immediately. You are legally required to: (a) notify the client in writing of the discovery, (b) ensure no further disturbance of the ACMs, (c) secure the area to prevent exposure, (d) advise the client that only a registered asbestos removal contractor (accredited by the Department of Employment and Labour) may remove asbestos-containing materials. Removing asbestos yourself is a criminal offence — Section 24 of the Regulations prohibits unlicensed asbestos work. The client must engage an approved asbestos contractor at their cost. Do not resume work until the asbestos has been properly removed and clearance certificates issued. Your refusal to continue is a legal obligation, not a choice.

**Q: 102** I disturbed asbestos tiles before realising what they were. I've now been exposed. What are my rights, and what should I do immediately?
**A:** Immediate steps: (1) Stop work and leave the area, (2) Do not brush off or vacuum dust from your clothing — wet-wipe or shower and change, (3) Report the exposure to the client and your COIDA administrator within the required timeframe, (4) Seek medical assessment and request documentation of the exposure, (5) Ensure the area is properly decontaminated by a licensed asbestos contractor. Under COIDA, you may have a claim for occupational disease if you develop an asbestos-related condition in future — document the exposure date, duration, concentration (if known), and type of asbestos. Asbestos-related diseases have long latency periods (10–40 years). Keep this documentation for life. If the client knew or should have known about the asbestos and failed to warn you, you may also have a civil claim for negligence.

**Q: 103** A client wants me to remove vinyl asbestos tiles myself to "save money" and promises not to tell anyone. She'll pay me double my normal rate. Can I do this if I wear a mask?
**A:** Absolutely not. This is a criminal offence under the Asbestos Regulations 2020. Only contractors holding an asbestos removal permit from the Chief Inspector of the Department of Employment and Labour may remove asbestos-containing materials. Individual penalties include fines and imprisonment. A dust mask (P1/P2) is completely inadequate for asbestos — removal requires full PPE including respiratory protective equipment (RPE), negative pressure enclosures, and HEPA-filtered vacuums. The client's promise of secrecy is meaningless — if you or a future occupant develops mesothelioma, the prosecution will trace the removal to you. The double payment is not worth a criminal record or your life. Refuse the job and report the request to the Department of Employment and Labour.

**Q: 104** I discovered asbestos sheeting used as an underlay beneath floor tiles in a commercial building. The building owner says it was "grandfathered in" because it predates the asbestos ban. Does that mean I can work around it?
**A:** No. There is no "grandfathering" exception that allows unsafe work with asbestos. The Asbestos Regulations 2020 apply to all asbestos-containing materials regardless of installation date. If your work will disturb the asbestos sheeting (e.g., removing tiles adhered to it, drilling into it, cutting it), you must follow the full regulatory framework: notification, risk assessment, licensed removal, and disposal at an approved hazardous waste facility. The fact that the asbestos was legally installed in the 1960s does not exempt it from current safety requirements. The building owner is responsible for asbestos management and must engage a licensed assessor to identify all ACMs before any renovation work begins. Do not proceed until a licensed asbestos professional has assessed and managed the risk.

**Q: 105** A client asked me to install tiles over old asbestos floor tiles rather than removing them. Is encapsulation (tiling over asbestos) legal and safe?
**A:** Encapsulation (covering asbestos-containing materials without disturbing them) is a recognised asbestos management strategy under the Asbestos Regulations, provided: (a) the ACMs are in good condition (not friable, not damaged), (b) the encapsulation method is approved (e.g., sealing with a penetrating encapsulant before covering), (c) the covering material will not require penetration of the ACM layer in future, (d) an asbestos register is maintained for the building recording the location of the covered asbestos. However, you must not disturb the asbestos tiles during preparation — no sanding, grinding, or aggressive surface preparation. If the existing tiles are loose or damaged, encapsulation is not appropriate. You are not legally required to be a licensed asbestos professional to encapsulate, but you should document the encapsulation and advise the client to update the building's asbestos register.

**Q: 106** My labourer cut his hand deeply with a tile cutter. I don't have a first aid kit on site. Am I legally required to have one, and what are the consequences of not having it?
**A:** Yes. Section 8 of the OHSA and General Safety Regulation 3 require every employer to provide first aid equipment (a first aid box) and ensure a trained first aider is available. For construction sites, Regulation 3 specifies the contents of the first aid box based on the number of employees. Failure to provide first aid equipment is an offence under the OHSA — the Department of Employment and Labour can issue a contravention notice and fine. More seriously, if the lack of first aid exacerbated the injury (e.g., infection, greater blood loss), you face increased COIDA liability and potential civil claims for negligence. Immediate action: buy a regulation-compliant first aid kit today, and send at least one employee on a first aid course (St John or Red Cross). The cost is minimal compared to the liability.

**Q: 107** I was working on a site where the client refused to provide water for mixing adhesive because "she's on a water restriction." I had to use collected rainwater. The adhesive didn't perform correctly. Who's at fault?
**A:** Adhesive manufacturers specify potable (clean) water for mixing. Rainwater may contain contaminants (dust, organic matter, acidic pH) that affect adhesive performance. The client has a duty under the OHSA Section 8 to provide adequate welfare facilities including clean water for construction purposes. If she refused, she breached this duty and contributed to the defective work. However, you also had a duty to ensure the water was suitable — using visibly contaminated water without testing was negligent. The liability is likely shared. Going forward: specify in your contract that the client must provide clean, potable water for construction use, or bring your own water and price it into the job. Do not compromise on water quality — it affects adhesive performance, screed curing, and grout integrity.

**Q: 108** A client has a rooftop terrace that needs tiling. There's no edge protection (railing) and a 4-storey drop. I refused to work without scaffolding and guardrails. The client says I'm "being difficult." What does the law say?
**A:** You are absolutely correct, and the client is endangering your life. The Construction Regulations 2014 (under OHSA) require edge protection (guardrails, toe boards, or safety nets) for any work at height where a fall could occur. Regulation 8 specifically addresses fall protection — working on an unguarded 4-storey edge without fall protection is a criminal offence by the employer (you, if self-employed, or your client as the site controller). You have a legal right to refuse unsafe work under Section 14 of the OHSA, and it is unlawful for any person to discriminate against you for exercising this right. Do not work on that roof without proper edge protection, scaffolding with guardrails, or a harness and lifeline system. Report the client to the Department of Employment and Labour if they pressure you to work unsafely.

**Q: 109** I discovered a gas leak while preparing a floor for tiling. The client knew about it and "thought it wasn't serious." Can I refuse to work until it's fixed?
**A:** Yes. A gas leak is a serious hazard that creates risk of explosion, fire, and asphyxiation. Under Section 14 of the OHSA, every employee (including self-employed contractors) has the right to leave any workplace where they have reasonable grounds to believe there is an imminent danger to their health or safety. You are legally protected from any adverse action for exercising this right. Document the hazard, notify the client in writing that work is suspended until the leak is repaired by a registered gas practitioner (per PER regulations), and request a gas compliance certificate before resuming. Do not attempt to fix the leak yourself — gas work must be done by a registered gas installer. If the client refuses, report to the local gas supplier and the Department of Employment and Labour.

**Q: 110** I don't provide hard hats to my 2-person team because we're "just tilers" and not on a construction site with falling objects. Is this legally acceptable?
**A:** It depends on the site-specific risk assessment. The OHSA requires PPE (Personal Protective Equipment) to be provided where hazards exist. On a standard residential tiling job with no overhead work, falling object risk is minimal, and hard hats may not be required. However, on construction sites with multiple contractors, scaffolding, or overhead work, hard hats are mandatory under the Construction Regulations. Other PPE is non-negotiable for tilers: safety boots (steel toe — tile shards are sharp), safety glasses (tile cutting creates flying fragments), knee pads, and dust masks (silica dust from cutting is a serious respiratory hazard). Silica dust exposure is regulated under the OHSA Hazardous Chemical Substances Regulations — cutting tiles dry without extraction or respiratory protection is a violation.

**Q: 111** I was tiling a bathroom and hit an electrical cable embedded in the screed. There was no electrical plan showing the cable. The client is blaming me. Am I liable for the repair?
**A:** If there was no electrical plan or indication of the cable's presence, you are not liable — you had no way of knowing the cable was there. The client (or previous contractor) is responsible for providing accurate information about embedded services before tiling work begins. However, if the cable was visibly marked (e.g., warning tape above it in the screed) or you drilled deeper than necessary for the tiling application, you may share liability. Under standard contract terms, hidden services are the employer's risk unless the contractor was negligent in failing to detect obvious signs. Document the absence of plans and warning markers. The repair cost should be borne by the client or their insurer.

**Q: 112** A client wants me to remove lead paint from walls before tiling the bathroom skirting. Is this safe, and do I need special training?
**A:** Lead paint removal is hazardous and regulated. Inhaling or ingesting lead dust causes serious neurological damage. The Hazardous Chemical Substances Regulations under OHSA require: risk assessment, air monitoring if disturbance is significant, respiratory protection (P3 filter minimum), containment, and disposal as hazardous waste. For small areas (like skirting boards), wet scraping with HEPA vacuum cleanup may be acceptable with proper PPE. For large areas, a licensed hazardous materials contractor is required. You should not undertake lead paint removal without understanding the risks and proper procedures. The paint's lead content can be tested with a simple swab kit (available at hardware stores). If lead is present and the area is extensive, refer the client to a specialist.

**Q: 113** My labourer is refusing to work in a basement with visible mould on the walls. He says it's a health hazard. Is he right, and can he legally refuse?
**A:** Yes, he is right, and yes, he can legally refuse. Mould produces mycotoxins and spores that cause respiratory illness, allergic reactions, and in severe cases, neurological effects. Under Section 14 of the OHSA, an employee may refuse to work in conditions they reasonably believe pose an imminent danger to their health. Mould remediation should be conducted by a professional with appropriate PPE (respirators, gloves, containment) before any other work proceeds. You, as the employer, also have a duty of care under Section 8 of the OHSA to provide a safe working environment. Do not force employees to work in mould-contaminated areas. The client must engage a mould remediation specialist. Document your refusal and the health basis for it.

**Q: 114** I was asked to tile a floor in a room that had recently been fumigated for termites. The fumigator said it's "safe after 24 hours." It's been 36 hours. Is it actually safe for me and my labourers?
**A:** Fumigation safety depends on the chemical used, the concentration, ventilation, and the fumigator's certification. "Safe after 24 hours" is a generalisation — some fumigants (like sulfuryl fluoride) require aeration periods of 6–24 hours plus ventilation, while others may require longer. The fumigator should provide a clearance certificate confirming safe re-entry levels measured with appropriate detection equipment. Do not take their word for it — request the written clearance certificate with gas concentration readings. Under the OHSA Hazardous Chemical Substances Regulations, you have a right to information about chemical hazards in the workplace (Section 41). If no clearance certificate is available, refuse entry and demand independent air quality testing. Your health is not worth risking for a day's work.

**Q: 115** I don't have contractor's all-risk insurance. I keep telling myself I'll get it "next month." What's the real risk if something goes wrong on a job?
**A:** The risk is potentially business-ending. Without insurance, you are personally liable for: (a) damage to the client's property (e.g., flooding an apartment below), (b) injury to third parties (e.g., a tile falling and hitting someone), (c) theft or damage to your tools, (d) defective work claims that require complete removal and redoing at your cost. A single liability claim can exceed R500,000 — enough to bankrupt a small contractor. Contractor's all-risk insurance typically costs R500–R2,000 per month depending on turnover and coverage limits. Public liability insurance (covering third-party injury and property damage) is the minimum you should have. Tool insurance and professional indemnity insurance are also advisable. Operating without insurance is gambling your entire livelihood on nothing going wrong — eventually, something will.

**Q: 116** I'm tiling a restaurant kitchen. The client wants the work done during business hours while the kitchen is operational. Is this even legal from a health and safety perspective?
**A:** Tiling in an operational commercial kitchen creates multiple hazards: wet floors (slip risk), dust contamination of food (health violation), noise disruption, and potential chemical contamination from adhesives and sealers. The Foodstuffs, Cosmetics and Disinfectants Act and municipal health bylaws prohibit conditions that may contaminate food. Working during operational hours likely violates these regulations. The Occupational Health and Safety Act also requires a safe working environment — an active kitchen with hot oil, sharp knives, and wet floors is not safe for tiling work. Advise the client that work must be done after hours or during a scheduled closure. If they insist, require written confirmation that they accept all liability for health code violations and safety incidents.

**Q: 117** I found a bee hive inside a wall cavity while preparing for tiling. The client wants me to just "seal it in" with the tiles. Is this safe and legal?
**A:** No. Sealing a live bee hive into a wall is inhumane, creates a future hazard (if the bees find another exit into the building), and may violate municipal bylaws regarding pest control. African honey bees can be defensive and dangerous in large colonies. The correct procedure is to engage a registered bee removal specialist who will either relocate the colony (preferred) or exterminate it if relocation is not possible. Do not attempt DIY bee removal — multiple stings can be fatal, especially to those with allergies. The cost of professional bee removal is the client's responsibility — it is a pre-existing condition unrelated to your tiling scope. Document the discovery and suspend work until the hive is safely removed.

**Q: 118** I was tiling near a swimming pool. My labourer can't swim and nearly fell in. Am I required to provide water safety training or life jackets for poolside work?
**A:** Under Section 8 of the OHSA, you must assess risks and take reasonable measures to protect employees. For poolside work, this includes: warning signs, temporary barriers around the pool edge, non-slip footwear, and ensuring workers are aware of the hazard. Life jackets are not typically required for poolside tiling work but may be reasonable if the pool is deep and workers carry heavy materials near the edge. The most practical protection is a temporary fence or barrier keeping workers at a safe distance from the pool edge. If a worker cannot swim, do not assign them to work within fall distance of the pool without additional safeguards. Document your risk assessment and safety measures.

**Q: 119** I accidentally drilled into a water pipe while preparing a substrate. Water flooded the apartment below. The body corporate says I'm liable for all damage — the flooded apartment, the common area ceiling, and their legal costs. Could this really cost me hundreds of thousands?
**A:** Yes. Water damage in multi-unit buildings cascades rapidly — the apartment below, electrical systems, common areas, and mould remediation can easily exceed R200,000–R500,000. If you don't have public liability insurance, you are personally liable for all reasonable repair costs. Your defence depends on whether the pipe was marked on available plans (if not, the body corporate or owner may share liability) and whether you took reasonable precautions (stud finders, service detection equipment). However, as the contractor on site, the primary liability falls on you for damage caused by your actions. The body corporate's claim for legal costs is only valid if they were reasonable and necessary — you can challenge excessive costs. This is exactly why contractor's liability insurance is essential.

**Q: 120** I was offered a job tiling a pharmaceutical cleanroom. The client says I need to wear a full bunny suit and go through decontamination. Do I charge extra for this, and is it my responsibility to provide the suit?
**A:** Yes, charge extra — cleanroom tiling requires significantly more time, specialised materials (low-VOC adhesives and grouts approved for cleanroom environments), and PPE that reduces worker efficiency. The bunny suit and decontamination protocol are the client's obligation to provide as part of site-specific safety and contamination control requirements — they know their cleanroom classification and protocols. Your responsibility is to comply with the protocols once provided. The additional costs include: extended preparation time, specialised materials (often 2-3x standard material cost), reduced labour productivity (suiting up/de-suiting), and potentially background checks or medical clearances required for pharmaceutical facility access. Quote this as a separate line item: "Cleanroom protocol compliance and specialised materials."

## 8. TOOLS, THEFT & INSURANCE NIGHTMARES

**Q: 121** My R85,000 trailer full of tools was stolen from outside a client's property overnight. I had no insurance. The client says it's not her responsibility because I chose to park there. Is she right?
**A:** Generally, yes. Unless your contract specifically required the client to provide secure parking or storage for your tools, the risk of theft is yours as the contractor. Your tools and equipment are your business assets — protecting them is your responsibility. The client's duty of care does not extend to safeguarding your property unless she agreed to it or her negligence created the risk (e.g., she told you to park in a known high-crime area when secure parking was available). Without insurance, you bear the full loss. Your only potential recourse is: (a) claiming from the client's household insurance if her policy covers contractors' tools (unlikely), (b) SAPS case number for possible recovery (statistically unlikely). This is a hard lesson: tools and equipment insurance is non-negotiable for contractors.

**Q: 122** I have tools insurance, but the insurer is refusing my claim because the tools were stolen from an "unattended vehicle" and the policy requires tools to be in a "locked, alarmed vehicle." My vehicle was locked but not alarmed. Do I have any recourse?
**A:** Read your policy carefully — insurance policies are interpreted strictly, and if the policy requires an alarmed vehicle, you must comply. However, you can argue: (a) the term "alarmed" is ambiguous — does it mean a factory-fitted alarm or any alarm? (b) the insurer did not draw this exclusion to your attention clearly when you took out the policy (Duty of Fair Treatment under the Financial Sector Regulation Act), (c) a locked vehicle without an alarm is still "secure" and the insurer's requirement is unreasonable. Lodge a complaint with the insurer's internal complaints department first, then escalate to the Ombud for Short-Term Insurance if unresolved. In future, read every exclusion in your policy and comply with all security requirements — insurers deny claims on technicalities.

**Q: 123** A client's domestic worker stole my tile cutter while I was on lunch break. The client caught her and she confessed, but she has no money to repay. Can I claim from the client's insurance?
**A:** The domestic worker's theft creates a complex liability question. If she was the client's employee acting within the scope of her employment (even wrongfully), the client may be vicariously liable — but theft is generally outside the scope of employment. The client's household insurance typically covers the client's own contents, not contractors' tools. Your claim is against the thief (who has no assets) or potentially the client if she was negligent (e.g., she knew the worker had a history of theft and gave her access to your tools). Your best remedy is your own tools insurance. If you have none, a civil claim against the domestic worker will likely be unenforceable. Report the theft to SAPS — a criminal conviction may support a future civil claim if she acquires assets.

**Q: 124** I bought a second-hand tile cutter from another contractor. It turned out to be stolen property. The police seized it. Can I claim the purchase price back from the seller?
**A:** If the seller knew or should have known the item was stolen, you can claim the purchase price back on the grounds of fraud or breach of implied warranty of title. If the seller was also an innocent purchaser, your claim is more difficult — you both suffered from the theft. The general rule is that stolen property remains the property of the original owner, and no title passes, even to an innocent purchaser (nemo dat quod non habet — no one gives what they do not have). Your remedy is against the thief (if caught) or the seller (if they warranted clear title). Going forward: always request proof of purchase or ownership when buying second-hand equipment, buy from reputable sources, and keep receipts. Buying stolen goods, even innocently, results in total loss.

**Q: 125** I'm storing tools at a friend's workshop. His premises were burgled and my tools were stolen. His insurance says they only cover his own tools, not mine stored there. Is this correct?
**A:** Unless your friend specifically listed your tools on his policy or has a "goods in trust" extension, his insurance will not cover your tools. Most business contents policies cover only the policyholder's property. Your tools are your responsibility to insure — "goods in trust" or "tools anywhere" coverage on your own policy is what you need. Some insurers offer "tools in transit and storage" extensions that cover tools while at temporary locations. Without this, you have no insurance claim. You may have a claim against your friend for negligence if he failed to take reasonable security measures (e.g., didn't lock the door, left the alarm off) — but suing a friend is rarely worth the relationship damage. The lesson: never assume someone else's insurance covers your property.

**Q: 126** My truck with all my tools was hijacked in Johannesburg. The insurer says they'll only pay "market value" which is 40% less than replacement cost. Can I challenge this?
**A:** Insurance policies typically pay the lesser of market value or sum insured. "Market value" means the value of the tools at the time of loss (depreciated), not the cost of new replacements. You can challenge the insurer's valuation by: (a) obtaining independent valuations from tool suppliers showing current replacement prices, (b) providing maintenance records showing the tools were in excellent condition, (c) checking if your policy has a "new for old" or "replacement cost" extension. If the policy clearly states "market value" and you agreed to it, your challenge is limited to disputing the insurer's depreciation calculation. When renewing your policy, consider upgrading to replacement cost coverage — it costs more but eliminates the depreciation shortfall.

**Q: 127** I hired equipment (a floor grinder) from a hire company. It was stolen from site. Their contract says I'm liable for the full replacement value regardless of fault. Is this enforceable?
**A:** Hire contracts typically include strict liability clauses making the hirer responsible for loss or damage during the hire period. These clauses are generally enforceable under South African contract law because: (a) they are clearly stated in the contract, (b) the hire company has no control over the equipment once it leaves their premises, (c) insurance options are usually offered at hire. However, if the theft was due to the hire company's negligence (e.g., they delivered to the wrong address, failed to provide security instructions), you may have a counterclaim. If you were forced to sign the contract without opportunity to read it (contract of adhesion), you may challenge the term as unfair under the CPA. Going forward: always take the insurance option offered by hire companies — it is cheaper than replacing stolen equipment.

**Q: 128** My employee was using my company vehicle and crashed it while under the influence. The vehicle is a write-off and my tools inside were destroyed. My insurance won't pay because he was drunk. What now?
**A:** Standard vehicle and tools insurance policies exclude loss caused by driving under the influence — this exclusion is absolute and non-negotiable. Your claim will be denied. Your remedy is against the employee: you can claim damages from him for the vehicle and tools (suing an employee is legally permissible but practically difficult if he has no assets). You can also report him to SAPS for drunk driving. From an employment law perspective, this is gross misconduct warranting immediate dismissal (after a fair disciplinary hearing). Going forward: implement a zero-tolerance alcohol policy for company vehicle use, conduct random breathalyser tests, and ensure your insurance policy is fully understood by all employees. Consider whether the employee's state of intoxication was obvious and you should have prevented him from driving.

**Q: 129** I have public liability insurance but not "works in progress" insurance. A fire in an adjacent apartment spread and damaged tiles I'd just installed. The client wants me to replace them at my cost. Am I covered?
**A:** Public liability insurance covers damage you cause to third parties — it does not cover damage to your own work or materials caused by external events. The fire in the adjacent apartment is not your fault, so public liability does not respond. "Works in progress" or "contract works" insurance would cover this — it protects the work and materials on site against fire, theft, storm, and other perils. Without it, you are not insured for this loss. The client's building insurance may cover the fire damage — he should claim from his insurer. If the fire was caused by a third party's negligence, that third party is liable. Do not absorb the cost of damage you did not cause — advise the client to claim from his building insurer or the negligent party.

**Q: 130** I bought a "lifetime warranty" tile cutter online from an international supplier. It broke after 18 months. The supplier has disappeared. Is there any way to enforce the warranty?
**A:** Enforcing a warranty against a foreign supplier who has disappeared is practically impossible for a small contractor. If you paid by credit card, you may have a chargeback claim with your bank (time limits apply — usually 120 days from discovery). If the supplier had a South African distributor or agent, they may honour the warranty under the CPA (implied warranty applies to all goods sold in South Africa, even imported). If you imported it yourself directly, the CPA still technically applies (the supplier is the "importer" under Section 1), but enforcement against a defunct foreign entity requires international litigation — not viable for a tool worth a few thousand Rand. The lesson: buy tools with local warranty support from established suppliers. "Lifetime warranties" from unknown online sellers are worthless if the seller vanishes.

## 9. SANS STANDARDS, TECHNICAL SPECIFICATIONS & INSTALLATION FAILURES

**Q: 131** A client wants tiles laid on a screed that was poured 5 days ago. The architect says it's fine. I know SANS 10400-J requires 28 days. If I refuse and lose the job, can I be penalised? If I agree and it fails, am I liable?
**A:** You are 100% correct to refuse. SANS 10400-J (Table 2) requires a minimum 28-day curing period for concrete screeds before tiling, with moisture content below a specified percentage (typically tested with a CM meter or calcium carbide test). Tiling on a 5-day-old screed will result in adhesive failure, efflorescence, and tile debonding because the screed has not achieved sufficient strength and is still releasing moisture. If you proceed against your professional judgment, you are fully liable for the failure — the architect's approval does not transfer liability to him. His approval was negligent (potentially exposing him to liability too), but your liability as the installer is primary. Stand your ground. Provide the client with the SANS reference and offer to perform a moisture test as proof. Losing one job is better than being sued for a R100,000 redo.

**Q: 132** I tiled a shower floor with 600x600mm tiles and didn't use a waterproofing membrane because the client said "the screed guy already waterproofed." Six months later, the ceiling below is water-damaged. Who's liable?
**A:** SANS 10400-P (Drainage) and SANS 10400-J require waterproofing in wet areas (showers, baths) regardless of what the screed contractor did. As the tiling contractor, you have a professional duty to verify that adequate waterproofing exists before tiling. If you tiled over an unverified or inadequate waterproofing layer, you share liability for the water damage. The waterproofing is part of the tiling scope in wet areas — passing responsibility to the screed contractor is not a valid defence unless you verified his work and have written confirmation. Going forward: always specify waterproofing as part of your quote for wet areas, conduct a flood test (24-hour ponding test) before tiling, and obtain written confirmation of waterproofing from the previous contractor or do it yourself.

**Q: 133** I installed tiles with 2mm grout joints as specified by the client. The tiles have expanded slightly and the joints are now virtually non-existent, causing chipping. The client blames my installation. Is 2mm too narrow?
**A:** SANS 1449 and good tiling practice recommend minimum grout joint widths based on tile type, size, and substrate stability. For ceramic tiles, 2mm is generally acceptable for walls but may be too narrow for floors subject to foot traffic and thermal movement. For large format tiles (400mm+), wider joints (3–5mm) are recommended to accommodate substrate movement and tile expansion. If the tiles were installed with no expansion joints perimeters (SANS 10400-J requires expansion joints at perimeters and at intervals not exceeding 4.5m in each direction for interior floors), the failure is a design/installation issue, not just the joint width. Your liability depends on whether you advised the client that 2mm joints on floors carry a risk. If you simply followed her instruction without professional advice, you share liability.

**Q: 134** A tile supplier's technical rep recommended a specific adhesive for a glass mosaic pool installation. The adhesive failed after 3 months. The supplier says "we only recommended it — the choice was yours." Is the supplier liable for bad technical advice?
**A:** Under the CPA Section 54, a supplier who provides advice or recommendations must exercise reasonable care and skill. If the technical rep gave negligent advice (recommending an adhesive unsuitable for glass mosaic in a submerged pool environment), the supplier is liable for the consequential damage. Glass mosaic in pools requires specialised epoxy or polymer-modified adhesive rated for permanent immersion — standard cementitious adhesive is not suitable. Document the recommendation: what was said, by whom, when, and in what context. If the supplier's technical data sheet confirms the product is not rated for submerged glass mosaic, the advice was clearly negligent. Your claim is against the supplier for the cost of removal, replacement, and remediation. Preserve any written or email recommendations as evidence.

**Q: 135** I tiled over an existing tiled floor because the client didn't want to pay for removal. I roughened the old tiles and used a bonding agent. Now the new tiles are debonding. Is this my fault?
**A:** Tiling over existing tiles is generally not recommended by manufacturers or SANS standards, but it can work if done correctly: the existing tiles must be firmly bonded (no hollow sounds), thoroughly cleaned (no grease, wax, or soap residue), mechanically roughened or primed with a suitable bonding primer, and the new adhesive must be compatible. If you followed best practice and the debonding is due to the old tiles coming loose (indicating they were not as well-bonded as they appeared), the failure is a latent defect in the substrate, not your installation. However, if you failed to properly prepare the surface or used an unsuitable adhesive, the liability is yours. Going forward: always document the substrate condition before tiling over existing tiles, test adhesion of old tiles with a hammer tap test, and include a written disclaimer for tiling over existing finishes.

**Q: 136** I used a silicone grout in a shower because the client wanted "the best." After 4 months, the grout is mouldy and discoloured. The client is furious because "the best" shouldn't mould. Is this my fault?
**A:** All grout in wet areas is susceptible to mould growth if not properly maintained — even "mould-resistant" grouts will eventually mould if not cleaned and ventilated. Silicone grout is actually more resistant to mould than cementitious grout, but no grout is mould-proof. Mould growth is a maintenance issue, not an installation defect. Under the CPA, goods must be fit for purpose — silicone grout is fit for wet area application. The mould is caused by: inadequate ventilation (no extractor fan or window), failure to clean grout regularly, or soap/shampoo residue accumulation. Explain this to the client and offer to clean and reseal the grout (a maintenance service, not a warranty claim). If she expected a maintenance-free solution, she had unrealistic expectations — no such product exists for shower grout.

**Q: 137** I installed underfloor heating under tiles. The heating cable failed after 11 months. The electrician who installed the cable blames my tile adhesive for overheating the cable. The adhesive manufacturer says their product is heat-rated to 80°C. Who's actually liable?
**A:** This requires expert forensic investigation. Underfloor heating cables should not exceed 27-29°C surface temperature for comfort — if the cable is reaching 80°C, the heating system is either incorrectly specified (wattage too high for the area), incorrectly installed (insulation issues), or incorrectly controlled (thermostat fault). Tile adhesive rated to 80°C is more than adequate for normal underfloor heating. The electrician's claim that your adhesive caused overheating is scientifically dubious — adhesive does not generate heat. Independent testing should determine: the actual cable temperature, the adhesive's heat resistance certification, and whether the heating system was installed per manufacturer specifications. If the cable temperature exceeded design limits due to electrical fault, the electrician is liable. Your liability only arises if you covered the heating system without verifying it was tested and operational first.

**Q: 138** I used an uncoupling membrane on a wooden floor substrate as recommended. The tiles still cracked. The membrane manufacturer says I installed it incorrectly. I followed their video tutorial exactly. How do I prove my installation was correct?
**A:** Document everything: photographs of each installation step, the batch number of the membrane, the specific video tutorial you followed (with date accessed), the adhesive batch and mixing ratio, ambient temperature records, and the substrate condition before installation. Wooden floors are inherently unstable (expansion/contraction with humidity) — even with an uncoupling membrane, large format tiles or rigid tiles may crack. SANS 10400-J requires timber floors to be structurally stable, adequately ventilated below, and properly prepared before tiling. If the floor had excessive deflection (bounce), no membrane will prevent cracking — this is a structural issue, not a tiling issue. Commission an independent inspection by a registered professional engineer or building inspector to determine the root cause. If the wooden floor exceeded allowable deflection limits, the builder is liable, not you.

**Q: 139** A client wants me to install natural stone tiles (travertine) in a bathroom using the same method I use for ceramic tiles. I know stone requires different adhesive, sealing, and maintenance. If I explain this and she still insists on ceramic tile method, can I just do what she asks?
**A:** No. Natural stone is porous, moisture-sensitive, and requires: (a) a polymer-modified adhesive (white adhesive to prevent staining), (b) pre-sealing before installation to prevent staining from grout, (c) flexible grout (not standard cementitious), (d) post-installation sealing, and (e) awareness that stone will patina and change over time. Installing stone like ceramic will result in staining, efflorescence, and premature degradation. If you proceed knowing the method is wrong, you are professionally negligent regardless of the client's instructions. Refuse the job unless the client agrees to the correct stone installation method. Provide her with written technical information from the stone supplier. Your professional duty overrides a client's uninformed preference.

**Q: 140** I tiled a balcony with no fall to the drain — it was perfectly level because the client wanted "a flat look." Now water pools and the tiles are lifting. She blames me. But she specifically asked for level!
**A:** A professional tiler has a duty not to install tiles in a manner that contravenes building regulations or manufacturer specifications, even if the client requests it. SANS 10400-P requires external balconies to have a minimum fall of 1:50 to drains. Installing a level balcony creates a water trap that will inevitably fail — the client may have asked for it, but you should have refused. This is professional negligence. Your only defence is if you advised against it in writing and the client signed a waiver acknowledging the risk — but even a waiver may not fully protect you if the installation contravenes SANS. Going forward: never compromise on falls in wet areas. Explain that SANS requires a minimum fall and that a flat balcony is not possible for a professional installation.

**Q: 141** I installed tiles in a house built on expansive clay soil in Centurion. After the first rainy season, the floor heaved and the tiles cracked in a wave pattern. The client says my tiles are defective. Is this my problem?
**A:** Wave-pattern cracking following the soil's expansion and contraction pattern is a substrate movement issue, not a tile defect. Expansive clay (smectite clay) swells when wet and shrinks when dry, causing significant floor movement. SANS 10400-H addresses foundations on expansive soils — the house should have been designed with specific foundation depth, drainage, and soil treatment. If the house was properly designed for expansive soils and still moved excessively, this may be a design or construction defect (the builder's liability). If the house was not designed for expansive soils at all, the builder and architect are negligent. Your tiles are not defective — they cracked because the floor moved beyond reasonable limits. An independent geotechnical assessment will confirm the cause. Document the crack pattern — it will match the soil heave pattern, proving substrate failure.

**Q: 142** I used a premixed (bucket) adhesive from a reputable brand for a wall tiling job. After 6 weeks, tiles started falling off. The manufacturer says premixed adhesive is only suitable for dry areas and I used it in a bathroom with occasional shower spray. Is this my fault?
**A:** Premixed (ready-to-use) adhesives are typically organic/polymer-based and have different performance characteristics from cementitious adhesives. Many premixed adhesives are rated only for dry to intermittently wet areas, not for direct water exposure. As the installer, you have a duty to verify that the adhesive is suitable for the application — "reputable brand" does not mean "suitable for all applications." The technical data sheet (TDS) specifies the adhesive's classification (e.g., Type D1, D2 under EN 12004) and its water resistance rating. If you used a D1 adhesive (no water resistance) in a wet area, the fault is yours. However, if the product was mislabelled or the salesperson recommended it for bathroom use, the supplier shares liability. Always check the TDS suitability classification before specifying any adhesive.

**Q: 143** I grouted a floor with an epoxy grout because the client wanted "the strongest possible grout." The grout has stained the porous tiles because I didn't seal them first. The client wants the tiles replaced. Is epoxy grout staining my liability?
**A:** Yes. Epoxy grout is extremely strong and stain-resistant, but it will stain porous tiles (natural stone, unglazed ceramics, some porcelain) during installation because the epoxy resin penetrates the tile body before curing. The standard procedure for epoxy grout with porous tiles is to pre-seal the tiles before grouting. If you failed to pre-seal, the staining is your fault. The TDS for the epoxy grout almost certainly warns about this. The tiles may need replacement if the staining cannot be removed chemically (some epoxy stains can be partially removed with specialised cleaners, but full removal is rare). Going forward: always test epoxy grout on a sample tile first, and pre-seal any porous tile before epoxy grouting. Include this step in your quote.

**Q: 144** I installed tiles in an industrial freezer room (-18°C). The tiles and adhesive both failed within 2 months. The client says I used the wrong products. Were there "right" products for this application?
**A:** Standard ceramic tiles and cementitious adhesives are not rated for temperatures below 0°C, especially with freeze-thaw cycling. Freezer room flooring requires: (a) tiles rated for freeze-thaw conditions (porcelain with low water absorption, typically <0.5%), (b) frost-resistant adhesive (specialised polymer-modified adhesive rated for sub-zero application), (c) flexible grout with freeze-thaw resistance, (d) appropriate expansion joints to accommodate thermal cycling. If you used standard products without verifying their low-temperature rating, you are liable. However, if the client specified standard products knowing the application, liability may be shared. Freezer room flooring is a specialist application — if you are not experienced in this area, you should have declined the job or engaged a specialist to specify the correct system.

**Q: 145** I tiled a concrete floor that had been painted with epoxy paint. I scabbled the surface and used a bonding agent, but the tiles lifted after 4 months. Did I do enough preparation?
**A:** Epoxy paint creates a non-porous, chemically resistant barrier that is extremely difficult to tile over. Mechanical scabbling alone often does not remove all paint residue from the concrete pores, and bonding agents may not adhere to epoxy. The correct preparation is: complete removal of all paint by shot-blasting or grinding to bare concrete (not just scabbling), followed by a suitable primer compatible with the adhesive system. If the epoxy paint was not fully removed, the adhesive bond is compromised at the paint layer. Your liability depends on whether your preparation met industry standards. If you simply scabbled without removing all paint, your preparation was likely inadequate. For epoxy-coated floors, shot-blasting is the industry standard preparation method before tiling.

**Q: 146** I used a 12mm trowel notch for 600x600mm tiles as recommended by the adhesive manufacturer. The client had her own "tile expert" inspect the work and he says I should have used a 20mm trowel. Now she's demanding removal and redo. Who's right?
**A:** Trowel notch size is determined by: tile size, tile flatness (lippage tolerance), substrate flatness, and adhesive manufacturer recommendations. A 12mm trowel is generally suitable for 600x600mm tiles if the substrate is flat and the tiles have good dimensional consistency. A 20mm trowel is more commonly used for very large format tiles (800mm+) or uneven substrates. If the adhesive manufacturer's TDS specifies 12mm for this tile size, you followed the correct specification. The client's "expert" is entitled to his opinion, but the manufacturer's published TDS carries more weight in a dispute. If the tiles are fully bedded (verified by lift-off tests showing >90% coverage), your installation is correct. Do not accept removal demands without independent technical assessment. The client's expert should provide his assessment in writing with reference to specific standards.

**Q: 147** I installed tiles with a "rectified" edge (precision-cut for minimal joints). The client complains about visible lippage. Rectified tiles are supposed to be perfectly flat — is this a tile defect or an installation issue?
**A:** Rectified tiles have machined edges for consistent sizing, but they are not automatically perfectly flat — "rectified" refers to the edges, not the body flatness. Lippage in rectified tile installations is usually caused by: (a) substrate unevenness (rectified tiles magnify any substrate irregularity), (b) improper tile levelling system use (or failure to use one), (c) tile warpage (even rectified tiles can have slight warpage per SANS 1449 tolerances), (d) adhesive bedding technique. SANS 10107 (code of practice for tiling) specifies maximum allowable lippage. If the lippage exceeds these limits, it may be an installation issue. If the tiles themselves exceed flatness tolerances under SANS 1449, it is a tile defect. An independent tiling inspector can measure both the installation quality and the tile flatness to determine the cause.

**Q: 148** A client wants me to tile a staircase with 600x600mm polished porcelain tiles. I know this is a slip hazard and the treads are too small for the tile size. She insists. What do I do?
**A:** Refuse. Staircase tiling with large format polished porcelain is a serious safety hazard. SANS 10107 and SANS 10400-T (fire protection) address stair safety. Polished porcelain has an R9 or lower slip rating — inadequate for stair treads where SANS 10107 recommends R10–R11 for dry internal stairs and R11–R12 for external or potentially wet stairs. A 600mm tile on a standard 250–280mm tread will require excessive cutting and create narrow, fragile edges. As a professional, you have a duty of care to refuse work that creates a foreseeable safety risk. If you install it and someone falls, you face delictual liability for negligence. Provide the client with safer alternatives: smaller format anti-slip tiles, stair-nosing profiles, or tiles with appropriate R-rating. Document your refusal and safety concerns in writing.

**Q: 149** I installed tiles over a Damp Proof Course (DPC) membrane in a basement. The tiles lifted because moisture built up under the DPC and couldn't evaporate. Is this a design flaw or my installation error?
**A:** This is a design flaw. A DPC is designed to prevent moisture ingress from below — but if it is completely sealed under tiles with impermeable adhesive, any residual moisture or trapped vapour has nowhere to go and will eventually cause adhesive failure. Basement floors with active moisture should use a ventilated system or a moisture-tolerant installation method: (a) a vapour-permeable adhesive system, (b) a ventilated cavity system (raised floor), (c) or active moisture management (sump pump, drainage). If the architect or builder specified tiling directly over a DPC without addressing vapour management, the design is at fault. As the tiler, you should have flagged the moisture risk before installation — but the primary liability lies with whoever designed the system. An independent building inspector can confirm moisture levels and system design adequacy.

**Q: 150** I tiled a residential floor and the client wanted the tiles laid "diagonally" at 45 degrees. Now she's complaining that the room looks "crooked" because the walls aren't perfectly square. She wants me to redo it for free. Do I have to?
**A:** Diagonal tile layouts accentuate any wall irregularity because the tile edges create strong diagonal lines that the eye follows. If the walls are not perfectly square (which is common in residential construction), diagonal laying will make the skewness visible — this is not an installation error but a geometric reality. When the client chose diagonal layout, she implicitly accepted that wall irregularities would be more visible. You should have warned her of this risk before starting — a good practice is to snap chalk lines showing the diagonal layout before tile installation so the client can see how the layout follows the room geometry. If she approved the layout after seeing the lines, you have no liability. If you did not warn her, offer a goodwill adjustment (perhaps a border tile to disguise the skew) but do not accept a full redo.

## 10. TOWNSHIP POLITICS, NEIGHBOUR DISPUTES & UNUSUAL SITE CONDITIONS

**Q: 151** I'm a white contractor working in a township. The local business forum says I need their "permission" to work there and must pay a 10% levy. Is this legal?
**A:** No. No business forum, community organisation, or informal group has a legal right to demand payment for the privilege of working in any area of South Africa. Such demands constitute extortion under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act (POCA). However, this is a complex reality — local business forums in some townships have significant influence and can disrupt work through protest action. The practical approach: engage respectfully with local business forums, explain your employment of local labour, and seek cooperation rather than confrontation. Report extortion demands to SAPS. The Gauteng Township Economy Act and similar provincial legislation aim to protect all businesses operating in townships. If you experience threats or violence, obtain a protection order from the Magistrate's Court under the Protection from Harassment Act. Always employ local labour where possible — this builds community support.

**Q: 152** I was tiling in a rural Eastern Cape village. The traditional council (inkosi) says I need to pay "umngeno" (a fee for working in his area) before I can start. Is this a legitimate requirement?
**A:** Traditional councils have authority over communal land under the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003. On communal land (not privately owned land), the traditional council can regulate economic activities and impose reasonable fees for development. However, on privately owned land with a registered title deed, the traditional council has no legal authority to levy fees — the owner has the right to engage any contractor. If the property is on communal land, some form of community engagement or fee may be customary and practically necessary. If it is privately owned, the fee is not legally enforceable — though refusing to pay may create practical difficulties. Clarify the land tenure status before quoting. If privately owned, politely decline and proceed with the owner's authorisation. If communal land, negotiate the fee as a project cost.

**Q: 153** My tiling work in a complex caused vibration that cracked the neighbour's wall. The neighbour is threatening me with a lawyer. Am I liable, or is the client who hired me?
**A:** Under the neighbour law principle (sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas — use your property so as not to harm another's), the person who caused the nuisance is primarily liable. As the contractor who created the vibration, you are the direct cause. However, the client (property owner) is also liable as the person who engaged you to work on their property. The neighbour's claim is against both of you jointly and severally. Your defence: (a) the vibration was reasonable for tiling work (tile cutting and scabbling produce unavoidable vibration), (b) the neighbour's wall was structurally unsound and would have cracked from any minor vibration, (c) you used standard equipment and techniques. If the wall was already compromised (latent defect), the neighbour's claim may fail or be reduced. Notify your public liability insurer immediately.

**Q: 154** I was working in a complex where the neighbour complained to the body corporate about "excessive noise" at 10am on a Tuesday. The body corporate issued a warning letter threatening fines. Can they actually fine me, or only the unit owner?
**A:** The body corporate can only enforce its conduct rules against owners (or occupiers) of units within the scheme — not against third-party contractors. The owner who engaged you is the person liable for any breach of conduct rules, including noise restrictions. The body corporate can fine the owner (unit holder) for noise violations. The owner may then claim indemnification from you if the noise was excessive or outside permitted hours — but 10am Tuesday is within standard working hours (07:00–18:00 per most bylaws and conduct rules). The body corporate's fine is likely unenforceable if the noise occurred during permitted hours. Advise the client to challenge the fine and point out that tiling work during daytime hours is reasonable and lawful. The body corporate must specify what bylaw or conduct rule was violated.

**Q: 155** I'm tiling a house in a heritage area in the Western Cape. The client didn't tell me it's a heritage property. The neighbour reported the work to Heritage Western Cape and they've issued a stop-work order. Can I claim my standing time costs from the client?
**A:** Yes. The National Heritage Resources Act 25 of 1999 requires any alteration to a heritage-protected property to have a heritage permit from the relevant heritage authority (Heritage Western Cape for the Western Cape). Working without a permit is an offence, and the owner has a duty to inform contractors of heritage restrictions. If the client failed to disclose the heritage status and obtain the required permit, she is liable for all costs arising from the stop-work order, including your standing time, remobilisation costs, and any demurrage on hired equipment. This should be treated as a variation or delay caused by the employer. Going forward: always ask whether a property has heritage protection before quoting, and include a clause requiring the client to obtain all necessary permits before work commences.

**Q: 156** I was tiling in a house and the neighbour's domestic worker accused me of stealing her cellphone. I didn't. The neighbour now won't let me park near her house and is badmouthing me to other residents. What can I do?
**A:** False accusations of theft are defamatory if published to a third party (the other residents). You can: (a) demand a retraction and apology in writing, (b) if the accusation was made to SAPS and no theft charge was laid, the false report may constitute crimen iniuria (unlawful impairment of dignity), (c) if reputational damage has resulted in lost business, you may sue for defamation in the Magistrate's Court. The neighbour's parking ban is only enforceable if it is on her property — public roads cannot be restricted. For the theft accusation: if you are confident of your innocence, offer to cooperate with any investigation (though SAPS is unlikely to investigate a missing cellphone without evidence). Document all defamatory statements and their witnesses.

**Q: 157** I need to tile a flat in a Sandton high-rise. The body corporate won't give me access to the service lift because I don't have "their" contractor insurance. Can they legally block me?
**A:** Body corporates under the Sectional Titles Schemes Management Act can impose reasonable rules for the use of common property, including requiring contractors to provide proof of insurance before using common facilities. This is generally lawful provided the rule is applied uniformly and is reasonable. If their rule requires all contractors to have public liability insurance and show proof, that is reasonable. If they are blocking you while allowing other uninsured contractors access, that is discriminatory and unlawful. Provide your public liability insurance certificate — if you don't have insurance, this is a wake-up call to get it. The body corporate cannot impose arbitrary restrictions but can enforce reasonable safety and insurance requirements for common property access.

**Q: 158** A client in a security estate won't give me the access code for the estate gate because "it's confidential." She says I must wait at the gate for her to let me in every morning. This wastes 30 minutes daily. Can I charge for standing time?
**A:** Yes. Access to the site is the client's responsibility under standard contract terms. If she fails to provide reasonable access, causing delay to your work, the delay is an employer-caused delay entitling you to: (a) a time extension, (b) standing time costs (wages for idle labour, vehicle waiting time), and (c) remobilisation costs if the delay exceeds half a day. Document each day's delay with arrival time and access time. Request the access code in writing, or request that the client arrange a contractor access pass with the estate security. If she refuses all reasonable alternatives, include the standing time in your variation claim. This is a standard contractual entitlement — the client cannot unreasonably withhold access and expect you to absorb the cost.

**Q: 159** I was tiling a house and the neighbour's tree roots had lifted the concrete floor by nearly 80mm. The client wants me to tile over it anyway with a "thick bed" of adhesive. I know this will fail. What do I tell her?
**A:** Tiling over a floor lifted 80mm by tree roots is a temporary fix that will fail — the roots will continue growing and lift the tiles. SANS 10400-J requires substrates to be structurally sound and stable. The correct solution is tree root removal or barrier installation by an arborist, followed by floor repair or replacement. As a professional, you must refuse to install tiles over an obviously unstable substrate — doing so would be negligent and you would be liable when it fails. Provide the client with a written explanation: tree roots will continue to expand, the lifting force will crack tiles and debond adhesive, and the correct remediation is arboricultural work before tiling. If she insists, require a signed waiver acknowledging that you have no warranty on the installation — but even a waiver may not fully protect you from negligence claims.

**Q: 160** I tiled a house in a mining town (Welkom). After 6 months, the tiles started showing orange staining. The client's borehole water has high iron content. Is this my problem or the water's?
**A:** Iron-rich borehole water causes orange/brown staining (iron oxidation) on porous tiles and grout. This is a water quality issue, not an installation defect. However, your liability depends on whether you should have anticipated the problem: (a) if you used unglazed or porous tiles in an area where iron staining is known (common in mining towns with borehole water), you may share liability for material selection, (b) if you used impervious porcelain tiles and the staining is on the grout only, this is purely a maintenance issue — grout should be sealed to reduce staining. Advise the client to: test borehole water quality, install iron filtration, seal grout annually, and use appropriate cleaning products (acid-based cleaners remove iron stains). The borehole water quality is not your responsibility unless you guaranteed the installation against all water conditions.

**Q: 161** I was tiling in a complex where the security guard refused to let my delivery truck in because the driver didn't have an ID document. The load was time-sensitive (wet screed). The screed set in the truck. Who pays for the wasted screed?
**A:** The client is responsible for ensuring site access for deliveries. If the security guard was enforcing complex rules and the client failed to arrange access or pre-register the delivery, the wasted screed is a client-caused delay. The same applies if the client knew about the ID requirement and didn't inform you. Document: the delivery time, the security guard's refusal and reason, your notification to the client, and the screed's condition. Charge the client for the wasted screed and re-delivery. Going forward: include in your contract that "the client shall ensure unimpeded site access for all deliveries and personnel. Costs arising from access restrictions shall be for the client's account."

**Q: 162** I'm a small contractor. A big developer wants me to sign a contract that says I must carry R5 million public liability insurance. My current policy is only R1 million. The developer won't budge. What are my options?
**A:** Your options: (a) increase your public liability cover to R5 million (contact your broker — increasing from R1m to R5m typically adds R300–R800/month), (b) negotiate a lower limit (R2m or R3m), (c) accept the contract and arrange a top-up policy for this specific project, (d) walk away. R5 million is standard for commercial construction contracts — it is not unreasonable for the developer to require this. Many contractors start with R1m cover and increase it as they grow. The cost of R5m cover is a small price for access to commercial work. Contact your insurance broker to quote the increase before responding to the developer — showing you are arranging the cover demonstrates professionalism and may improve your negotiating position.

**Q: 163** I was tiling a house and the neighbour complained that dust from my tile cutting was settling on her washing. She threw a bucket of dirty water over my labourer. Can I lay a charge, and can she stop my work?
**A:** Throwing dirty water over someone constitutes common assault — you can lay a charge at SAPS. She cannot unilaterally stop your work — only a court order (interdict) or a lawful directive from a municipal authority can stop construction work. However, her complaint about dust is valid — you have a duty under the OHSA and municipal bylaws to control dust and nuisance. Mitigate dust by: cutting tiles outdoors away from neighbours, using wet cutting (water suppression), erecting dust barriers, and scheduling cutting during reasonable hours. If you take reasonable dust control measures and she still complains, her assault is unjustified. Document the assault (photos of your labourer's condition, witness statements) and lay the charge. Do not escalate the confrontation — remain professional.

**Q: 164** A client's property is on a steep slope in Cape Town. My truck can't access the site — I need to carry all materials up 80 steps. This will double my labour cost. Can I claim a variation?
**A:** Yes. Difficult site access that was not apparent from the initial site inspection (or was not disclosed by the client) constitutes a changed condition entitling you to a variation order. Document the access difficulty with photographs and video. Calculate the additional cost: extra labour for manual carrying, additional time, potential need for smaller vehicles, and equipment hire (e.g., material hoist). Submit a variation claim before commencing the additional work — if you proceed without notifying the client of the extra cost, you may struggle to claim later. Include a standard clause in your quotes: "Pricing is based on normal vehicular access to the work area. Additional costs arising from restricted access will be charged as a variation."

**Q: 165** I discovered human remains (old bones) while excavating for a new screed in a heritage area. The client wants me to keep digging and "just bury them deeper." What do I do?
**A:** STOP immediately. Under the National Heritage Resources Act and the Human Tissue Act, the discovery of human remains must be reported to SAPS and the relevant heritage authority immediately. Moving, disturbing, or concealing human remains is a criminal offence. Do not touch the remains or the surrounding area. Contact SAPS and the South African Heritage Resources Agency (SAHRA) or provincial heritage authority. The area may be declared a heritage site requiring archaeological investigation before work can resume. The delay and associated costs (archaeological monitoring, potential redesign) are the client's risk. Do not follow the client's instruction to bury the remains deeper — this would make you criminally liable as an accessory.

**Q: 166** I was tiling a house and the municipal inspector arrived, saying I need a building plan approval for "alterations and additions" because the floor level is changing by more than the threshold. Is this correct?
**A:** Municipal bylaws vary, but most require building plan approval for: changes to floor levels that affect door thresholds or step heights, structural alterations, changes to drainage, and any work affecting the building envelope. If your tiling changes the floor level such that door clearances are affected or step heights change, building plan approval may indeed be required. However, cosmetic tiling (same floor level, no structural changes) typically does not require approval. The inspector's authority is limited to their municipality's bylaws — ask them to specify which bylaw provision requires approval. If you proceed without approval and it is required, the municipality can issue a stop-work order and fine the property owner. The responsibility for obtaining plan approval lies with the owner, not you — but you should verify approval before starting if structural changes are involved.

**Q: 167** I tiled a client's house and his wife loved it, but the husband hates it. He's demanding I remove it and redo it in a different colour — at my cost. They are married in community of property. Does the husband's dissatisfaction override the wife's approval?
**A:** In a marriage in community of property, either spouse can bind the joint estate for household necessaries and improvements — tiling a home qualifies. The wife's approval of the work (and presumably her instruction to proceed) is binding on the joint estate. The husband's personal aesthetic preference does not override the contract or constitute a defect. If the work is technically sound and the wife (who engaged you) is satisfied, the husband has no legal basis to demand removal at your cost. If the couple disagrees, that is a domestic matter, not your contractual issue. Do not engage in their marital dispute — your contract is with the person who engaged you (the wife), and her approval of completed work constitutes acceptance. If he is violent or threatening, remove yourself from the situation and communicate only with your contracting party.

**Q: 168** I was tiling in a block of flats and the tenant in the flat below complained that water from my wet cutting was leaking through her ceiling. I was cutting in the client's bathroom with the drain open. How did water get downstairs?
**A:** Water from wet cutting finds the path of least resistance — if the floor has cracks, unsealed pipe penetrations, or gaps around drainage pipes, water will seep through to the unit below. This indicates either: (a) a latent defect in the floor slab (insufficient waterproofing around pipe penetrations — the building developer's fault), or (b) your failure to contain cutting water (your responsibility). When wet cutting indoors, always: use a cutting tray to capture water, avoid cutting directly over drains where water can flow down pipe penetrations, use minimal water, and place absorbent mats around the cutting area. If the water ingress was due to building defects (unsealed penetrations), the body corporate is liable for the downstairs damage. If it was due to your water management, you are liable.

**Q: 169** I'm a one-person contractor. I injured my back on site and can't work for 6 weeks. I have no income and no insurance. Is there any government support I can claim?
**A:** As a self-employed person, you are not covered by the Compensation Fund (COIDA covers employees, not principals). You have no UIF benefits because you don't contribute to UIF as a self-employed person (unless you voluntarily registered). Your options are limited: (a) if you have a provident fund or retirement annuity, check for disability benefits, (b) if the injury was caused by a third party's negligence (e.g., unsafe site conditions provided by the client), you may have a civil claim against them, (c) SASSA disability grant has strict criteria and long processing times — unlikely to help immediately. Going forward: consider income protection insurance for self-employed contractors (available from major insurers). It typically covers 75% of average monthly income during disability periods and is the single most important insurance for a sole trader.

**Q: 170** I was offered a government housing project (RDP house tiling) through a contact in the municipality. He says I need to pay a "facilitation fee" of R15,000 to "secure the tender." Is this how government tenders work?
**A:** No. This is bribery, which is a criminal offence under the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act (PRECCA). Paying a facilitation fee to secure a government tender constitutes corruption — both you and the municipal official face criminal prosecution, imprisonment of up to life for severe cases, and blacklisting from all government procurement. All government tenders must be advertised publicly and awarded through a transparent process (preferential procurement points system based on price and B-BBEE). Any "shortcut" involving payment to an official is corruption. Report the approach to the National Treasury's Tender Fraud hotline or the Public Protector. Do not pay — even if you "win" the tender, the contract can be set aside, and you will be prosecuted.

## 11. WARRANTY, RETENTION & POST-COMPLETION HEADACHES

**Q: 171** I completed a tiling job 14 months ago. The client is now claiming grout cracking and demanding I fix it for free, saying I "guaranteed the work for 5 years." I never said that. What's my actual warranty obligation?
**A:** Under common law, a contractor's workmanship warranty is typically 1 year for non-structural work (tiling falls into this category). The NHBRC 5-year warranty applies only to new home construction enrolled with the NHBRC and covers structural defects, not tiling finishes. The CPA Section 56 provides an implied 6-month warranty for defects that existed at the time of supply (the consumer can demand repair, replacement, or refund within 6 months without proving the defect existed). After 6 months, the consumer must prove the defect existed at the time of supply. At 14 months, grout cracking is more likely caused by: substrate movement, structural settlement, temperature cycling, or inadequate maintenance — not by your original workmanship. Offer to inspect at a call-out fee, but do not accept a free repair obligation unless the defect is proven to be your fault.

**Q: 172** A client is holding 10% retention on a R120,000 residential tiling job for 12 months. I need the cash flow now. Is 12-month retention on residential tiling even legal?
**A:** Retention is a contractual matter — if you agreed to 12-month retention in your contract, it is legally binding. However, 12-month retention for residential tiling is excessive by industry standards. Standard practice for residential work is: no retention (payment on completion), or 5% retention for 3 months (defects liability period). For commercial work under JBCC, 5% is standard until practical completion, then release at final completion. If you did not agree to retention in your contract and the client is unilaterally withholding payment, that is breach of contract — sue for the full amount. If you did agree but now need cash flow, you can offer the client a retention bond (guarantee from an insurance company) in exchange for early release — the client holds the bond instead of cash, and you get your money (minus the bond premium).

**Q: 173** I subcontracted a plumbing contractor to do the wet area waterproofing before I tiled. His waterproofing failed and I had to remove my tiles to fix it. He's gone out of business. Can I claim from my professional indemnity insurance?
**A:** Professional indemnity insurance covers YOUR professional negligence, not the negligence of subcontractors you engage. If the waterproofing failure was the plumber's fault (not yours), your PI insurance will not respond — the claim is against the plumber. Since he's out of business, your claim may be unenforceable. This illustrates why you should: (a) verify subcontractor insurance before engagement, (b) include indemnity clauses in subcontract agreements, (c) consider contractors' all-risk insurance with "subcontractor default" coverage. If you had a duty to inspect and approve the waterproofing before tiling, and you failed to do so, your negligence may trigger PI cover — but only for your portion of liability, not the plumber's. Going forward: always verify subcontractor waterproofing with a flood test before tiling.

**Q: 174** A client from a job I completed 3 years ago called me back because she wants a "warranty inspection." I never offered an ongoing warranty inspection service. Do I have any obligation to return?
**A:** No. Your warranty obligation is limited to repairing defects that existed at the time of installation and that manifest within the warranty period (typically 1 year for workmanship). A "warranty inspection" 3 years later is not a standard obligation — the warranty has likely expired, and even if it hadn't, you are only required to respond to reported defects, not conduct proactive inspections. You can offer a paid inspection service (call-out fee plus hourly rate) as a goodwill gesture, but you have no contractual or legal obligation to conduct free inspections years after completion. Be polite but clear: "Our workmanship warranty covered the first year. I'd be happy to conduct a paid inspection if you have specific concerns."

**Q: 175** I repaired a tile defect under warranty. The repair required a slightly different shade of grout because the original was discontinued. The client says the repair is unacceptable because the grout doesn't match exactly. Do I need to redo the entire floor?
**A:** No. Warranty repairs are intended to restore functionality, not necessarily achieve perfect aesthetic match — especially when the original materials are discontinued. The legal standard is "reasonable repair" — a repair that restores the floor to functional use with acceptable appearance, considering the circumstances. If the colour difference is minor and only visible upon close inspection, the repair meets the standard. If the client wants a perfect match, she would need to accept a full re-grout (at her cost, not warranty). Document that the original grout was discontinued and that you used the closest available match. Offer a good-faith discount on a full re-grout if she insists on perfection — but do not absorb the cost of aesthetic perfection when you have met your warranty obligation.

**Q: 176** I sold my tiling business to another contractor. A former client is now claiming defects on work I did before the sale. Is the new owner liable, or am I?
**A:** Warranty liability depends on the sale agreement. If the business was sold as a going concern (including all assets, liabilities, and ongoing contracts), the buyer typically assumes warranty obligations for work done before the sale — unless the contract specifically excluded pre-sale warranties. If you sold only the assets (tools, vehicle, client list) without assuming liabilities, you remain liable for pre-sale defects. The client can sue either: you (as the original contractor who did the work) or the new owner (if he holds himself out as continuing the business). To protect yourself in a business sale: include a clause requiring the buyer to honour all warranties, obtain warranty run-off insurance, and retain records of completed work for the warranty period.

**Q: 177** I completed a job and the client signed off, paid in full, and gave me a glowing review. Six months later, she sold the house and the new owner is claiming the same work is defective. Can the new owner claim against me?
**A:** The new owner's ability to claim depends on: (a) whether the defect was latent (not discoverable on reasonable inspection) at the time of the original client's sign-off, and (b) whether there is a contractual or statutory warranty that transfers to subsequent owners. Under common law, the original client's sign-off with full knowledge of the condition generally prevents later claims for patent (visible) defects. However, latent defects that were not discoverable at sign-off may still support a claim. The CPA Section 56 warranty applies to the "consumer" — the person who contracted for the service. The new owner was not the consumer and cannot claim under the CPA. His claim, if any, would be against the seller (previous owner) who sold him a property with defects. Your contractual warranty was to the original client, not to future owners.

**Q: 178** A client wants me to give a 10-year written warranty on a tiling job. I normally give 1 year. If I sign her 10-year document, what am I actually committing to?
**A:** A 10-year written warranty is a contractual guarantee that creates a legally binding obligation to repair any defects arising within 10 years, regardless of cause (unless the warranty document specifies exclusions). This is significantly beyond industry standard (1 year for workmanship) and exposes you to enormous liability — structural movement, subfloor failure, building settlement, and material degradation over 10 years are not within your control as a tiler. If you are considering this: (a) limit the warranty to "defects in workmanship and materials due to faulty installation only" — excluding substrate movement, structural defects, and misuse, (b) require the client to maintain the tiles per manufacturer guidelines, (c) cap your liability at the original contract value, (d) exclude consequential damages. Better yet: refuse. Explain that 1 year is industry standard and that longer warranties require insurance backing that significantly increases the project cost.

**Q: 179** I repaired a defect under warranty. The same defect recurred 3 months later in the same area. Is my warranty period extended by the repair, or does the original warranty still expire on the original date?
**A:** Under common law, a warranty repair does not automatically extend the original warranty period unless the warranty document or your contract specifically states that repairs carry their own warranty period. However, if the same defect recurs after a repair, this suggests the original repair was inadequate — you may have a fresh obligation to repair again if the recurrence is within a reasonable time of the original repair. Some contractors offer a "90-day repair warranty" as a goodwill gesture. The fairest approach: if the same defect recurs shortly after your repair, treat it as an extension of the original warranty obligation and repair it without charge. This builds reputation and avoids disputes. If the recurrence is due to a different cause (e.g., new substrate movement), that is a new issue, not a warranty repair.

**Q: 180** A client is demanding I replace her entire bathroom floor because one tile has a hairline crack. The bathroom is 3 years old. She says the crack proves poor workmanship. Is replacing one tile reasonable, or must I redo the whole floor?
**A:** Replacing one tile is the reasonable remedy. A single hairline crack in a 3-year-old bathroom does not justify full floor replacement unless: (a) the crack indicates a systemic failure (e.g., all tiles will crack soon), or (b) the exact tile is discontinued and no close match exists. At 3 years, your workmanship warranty has expired. The crack could be caused by: dropped object impact, structural movement, temperature shock (hot water spill), or normal wear. Offer to replace the single tile at your cost (if you feel goodwill is warranted) or at the client's cost. If the client wants the entire floor redone, she must prove systemic failure caused by your original workmanship — which is unlikely after 3 years. Do not allow unreasonable demands to escalate — offer reasonable solutions and stand firm.

## 12. TAX, ACCOUNTING & CASH-IN-HAND TRAPS

**Q: 181** A client paid me R35,000 cash and asked for no invoice because "she doesn't want her husband to know how much she spent." I deposited the cash in my bank account. Can SARS question this deposit?
**A:** Yes. SARS has extensive data-matching capabilities and can flag unexplained cash deposits. Under the Tax Administration Act, SARS can require you to explain the source of any deposit. If the amount represents taxable income that you did not declare, you are evading tax. The client's motive (hiding spending from her husband) is irrelevant to your tax obligation. You must declare all income, whether invoiced or not, whether cash or EFT. If SARS determines you deliberately understated income, penalties range from 10% to 200% of the tax shortfall, plus interest and potential criminal prosecution. The correct action: issue the invoice (the client's marital issues are not your problem), declare the income, and pay the tax. If she doesn't want an invoice, that is her choice — but your obligation to declare income is independent of invoicing.

**Q: 182** I quoted a job excluding materials (labour only). The client bought materials and wants a "labour only" invoice from me. She says she'll claim the materials on her tax. Is there any risk to me in issuing a labour-only invoice when I know she's inflating her material claim?
**A:** If you are genuinely providing labour only, your labour-only invoice is accurate and creates no risk for you. The client's material claims are between her and SARS — you are not responsible for her tax claims. However, if she asks you to invoice for "materials" that you did not supply (so she can claim a larger deduction), that is tax fraud and you are complicit if you issue the false invoice. Only invoice for what you actually supplied or did. If your relationship with the client involves you procuring materials and on-selling them, you should include materials on your invoice at cost (or with your markup) and declare the full amount as income. Separate labour and material invoicing is common and legitimate — just ensure it reflects reality.

**Q: 183** I'm a sole proprietor. Can I pay my child's school fees from my business account and claim it as a business expense because I "need him in school so I can work"?
**A:** No. School fees are a personal expense, not a business expense. SARS applies the "in the production of income" test — expenses must be incurred for the purpose of producing income, not for personal or domestic purposes. The fact that having your child in school enables you to work does not make school fees a deductible business expense. Claiming personal expenses as business expenses constitutes tax fraud. If SARS audits you and disallows the deduction, you will owe the additional tax plus penalties (up to 200% for intentional evasion) and interest. Keep personal and business expenses strictly separate. If you pay personal expenses from your business account, record them as "owner's drawings" — not business expenses.

**Q: 184** A client paid me in full but the payment was from a third-party account (her son's account). SARS is asking me to explain why the payer name doesn't match the client name. Is this a problem?
**A:** It is not inherently a problem — third-party payments are common and legal (e.g., family members paying on behalf of the contracting party). The issue for SARS is whether the payment represents taxable income and whether all income is properly declared. As long as: (a) you declared the full income, (b) you have a contract or invoice linking the work to the client (not the son), and (c) the amount is reasonable for the work done, there is no tax issue. Provide SARS with: the contract with the original client, your invoice, and an explanation that the son paid on her behalf (a simple affidavit from the client confirming this is helpful). If the amount is large and unexplained, SARS may investigate further under anti-money laundering legislation (FICA). Keep documentation linking the payment to legitimate business income.

**Q: 185** I take materials from my business stock for my own home renovation. I don't record this as a sale — I just use them. What's the tax treatment?
**A:** Taking business stock for personal use is called "stock appropriation" or "drawings in kind." SARS requires you to account for this as if you sold the materials to yourself at market value. The market value of the materials is taxable income to your business (you cannot reduce your business income by the cost of materials you personally consumed) and represents drawings from the business. If you don't declare this, you are understating business income. The correct treatment: record the market value of materials taken as a "drawing" (reducing your equity/capital account) and include the value in your business turnover. If you are VAT-registered, you must account for output VAT on the market value of the goods taken for personal use — this is a "deemed supply" under the VAT Act.

**Q: 186** I "employ" my 16-year-old son on weekends to help carry tiles. Do I need to register him for UIF and PAYE, and are there child labour laws I need to worry about?
**A:** Under the Basic Conditions of Employment Act, children under 15 cannot be employed (with limited exceptions for family businesses and light work). At 16, your son can work but with restrictions: no hazardous work (construction sites are hazardous), limited hours (not during school hours, maximum hours per week), and he must have a certificate of fitness if required by the Sectoral Determination. Construction work by a 16-year-old raises child labour concerns — tile carrying on an active construction site is arguably hazardous. UIF and PAYE: if he works more than 24 hours per month, UIF applies. PAYE applies if his earnings exceed the tax threshold (currently R95,750 per year for under-65s). The safer approach: keep his hours minimal, ensure no hazardous tasks, pay him an allowance rather than wages, and document that his participation is family assistance, not employment.

**Q: 187** I received a SARS auto-assessment that shows I owe R12,000 more tax than I calculated. The auto-assessment includes income from clients I invoiced but who never paid me. Can SARS tax me on income I never actually received?
**A:** If you are on the invoice (accrual) basis for tax, SARS taxes you when you invoice, not when you receive payment. Most small contractors are automatically on the accrual basis unless they qualify for and elect the cash basis (turnover below R2.5 million and meet other criteria). If you are on the accrual basis, SARS is correct — you are taxed on invoiced income regardless of whether it was paid. However, if the debt has become irrecoverable (you've exhausted collection efforts and can prove the debtor cannot pay), you can claim a bad debt deduction under Section 11(i) of the Income Tax Act. To avoid this issue going forward: consider electing the cash basis if eligible (you are taxed only on income actually received), or write off bad debts promptly and claim the deduction.

**Q: 188** I'm paying my casual labourers cash daily without any records. A labour inspector is coming tomorrow. How much trouble am I in?
**A:** Significant trouble. Paying employees cash without records violates: Section 33 of the BCEA (payslip requirement), Section 4 of the UIF Act (failure to register and contribute), Section 81 of COIDA (failure to register), and potentially the Income Tax Act (failure to deduct PAYE if applicable). The labour inspector can issue compliance notices for each violation, with fines escalating for repeat offences. For a first offence, expect: a written warning and compliance order, fines per employee per violation (potentially R10,000+ total), and a requirement to regularise all workers within a specified period. Your best course of action: be cooperative, acknowledge the issue, explain that you are a small business learning compliance, and commit to immediate regularisation. The inspector has discretion — cooperation and genuine intent to comply often result in more lenient treatment than confrontation.

**Q: 189** I used my personal vehicle for both business and private use. Can I claim the full cost of the vehicle (fuel, maintenance, insurance) as a business expense?
**A:** No. You can only claim the portion of vehicle expenses attributable to business use. SARS requires a logbook recording business and private kilometres. The deductible amount is calculated as: (business kilometres / total kilometres) x total vehicle expenses. If you don't keep a logbook, SARS will disallow the deduction entirely. You cannot claim the full cost unless the vehicle is used 100% for business (rare for a small contractor who drives home). Keep a detailed logbook: date, starting odometer, destination, purpose of trip, and closing odometer. SARS audits vehicle claims frequently — inadequate documentation is one of the most common reasons for additional tax assessments.

**Q: 190** I bought a laptop and smartphone for my tiling business. Can I claim the full cost in the year of purchase, or must I depreciate them over several years?
**A:** For income tax purposes, electronic equipment falls into different wear-and-tear categories. Laptops and smartphones are typically written off over 3 years (one-third per year) under SARS wear-and-tear allowances. However, if the cost of each item is relatively low (under R7,000 per item), SARS may allow immediate write-off as a "small item" under the de minimis principle. If you are VAT-registered, you can claim the input VAT in the period of purchase. Keep all invoices and record the assets in an asset register. When you eventually sell or dispose of the assets, there may be tax implications (recoupment of wear-and-tear or capital gains). For a small contractor, the key point is: claim the deduction over the wear-and-tear period (3 years), not all at once, unless the item falls below the small asset threshold.

## 13. THE TRULY BIZARRE — EDGE CASES & STRANGE BUT REAL

**Q: 191** A client's cat died while I was tiling her house. She says the tile adhesive fumes poisoned it and she wants R25,000 for "emotional damages" and a new purebred cat. The cat was 16 years old. Am I liable?
**A:** Cementitious tile adhesives contain Portland cement, sand, and polymer additives — none of which release toxic fumes at concentrations that would harm a cat at normal room ventilation. A 16-year-old cat has exceeded the average lifespan and death from natural causes is far more likely than adhesive poisoning. For liability: (a) she must prove causation (the adhesive caused the death) — a veterinary post-mortem would be required, (b) she must prove negligence (you used the product incorrectly or failed to ventilate), (c) emotional damages for pet death are limited in South African law — pets are property, and damages are limited to replacement value (not emotional value). Her claim is highly unlikely to succeed. Do not admit any connection between your work and the cat's death. Offer sympathy but not compensation. If she sues in Small Claims Court, her lack of evidence will doom her case.

**Q: 192** I was tiling a synagogue and accidentally damaged a mezuzah (a small religious scroll case) on the door frame. The rabbi says it must be replaced by a kosher scribe at a cost of R8,000 and the room must be "re-consecrated." Am I liable for the religious ceremony costs, or just the physical damage?
**A:** Under South African delict law, liability is limited to patrimonial loss — actual financial loss directly caused by your negligence. The replacement cost of the mezuzah (the physical object) is your liability. The cost of a kosher scribe and religious ceremony is more complex: if the ceremony is a necessary part of the replacement process (i.e., a mezuzah is not considered valid without proper installation by a scribe), that cost may be part of the direct loss. However, "re-consecration" of the room is likely a separate religious matter, not a direct financial loss caused by the physical damage. Offer to pay for the mezuzah replacement and the scribe's installation. Dispute the re-consecration cost as not being patrimonial loss — it is a spiritual/religious matter, not compensable in a South African court. Be respectful but firm.

**Q: 193** A client's pet python escaped its enclosure while I was working and slithered under the newly laid tiles through an expansion joint gap. The client wants me to remove the tiles to rescue the snake. I haven't been paid yet. Do I have to destroy my work to rescue a snake?
**A:** This is a truly unusual situation. You do not have a legal obligation to destroy your completed work to rescue a pet, even one trapped in the structure. However, as a matter of practicality and goodwill: if the tiles have not yet fully set (within 24–48 hours of installation), removing a few tiles to rescue the snake is feasible without destroying the entire installation. After the adhesive has cured, removing tiles causes damage. Your position: the snake's escape is not your fault — you did not create the expansion joint gaps (they are part of normal tiling practice), and you did not release the snake. Offer to remove a minimal number of tiles at the client's cost (materials and labour) if the rescue is possible with minimal damage. Do not agree to remove the entire floor. If the client refuses to pay because of the snake situation, that is not a valid defence — the work was completed and payment is due.

**Q: 194** I tiled a floor for a client who is a sangoma. She now says the tile pattern I created "disrupted the ancestral energy lines" in her consultation room and is causing her to lose clients. She wants a full refund and R30,000 for lost income. Is this claim legally valid?
**A:** No. A claim for lost income due to "disrupted ancestral energy lines" is not a recognised cause of action under South African law. Courts do not recognise spiritual or supernatural harm as compensable damage unless it involves recognised delictual wrongs (e.g., defamation, theft, property damage). The tiles were installed as a physical product — their alleged spiritual effects are not a defect under the CPA or any building standard. Her claim would fail in any court. However, be culturally sensitive in your response — explain that while you respect her beliefs, your obligation is limited to the physical installation of tiles, and there is no defect in the workmanship or materials. If she sues in Small Claims Court, the commissioner will dismiss the claim on the basis that no legally recognisable damage has been established.

**Q: 195** I was tiling a house and the client's husband accused me of having an affair with his wife because I was "in the house too long." He threatened to assault me and told me to leave immediately. I'd completed 70% of the work and hadn't been paid. What do I do?
**A:** Your personal safety is the priority — leave immediately if threatened with violence. Once safe: document everything (the threat, what was said, any witnesses), lay a charge of assault or crimen injuria at SAPS, and contact the wife (your contracting party) in writing to confirm the work suspension and the reason. The husband's threats constitute breach of the peaceful possession obligation — the client must provide a safe working environment. You are entitled to: (a) payment for work completed (quantum meruit), (b) costs of materials ordered, (c) demobilisation costs, and (d) lost profit on the uncompleted portion (if you can prove it). The marital dispute is between the spouses — do not engage in it. Communicate only with your contracting party (the wife) in writing. If she confirms the contract continues, arrange access when the husband is not present.

**Q: 196** A client asked me to install "smart tiles" with embedded LED lights she bought from a Chinese website. The tiles have no SANS certification, no CE marking, and no IP rating. I refused because they're electrically unsafe. She's threatening bad reviews. Should I stand my ground?
**A:** Absolutely stand your ground. Installing uncertified electrical products is a criminal offence under the Electrical Installation Regulations (OHSA) and the National Regulator for Compulsory Specifications Act. If the LED tiles cause a fire or electrocution, you face criminal prosecution, civil liability, and professional ruin. No certification (SANS, CE, or NRCS mark) means the product has not been tested for South African electrical safety standards. IP rating (Ingress Protection) is critical for any electrical product in a bathroom or wet area — without it, the product is dangerous. Explain to the client that you are legally prohibited from installing uncertified electrical products and that her supplier has sold her an illegal product in South Africa. Offer to install certified LED lighting solutions from reputable local suppliers instead. Bad reviews are preferable to a criminal record or a death on your conscience.

**Q: 197** I was tiling in a client's house and accidentally broke a framed photo of her deceased mother. She's demanding R50,000 for the "irreplaceable sentimental value." The frame was worth R200. How much am I actually liable for?
**A:** Under South African law, damages for damage to property are limited to patrimonial loss — the actual financial value of the item. For a framed photograph, this is: the cost of the frame (R200) plus the cost of reprinting the photograph (if a negative or digital file exists). South African courts do not award damages for sentimental value of personal items unless the item is unique and has objective value (e.g., an antique, an original artwork). A standard framed photograph, while sentimentally valuable, is not irreplaceable if the image can be reprinted. Offer to pay for: (a) a replacement frame of similar quality, (b) professional photo restoration and reprinting, and (c) a sincere apology. If no digital file or negative exists, the emotional impact is unfortunate but not legally compensable beyond the frame's actual value.

**Q: 198** A client wants me to tile a room that she uses for marijuana cultivation. The tiles are for the grow room floor. The cultivation is illegal (she has no medical licence). If I do the work, am I committing a crime?
**A:** Under the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992 and the recent Constitutional Court ruling (Prince v President), private cannabis use and cultivation for personal use is decriminalised (up to a reasonable quantity for personal use). However, commercial-scale cultivation without a licence remains illegal. If the grow room is clearly for commercial sale (not personal use), you may be committing the common law crime of aiding and abetting an illegal activity by facilitating it. While prosecution of a tiler is unlikely, you are associating with criminal activity. If police raid the premises, you may be questioned and your tools seized as evidence. The ethical and legal answer is: decline the job if the cultivation is clearly illegal commercial activity. If it is for personal medical use (with a valid prescription), the legal risk is minimal.

**Q: 199** I completed a tiling job and the client paid me. Two weeks later, she called to say her feng shui consultant says the tiles are laid in an "inauspicious direction" and I must remove and relay them facing a different way — at my cost. Do I have any obligation?
**A:** No. Feng shui is not a building standard, not part of your contract, and not a defect under the CPA or any South African law. Tile direction is an aesthetic choice — unless the contract specified a particular direction, you laid the tiles in a professional manner and the work is complete. The client's post-hoc aesthetic preference, however motivated, does not create a warranty obligation. Offer to relay the tiles at her cost as a variation (full price for removal and reinstallation), but do not accept this as a warranty claim. If she threatens non-payment for future work or negative reviews, remind her that the work was completed to specification and paid for — the feng shui issue is a personal preference that arose after completion and acceptance.

**Q: 200** I've been a tiling contractor for 20 years. A client asked me the strangest question ever: "If my house burns down, will the tiles survive, and will you reinstall them for free?" What's the actual answer?
**A:** Ceramic and porcelain tiles are non-combustible and can survive temperatures up to 1,000°C+ — they may survive a house fire while the rest of the house burns. However, the adhesive and grout will likely fail due to thermal shock and the screed/subfloor will be damaged. "Surviving" tiles will almost certainly need to be removed because the substrate beneath them is compromised. Regarding reinstallation: your warranty covers defects in workmanship under normal conditions — a house fire is not a workmanship defect. If the fire was caused by your negligence (e.g., faulty electrical work you did — though you shouldn't be doing electrical work as a tiler), you might have liability. Otherwise, fire damage is an insurance matter — the client's building insurance covers rebuilding, including new tiling. No, you do not reinstall for free after a fire. Charge your normal rates for the rebuild — insurance typically covers contractor costs.
