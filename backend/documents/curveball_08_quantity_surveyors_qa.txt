# PERSONA 8: QUANTITY SURVEYORS — 200 AWKWARD, STRANGE & UNEXPECTED Q&A

---

## WASTAGE ALLOWANCES & MEASUREMENT QUIRKS

**Q:** 1. A client is insisting we only allow 3% wastage on a 600x600mm porcelain tile laid in a herringbone pattern in an L-shaped room with six doorways. Is 3% legally defensible under the ASAQS guidelines?
**A:** No. ASAQS guidelines and standard QS practice specify 5% wastage for standard straight-lay, 7% for diagonal, 10-12% for herringbone, and 12-15% for mosaic patterns. Herringbone in an irregular room with multiple cuts at doorways realistically requires 10-15%. If you price at 3%, you will face a substantiation claim within weeks. Document the pattern requirement in the bill of quantities and apply the correct percentage per SMM7 Section 9.

**Q:** 2. A developer wants me to measure tiling nett — no wastage at all — because he says the contractor should "know how to order properly." Can I issue a BOQ with zero wastage allowance?
**A:** Legally you can, but it is professionally negligent. SMM7 requires quantities to include an allowance for wastage as a percentage of the nett area. A BOQ with 0% wastage is an express invitation to disputes, variation claims, and contractor failure. The contractor will either inflate their rate to cover hidden wastage or claim every offcut as a variation. Include the standard percentage and note it in the Preambles.

**Q:** 3. We have a circular entrance foyer with a 6m diameter. The tiling is a 300x300mm mosaic medallion pattern radiating from centre. What wastage percentage applies, and how do I justify it to the quantity surveyor auditing my bill?
**A:** Circular rooms with radial patterns attract 15-20% wastage due to radial cuts and pie-shaped offcuts. Every tile around the perimeter requires a unique angled cut. ASAQS guidelines recognise that geometric irregularity increases cutting waste. Break it out as a separate provisional item with detailed method statement. Reference SANS 1449 for tile dimension tolerances that may compound the waste factor.

**Q:** 4. A builder has claimed that my 5% wastage allowance on a straight-lay 1200x600mm large-format tile is too high because "the tiles are bigger so fewer cuts." Is there any validity to this?
**A:** Surprisingly, some validity. Large-format tiles (1200x600mm) laid straight in regular rooms can operate at 4-5% wastage, at the lower end of the range, because there are fewer tiles per m2 and fewer grout lines. However, the critical risk shifts from number-of-cuts to breakage during handling and the financial value of each broken tile. One broken 1200x600 tile = 0.72m2 lost. Keep 5% but note the handling risk. If the room is irregular or has many penetrations, revert to 6-7%.

**Q:** 5. I measured a complex tiling layout using BIM software that calculated exact cut pieces. Can I reduce the wastage allowance to 2% since the cuts are pre-determined?
**A:** No. BIM optimises cutting layouts but cannot eliminate manufacturing shade variation, breakage during transport, tiles damaged by other trades, or correction of installation errors. SMM7 still requires a minimum wastage provision. BIM-generated cutting schedules are useful for ordering but do not override the need for contingency. Maintain at least 5% even with BIM optimisation.

**Q:** 6. The architect has specified a feature wall with three different tile sizes in a random modular pattern (600x600, 600x300, and 300x300mm mixed). How do I calculate wastage when the ratio between sizes isn't fixed?
**A:** This is a measurement nightmare. When ratios are unspecified, apply the highest wastage percentage to all three sizes — 10-12% — because random modular patterns generate unpredictable cuts. Insist the architect specifies either the exact ratio (e.g., 40:30:30) or that the pattern be treated as a proprietary supply-and-install package at a provisional sum. Do not attempt to measure this as three separate items with standard wastage.

**Q:** 7. A contractor has returned a tender with zero wastage allowance but his unit rate per m2 is 15% higher than the next tenderer. Should I normalise the rates or accept them as stated?
**A:** Normalise during tender analysis. Extract the wastage component from both rates and compare on a like-for-like basis. The contractor has likely loaded the wastage cost into the all-in rate. This is a common tender strategy — it shifts risk to the contractor but makes rate comparison difficult. Create a tender analysis sheet showing the nett rate, wastage component, and loaded rate for each tenderer. Report both figures in your tender recommendation.

**Q:** 8. For a 2000m2 warehouse floor, the specification requires expansion joints in a 1.2m x 1.2m grid pattern. Do I deduct the area of the expansion joints from the tiling quantity, and do I measure the joint filler separately?
**A:** Yes to both. The area occupied by expansion joints (approximately 0.5-1% of total floor area depending on joint width) is deducted from the tiling quantity. The joint filler — whether brass strip, PVC, or compressible filler — is measured as a separate linear item in metres. The joint depth and width determine the filler quantity. Note that the grid pattern also increases cutting waste to 7-8% due to the additional perimeter cuts at each bay.

**Q:** 9. A swimming pool has a 150mm wide mosaic waterline band, but the pool perimeter is 85m long and curved. The mosaic comes in 300x300mm sheets. How do I measure the cutting waste for a curved band?
**A:** The curved geometry means every sheet requires partial cutting to follow the radius. Measure the linear metres of waterline band as a separate item, then apply 20-25% cutting waste for curved application. The individual tesserae (small mosaic pieces) allow some flexibility to follow curves, but the sheet backing must be cut to match the curvature. If the radius is tighter than 3m, the waste approaches 30% as individual tesserae must be laid rather than full sheets.

**Q:** 10. I need to re-measure a completed project for final account. The as-built shows 5% more tiling area than my BOQ because the contractor chose his own layout to minimise cuts. Do I pay the extra 5% area?
**A:** No, unless the extra area represents additional work instructed or agreed. The contractor's choice of layout to optimise his own efficiency does not entitle him to payment for additional area beyond the measured works. Re-measurement is based on nett completed dimensions per the contract drawings or architect's instructions, not the contractor's layout preference. If the contractor used a different laying direction that increased coverage area without instruction, that is his commercial risk.

## LABOUR CONSTANTS & PRODUCTIVITY

**Q:** 11. The project is a hotel with 450 identical bathrooms. Can I apply a learning curve factor to reduce the tiling labour constant from 0.5 hrs/m2 to 0.35 hrs/m2 for bathrooms after the first 50?
**A:** Yes, but with caution. Repetitive identical units allow a learning curve factor of 15-20% reduction after the first batch. However, the labour constant must still comply with the Basic Conditions of Employment Act — you cannot reduce the constant below what a competent tiler reasonably requires to achieve the specified quality. Document the learning curve assumption in the cost report. Typical progression: Batch 1-50 at 0.5 hrs/m2, Batch 51-200 at 0.42 hrs/m2, Batch 201-450 at 0.38 hrs/m2.

**Q:** 12. A contractor has priced tiling at 0.25 hrs/m2 for 1200x600mm large-format tiles. This seems impossibly fast. Is there a minimum labour constant I should enforce?
**A:** 0.25 hrs/m2 (4m2 per hour) is achievable for large-format tiles in ideal conditions — large open areas, good access, straight lay, minimal cutting, experienced tilers. However, it is at the extreme low end. The standard range is 0.3-0.4 hrs/m2. If the contractor has underpriced and later discovers he needs 0.4 hrs/m2, he may cut quality to meet productivity targets. Include a quality hold point at 10% completion to verify workmanship before the full area is laid.

**Q:** 13. For tiling to a curved feature wall with 200mm radius columns, what labour constant multiplier should I apply, and is there an ASAQS reference?
**A:** Curved surfaces attract a 30-50% premium on the standard labour constant due to the additional cutting, template-making, and slower placement. A column with 200mm radius is particularly challenging — every tile requires a cut, and the back of each tile may need buttering to conform. Apply a 1.5x multiplier to the base labour constant. For 0.5 hrs/m2 base, use 0.75 hrs/m2. There is no specific ASAQS publication on curved tiling, but the principle of geometric complexity is well established in QS practice.

**Q:** 14. A project requires tiling to be done in two phases — Phase 1 in March (end of rainy season) and Phase 2 in July (dry season). Should I use different labour constants for the two phases due to weather impact?
**A:** Yes, if the work is external or in unenclosed buildings. External tiling during March in KZN or Mpumalanga may face rain delays, slower adhesive curing, and reduced productivity. Apply a 10-15% weather productivity factor to Phase 1. For enclosed buildings with weatherproofing, the factor reduces to 5%. This should be noted in the cost plan assumptions. If the contract is fixed price lump sum, the contractor carries this risk and you need not adjust constants.

**Q:** 15. I'm pricing tiling for a high-security prison. The contractor must pass through four security checkpoints, tool counts are required at each entry/exit, and work can only occur between 08:00 and 15:00. What labour constant adjustment applies?
**A:** This is an extreme but real scenario. The effective working day is reduced from 8 hours to 7 hours (with setup and shutdown time at each checkpoint). Tool counts and security screening add 30-45 minutes per day per worker. Apply a 1.25x multiplier to standard labour constants. Additionally, the contractor's preliminaries will increase due to security vetting costs, dedicated supervisors, and restricted material delivery windows. This is a project-specific risk that justifies a separate adjustment in the cost plan.

**Q:** 16. The specification requires all floor tiles to be laid diagonally in every room. The architect says this is "standard and shouldn't cost more." What is the actual labour cost difference I should include?
**A:** Diagonal laying increases the labour constant from 0.4-0.5 hrs/m2 (straight lay) to 0.6-0.7 hrs/m2 — a 40-50% increase in labour cost. Additionally, diagonal patterns increase cutting waste from 5% to 7% and adhesive consumption may rise 5-10% due to the additional cut edges requiring bedding. The architect's assertion is incorrect. Document the cost differential in your cost plan and flag it as a potential value engineering item if the budget is constrained.

**Q:** 17. A shopping mall project requires tiling to be done only during night shifts (22:00 to 06:00) while the mall remains operational. How do I adjust the labour constant and rates?
**A:** Night shift work attracts a minimum 1.5x multiplier on the base labour constant. Specific factors: reduced lighting quality increases layout and levelling time by 10-15%; night shift premium wages (typically 1.3-1.5x day rate); fatigue factor reducing productivity by 10% in the early morning hours; security and access coordination overhead. The total effective cost increase is 60-80% above day rates. Additionally, noise restrictions may limit power tool use. This should be a separate cost centre in the estimate.

**Q:** 18. For a herringbone pattern with 100x600mm wood-look planks, the contractor claims he needs 0.9 hrs/m2. Is this excessive, or should I accept it?
**A:** 0.9 hrs/m2 is at the upper end but justifiable for herringbone with long thin planks. The standard range for herringbone is 0.7-0.9 hrs/m2. The 100x600mm format requires careful alignment at every joint to maintain the zigzag pattern. Each tile must be precisely placed because errors compound geometrically. For context, the labour cost at R120/hour would be R108/m2 just for labour — before material, adhesive, or profit. If budget is an issue, consider a 200x1200mm plank in a simpler pattern.

**Q:** 19. I need to price tiling to a staircase with 24 steps, each 1.2m wide, with bullnose nosing tiles. The tread is 280mm and riser 175mm. What is the all-in labour constant?
**A:** Stair tiling is measured separately from floor tiling and attracts a significant premium. Measure treads and risers separately in m2, then apply a labour constant of 0.8-1.0 hrs/m2 compared to 0.5 hrs/m2 for floors. The bullnose nosing adds cutting complexity and the need for template work. Each step requires at least 4 cuts (2 per tile for tread, 2 for riser). Total area = 24 steps x 1.2m x (0.28m + 0.175m) = 12.96m2. Labour = 12.96m2 x 0.9 hrs/m2 = 11.66 hours. Price as a separate item to floor tiling.

**Q:** 20. A data centre project requires anti-static vinyl tile flooring (not ceramic). The labour constant is 0.3 hrs/m2. But the specification requires full adhesion with conductive adhesive and copper earthing strips at 3m intervals. Should I adjust the constant?
**A:** Yes. The conductive adhesive application is more critical than standard — it requires 100% coverage with no voids, and the copper earthing strips add layout and installation time. Increase the labour constant to 0.4-0.45 hrs/m2. The earthing strips are measured separately in linear metres. Additionally, the conductive adhesive is 40-60% more expensive than standard, and applicators may need specialist training. This is a specialist trade — not all tiling contractors are competent. Consider a provisional sum for specialist supply and install.

## PROVISIONAL SUMS, PC RATES & PRIME COST ITEMS

**Q:** 21. The architect has specified an imported Italian marble tile but provided no indication of price. Should I make it a provisional sum or a PC rate, and what's the difference in contract administration?
**A:** A PC (Prime Cost) item is a monetary allowance for the supply of an item where the exact product is known but the price is not yet finalised. A provisional sum is for work where neither the scope nor the price is fully determined. For known-specification Italian marble, use a PC rate with a best-estimate value based on recent comparable imports. The difference: PC rates are adjusted against the actual invoice with the contractor's attendance and profit applied; provisional sums require full substantiation of both cost and scope. PC items give the client more cost certainty and simpler final account administration.

**Q:** 22. A provisional sum of R850,000 was included for tiling. The actual cost came to R1.2 million. The contractor is demanding payment of the full R1.2 million plus 15% attendance and profit. Is this correct?
**A:** Not automatically. Under JBCC or FIDIC contracts, provisional sums are expended only on the architect's instruction. The contractor must provide full substantiation — quotations, invoices, labour records — for the amount expended. The 15% attendance and profit applies only to the nett amount properly expended, not the ceiling of the provisional sum. If the overspend was due to contractor inefficiency or poor procurement, the excess may be disallowed. The final account must show the breakdown: provisional sum allowed, actual cost substantiated, attendance and profit calculated on actual, nett adjustment.

**Q:** 23. The client wants to nominate a specific tile supplier and have the contractor purchase from them. How do I structure this in the BOQ to avoid a double margin situation?
**A:** Structure it as a nominated subcontract or a PC item with restricted procurement. In the BOQ, include the estimated tile supply value as a PC rate. State that the contractor's attendance and profit on PC items is limited to 10% (or as specified in the contract). The contractor cannot add his full margin on top of a price he has no control over. If the supplier is nominated, the contractor's risk is limited to handling, storage, and installation — his margin should reflect only those activities.

**Q:** 24. A provisional sum was included for tiling to areas "as indicated on drawings." During construction, additional areas not shown on drawings were identified as requiring tiling. Is this a variation or expenditure of provisional sum?
**A:** This is a classic QS trap. If the provisional sum was for "tiling to areas shown on drawings" and additional areas are identified, the additional work is a variation — it falls outside the described scope of the provisional sum. The provisional sum is expended only against the originally contemplated work. Additional work requires an architect's instruction and is priced as a variation at contract rates if applicable, or daywork if no rates exist. Document the distinction carefully in the interim payment certificates.

**Q:** 25. The client supplied tiles directly to site (client's own purchase). The contractor now wants 25% attendance and profit on the tile value for handling and installation. Is 25% reasonable?
**A:** 25% is excessive for client-supplied materials. Standard practice for contractor attendance on client-supplied goods is 5-10% of the material value for handling, storage, and protection, plus the full installation rate. The 25% suggests the contractor is trying to recover lost supply margin. Under the CPA, excessive margins may be challenged as unfair. A fair breakdown: 7.5% for handling/storage, plus the measured installation rate (labour, adhesive, tools, profit on labour). Push back with market benchmarks.

**Q:** 26. I included a PC rate of R450/m2 for porcelain tiles based on a 2024 price list. The project starts in 2026 and the supplier has increased prices by 18%. Who carries the escalation — client or contractor?
**A:** Escalation on PC items is typically carried by the client if the contract includes a price adjustment clause (e.g., JBCC Formula B or C). Without a price adjustment clause, the contractor carries the risk if he signed at 2024 rates. However, PC rates are intended to be adjusted to actual cost — the contractor should notify the QS of the price increase and request an adjustment to the PC rate before placement of the order. If the contractor proceeded without notification, he may have accepted the risk. Review the contract's provisions on PC rate adjustment.

**Q:** 27. A subcontractor has priced tiling at R380/m2 all-in, but wants me to include a provisional sum of R50,000 for "adhesive and grout selection pending final colour confirmation." Is this legitimate?
**A:** Questionable. Adhesive and grout are standard inclusion items in any tiling rate. Unless the specification requires a specialised adhesive system (e.g., epoxy grout, SBR-modified adhesive for submerged applications), adhesive and grout should be included in the all-in rate. The R50,000 provisional sum suggests the subcontractor has either underpriced the main work or is uncertain about the specification. Request a detailed breakdown of what the R50,000 covers. If it's just for standard adhesive, reject it as a duplication.

**Q:** 28. The specification lists three acceptable tile suppliers (A, B, and C) with "or equal approved." I included a PC rate based on Supplier A's pricing. The contractor chose Supplier C whose tiles are 25% cheaper. Does the client keep the saving?
**A:** Yes, subject to the architect's approval of the substitution. Under SMM7 and standard contract conditions, if the contractor achieves a saving against a PC item through legitimate procurement, the saving accrues to the client. The contractor is entitled only to his attendance and profit on the actual amount expended (10% on the lower cost). However, the contractor cannot substitute without written approval. If approved, adjust the PC item in the final account to reflect actual cost.

**Q:** 29. I need to include a provisional sum for tiling repairs during the defects liability period. How much should I allow, and how is it different from the retention fund?
**A:** A provisional sum for DLP repairs is unusual — normally, defects are rectified by the contractor at his own cost during the DLP. However, if the client wants a cash allowance for engaging third parties to rectify defects (e.g., if the original contractor is insolvent), include 2-3% of the tiling contract value as a contingency, not a provisional sum. The retention fund (5-10% of certified amounts) is the primary security. A separate provisional sum would only apply in specific high-risk scenarios — document the rationale if you include it.

**Q:** 30. The contract includes a PC rate for tiles but the nominated supplier has gone into business rescue. The contractor sourced equivalent tiles from another supplier at 30% higher cost. Who pays the difference?
**A:** The client pays the difference if the nominated supplier was specified by the client or architect. The contractor fulfilled his obligation by sourcing an equivalent approved product. The 30% increase is a risk of nominated supply — the client accepted this risk when nominating. Document the situation, obtain architect's approval for the equivalent product, and adjust the PC rate to the actual cost plus contractor attendance. This is not a contractor risk unless the contractor unilaterally changed suppliers without approval.

## ESCALATION, INDICES & CURRENCY

**Q:** 31. Should I use CPIX, PPI, or the BER building cost index for escalation on a tiling contract with a 14-month construction period? The contract uses JBCC Formula B.
**A:** JBCC Formula B uses a weighted formula combining CPIX (Consumer Price Index) and PPI (Producer Price Index) with specific indices for labour and materials. For tiling specifically, the labour component typically uses the CPIX wage index (unskilled, semi-skilled, skilled), while materials use the PPI for building materials. The BER building cost index is useful for conceptual estimates but JBCC contracts require the formula specified in the contract data. Check the contract — it will specify which indices apply. Do not arbitrarily select an index.

**Q:** 32. A tiling contract is valued at R4.2 million with 60% imported tiles from Italy. The Rand has depreciated 12% against the Euro since tender. The contract has no price adjustment clause. What are the contractor's options?
**A:** Without a price adjustment clause (JBCC Formula A = fixed price), the contractor carries the currency risk. His options are limited: (1) absorb the loss; (2) claim a variation if the contract has a "material adverse change" clause (rare in standard JBCC); (3) attempt to renegotiate; (4) if the depreciation was due to unforeseeable government action, argue frustration of contract (very high legal threshold). This is exactly why contractors should hedge currency exposure or include escalation clauses for import-heavy contracts. The contractor's only realistic recourse is commercial negotiation — he has no contractual entitlement.

**Q:** 33. I need to include forward cover costs in my estimate for imported tiles. What percentage should I allow for currency hedging, and who typically arranges it?
**A:** Forward cover costs vary with tenor and volatility but typically range from 1.5-4% of the import value for 6-12 month cover. In the current market (2025), 1-year EUR/ZAR forward points cost approximately 2-3%. The party arranging forward cover depends on the procurement structure: if the contractor imports directly, he arranges cover; if the supplier imports, the supplier's cost is built into their price. For QS estimates, add 2-3% to the landed cost of imported materials for projects exceeding 6 months. Note it as a currency risk provision in the cost plan assumptions.

**Q:** 34. The client wants to fix the tiling contract price for 24 months with no escalation. The tiling value is R6 million, 40% of which is imported. How do I advise on the risk?
**A:** This is high-risk for both parties. A 24-month fixed price with significant import exposure is a gamble on Rand stability. For the contractor, the risk of margin erosion is substantial. Advise the client that a fixed-price arrangement will result in higher tender prices as contractors load risk premiums. Better alternatives: (1) JBCC Formula C (fluctuations in material costs only); (2) a capped escalation clause at 8%; (3) PC items for imported materials with price adjustment. If the client insists on fixed price, expect tenders to be 10-15% higher than formula-adjusted prices.

**Q:** 35. A contractor's tender includes a note: "Rates valid for 30 days only. Escalation thereafter at 1.5% per month." Is this enforceable under South African contract law?
**A:** Tender validity periods are standard and enforceable if clearly stated. The 30-day validity is normal. The 1.5% per month escalation thereafter is a unilateral condition — it becomes enforceable only if accepted by the client. Under the CPA, this could be challenged as an unfair contract term if the escalation rate exceeds actual cost increases. However, for commercial contracts between juristic persons, CPA protection is limited. Best practice: set a 60-90 day tender validity and use a formal escalation formula rather than a flat percentage.

**Q:** 36. I'm preparing a cost plan for a hotel that will be built in two phases, 18 months apart. The Phase 1 tiling cost was R2,800/m2. What escalation factor should I apply to Phase 2?
**A:** Apply the BER non-residential building cost index escalation, which has averaged 5-7% per annum (2022-2024). For 18 months, use a compound factor of 1.08 to 1.10 (8-10% total). However, tiling has two components: labour (escalating at wage inflation, currently 5-6% annually) and materials (more volatile, 4-10% depending on origin). For imported tiles, add currency risk of 2-4%. A reasonable all-in escalation for 18 months: 10-12% for local tiles, 12-16% for imported. Document the split in the cost plan.

**Q:** 37. The project uses terrazzo tiles from India. The supplier quotes in USD but the contract is Rand-denominated. Should I include the exchange rate in the BOQ preambles?
**A:** Yes, absolutely. State the exchange rate used for conversion (e.g., "Rates based on USD/ZAR at 18.50, being the SARB midpoint rate on [date]"). Specify that the contractor is responsible for monitoring exchange rate movement and that any adjustment will follow the contract's escalation formula or, if none exists, the contractor carries the risk. If the exchange rate is volatile, consider making the terrazzo a PC item in USD with Rand conversion at invoice date.

**Q:** 38. A contractor submitted a tender with rates in ZAR but noted that adhesive is priced "at current CIF value plus duties and VAT." Is this a disguised price adjustment clause?
**A:** Effectively yes. This wording converts what appears to be a fixed-price item into a cost-plus element for adhesive. The contractor is passing through import cost risk to the client for a component that should be included in the fixed rate. During tender analysis, either: (1) request clarification and ask the contractor to fix the adhesive cost; (2) quantify the potential exposure — if adhesive is 15% of the tiling cost and import volatility is 10%, the exposure is 1.5% of the tiling value; or (3) exclude this tender as non-compliant if the tender instructions required firm pricing.

**Q:** 39. How do I handle VAT on imported tiles in my cost plan? The tiles are R3.2 million CIF value from China.
**A:** VAT on imports is calculated as follows: Customs Value (CIF) + Import Duty (if applicable) + Customs Clearance Fees = Value for VAT purposes. Then add 15% VAT. For tiles from China, the import duty depends on the HS code — ceramic tiles typically attract 0-15% duty under SADC/EU trade agreements, but Chinese-origin tiles may face anti-dumping duties. Check the specific HS code (e.g., 6907.21 for ceramic flags and paving). Anti-dumping duties on Chinese ceramic tiles have been as high as 70-120% in recent years. Verify current ITAC rulings before finalising the estimate.

**Q:** 40. The client wants to pay the tile supplier directly in Euros and have the contractor invoice only for labour and local materials. What are the VAT implications?
**A:** Complex. If the client imports directly, they become the importer of record and must register for VAT importation if the value exceeds R50,000. The client pays VAT at import (or uses a registered clearing agent). The contractor then invoices only for local labour and materials at 15% VAT on his portion. The total VAT is split between the client's import VAT payment and the contractor's output VAT. However, this structure may breach the contract's payment provisions and creates two VAT points. It also complicates the final account. Advise against direct payment — better to use PC items with clear adjustment mechanisms.

## REMOVAL, DISPOSAL & ENVIRONMENTAL COSTS

**Q:** 41. A renovation project requires removal of 800m2 of existing floor tiles. The tiles were installed in 1987. How do I determine if asbestos testing is required before pricing removal?
**A:** Any building material installed before 2000 is presumed to potentially contain asbestos until proven otherwise. Under the Asbestos Regulations 2020 (OHSA), you must engage a registered asbestos inspector to sample and test the tiles and adhesive before removal work commences. Budget R15,000-25,000 for asbestos inspection and laboratory testing. If asbestos is present (common in vinyl tiles and older adhesives), removal must be performed by a licensed asbestos contractor at R1,500-5,000/ton disposal cost plus specialised removal. Do not price removal as standard demolition without asbestos clearance.

**Q:** 42. The existing tiles test positive for asbestos (2% chrysotile in the adhesive). The removal area is 600m2. What is the all-in removal and disposal cost per m2?
**A:** Asbestos tile removal is significantly more expensive than standard removal. Breakdown: (1) Notification to Department of Labour (required); (2) Licensed asbestos contractor removal: R120-180/m2; (3) Negative air pressure enclosure and HEPA filtration: R15,000-25,000; (4) Worker PPE and decontamination: R8,000-12,000; (5) Licensed landfill disposal at R1,500-5,000/ton (600m2 of tiles = approximately 18-24 tons = R27,000-120,000); (6) Clearance air testing: R12,000-18,000; (7) Waste manifest documentation. Total: R150-250/m2 all-in compared to R45-85/m2 for non-asbestos removal. This should be a separate provisional sum in the BOQ.

**Q:** 43. A shopping mall tenant installation requires removal of 300m2 of tiles that are only 2 years old and in perfect condition. The landlord wants the tenant to pay full removal cost. Is there any salvage value I should deduct?
**A:** Potentially. If the tiles are high-value (natural stone, premium porcelain) and undamaged, they may have salvage value of 10-30% of replacement cost. However, tile removal typically damages 50-70% of tiles due to the bond strength of modern adhesives. For porcelain on cement-based adhesive, expect 80%+ breakage. For natural stone on weak adhesive, salvage may be viable. Deduct estimated salvage value from removal cost, but document the assumption that salvage is at the contractor's risk — if tiles break during removal, the contractor bears the loss.

**Q:** 44. A project in Cape Town requires disposal of 45 tons of broken ceramic tiles. The contractor has quoted R850/ton for landfill disposal. Is this reasonable, and are there recycling alternatives?
**A:** R850/ton is at the upper end of Cape Town landfill rates (R350-650/ton for general construction waste at City of Cape Town facilities). Premium rates may apply for heavy inert waste or if private landfill is used. However, ceramic tile waste is recyclable — companies like Ceramin and certain aggregate producers accept clean ceramic waste at R150-350/ton. The recycled material is crushed for road base or new tile production. Specify in the tender that the contractor must investigate recycling options. Recycling may require transport to a specific facility — factor in transport cost.

**Q:** 45. I'm preparing a cost plan for a green building (GBCSA 6-star). The specification requires 95% of demolition waste to be diverted from landfill. How does this affect my tiling removal and disposal rates?
**A:** The 95% diversion target significantly affects disposal costs. Ceramic and porcelain tiles can be recycled but require separation from other waste streams and transport to approved recycling facilities. Budget R450-750/ton for sorted, recycled tile waste versus R350-650/ton for landfill. The premium is 20-40% due to additional labour for sorting, separate skip bins, transport to recycling facilities, and documentation for GBCSA submission. Include costs for waste audits, weighbridge tickets, and recycling certificates. The contractor must maintain a waste management plan with monthly reporting.

**Q:** 46. A contractor wants to include "disposal to licensed facility" in his rate but intends to dump on an illegal landfill for R80/ton. As the QS, what is my responsibility?
**A:** You have a professional obligation to ensure waste disposal complies with the National Environmental Management: Waste Act 2008. If you know or suspect illegal dumping, you must report it. In your tender evaluation, require contractors to name their intended disposal facility and provide proof of licensing. Include a condition of contract requiring weighbridge tickets and waste manifests for all disposals over 1 ton. The contractor, client, and potentially the QS can face criminal liability for illegal dumping under NEMWA. Do not turn a blind eye.

**Q:** 47. For a strip-out of existing tiles, the adhesive residue on the concrete floor must be ground off to achieve SANS 10109-1 flatness tolerance of 3mm in 3m. What grinding cost should I allow per m2?
**A:** Adhesive grinding/scarifying to achieve tolerance costs R35-65/m2 depending on adhesive type. Cement-based adhesive requires diamond grinding (R45-65/m2). Old latex-modified adhesive may require more aggressive scarifying (R55-75/m2). If the slab also needs self-levelling compound to achieve tolerance, add R65-120/m2. Survey the existing floor with a straightedge before pricing — if the slab itself is out of tolerance, screed correction is needed in addition to adhesive removal. Include a provisional sum for floor preparation until condition is verified.

**Q:** 48. The contract requires removal of existing tiles and disposal, but the site has no road access for a skip bin. Everything must be carried through an occupied office building and loaded 200m away. How do I price this?
**A:** This is a major logistical constraint that multiplies removal cost. Standard removal with skip access: R45-85/m2. Manual carry through occupied building with protection: R120-180/m2. Factors: (1) Work must occur after hours (1.5x labour cost); (2) Floor protection in transit corridors (R15-25/m2 of corridor); (3) Smaller batches due to manual handling (reduced productivity); (4) Dust suppression (negative air units at R5,000/week); (5) Security escort requirements. Measure as a separate item with detailed method statement. This should have been identified during site investigation.

**Q:** 49. A heritage building in the Western Cape requires removal of original 1920s Encaustic cement tiles for restoration. The tiles must be removed intact. What cost provision should I make?
**A:** Encaustic tile removal for restoration is specialist work. Allow R250-400/m2 for careful removal, cleaning, and palletising — compared to R45-85/m2 for standard demolition removal. The tiles must be lifted without breakage, adhesive softened with heat or chemical agents, cleaned by hand, and stored in numbered sequence for reinstatement. Only specialist heritage contractors should perform this work. Include provisional sums for: removal and cleaning (R300/m2), off-site storage (R2,500/month per pallet), and reinstatement (R400-600/m2). The Heritage Western Cape may require approval for removal — factor in application costs and time.

**Q:** 50. A project requires removing tiles from a swimming pool that was built over a sensitive groundwater aquifer. The tiles have copper sulphate leaching into the water. What environmental compliance costs must I include?
**A:** This triggers multiple regulatory requirements. The copper-contaminated water cannot be discharged to stormwater. Costs to include: (1) Water quality testing (R8,000-12,000); (2) Dewatering and water treatment before discharge (R25,000-40,000); (3) Licensed waste disposal of contaminated water (R500-800/kl); (4) Dust suppression during tile removal to prevent inhalation of copper residue; (5) Environmental management plan (R15,000-25,000); (6) DEFF notification if required. Total environmental compliance: R80,000-150,000 before any actual removal work. This must be a standalone provisional sum with specialist input.


**Q:** 51. I need to price tiling to the inside of a circular concrete planter with 1.8m internal diameter and 600mm height. The 300x600mm wall tiles must be cut to follow the curve. What labour constant and wastage apply?
**A:** Tiling to the internal face of a circular planter with 900mm radius is extremely labour-intensive. The 300x600mm tiles must each be cut with a taper to follow the curve, requiring template work and wet-saw cutting for every tile. Apply a labour constant of 1.0-1.2 hrs/m2 (2-2.5x standard wall tiling). Wastage will be 20-25% due to the tapered cuts. The circumference is 5.66m, so wall area = 5.66m x 0.6m = 3.4m2. At 1.1 hrs/m2, labour alone = 3.74 hours. Price as a specialist item, not standard wall tiling.

**Q:** 52. A shopping mall has 24 large circular columns (600mm diameter) clad in polished porcelain. The architect wants the tiles to wrap around with minimal joint visibility. What premium should I apply to the standard column tiling rate?
**A:** 600mm diameter columns with polished porcelain require precise cutting to achieve the "seamless" appearance. The tile width must match or subdivide the circumference (1,885mm). 300x600mm tiles would require 6.28 tiles per circumference, meaning significant cutting to achieve whole-number alignment. Premiums to apply: 40-50% on labour for precision cutting and dry-lay before fixing; 15% on adhesive for full bedding; 10% additional wastage for breakage during cutting of polished tiles. Consider specifying curved/pencil tiles designed for columns if available — they eliminate cutting but carry a material premium of 30-50%.

**Q:** 53. The project includes tiling to a curved staircase wall that rises 3.6m over a 6m run. The radius is 2.4m. How do I measure this, and what labour constant applies?
**A:** Measure the curved wall area in m2 (arc length x height). Arc length for a quarter-circle (typical stair) = 2πR/4 = 3.77m. Area = 3.77m x 3.6m = 13.57m2. However, the 2.4m radius is gentle enough that standard 300x600mm tiles can follow the curve with tapered cuts — no need for full radial cuts. Apply a 20-30% premium on the standard wall tiling labour constant (0.45 hrs/m2 base becomes 0.55-0.60 hrs/m2). The staircase itself (treads/risers) is a separate item with its own labour constant of 0.8-1.0 hrs/m2.

**Q:** 54. An architect has specified 100x600mm planks on a feature wall arranged in a 3D chevron pattern projecting 50mm from the substrate. How do I price this?
**A:** This is essentially custom carpentry masquerading as tiling. The 3D projection requires a timber or metal backing frame, precise angled cuts on every tile, and adhesive with additional build-up. Break the pricing into components: (1) Substrate framing at R250-350/m2; (2) Tile supply at standard rate; (3) Cutting at R180-250/m2 (every tile has at least one compound angle cut); (4) Adhesive build-up at 8-10 kg/m2; (5) Labour at 1.2-1.5 hrs/m2. Total cost likely R650-950/m2. Treat as a specialist provisional sum with shop drawing approval. Do not price as standard wall tiling.

**Q:** 55. A project has a conical roof structure that needs internal tiling — a 45-degree slope tapering to a point. Is this even measurable as tiling, or should it be a specialist cladding item?
**A:** Tiling to a conical surface is technically possible but highly specialised. As the slope approaches 45 degrees, standard tiling methods become unsafe and impractical. The tiles must be individually cut to taper from the base to the apex, and each row has a different circumference. Recommend treating this as a specialist cladding item rather than standard tiling. Labour constant: 1.5-2.0 hrs/m2. Wastage: 25-35%. Consider alternatives: metal cladding, shingles, or a proprietary conical tile system. Price as a provisional sum with a performance specification rather than a measured rate.

## WET AREAS, WATERPROOFING & SCREEDS

**Q:** 56. I'm costing a 50-unit apartment block. Every bathroom requires a 25mm thick screed with falls to the floor waste. How do I quantify the extra screed material compared to a flat screed?
**A:** Falls to waste add 10-15% to screed volume compared to a flat screed of nominal thickness. For a 50m2 bathroom floor with 25mm nominal thickness and 1:80 fall to waste: flat volume = 1.25m3. With falls, the average thickness increases to 28-30mm, volume = 1.4-1.5m3. The 10-15% premium applies to both material and labour. Additionally, the screed requires 3-5 days drying time before tiling (longer than flat screeds due to varying thickness). Include the drying time in the programme — it affects preliminaries.

**Q:** 57. A client wants to know the cost difference between waterproofing under bathroom tiles using a sheet membrane versus a liquid-applied system. What should I tell them?
**A:** Sheet membrane (torch-on or self-adhesive): R85-140/m2 including priming, laps, and detailing. Liquid-applied (polyurethane or cementitious): R65-110/m2 including priming and reinforcement at joints. The sheet system is more expensive but provides consistent thickness and is preferred for commercial applications. Liquid systems are cheaper and easier to apply to complex geometries but require skilled application to achieve the specified dry film thickness. For a 4m2 bathroom, the difference is R80-120 per bathroom — negligible in context. Specify the performance requirement and let the contractor choose the system.

**Q:** 58. A gymnasium project has 12 shower bays requiring floor tiles with falls to linear drains. The specification calls for 600x600mm tiles — is this practical with falls, and what cost impact should I flag?
**A:** 600x600mm tiles in small shower bays (typically 1.2m x 1.2m) with falls to a linear drain is problematic. Large tiles are difficult to cut with the required dual slope, and lippage is likely at the drain edge. Cost impacts: (1) Increased cutting waste to 12-15%; (2) Labour premium of 25-35% for dual-plane cutting; (3) Higher risk of defects and rectification. Recommend 300x300mm or smaller tiles for shower floors, or change to a single-plane fall with a point drain. Flag this as a design development issue before finalising the cost plan.

**Q:** 59. How do I cost the intersection between waterproofing and tiling in a wet area? Should waterproofing be a separate item or included in the tiling rate?
**A:** Always separate them. Waterproofing is a prerequisite trade that must be completed, tested (flood test for 48 hours per SANS 10400-L), and signed off before tiling commences. Measure waterproofing as a separate item in m2 with full-height wall upturns (150-200mm minimum) measured as an extra over. The tiling rate should state "to waterproofed surface prepared ready to receive tiles." Separating them prevents disputes about who is responsible for making good if the waterproofing fails after tiling is complete.

**Q:** 60. A project has a steam room requiring tiles rated for continuous moisture exposure above 50 degrees Celsius. The specification doesn't mention this — should I flag it?
**A:** Absolutely flag it. Standard ceramic wall tiles are not rated for steam room conditions. Steam rooms require: (1) Tiles with water absorption below 0.5% (fully vitrified); (2) Epoxy grout (standard cement grout degrades in steam); (3) SBR-modified or epoxy adhesive; (4) Full tanking waterproofing behind tiles; (5) Expansion joints every 3-4m (not 8-10m). The cost premium over standard bathroom tiling is 40-60%. If the specification doesn't address this, issue a query to the architect. Do not price as standard tiling — you will face a substantial variation claim.

**Q:** 61. The concrete slab in a ground-floor tiling area has no DPM (damp-proof membrane) and shows rising damp. The specification says "tile directly to slab." What do I include in my cost plan?
**A:** This is a latent defect in the substrate, not a tiling issue. Under SANS 10400-H, ground floors require DPM where the site is classified as having damp soil conditions. The correct sequence is: DPM, screed, adhesive, tiles. If no DPM exists, rising damp will destroy the tiling within 2-5 years. Include in your cost plan: (1) DPM installation (retrofit via surface-applied epoxy DPM: R45-75/m2); (2) Screed over DPM (R85-120/m2); (3) Then tiling. Total additional cost: R130-195/m2. Flag this as a design development issue requiring architect's instruction.

**Q:** 62. A hotel swimming pool has mosaic tiles that must be laid to a 3D curved surface (pool floor with gradual depth change). The QS on the completed project allowed standard mosaic labour rates. What should have been allowed?
**A:** 3D curved mosaic application to a pool shell requires 50-80% labour premium over flat wall mosaic application. The mosaic sheets must be cut and manipulated to follow the compound curve of the pool floor-to-wall transition. Labour constant: 1.0-1.3 hrs/m2 compared to 0.6 hrs/m2 for flat walls. Wastage: 15-20% due to cutting and manipulation. Additionally, the adhesive must be applied in stages to prevent sagging on vertical and overhead surfaces. The completed project's QS likely faced a significant variation claim or quality compromise.

**Q:** 63. I'm pricing tiling to a commercial kitchen floor that requires falls to drainage channels. The falls are 1:50. How does this affect my labour constant and material wastage?
**A:** A 1:50 fall is relatively gentle and most floor tiles (down to 200x200mm) can accommodate it without excessive cutting. Labour constant increases by 10-15% because each tile must be checked for level during placement. Wastage increases by 3-5% for edge cutting at drainage channels. The drainage channel itself (stainless steel or PVC) is measured separately in linear metres with appropriate falls built in. Total tiling premium for falls: 15-20% on standard floor tiling rates. Include a provisional sum for screed correction if the existing slab does not have the required falls.

**Q:** 64. A project has 20 balconies requiring tiles with full waterproofing and drip edges. The tiles are 600x600mm porcelain. What additional items must I measure beyond standard floor tiling?
**A:** Balcony tiling requires several additional items: (1) Full waterproofing system (R85-140/m2) with 150mm upturn to walls; (2) Screed with falls to outlet (1:80 minimum); (3) Tile bedding in flexible adhesive (SANS 10109-2 Class 3 recommended for exterior); (4) Movement joints at balcony perimeter and at building expansion joints; (5) Drip edge/trim at balcony edge (stainless or aluminium, measured in linear metres); (6) Balustrade fixing detail — tiles must be cut around post bases. Total additional cost over standard floor tiling: R180-280/m2 for waterproofing and detailing alone.

**Q:** 65. The architect wants a "tanking" specification for a basement plant room floor that will be hosed down regularly. Does tanking add cost beyond standard waterproofing?
**A:** Yes. Tanking implies a fully sealed envelope — floor and walls up to 150mm above highest water level — designed to resist hydrostatic pressure. Standard waterproofing is typically a single-layer application; tanking requires: (1) Multi-layer system (2-3 coats minimum); (2) Reinforced detailing at all penetrations; (3) Coving at floor-wall junction; (4) Flood testing for 72 hours; (5) Independent inspection and certification. Cost: R150-250/m2 for tanking versus R65-140/m2 for standard waterproofing. If there's hydrostatic pressure, also include a subsoil drainage system — this is civil engineering, not just tiling.

## ADHESIVE COVERAGE, SPECIFICATIONS & MATERIAL RATES

**Q:** 66. The specification requires "full bed adhesion" for all floor tiles. How much extra adhesive does this consume compared to standard notched trowel application, and what cost increase should I show?
**A:** Standard notched trowel application uses 4-6 kg/m2 depending on trowel size and tile format. Full bed adhesion (buttering both substrate and tile back) uses 7-10 kg/m2 — an increase of 50-70%. At R12-18/kg for standard flexible adhesive, the cost increase is R36-72/m2. For large-format tiles (1200x600mm), full bed is standard practice to prevent voids and cracking, so include it in the base rate rather than as an extra over. Document the consumption rate in the Preambles.

**Q:** 67. A project specifies epoxy grout for all floor tiles "for stain resistance." The tile area is 2,500m2. What is the cost impact of epoxy grout versus standard cement grout?
**A:** Epoxy grout is 4-6 times more expensive than cement grout. Cost comparison (per m2 of tiling): Cement grout: R15-25/m2. Epoxy grout: R65-120/m2. For 2,500m2, the additional cost is R125,000-237,500. Additionally, epoxy grout requires: (1) Specialist application (labour premium of 20-30%); (2) Strict temperature control during application; (3) Extended curing time; (4) Careful cleaning within 30 minutes of application. If stain resistance is the only driver, consider high-performance cement grout with additives at R30-45/m2 as a value engineering alternative.

**Q:** 68. The specification says "adhesive as per manufacturer's recommendation for the substrate and tile type." As the QS, how do I price this when the tile and substrate are unknown at cost plan stage?
**A:** This is a common but problematic specification. At cost plan stage, make reasonable assumptions and state them clearly: "Adhesive rates based on Class 1 flexible cementitious adhesive at R15/kg, consumption 5 kg/m2 for standard wall tiles, 7 kg/m2 for floor tiles." Include an allowance for primer (R8-12/m2) and grout (R18-25/m2). Add a note: "If the specification requires Class 2 or Class 3 adhesive, epoxy adhesive, or SBR additive, rates will be adjusted pro rata." This protects both the estimate accuracy and the client's budget.

**Q:** 69. A contractor has priced adhesive at R8/kg when the current market rate is R14-16/kg. Should I flag this as a risk in my tender report?
**A:** Yes, absolutely. An adhesive rate of R8/kg suggests the contractor either: (1) Is using inferior non-compliant adhesive; (2) Has made an arithmetic error; (3) Is deliberately buying below cost to win the tender and will substitute later. Under SANS 10109-2, tiling adhesive must meet minimum classification requirements for the application. Flag in your tender report: "Contractor X's adhesive rate of R8/kg is 45% below market rate. This represents a risk of non-compliance with SANS 10109-2 or a future variation claim. Recommend contractor clarification or acceptance of this risk."

**Q:** 70. The project requires tiling over existing vinyl asbestos tile (VAT) flooring that cannot be removed due to heritage restrictions. The new tiles are 10mm porcelain. What adhesive system is required, and how do I cost it?
**A:** Tiling over VAT requires an encapsulation system: (1) Apply a proprietary encapsulation primer/sealer over the VAT (R25-35/m2); (2) Screed over encapsulation layer (40mm minimum, R85-120/m2); (3) Flexible adhesive (Class 2 minimum due to the different substrates); (4) 10mm porcelain tiles. The total system build-up is 55-60mm — check door thresholds, ceiling heights, and transition strips. Total additional cost over standard installation: R130-185/m2. Obtain written confirmation from the adhesive manufacturer that their system is warranted over encapsulated VAT.

**Q:** 71. How do I handle the cost of SBR latex additive that the specification requires "in all wet area adhesives"? Is it a separate item or included in the adhesive rate?
**A:** SBR additive is typically dosed at 5-10 litres per 25kg bag of adhesive and costs R80-120/litre. The additive cost per m2 (at 5 kg/m2 adhesive consumption) is R16-24/m2. Include it as part of the adhesive rate for wet areas, but state the assumption clearly: "Wet area adhesive rates include SBR latex additive at manufacturer's recommended dosage." Do not make it a separate item — it complicates the BOQ and invites disputes about dosage rates. If the specification later requires a different additive (e.g., epoxy), adjust the all-in adhesive rate.

**Q:** 72. A project in Durban requires tiling to external walls. The specification doesn't mention adhesive class. Should I assume standard adhesive or flag the coastal exposure?
**A:** Flag it immediately. Durban's coastal climate (high humidity, salt-laden air, UV exposure) requires Class 3 adhesive for external tiling as a minimum — this provides enhanced flexibility and bond strength. Class 3 adhesive costs 25-35% more than Class 1. If you assume Class 1 and the architect later specifies Class 3 (which he should), the contractor has a legitimate variation claim of R20-35/m2 on adhesive alone. Issue a design query: "External tiling in coastal Durban — confirm adhesive classification required per SANS 10109-2 for external exposure."

**Q:** 73. For a 25mm thick bed of Class 3 adhesive under large-format external tiles, the consumption is 18-20 kg/m2. Is this measurable as "adhesive" or should it be treated as a screed?
**A:** A 25mm adhesive bed is effectively a screed and should be measured as such. Standard adhesive is 3-10mm thick; anything above 15mm is classified as a thick-bed or screed installation under SANS 10109. Measure it as: (1) Screed (25mm, polymer-modified): R120-160/m2; (2) Adhesive bed (3-5mm): R25-35/m2; (3) Tiles at standard rate. The combined system is a proprietary thick-bed installation. Do not simply multiply the kg/m2 rate — the application method, curing, and skill level are different from standard thin-bed adhesive work.

**Q:** 74. The specification requires "white adhesive for all light-coloured translucent tiles." Why is this specified, and what cost premium does it carry?
**A:** Light-coloured or translucent tiles (glass tiles, some porcelains) can show the grey colour of standard cement-based adhesive through the tile body, causing discolouration. White adhesive uses white cement instead of grey Portland cement and costs 15-20% more (R18-22/kg vs R14-18/kg). The premium is R6-10/m2. This is a legitimate specification requirement, not an architect's whim. Include it in the adhesive rate for light-coloured tiles and note the assumption. If the contractor uses grey adhesive and the tiles show staining, it is a defect requiring full replacement.

**Q:** 75. A project has both floor tiles (600x600mm) and mosaic tiles (50x50mm tesserae) in adjacent areas. The adhesive consumption varies dramatically. How do I structure the BOQ to avoid rate confusion?
**A:** Separate the items clearly. Do not combine different tile formats under a single "floor tiling" rate. Structure as: (1) Floor tiling — 600x600mm porcelain, 5 kg/m2 adhesive; (2) Floor tiling — 50x50mm mosaic sheets, 3-4 kg/m2 adhesive but significantly higher grout consumption (2-3x due to more joints). The mosaic's total material cost may be similar despite lower adhesive use because grout is the dominant material. Show adhesive and grout as separate quantities within each item so the rate build-up is transparent. Alternatively, specify that mosaic work is a provisional sum with specialist supply and installation.

## BUILDING TOLERANCES, SUBSTRATE PREPARATION & DAYWORK

**Q:** 76. The specification states the slab must comply with SANS 10109-1 (3mm in 3m flatness). During pre-tiling inspection, 40% of the floor exceeds this tolerance. Who pays for screed correction, and how much should I allow?
**A:** Under JBCC and standard contract conditions, the contractor is responsible for providing a substrate that meets the specified tolerance. If the main contractor handed over a defective slab, the tiling contractor can either: (1) Price the screed correction as a variation (R65-120/m2 for self-levelling compound or R85-140/m2 for screed); or (2) Include it in his tender if he inspected the slab before tendering. If the tiling contractor tendered without inspecting, he may have accepted the risk. As QS, verify the handover documentation. If the slab was accepted "as-is," the tiling contractor's claim may be valid. Allow R85-120/m2 for screed correction as a provisional item until slab condition is verified.

**Q:** 77. A daywork sheet shows a tiler working 11 hours on a Saturday to complete emergency rectification of water-damaged tiles. The rate is R1,200/day. What should the daywork rate be for the overtime hours?
**A:** ASAQS daywork schedules specify: (1) Standard daywork rate: 8 hours at the scheduled rate; (2) Overtime Monday-Saturday: 1.5x hourly rate for hours 9-10, 2x for hours 11-12; (3) Sunday/Public Holiday: 2x for all hours. For R1,200/day (R150/hour): First 8 hours = R1,200. Hours 9-10 at 1.5x = R450. Hours 11 at 2x = R300. Total = R1,950. Plus tool allowance, transport, and supervision. Daywork must be substantiated with time sheets signed by both the contractor and the client's representative. Unsubstantiated daywork claims can be rejected.

**Q:** 78. The contract allows daywork "only when no applicable contract rate exists." A variation requires tiling to a curved surface with no applicable rate. The contractor submits daywork at R1,850/day for a tiler. Is this reasonable?
**A:** R1,850/day for a specialist tiler on daywork is at the upper end but not unreasonable for 2025, depending on location and skill level. The ASAQS Schedule of Daywork Rates (2024/2025) suggests R850-1,200/day for a standard tiler, but specialist tilers (stone, mosaic, curved work) command R1,400-1,800/day. Verify: (1) The tiler's skill level and certification; (2) Whether the rate includes supervision, tools, and transport; (3) Market rates in the specific region (Gauteng vs Eastern Cape). If substantiated, accept it. If not, request a breakdown or apply the contract's default daywork formula ( labour cost + 15% profit + 10% attendance).

**Q:** 79. A contractor's daywork sheet includes "waiting time" of 3 hours because the waterproofing flood test was not completed on schedule. Can waiting time be claimed as daywork?
**A:** Only if the delay was caused by the client or another contractor, and the waiting tiler could not be productively redeployed. Under JBCC, the contractor can claim standing time for his own employees at direct cost plus a percentage for profit and attendance — typically 80-100% of the productive daywork rate. However, the contractor has a duty to mitigate — he should have been given advance notice of the delay and redeployed the tiler to another area. If the contractor knew about the delay and still brought the tiler to site, challenge the claim. Substantiation requirements: written notification of delay, time sheets, and evidence that redeployment was not possible.

**Q:** 80. The main contractor's concrete slab has a 12mm deviation over 3m in the tiling area — four times the SANS 10109-1 tolerance. The tiling subcontractor wants R95/m2 for screed correction. The main contractor says the tiles can "just be laid thicker." Who is right?
**A:** The tiling subcontractor is correct. A 12mm deviation cannot be corrected by thicker adhesive — cement-based adhesive is designed for 3-10mm beds, maximum 15mm in a single application. Above 15mm, adhesive shrinks excessively, loses bond strength, and may crack. Screed correction is required: either self-levelling compound (for deviations up to 20mm) or new screed (for larger deviations). The main contractor is responsible for providing a compliant substrate. As QS, support the tiling subcontractor's claim. If the main contractor refuses, instruct him to rectify at his own cost before tiling commences. R95/m2 for screed correction is reasonable.

**Q:** 81. A project requires tiling to an existing epoxy-coated floor. The epoxy is sound and well-bonded. Can tiles be laid directly over epoxy, and what cost adjustment is needed?
**A:** Yes, but with specific preparation. The epoxy surface must be mechanically abraded (diamond-ground) to create a keyed surface — smooth epoxy will not bond with cementitious adhesive. Cost adjustments: (1) Diamond grinding of epoxy: R25-40/m2; (2) Epoxy primer or bonding bridge: R15-25/m2; (3) Standard adhesive and tiles. Do not allow tiling direct to unabraded epoxy — it will fail. Include the grinding and priming costs in the tiling item or as separate preparation items. Obtain a warranty statement from the adhesive manufacturer confirming compatibility with the existing epoxy system.

**Q:** 82. I'm preparing a final account and the contractor has claimed 340m2 of "additional screed correction" that was not instructed. The screed was within tolerance. How do I deal with this?
**A:** Reject the claim. Uninstructed work is not compensable under any standard contract form. The contractor cannot self-authorise additional work and then claim payment. Your response: "No instruction was issued for screed correction beyond the contract requirements. The slab was within specified tolerance of 3mm in 3m as verified by [inspection date/reference]. The contractor's decision to apply additional screed was at his own risk and cost." If the contractor disputes the tolerance verification, refer to the pre-tiling inspection records. If no records exist, this is a lesson in documentation — always record substrate condition before tiling.

**Q:** 83. For a renovation, the existing floor has multiple layers of old tiles — ceramic over vinyl over asbestos tile. The contractor says removal to substrate will cost R350/m2. Is this reasonable?
**A:** Potentially. Multi-layer removal is exponentially more complex than single-layer: (1) Top ceramic layer: R45-85/m2; (2) Middle vinyl layer with adhesive: R35-55/m2; (3) Bottom asbestos tile layer (licensed removal): R150-250/m2; (4) Substrate grinding and preparation: R45-65/m2. Total: R275-455/m2. The R350/m2 quote is within range. However, if the full depth removal affects floor-to-ceiling heights or door thresholds, include additional costs for threshold modification or screed build-up. Asbestos discovery after work commences is a compensation event — the contractor could not reasonably have known the full extent.

**Q:** 84. The specification says "tiles to be laid to a flat surface." The slab has a 1:100 slope for drainage. Is this a contradiction, and how do I cost it?
**A:** "Flat" in tiling terminology means "level within tolerance" — it does not mean "perfectly horizontal." A 1:100 slope is standard for wet areas and is entirely compatible with tiling. The tiles follow the slope. No cost premium is required for the slope itself, provided it is uniform. However, if the slope is irregular or exceeds 1:50, a screed correction may be needed. Cost the tiling as standard floor tiling but verify the slope is consistent during the pre-start inspection. If the slope varies (e.g., 1:80 in one area, 1:40 in another), screed correction may be needed to achieve uniform drainage — include a provisional sum.

**Q:** 85. A daywork claim includes "foreman supervision" at R2,400/day for a tiling gang of 4 workers. The contract says daywork rates include "all costs incidental to the work." Is supervision claimable separately?
**A:** Depends on the contract wording. Standard JBCC daywork provisions include direct labour, materials, plant, and "incidental costs" but exclude dedicated supervision unless specifically allowed. If the foreman is directly working alongside the gang (hands-on), his cost is included in the all-in daywork rate. If he is purely supervising (not laying tiles), the rate may be claimable as a separate supervision item at a reduced percentage. Typical approach: allow 10-15% on daywork labour for supervision, or a fixed daily supervision rate of R1,200-1,600/day. Check your contract's daywork schedule — if it says "all-inclusive," challenge separate supervision claims.

## CASH FLOW, RETENTION, COMPLETION & CONTINGENCY

**Q:** 86. A tiling subcontract is valued at R3.8 million over 6 months. How do I structure the monthly payment certificates, and what retention should I hold?
**A:** Under JBCC, the main contractor holds 5-10% retention (typically 10% up to 50% of contract value, then 5%). For the subcontractor: (1) Monthly progress payments based on measured completion; (2) Retention at 5-10% of certified value; (3) Half retention released at Practical Completion; (4) Half at Final Completion (end of defects liability period). Monthly certificates require the QS to measure completed work against the schedule of rates. For tiling, this means physically measuring completed areas, not just accepting the contractor's claim. Structure the S-curve: slower at start (layout, setting out), accelerating in months 2-4, tapering in month 5-6 (snagging, rectification).

**Q:** 87. The contractor wants 30% of the tiling value paid as a "mobilisation advance" because he needs to import tiles from Italy. Is this standard practice?
**A:** Mobilisation advances are not standard for tiling works unless the contract specifically provides for them. However, for imported materials with long lead times (8-12 weeks for Italian tiles), a materials advance is justifiable. Structure it as: (1) Materials advance up to 50% of the material value, secured by a bank guarantee or insurance bond; (2) The advance is recovered through payment certificates at 25% per month over 4 months; (3) No advance on labour — that is paid through monthly progress. If the contractor cannot provide a bank guarantee, do not grant an advance. Alternatively, make the tiles a PC item paid directly by the client.

**Q:** 88. At Practical Completion, 5% of the tiling has minor defects (grout colour variation, 3 cracked tiles). Can I certify Practical Completion and hold the full retention, or must I delay certification?
**A:** Under JBCC, Practical Completion can be certified if the works are "substantially complete and can be used for their intended purpose." Minor defects that do not prevent use do not delay Practical Completion. Certify PC with a defects list (snagging list) attached. The retention remains held — half released at PC, half at Final Completion after defects are rectified. The contractor is contractually obliged to rectify defects during the DLP. If defects are major (waterproofing failure, 20% loose tiles), PC is not achievable and the contractor must rectify before certification.

**Q:** 89. A project's tiling contingency allowance is 8% of the tiling value (R5.2m value = R416,000 contingency). The project manager wants to use the contingency to upgrade from ceramic to porcelain tiles mid-project. Is this appropriate?
**A:** No. Contingency is for unforeseen risks and scope changes, not for specification upgrades. Using contingency for an upgrade is a scope change that requires a formal variation order. The correct process: (1) Client instructs the variation via the architect; (2) Contractor prices the nett cost difference; (3) If within budget, the variation is approved and funded from the project's variation reserve or savings elsewhere, not from risk contingency. Contingency is for events like: substrate defects requiring additional screed, discovery of asbestos, force majeure delays, or design errors requiring remedial work.

**Q:** 90. For a R12 million tiling contract, what is an appropriate risk-adjusted contingency, and how do I justify it to the client who wants zero contingency?
**A:** A risk-adjusted contingency considers specific project risks, not just a flat percentage. For a R12m tiling contract: (1) Substrate condition risk (20% probability x R200,000 impact = R40,000); (2) Imported material currency risk (30% x R300,000 = R90,000); (3) Design variation risk (40% x R150,000 = R60,000); (4) Latent defect risk (15% x R400,000 = R60,000). Expected value = R250,000 (2.1%). Add management reserve of 3%. Total: 5-6% (R600,000-720,000). Explain to the client: "Contingency is not spent money — it is a provision for risks. If risks don't materialise, it remains unspent. Zero contingency means the project has no resilience."

**Q:** 91. The contractor's payment application includes 80% of the tiling value but only 55% of the tiling is physically complete. How do I deal with this in the payment certificate?
**A:** Pay only for work properly executed and measured. The QS must physically verify completion percentages against the schedule of rates. If the contractor has overclaimed, issue an interim payment certificate for 55% (or your measured percentage) with a written explanation: "Payment certified at 55% based on physical measurement on [date]. The difference of 25% claimed is not yet due." If the overclaiming is persistent (3+ certificates), escalate to the principal agent. The contractor may be front-loading his cash flow — common but not grounds for overpayment. Consistent overclaiming may also indicate cash flow problems, which is an early warning sign of contractor distress.

**Q:** 92. A tiling subcontractor has gone into business rescue after completing 60% of the works. The main contractor needs to engage a replacement. How is this valued in the final account?
**A:** Under JBCC, if a subcontractor fails, the main contractor may engage a replacement at the best reasonable market rate. The additional cost (if any) over the original subcontract rate is a compensation event if the main contractor was not responsible for the failure. The QS must: (1) Value work completed by the original subcontractor at the original rates; (2) Value remaining work at the replacement subcontractor's rates; (3) Calculate the nett additional cost; (4) Determine if the main contractor contributed to the failure (e.g., late payment). If not, the additional cost is recoverable from the client as a delay/disruption cost. Document everything — the replacement engagement, cost comparison, and programme impact.

**Q:** 93. The client wants to defer 20% of the tiling works (basement level) to Phase 2, 18 months later. The contractor has already ordered tiles for the full project. Who carries the cost of storage and price escalation?
**A:** If the client instructed the deferral, it is a variation/delay event. The contractor is entitled to: (1) Storage costs for tiles held for 18 months (warehouse rental, insurance, handling — R2,500-4,000/month per pallet); (2) Price escalation on tiles if the contract has a price adjustment clause; (3) If fixed price, no escalation but the contractor may claim increased costs as a result of the deferral if it was unforeseeable; (4) Double handling costs. As QS, calculate the storage and handling cost and add it to the Phase 1 final account or carry it forward as a preliminaries item in Phase 2. Document the client's instruction clearly — this is not the contractor's risk.

**Q:** 94. For a project with tiling spread over 14 months, how do I prepare an S-curve cash flow projection, and what monthly percentages should I apply?
**A:** A typical tiling S-curve for a 14-month programme: Months 1-2: 5% (mobilisation, setting out, first areas); Months 3-6: 10%, 15%, 15%, 15% (ramp-up to peak production); Months 7-10: 12%, 10%, 8%, 5% (tapering as areas complete); Months 11-12: 3%, 2% (snagging, final areas); Months 13-14: 5% (retention release, practical completion). This assumes consistent labour and material availability. For projects with long lead times on imported tiles, front-load the material component. The S-curve is used for: contractor cash flow planning, client's funding arrangements, and bank draw-down schedules.

**Q:** 95. The contractor wants retention released early because he has "provided a performance bond covering the full contract value." Is this a valid argument?
**A:** No. A performance bond and retention serve different purposes. The performance bond secures the contractor's performance of the entire contract. Retention secures the quality of completed work during the defects liability period. The bond does not cover defects liability — it expires at Practical Completion. Retention is held until Final Completion to ensure the contractor rectifies defects. Releasing retention early because a bond exists is a misunderstanding of contract security mechanisms. The contractor can, however, request replacement of retention with a retention bond (typically at 1-2% cost of the bond amount) — this releases cash but maintains security for the client.

## PRELIMINARIES, ATTENDANCE & PROFIT

**Q:** 96. A tiling subcontractor's tender includes "preliminaries and general" at 8% of his subcontract value. What should this cover for a tiling-only subcontract?
**A:** For a tiling subcontractor on a residential project, 8% preliminaries should cover: (1) Site supervision and management (R25,000-45,000/month); (2) Small tools and equipment (tile cutters, mixers, trowels — R15,000-25,000 one-off); (3) Site establishment (storage container, signage — R8,000-15,000); (4) Transport and delivery; (5) Quality control and testing; (6) Health and safety (PPE, inductions — R5,000-10,000). 8% is reasonable for a contract over R1 million. For smaller contracts, preliminaries are proportionally higher (12-18%). If the subcontractor's preliminaries exceed 12%, request a breakdown to verify the items are legitimate tiling preliminaries, not loaded profit.

**Q:** 97. The main contractor is charging 15% attendance and profit on the tiling subcontract. The tiling subcontract value is R4.5 million. What does the R675,000 cover, and is 15% reasonable?
**A:** The 15% covers: (1) Builder's attendance — providing access, scaffolding, power, water, cleaning, security (5-8%); (2) Coordination and management of the subcontractor (2-3%); (3) Profit (3-5%); (4) Risk and overhead allocation (2-3%). Total 12-19%. For a R4.5m subcontract, 15% (R675,000) is reasonable. However, if the main contractor is providing minimal attendance (the tiling contractor has his own access, power, and scaffolding), challenge the percentage — it may be excessive. Request a breakdown. If attendance is genuinely limited, 10-12% is more appropriate.

**Q:** 98. A main contractor has included scaffolding in his preliminaries at R180,000 for the entire project. The tiling subcontractor says he needs his own scaffolding for high-level atrium work because the main contractor's scaffolding is being struck. Who pays?
**A:** This is a coordination failure. If the main contractor's scaffolding programme does not cover the tiling work, the tiling contractor must provide his own access equipment — mobile scaffold towers, mast climbers, or temporary scaffolding. The cost (R8,000-15,000/week for mobile towers) is claimable as additional attendance or a variation if the tender documents indicated that scaffolding would be available. As QS, check the tender documents and preliminaries description. If the main contractor's preliminaries stated "scaffolding for all trades," the tiling contractor's additional cost is recoverable from the main contractor. If not stated, the tiling contractor should have allowed for his own access.

**Q:** 99. The tiling contractor wants a dedicated site office because he says the main contractor's site office is "too far from the work area." Is this a legitimate preliminary cost?
**A:** Generally no. Standard site facilities (office, canteen, toilets) provided by the main contractor are for all trades. A dedicated site office for a subcontractor is only justified if: (1) The subcontractor is the sole or primary contractor on a remote section of the site; (2) The main contractor's facilities are demonstrably inadequate; (3) The subcontract value exceeds R10 million (large enough to warrant separate facilities). For a standard tiling subcontract, a dedicated office is an excessive preliminary cost. The subcontractor can use a toolbox meeting area or share office space. If the subcontractor insists, cap the preliminary at a reasonable figure or exclude it from the certified amount.

**Q:** 100. A contractor's preliminaries include "B-BBEE verification and compliance costs" of R35,000. Is this a legitimate preliminary item for a private commercial project?
**A:** B-BBEE compliance costs are legitimate business costs, but their treatment as a preliminary item depends on the project. For public sector projects (PFMA/PPPFA compliance), B-BBEE certification is mandatory and the cost is claimable. For private projects, B-BBEE compliance is the contractor's commercial cost of doing business — it should be included in his overhead, not itemised as a preliminary. However, if the client specifically requires B-BBEE verification as a contract condition, the cost may be treated as a preliminary. For private projects, challenge this item — it is typically 1-2% of overhead, not a line item.

## FF&E, DEPRECIATION & ASSET CLASSIFICATION

**Q:** 101. A corporate client wants to know whether loose-laid carpet tiles in their office are classified as FF&E or permanent works for depreciation purposes. How does this affect my cost reporting?
**A:** Loose-laid carpet tiles are FF&E (Furniture, Fittings, and Equipment) and depreciate over 5-7 years for tax purposes. Adhered carpet tiles or glued-down vinyl are permanent works (improvements) and depreciate over the building's useful life (20-50 years). This distinction affects: (1) Depreciation rate and tax deduction timing; (2) Insurance valuation (FF&E insured separately from building); (3) Tenant vs landlord responsibility in lease agreements. Report loose-laid carpet tiles under a separate FF&E cost centre in your cost report. Do not include them in the building cost per m2 — they are a fit-out cost.

**Q:** 102. A shopping mall tenant installation includes R2.4 million of tiling. The lease says the tenant must remove all alterations at lease end. How do I cost the "make good" provision, and who capitalises the tiling?
**A:** This is a lease accounting issue (IFRS 16). If the tenant must remove tiling at lease end, the tiling is a "leasehold improvement" that must be depreciated over the shorter of the lease term or useful life. The make-good provision (removal and reinstatement cost) must be recognised as a liability at present value. Cost the make-good: removal at R45-85/m2 + screed reinstatement at R85-120/m2 + making good at R60-100/m2 = R190-305/m2 total. Discount to present value using the tenant's cost of capital. As QS, provide the make-good cost estimate for the accountant's IFRS 16 calculation.

**Q:** 103. A hotel chain wants to separate "owner-supplied" tiles (brand standard, permanent) from "operator-supplied" tiles (design variation, replaceable). How do I structure this in the BOQ?
**A:** Structure as two distinct work packages: (1) Owner's scope — base build tiles (bathroom floors, public areas) measured as permanent works with full installation rate; (2) Operator's scope — FF&E tiles (feature walls, restaurant floors) measured as supply-and-install packages with shorter depreciation. In the BOQ, use separate section headings. For the owner's scope, apply building-level contingencies (8-10%). For the operator's scope, apply FF&E-level contingencies (5%). This separation is critical for: funding (owner's loan vs operator's equity), depreciation schedules, and replacement reserves.

**Q:** 104. A school building's tiling was funded by a donor who wants it treated as a separate asset for audit purposes. Can I create a separate cost code and depreciation schedule just for the tiling?
**A:** Yes. Create a distinct cost code (e.g., 03-FIN-TILE-DONOR) in the cost report. The donor-funded tiling is recorded as a restricted asset — it cannot be written off through general maintenance and must be tracked separately. Depreciation is still over the building's useful life (25-50 years for institutional buildings), but the funding source is tagged. The QS must ensure the cost code captures: tile supply, installation, adhesive, grout, cutting, wastage, and any associated preparation. This is an accounting requirement, not a QS standard practice, but the cost data must support the audit trail.

**Q:** 105. For insurance reinstatement costing, a factory fire destroyed 3,000m2 of floor tiling. The insurer says "replace with equivalent" but the original tiles were discontinued 8 years ago. How do I quantify the reinstatement cost?
**A:** Insurance reinstatement is based on "new for old" — the cost to replace with equivalent current-specification tiles, not discontinued originals. Quantify: (1) Current equivalent tile price (same size, material, slip resistance rating); (2) Full installation cost including removal of fire-damaged tiles (R45-85/m2), screed preparation, adhesive, grout, cutting; (3) Professional fees (QS, architect — typically 10-15% of works); (4) VAT; (5) Contractor's preliminaries and profit; (6) Temporary accommodation/downtime cost (separate business interruption claim). Do not use the original 8-year-old rates — inflation alone would make them inadequate.

## STAIRS, NOSINGS & VERTICAL SURFACES

**Q:** 106. A fire escape staircase has 48 steps with 1.2m width, requiring anti-slip nosing strips inset into the tiles. How do I measure this, and what labour premium applies?
**A:** Measure stair tiling as: (1) Treads in m2 (number x width x going depth); (2) Risers in m2 (number x width x riser height); (3) Nosing strips in linear metres. Labour premium: 80-100% over standard floor tiling due to: precision cutting for nosing insets, step-by-step layout to ensure consistency, working on confined stairwells, and additional cutting waste (12-15%). The nosing strips (brass, aluminium, or carborundum) are measured separately in linear metres with end caps and fixings. Anti-slip treatment adds R150-300/linear metre for brass nosing. Total stair tiling cost: R450-750/m2 all-in compared to R280-420/m2 for floors.

**Q:** 107. An architect wants 300x600mm tiles on stair risers laid vertically (portrait orientation). The riser height is 175mm. Is this practical, and what wastage does it generate?
**A:** Portrait orientation on a 175mm riser means every tile must be cut from 600mm to 175mm, leaving a 425mm offcut. Unless the offcut can be used on treads or elsewhere, wastage is 70%. This is economically and environmentally wasteful. Recommend: (1) Use 200x600mm tiles that match the 300x600mm tread tiles — minimal cutting; (2) Use a 150x600mm tile designed for risers; (3) Lay the 300x600mm tile horizontally on the riser (landscape), cutting to 175mm height but using the 300mm width — wastage is 41% but offcuts are 300x175mm, potentially reusable. Flag the wastage issue to the architect before finalising the cost plan.

**Q:** 108. A public building requires tactile warning tiles (podotactile strips) at the top and bottom of every staircase and at changes in floor level. How do I cost these, and are they measured separately?
**A:** Tactile warning indicators (TWI) are required under SANS 10400-S (facilities for persons with disabilities) and are measured separately from standard tiling. Cost components: (1) Tactile tile units (stainless steel or polyurethane): R350-650/linear metre; (2) Installation: R80-120/linear metre; (3) Cutting and integration with surrounding tiles: R40-60/linear metre. Total: R470-830/linear metre. A typical staircase requires 2.4 linear metres (1.2m wide x 2 locations). Do not include TWI in the standard tiling rate — it is a specialist accessibility item with compliance implications.

**Q:** 109. A spiral staircase with 1.5m diameter requires tiling to treads only (open risers). Each tread is a wedge shape. How do I measure the tiling, and can standard rectangular tiles work?
**A:** Wedge-shaped spiral stair treads require every tile to be radially cut — no standard rectangular tile will fit without cutting. The narrow end of the tread may be as small as 150mm, the wide end 450mm+ for a 1.5m diameter spiral. Options: (1) Cut standard tiles to wedge shape — wastage 30-40%, labour premium 100%; (2) Order custom-cut wedge tiles from the manufacturer — higher material cost but minimal waste; (3) Use small format tiles (100x100mm) that can accommodate the geometry with less cutting. Measure the actual tread surface area in m2 and apply a 1.5x labour constant with 35% wastage. Price as a specialist item.

**Q:** 110. The specification requires a 150mm high skirting tiled to match the floor tiles on every staircase. Is this measured with the floor tiling, the stair tiling, or separately?
**A:** Stair skirting is measured separately from both floor and stair tread/riser tiling. It is a wall tiling item measured in linear metres with a stated height. The labour constant is higher than floor skirting because: (1) The skirting follows the rake of the staircase, requiring angled top and bottom cuts on every piece; (2) Each piece must be cut to fit between the stringer and the stairwell wall; (3) Working position is awkward. Labour: 0.7-0.9 hrs/m2 (wall tiling rate). Wastage: 10-12% due to angled cuts. Measure in linear metres of skirting, convert to m2 (length x 0.15m height), and price at wall tiling rates with 25% premium.

## EXPANSION JOINTS, MOVEMENT JOINTS & CONTROL JOINTS

**Q:** 111. A 60m x 40m shopping mall floor requires movement joints every 8m in both directions. Do I measure the joint as a deduction from the tiling area or as an extra over item?
**A:** Measure both: (1) Deduct the joint area from the tiling quantity — a 10mm wide joint every 8m in a 60m x 40m floor = 10 joints one way (600 linear metres) + 7 joints the other way (280 linear metres) at 0.01m width = 8.8m2 deducted from tiling; (2) Measure the joint filler (compressible backing rod + polyurethane sealant or brass strip) as a separate linear item. The joint filler rate per linear metre includes: compressible foam backing (R8-12/m), primer (R5-8/m), sealant (R25-45/m for polyurethane, R120-200/m for brass strip). Total joint cost: R35-220/linear metre depending on specification.

**Q:** 112. The architect wants "invisible" expansion joints — flush with the tile surface and colour-matched. Is this achievable, and what does it cost compared to standard brass strips?
**A:** "Invisible" movement joints use colour-matched silicone or epoxy sealant instead of metal strips. This is achievable but has limitations: (1) Silicone joints attract dirt and may discolour over time in high-traffic areas; (2) Movement capacity is typically 10-15% of joint width (less than brass systems); (3) The joint must be wider (15-20mm vs 10mm for brass) to achieve equivalent movement capacity. Cost: colour-matched silicone joint R45-75/linear metre vs brass strip R120-200/linear metre. Silicone is cheaper initially but requires more frequent replacement (5-7 years vs 15-20 years for brass). Life-cycle cost may favour brass.

**Q:** 113. A structural engineer has specified expansion joints at 6m intervals instead of the standard 8-10m. The tiled area is 5,000m2. What is the cost impact of the closer spacing?
**A:** At 8m spacing: joints every 8m in both directions = 1,250 linear metres of joint. At 6m spacing: joints every 6m = 2,083 linear metres — a 67% increase. Cost impact: (1) Additional joint material at R50/linear metre = R41,650 extra; (2) Additional labour for cutting and joint installation; (3) Reduced tiling productivity due to more interruptions; (4) More visible joints (aesthetically undesirable). Total additional cost: R50,000-70,000. If the structural engineer's specification is conservative, challenge it — tiling to a concrete slab with mesh reinforcement typically allows 8-10m joint spacing per SANS 10109-1. The engineer may have over-specified.

**Q:** 114. The building has an expansion joint where two wings meet at a 15-degree angle. The floor tiles must cross this joint. What detail is required, and how do I cost it?
**A:** Angled expansion joints require a flexible joint system that accommodates movement in two planes. The standard detail: (1) Rake out the existing joint to 20-25mm wide; (2) Install a flexible joint cover plate (aluminium or stainless) that spans the joint and is fixed to one side only; (3) Bed the plate and tile up to it on both sides with flexible adhesive; (4) Seal with colour-matched flexible sealant. Cost: R350-650/linear metre for the joint detail including cover plate, compared to R50-120/linear metre for a standard straight joint. The cover plate is measured separately in linear metres. This is a specialist detail — obtain a shop drawing from the supplier.

**Q:** 115. A 200-unit apartment building has tiled balconies. Each balcony has a perimeter movement joint where the balcony slab meets the building. Over 200 units, these joints total 1,800 linear metres. The contractor omitted them from his tender. Is this a variation?
**A:** If the movement joints were shown on the drawings or specified in the contract documents, they are part of the contract scope — not a variation. The contractor's omission is a tender error. However, if the joints were not shown or specified, and the contractor could not reasonably have known they were required, it may be a variation or a provisional item. As QS, check the contract documents. If the joints were specified, instruct the contractor to install them at his own cost. If not specified, treat as a variation at contract rates if applicable, or daywork/fair rates if no applicable rate exists.

## DPC, MOISTURE BARRIERS & SUBSTRATE ISSUES

**Q:** 116. A ground floor office building has no DPM and the concrete slab shows moisture readings of 85% RH. The tiling specification says "tile to prepared concrete slab." What is the minimum moisture treatment required, and what does it cost?
**A:** At 85% RH, the slab is excessively wet for direct tiling — the adhesive bond will fail and efflorescence will occur. Under SANS 10400-H, maximum permissible moisture for tiling is 75% RH (or 5% CM method). Treatment options: (1) Surface-applied epoxy DPM: R45-75/m2 — effective up to 97% RH; (2) Install a ventilated cavity system: R120-180/m2; (3) Wait for natural drying (reduces 1% RH per week under ideal conditions — 10+ weeks from 85% to 75%). The epoxy DPM is the most cost-effective. Include it as a separate substrate preparation item. Do not proceed with tiling until moisture levels are verified post-treatment.

**Q:** 117. A basement parking garage has water seeping through the slab from groundwater. The client wants tiles installed anyway. What is my professional obligation as QS?
**A:** Your obligation is to advise the client that tiling over an active water leak will fail within months and to include the cost of proper waterproofing in your estimate. The correct sequence: (1) Stop the water ingress (negative-side waterproofing injection: R350-550/m2, or subsoil drainage); (2) Allow the slab to dry; (3) Apply waterproofing; (4) Then tile. If the client insists on tiling without addressing the leak, document your advice in writing. Under the SACQSP code of conduct, you must not knowingly cost work that will fail. The total waterproofing + tiling cost may exceed alternative floor finishes (e.g., sealed concrete, epoxy coating).

**Q:** 118. A timber suspended floor (first floor of a heritage building) requires tiling. The architect says "tile directly to the timber." What is the correct substrate preparation, and what does it cost?
**A:** Tiling direct to timber is inadvisable — timber moves with humidity, causing tiles to crack. The correct system: (1) Overlay timber with 18mm plywood or 12mm tile backer board, screwed at 150mm centres (R65-95/m2); (2) Apply flexible adhesive (Class 2 minimum, Class 3 preferred); (3) Install movement joints every 3-4m (closer than concrete due to timber movement); (4) Use flexible grout. Total preparation: R85-130/m2 before tiles. If the timber is uneven (common in old buildings), add levelling compound at R45-75/m2. Document this as a separate substrate preparation item — do not include it in the tiling rate without clear assumptions.

**Q:** 119. An existing factory has a steel mezzanine floor with a 3mm chequer plate surface. The client wants porcelain tiles on this floor. How is this achieved, and what is the cost per m2?
**A:** Tiling to steel requires a bonded screed system: (1) Mechanically abrade the chequer plate to remove oil and create profile (R15-25/m2); (2) Weld mesh or shear studs to the steel for screed bond (R25-40/m2); (3) Apply 40-50mm polymer-modified screed (R120-160/m2); (4) Allow 14-21 days drying; (5) Flexible adhesive and tiles. Total preparation: R185-245/m2 before tiling. Additionally, the steel structure must be checked by an engineer for the additional dead load (screed + tiles = 80-110 kg/m2). If the steel cannot support the load, consider lightweight tile backer boards (R95-140/m2) instead of screed.

**Q:** 120. A project has an underfloor heating system embedded in the screed. The tile specification doesn't mention underfloor heating compatibility. What adhesive and grout specification should I include?
**A:** Underfloor heating creates thermal cycling (daily temperature swings of 10-20 degrees) that causes differential expansion between tiles, adhesive, and screed. The specification must require: (1) Flexible adhesive (Class 2 or 3) with polymer modification — standard Class 1 adhesive will fail; (2) Flexible grout (polymer-modified or epoxy) — standard cement grout will crack; (3) Movement joints every 8-10m and at all perimeter boundaries; (4) Tiles to be fixed only after the heating system has been commissioned and run for 7-14 days to drive out residual moisture. Cost premium: R15-25/m2 for upgraded adhesive and grout. If the specification doesn't address this, issue a query — it is a design omission.

## INSULATION, SOUND & FIRE PROTECTION

**Q:** 121. A luxury apartment building requires acoustic separation between floors to meet Part K of SANS 10400. The specified "floating screed" over insulation adds 65mm to the floor build-up. How does this affect my tiling cost?
**A:** The floating screed system affects tiling costs in several ways: (1) The screed itself (65mm thick, reinforced, over XPS insulation): R180-250/m2; (2) Longer drying time (1 day per mm = 65 days minimum); (3) Tiling can only commence after the screed is fully dry and a moisture test confirms readiness; (4) Programme impact — the drying time extends the critical path, increasing preliminaries; (5) The screed may require a liquid DPM if the insulation is vapour-permeable: R25-40/m2. Total impact: R230-320/m2 for the screed system plus programme extension of 6-8 weeks. Tiling rate itself is unaffected if the screed is flat and dry.

**Q:** 122. A cinema complex requires 55dB airborne sound insulation between auditoriums. The floor finish is carpet in the seating area and tiles in the circulation areas. How do I ensure the tiled areas meet the same acoustic rating?
**A:** Hard floor finishes like tiles reflect sound and do not contribute to airborne insulation — they reduce it. To achieve 55dB Rw in circulation areas: (1) The floor structure itself must provide the insulation (concrete slab minimum 150mm, or timber joist with insulation); (2) A resilient layer (rubber mat or acoustic underlay) under the screed: R45-85/m2; (3) Perimeter isolation strips where the screed meets walls; (4) Ceiling treatment in the room below (acoustic ceiling). The tiling itself does not provide acoustic insulation — it is the substrate build-up that matters. If the structure is inadequate, upgrading it is a structural cost, not a tiling cost.

**Q:** 123. A fire engineer has specified fire stopping around all service penetrations through tiled floors. The M&E contractor says this is his scope. Who is responsible, and how is it measured?
**A:** Fire stopping is the M&E contractor's scope — it is a fire protection system, not a tiling item. However, the tiling contractor must: (1) Cut tiles to accommodate the fire stopping collars/sleeves; (2) Make good tile edges around the fire stopping; (3) Ensure tiles are not fixed over fire stopping that requires maintenance access. The interface cost (tile cutting and making good around penetrations) is the tiling contractor's responsibility and should be included in his rate. The fire stopping itself (intumescent collars, fire-rated sealant) is measured separately under M&E fire protection. Do not double-measure.

**Q:** 124. A data centre has a raised access floor (600mm void) with tiles laid on top. The specification requires the floor to support 12kN/m2. How does this affect the tiling specification and cost?
**A:** Raised access floors at 12kN/m2 are heavy-duty pedestals and stringer systems (R450-750/m2 for the flooring system alone). The tiles on top must be: (1) Fully supported by the floor panels — no unsupported edges; (2) Fixed with adhesive suitable for metal panel substrates; (3) Static-dissipative if the data centre requires it. The tiling cost is additional to the raised floor system: tiles at R180-350/m2 + adhesive R25-35/m2 + labour at 0.35 hrs/m2 (faster than standard due to flat, prepared panels). The critical issue: the raised floor panels must be precisely level — any panel rocking will crack tiles. Include panel levelling verification as a hold point.

**Q:** 125. An energy-efficient building specification requires insulation under all ground floor screeds. The insulation is 50mm XPS board. What is the all-in cost, and does it affect the tiling programme?
**A:** 50mm XPS insulation under screed: R45-75/m2 including supply and installation. The insulation is laid on the DPM, then the screed is poured over it. Programme impact: minimal — insulation installation is fast (0.05 hrs/m2). However, the screed over insulation requires: (1) Reinforcement mesh to prevent cracking (R15-25/m2); (2) Longer drying time — insulation slows heat transfer from the ground, increasing screed drying by 20-30%; (3) The screed must be a minimum 65mm thick over insulation (vs 25mm direct to slab) — additional concrete cost: R35-55/m2. Total additional cost over direct-to-slab tiling: R110-180/m2 including programme extension.

## EXISTING BUILDINGS, DILAPIDATIONS & MAKING GOOD

**Q:** 126. A tenant vacating a retail space must reinstate the floor to "original condition" per the lease. They installed tiles over the original vinyl. Who pays for removal and reinstatement, and what does it cost?
**A:** The tenant pays. Lease reinstatement obligations require the tenant to remove their alterations and restore the premises to the condition at lease commencement (fair wear and tear excepted). Cost: (1) Tile removal: R45-85/m2; (2) Adhesive residue removal: R25-45/m2; (3) Substrate assessment and preparation; (4) New vinyl or floor finish as specified by landlord. Total: R120-220/m2 plus the cost of the new floor finish. As QS for the landlord, prepare a schedule of dilapidations with costed reinstatement. The tenant's make-good deposit (typically 3-6 months' rent) can be applied against these costs.

**Q:** 127. I'm preparing a dilapidations report for a 10-year-old office building. The ceramic floor tiles have numerous cracks but were installed by the landlord, not the tenant. Can I claim against the tenant for tile replacement?
**A:** No. Dilapidations claims only cover defects or damage caused by the tenant or the tenant's failure to maintain items for which they are responsible. If the landlord installed the tiles and they have cracked due to building settlement, substandard original installation, or fair wear and tear over 10 years, this is the landlord's maintenance responsibility. The tenant is only liable for: damage they caused (e.g., dropping heavy equipment), failure to report leaks that caused damage, or alterations without consent. Each item in the dilapidations schedule must be attributed to tenant liability or landlord maintenance.

**Q:** 128. A renovation project requires chasing electrical conduits through 800m2 of existing tiled walls. After the M&E work, the tiles must be made good. How do I cost the remedial tiling, and who pays?
**A:** This is a classic interface dispute. Under standard contract conditions, the M&E contractor is responsible for making good his own chasing work. The sequence: (1) M&E contractor chases and installs conduits; (2) M&E contractor makes good with compatible mortar; (3) Tiling contractor returns to replace tiles in the chased areas. The M&E contractor pays for steps 1 and 2. The tiling contractor is paid for step 3 — measure the actual area of tile replacement in m2 at the contract tiling rate. If the chasing was not shown on the original drawings (i.e., design change after tiling was complete), the additional tiling is a variation chargeable to the client.

**Q:** 129. An existing building survey reveals that 30% of floor tiles are loose due to original adhesive failure. The client wants to tile over the existing tiles to save removal cost. What is the correct advice?
**A:** Tiling over loose tiles is inadvisable — the new tiles will fail when the substrate beneath moves. The correct advice: (1) Remove all loose tiles and adhesive to a sound substrate; (2) Test remaining tiles for bond strength — if sound, they can remain; (3) Apply a levelling screed or self-levelling compound over the prepared surface; (4) Then install new tiles. Cost: partial removal at R65-95/m2 (more expensive than full removal due to selective work) + screed correction at R85-120/m2 + tiling at standard rate. Tiling over loose tiles may seem cheaper initially but carries 100% failure risk within 12-24 months. Document your advice to the client.

**Q:** 130. A contractor has cut a 300mm x 300mm access hatch through a tiled floor for drainage maintenance. The cut tiles must be replaced. Is this a variation, and how is it measured?
**A:** If the access hatch was shown on the contract drawings, it is part of the original scope — the contractor should have allowed for cutting and finishing the tile edges around the hatch in his original rate. If the hatch was an architect's instruction after tiling was complete, it is a variation. Measure: (1) Cut tiles: area of tiles removed in m2 at the contract rate; (2) Edge finishing: linear metres of cut edge requiring polishing or trim (R120-200/linear metre for polished porcelain); (3) Making good to adhesive bed and substrate. If the cut edge is visible and requires polishing, the linear metre rate for edge polishing is a separate item — standard rates do not include polished cut edges.

## COST OF QUALITY, TESTING & INSPECTION

**Q:** 131. The client wants an independent third-party inspection of all tiling works, with a pull-test on 5% of tiles. What does this cost, and who pays?
**A:** Independent tiling inspection including pull-testing (adhesion bond strength test per EN 1542 or similar) costs R15,000-25,000 per inspection visit, plus R450-750 per pull-test location. For a 3,000m2 project with 5% testing: 150 test locations x R600 average = R90,000 + R20,000 inspection fee = R110,000 total. Who pays: if the client requests additional testing beyond contract requirements, the client pays. If testing is specified in the contract, it is included in the preliminaries or as a separate testing provisional sum. Bond strength requirements: minimum 0.5 MPa for wall tiles, 1.0 MPa for floor tiles.

**Q:** 132. A pull-test on floor tiles shows adhesion of 0.3 MPa — below the 0.5 MPa minimum. The contractor says the tile supplier's adhesive is at fault. Who is responsible for the R180,000 remedial cost?
**A:** Responsibility depends on the contract structure: (1) If the contractor supplied and installed the adhesive, the contractor is responsible for both material and application; (2) If the adhesive was a client-nominated/nominated supplier product, the contractor is responsible for correct application, the supplier for product performance; (3) If the substrate was defective (dusty, too smooth, or too wet), the main contractor may share responsibility. The QS should not adjudicate liability — that is for the contract administrator. However, do not certify payment for the defective area until remedial work is completed satisfactorily. The R180,000 remedial cost is withheld from the contractor's payment pending resolution.

**Q:** 133. The specification requires "ISO 9001 certified installation contractor" for a hospital project. How does this certification affect the tiling rates?
**A:** ISO 9001-certified tiling contractors are a limited pool — perhaps 15-20% of the market. The certification indicates a quality management system but does not guarantee better workmanship. Cost impact: (1) Certified contractors may charge 5-10% premium for their "certified" status; (2) The certification itself costs the contractor R30,000-50,000 annually in audit fees — this is an overhead cost; (3) Hospital projects require additional compliance (infection control, documentation, traceability) which adds 10-15% to labour. Do not pay a premium for ISO 9001 alone — evaluate the contractor's actual hospital project experience. If the specification mandates certification, only certified contractors can tender.

**Q:** 134. A project requires every batch of adhesive to be tested for compliance with SANS 10109-2 before use. What is the testing cost per batch, and what batch size is standard?
**A:** SANS 10109-2 adhesive testing (open time, slip, shear adhesion, transverse deformation) at a SANAS-accredited laboratory costs R8,000-12,000 per batch test. A "batch" is defined by the manufacturer — typically one production batch (25kg bags from the same production run). For a large project using 500 bags of adhesive, there may be 3-5 batches. Total testing: R24,000-60,000. This is significant — include it as a testing provisional sum. Note: routine batch testing is unusual for standard projects; it is typically specified for critical projects (hospitals, airports, heritage). If the client requires it, they pay. If the contractor offers it as a quality assurance measure, it is his cost.

**Q:** 135. The client wants a 10-year warranty on the tiling installation. Standard industry warranty is 1 year (defects liability period). What cost premium should I include for the extended warranty?
**A:** A 10-year installation warranty is a significant risk transfer to the contractor. Standard practice: (1) 1-year defects liability period — included in standard rates; (2) Extended warranty (years 2-10) — requires a warranty bond or insurance policy. Cost: warranty insurance premium of 1.5-3% of the contract value for years 2-10. For a R5 million tiling contract: R75,000-150,000 additional. Alternatively, the contractor may increase his rates by 5-8% to self-insure the extended risk. Advise the client that a 10-year warranty is unusual for tiling — even tile manufacturers typically offer 10-25 years on product, not installation. The NHBRC provides 5-year structural warranty on new homes, but this covers structure, not finishes.

## VALUE ENGINEERING, COST-BENEFIT & ELEMENTAL ANALYSIS

**Q:** 136. A hotel project's tiling budget is R28 million, but the client needs to reduce it by 20% (R5.6 million). What value engineering options should I present, in order of least impact on quality?
**A:** Value engineering hierarchy for tiling: (1) Reduce wastage allowance from 7% to 5% by optimising room layouts and tile sizes — saving 2% of R28m = R560,000; (2) Substitute imported porcelain with equivalent local porcelain — saving 15-25% on material = R2-3 million; (3) Reduce feature wall areas (mosaic accents, decorative bands) — saving R800,000-1.2 million; (4) Standardise on fewer tile types (reduce from 12 SKUs to 6) — bulk discount saving R400,000-600,000; (5) Change laying patterns from diagonal to straight in low-visibility areas — labour saving R600,000-900,000; (6) Reduce skirting height from 150mm to 100mm — saving R200,000-300,000. Total potential: R4.5-6.8 million. Present options with cost-benefit analysis and aesthetic impact assessment.

**Q:** 137. An architect has specified three different slip resistance ratings for a shopping mall: R10 for retail, R11 for food court, R12 for service corridors. The R12 tiles cost 40% more than R10. Can we use R11 throughout and still comply with SANS 10107?
**A:** SANS 10107 provides guidance but not mandatory slip resistance ratings for every area. However, the Wet Pendulum Test values are widely accepted: (1) Retail (dry): P3/R9 minimum; (2) Food court (wet/dry): P4/R10 minimum; (3) Service corridors (wet/greasy): P5/R11 minimum. Using R11 throughout would comply with the minimum standard but the architect's specification of R12 for service corridors may be based on specific risk assessment. Value engineering proposal: R10 for retail, R11 for food court and service corridors — saving 30-35% on service corridor tiles. If the client accepts the risk, document the deviation from architect's specification with sign-off.

**Q:** 138. A cost plan shows tiling at R320/m2 for a student residence. The client's benchmark from a similar completed project is R240/m2. What are the likely reasons for the 33% variance, and how do I explain it?
**A:** Variance analysis: (1) Inflation — if the completed project was 2+ years ago, 12-15% inflation accounts for R36; (2) Specification difference — the current project may have porcelain vs ceramic in the benchmark (R40-60/m2 difference); (3) Substrate condition — current project may require screed correction (R20-30/m2); (4) Location — remote site vs urban (R15-25/m2 transport); (5) Programme — fast-track vs standard (R10-15/m2 overtime); (6) Labour rate escalation — minimum wage increases (R8-12/m2). Present the reconciliation to the client showing line-by-line variance. If the sum of justified variances equals the total variance, the estimate is validated. If not, review your rates.

**Q:** 139. A client wants to substitute 600x600mm rectified porcelain (R285/m2 laid) with 600x600mm pressed ceramic (R195/m2 laid). What are the hidden costs beyond the R90/m2 material saving?
**A:** Hidden costs of the substitution: (1) Pressed tiles have larger dimensional tolerances (+/- 1mm vs +/- 0.5mm for rectified) — wider grout joints required (3mm vs 2mm), more grout cost: R3-5/m2; (2) Pressed tiles may have slight lippage — more labour time for levelling: R5-8/m2; (3) If the specification required rectified edges for minimal joints, the architect may reject the substitution — design approval cost/time; (4) Pressed tiles have higher water absorption — may not be suitable for wet areas without upgrading adhesive class; (5) Client perception/prestige value — intangible but relevant for commercial projects. Net saving after hidden costs: R70-80/m2, not R90/m2.

**Q:** 140. An elemental cost analysis shows that tiling represents 4.8% of total building cost, but the industry benchmark for this building type is 3.5%. What could explain the 1.3% overspend, and how do I investigate?
**A:** Investigation steps: (1) Check the tile specification against the benchmark — higher grade, imported, larger format, or decorative features can explain 0.5-0.8%; (2) Check substrate preparation — extensive screed correction, waterproofing, or DPM installation can add 0.3-0.5%; (3) Check area measurement — has tiling been included in areas that typically don't get tiled in the benchmark (service corridors, plant rooms)? (4) Check labour constants — are diagonal patterns, complex layouts, or premium locations inflating the rate? (5) Check if the benchmark is current — old benchmarks may not reflect current slip resistance requirements, waterproofing standards, or labour costs. Prepare a reconciliation before presenting to the client.

## LEGAL, INSURANCE, NEIGHBOUR & DISPUTE ISSUES

**Q:** 141. A tiler fell through an opening on the first floor and was seriously injured. The contractor has COIDA cover but the tiler is claiming R2.4 million in damages from the client. What is the QS's involvement in this?
**A:** COIDA (Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act) provides no-fault compensation to employees, but a tiler injured due to unsafe working conditions may have a common law claim against the client if the client controlled the site or created the hazard. As QS, your role is: (1) Ensure your cost plan includes adequate health and safety provisions (typically 2-3% of contract value for construction work); (2) Verify the contractor's letter of good standing with the Department of Labour; (3) Ensure the contractor has public liability insurance (R5-10 million minimum); (4) Document that safety costs were allowed for in the estimate. The R2.4 million claim is an insurance matter — ensure the client's insurance broker is notified immediately.

**Q:** 142. A neighbouring property claims that dust from tile cutting has damaged their stock in an adjacent warehouse. They want R450,000 compensation. Is this a contractor liability or client liability?
**A:** Dust control is the contractor's responsibility under the Construction Regulations (OHSA). The contractor must implement dust suppression measures (water suppression, dust extraction, enclosure) when cutting tiles, especially near sensitive neighbours. The claim is initially against the contractor. However, if the client instructed work knowing the neighbour's sensitivity (e.g., the client was aware of the warehouse), there may be shared liability. As QS, check if the tender documents identified adjacent sensitive uses — if yes, the contractor should have allowed for enhanced dust control. If not identified, the client may share the cost. The contractor's public liability insurance should cover this.

**Q:** 143. A body corporate in a sectional title scheme has passed a special levy for lobby retiling. One owner refuses to pay, claiming the existing tiles are "fine." Can the body corporate enforce payment, and how does it affect my cost plan?
**A:** Under the Sectional Titles Schemes Management Act 2011, a body corporate can pass a special levy for maintenance, renewal, or improvement of common property with a majority resolution. Once validly passed, all owners are liable. The non-paying owner can be sued by the body corporate and may have a judgment registered against their unit. For your cost plan: (1) Confirm the resolution was properly passed before commencing work; (2) If the body corporate cannot collect from all owners, your contract is with the body corporate (a legal entity), not individual owners — you are still entitled to payment; (3) If payment delays are likely, include a higher retention (10%) and progress payment security. Do not accept individual owner payments.

**Q:** 144. A tiling contractor has placed a lien on a completed residential property because the client has not paid the final account of R340,000. The client says the work is defective. How does this affect final account certification?
**A:** A lien is a contractor's common law right to retain possession of the property until payment — but this applies only if the contractor is still in possession, which is unusual for tiling work (tiling is affixed to the property). More likely, the contractor has registered a builder's lien or is threatening litigation. As QS, your role in the final account: (1) Only certify payment for work that is complete and defect-free; (2) If defects exist, quantify the cost of rectification and withhold that amount; (3) If the dispute is genuine, recommend dispute resolution (mediation or adjudication) before payment; (4) Do not get involved in the legal dispute — your duty is to professionally quantify the completed work and any defects. The R340,000 should only be certified to the extent the work is satisfactory.

**Q:** 145. An insurance claim for fire damage requires "like-for-like" reinstatement of 15-year-old 200x200mm ceramic floor tiles that are discontinued. The insurer is offering R85/m2. Current equivalent tiles cost R145/m2. How do I dispute the insurer's valuation?
**A:** "Like-for-like" in insurance means equivalent current-specification replacement, not discontinued original. Prepare a reinstatement cost estimate showing: (1) Current market rate for equivalent 200x200mm ceramic tiles (R140-160/m2 supply); (2) Current installation rates (R120-180/m2 laid); (3) Removal of fire-damaged tiles (R45-85/m2); (4) Substrate preparation (R45-75/m2); (5) Total: R350-500/m2 all-in. The insurer's R85/m2 is inadequate — it may cover supply only, not installation. Engage a loss assessor if the insurer refuses to adjust. Your QS reinstatement estimate, supported by current market rates from 3 suppliers, is the basis for the claim.

**Q:** 146. A client wants to withhold R500,000 from the tiling contractor's final payment because "the grout colour doesn't match the sample exactly." The contract says "colour to be approved by architect." The architect approved the sample. Is the client's withholding valid?
**A:** No. If the architect approved the grout colour sample, and the installed grout matches the approved sample, the contractor has fulfilled his contractual obligation. Grout colour can appear different when wet vs dry, in different lighting, or across large areas — this is a known characteristic, not a defect. The client cannot unilaterally withhold payment for work that meets the contract requirements. As QS, certify payment for the completed work. Advise the client that their remedy, if any, is against the architect for approving an unsuitable sample — not against the contractor. If the grout genuinely does not match the approved sample, withhold only the cost of re-grouting (R35-55/m2), not R500,000 arbitrarily.

**Q:** 147. A contractor has submitted a claim for "loss of productivity" due to load shedding — 47 hours of power outages over 3 months affecting wet saw operation and adhesive mixing. Is this a valid claim under JBCC?
**A:** Under JBCC 2014 (Principal Building Agreement), load shedding is generally considered a "neutral event" — not a client risk event unless specifically allocated in the contract. The contractor is expected to manage his own power requirements (generator, battery tools, schedule work around load shedding). However, if the contract has a specific load shedding clause or if the frequency exceeded what was reasonably foreseeable when tendering (e.g., Stage 6 for extended periods when tender assumed Stage 2), the contractor may have a claim under the "unforeseeable physical conditions" clause. Quantify the claim: 47 hours x gang cost x productivity factor. As QS, assess the claim on its merits — do not reject it out of hand, but demand detailed substantiation.

**Q:** 148. A neighbour's vibration monitor recorded readings above the 5mm/s threshold during tile removal with jackhammers. The neighbour is demanding work stop and claiming structural damage to his property. What are the QS and contractor obligations?
**A:** Under the OHSA Construction Regulations and municipal bylaws, vibration from construction work must not exceed specified thresholds at property boundaries. The contractor's obligations: (1) Conduct a pre-construction condition survey of adjacent properties (dilapidation survey — R15,000-25,000); (2) Monitor vibration during work; (3) If threshold is exceeded, cease the activity causing the exceedance and implement mitigation (lower impact tools, vibration damping, restricted hours). The QS obligation: ensure the contractor's preliminaries include vibration monitoring costs (R8,000-12,000/week for monitoring equipment). If damage is proven, the contractor's insurance should cover it. If work must stop, the delay may be a contractor risk event.

**Q:** 149. A project is in a rural area of the Eastern Cape on tribal land. The traditional council requires a "community levy" of 3% of the contract value for any construction work. Is this legitimate, and how do I include it in the cost plan?
**A:** Traditional council levies on tribal land are a recognised reality of construction in rural South Africa. While not a statutory tax, they are a condition of working in the area — refusal to pay can result in work stoppages or community protests. Include the 3% as a "local authority/community levy" in the preliminaries or as a separate cost centre. For a R2 million tiling contract, R60,000. Obtain a written receipt from the traditional council — this is a deductible business expense for tax purposes. Do not treat it as a bribe — it is a community benefit levy, distinct from corruption. Factor it into the project cash flow — it is typically payable before work commences.

**Q:** 150. A client has been declared insolvent after 70% of the tiling work is complete. The contractor is owed R1.8 million. What is the QS's role in the insolvency, and can the contractor claim the tiles he supplied but are not yet paid for?
**A:** When a client is declared insolvent, all construction contracts are suspended. The QS's role: (1) Prepare a detailed valuation of work completed to the date of insolvency — this is a creditor's claim; (2) Verify retention held (typically 5-10%); (3) Advise on the contractor's statutory right to a lien or preference claim. Under the Insolvency Act, the contractor may have a right to remove unfixed materials (tiles not yet installed) if they are clearly identifiable as his property and not yet paid for — this is a "retention of title" claim. However, once tiles are fixed to the building, they become part of the property and cannot be removed. The contractor becomes a concurrent creditor for the unpaid balance — typically recovering cents in the rand. The contractor should have credit insurance for client insolvency risk.

## CARBON TAX, SUSTAINABILITY & GREEN BUILDING

**Q:** 151. The Carbon Tax Act 2019 applies to our cement supplier. Should I include a carbon tax provision in the screed and adhesive rates for a large commercial project?
**A:** Currently, the Carbon Tax Act primarily targets large industrial emitters (cement manufacturers with emissions above the threshold). The carbon tax on cement is approximately R15-35 per ton of cement. For a tiling project using 50 tons of cement in screeds and adhesive, the carbon tax component is R750-1,750 — negligible in a multi-million rand project. The supplier may pass this through as a 0.5-1% increase on cement products. Include a note in the cost plan: "Cement-based products may include carbon tax pass-through of 0.5-1%. Current impact is minimal but may increase with future carbon price escalations." Do not create a separate carbon tax line item — it is not material.

**Q:** 152. A GBCSA 6-star project requires Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs) for all tiling materials. What cost premium does this add, and is it the contractor's or supplier's responsibility?
**A:** EPDs are third-party verified lifecycle assessments of a product's environmental impact. Cost: R80,000-150,000 per EPD per product range, typically borne by the manufacturer (not the contractor or client). However, if the specified tiles do not have EPDs, the supplier may charge a premium of 3-5% for EPD-certified alternatives. For the contractor, EPD compliance adds administrative cost: sourcing EPD-compliant products, documentation for GBCSA submission (R15,000-25,000 consultant fee). Total project impact: 1-2% on tiling materials. Responsibility: the client specified GBCSA 6-star — the contractor complies at cost-plus if not allowed for in the original tender.

**Q:** 153. A client wants to know the "embodied carbon" of their tiling choice (porcelain vs ceramic vs natural stone) for carbon offset purposes. How do I provide this data?
**A:** Embodied carbon per m2 of flooring: (1) Glazed ceramic tile: 12-18 kg CO2e/m2; (2) Porcelain tile: 18-25 kg CO2e/m2 (higher firing temperature); (3) Natural stone (local granite): 8-15 kg CO2e/m2; (4) Natural stone (imported marble): 25-50 kg CO2e/m2 (transport dominates); (5) Recycled content tile: 6-10 kg CO2e/m2. Source data from: manufacturer's EPDs, thinkstep/OneClick LCA database, or the Green Building Council's materials database. For offset purposes: at R150-250 per ton CO2, the offset cost for 1,000m2 of porcelain is R2,700-6,250 — typically not material enough to drive specification decisions. Present the data but note that operational energy usually dominates lifecycle carbon.

**Q:** 154. A project requires tiles with recycled content (post-industrial or post-consumer) to achieve GBCSA points. What is the cost difference between standard and recycled-content tiles?
**A:** Recycled-content tiles are typically 5-15% more expensive than standard tiles due to: (1) Limited production runs; (2) Additional sorting and processing of recycled material; (3) Smaller manufacturer base. Cost premium: R15-45/m2 on a R300/m2 tile. However, the GBCSA points gained may unlock overall project rating benefits that justify the premium (e.g., higher rental values for green-certified buildings). For QS purposes, include the recycled-content option as an alternative in the cost plan with the GBCSA consultant confirming the point value. If the project is borderline for its target rating, the recycled tiles may be essential — not optional.

**Q:** 155. The client wants to use locally manufactured tiles "to reduce the carbon footprint of transport." Local tiles cost R185/m2; equivalent imported tiles cost R145/m2. How do I present the transport carbon and cost trade-off?
**A:** Transport carbon comparison: (1) Imported tiles (Italy, 10,000km by sea): approximately 8-12 kg CO2e/m2 for transport; (2) Local tiles (500km by road): approximately 3-5 kg CO2e/m2. Carbon saving: 5-7 kg CO2e/m2. At R200/ton CO2 offset, the carbon value is R1-1.40/m2 — far less than the R40/m2 price premium for local tiles. However, the client may have non-carbon reasons for local procurement: B-BBEE compliance, shorter lead times, supporting local industry, or supply chain resilience. Present both the carbon analysis and the broader procurement benefits. Let the client make the value judgment — your role is to quantify, not advocate.

## PROGRAMME, COORDINATION & TRADE INTERFACES

**Q:** 156. The critical path shows tiling must start in Week 12 but the waterproofing subcontractor is 4 weeks behind. The tiling contractor says his gang will be available in Week 12 and will charge standing time if delayed. How do I cost this delay?
**A:** The delay is a compensation event if caused by the client or another nominated subcontractor. Cost the standing time: (1) Tiler gang (4 workers) at daywork standing time rate: R2,800-3,600/day x 20 days (4 weeks) = R56,000-72,000; (2) Foreman supervision: R1,800/day x 20 = R36,000; (3) Non-productive overheads (site accommodation, small tools): R8,000-12,000. Total: R100,000-120,000. However, the contractor must mitigate — if he can redeploy the gang to another project, the standing time claim reduces. As QS, accept only the non-mitigable cost with substantiation. Also claim the 4-week programme extension and associated preliminaries if applicable.

**Q:** 157. A shopping mall project has 45 tenants with individual shop-fitting programmes. The common area tiling must be completed before tenant shop-fitting starts. How do I programme the tiling to avoid delay claims from 45 tenants?
**A:** This is a complex logistics challenge. The tiling programme must: (1) Be completed at least 4 weeks before the first tenant access date (to allow curing, inspection, and snagging); (2) Be sequenced to match tenant handover zones — prioritise areas with early tenant access; (3) Include buffer time for substrate preparation (often delayed by preceding trades); (4) Account for restricted access — mall operational hours, delivery constraints, security. As QS, include "phased completion" preliminaries — the contractor may need to demobilise and remobilise between zones. Cost: 10-15% premium on preliminaries for phased working. Also include "tenant interface management" at R15,000-25,000/month in the main contractor's preliminaries.

**Q:** 158. The tiling contractor says he cannot start because the screed is "too wet" — moisture reading 78% RH. The programme is already 3 weeks behind. The main contractor says "just tile it, it's fine." What is the QS position?
**A:** The QS should support the tiling contractor's refusal. Tiling at 78% RH (above the 75% maximum per adhesive manufacturer specifications) will result in adhesive failure — the cost of rectification (removal, re-preparation, re-tiling at R280-450/m2) far exceeds the delay cost. As QS: (1) Quantify the delay cost if the programme is extended (preliminaries, standing time); (2) Quantify the rectification cost if tiling proceeds on a wet screed (R280-450/m2 x affected area); (3) Recommend waiting for the screed to dry or applying a surface DPM (R45-75/m2, 2-day application). The delay cost is the lesser evil. Document the moisture readings and advise the principal agent.

**Q:** 159. A project requires tiling to be completed in a 4-week window between handover from the screed contractor and the arrival of the shop-fitting contractor. The area is 2,400m2. Is 4 weeks achievable, and what gang size is needed?
**A:** 2,400m2 in 4 weeks = 600m2 per week = 120m2 per day (5-day week). At a productivity rate of 8-10m2 per tiler per day, you need 12-15 tilers. With a typical gang of 4 tilers + 2 labourers, you need 3-4 gangs working simultaneously. This is achievable if: (1) All substrate preparation is complete; (2) Tiles are on site and no delivery delays; (3) Adequate workspace for 3-4 gangs; (4) No restrictive working hours. Cost the gang sizes accordingly and include weekend working (1.5x rates) as a contingency if the programme slips. Also factor 1 week for snagging before handover to the next trade.

**Q:** 160. The main contractor wants to "save time" by having tilers work in the same area as electricians who are chasing walls. The tiling contractor refuses on safety grounds. Who is correct, and what does this cost?
**A:** The tiling contractor is correct. OHSA prohibits work in the same area where another trade is creating hazards (dust, falling objects, electrical risk). The "simultaneous working" the main contractor wants is unsafe and exposes all parties to liability. Cost of sequencing: (1) If electricians work first: 1-week delay to tiling start = preliminaries extension R15,000-25,000 + potential standing time for tiling gang; (2) If tiling works first: electrician's delay = similar cost to electrical trade. The correct answer is programmed sequencing with clear handover points. As QS, do not accept "simultaneous working" as a programme solution — it is a safety violation. Cost the proper sequence.

## RURAL, TRADITIONAL & HERITAGE PROJECTS

**Q:** 161. A project in rural KZN on traditional council land requires tiling to a community hall. The traditional council insists that 30% of the labour must be local community members, most of whom have never laid tiles. How does this affect the labour constant and cost?
**A:** Local labour requirements (a common condition of working on tribal land) increase the labour constant by 40-60%. Inexperienced workers require: (1) Extended supervision — 1 experienced tiler per 2 trainees (vs 1 per 4 experienced workers); (2) Slower production — trainee output is 40-50% of experienced tiler; (3) Higher rectification rate — 10-15% of trainee work may need redoing; (4) Training time — 2-3 weeks before productive output. Adjusted labour constant: 0.7-0.8 hrs/m2 vs 0.5 hrs/m2. Cost increase: R25-40/m2 on labour. Include a "community training and supervision" provisional sum of R35,000-50,000. Document the requirement in the cost plan assumptions.

**Q:** 162. A heritage building in the Western Cape has 1890s geometric floor tiles that must be preserved and re-laid after foundation repairs. The tiles are loose but intact. What is the specialist cost for this work?
**A:** Heritage tile preservation and reinstatement costs: (1) Careful removal, cleaning, and numbered inventory: R350-500/m2; (2) Off-site storage in numbered crates: R15,000-25,000 total; (3) Substrate repair and preparation: R120-180/m2; (4) Re-laying original tiles to exact pattern (using original lime mortar, not modern adhesive): R450-650/m2; (5) Grouting with lime putty: R45-75/m2; (6) Heritage consultant supervision: R25,000-40,000. Total: R1,000-1,500/m2 compared to R250-400/m2 for new tiling. Heritage Western Cape approval is required — include application and approval time (6-12 weeks) in the programme. Only heritage-accredited contractors may perform the work.

**Q:** 163. A rural school project requires tiling but the nearest building supply merchant is 180km away. The contractor wants a "remote location" premium of 25%. Is this justified?
**A:** A 25% premium for 180km from the nearest merchant is at the upper end but not unreasonable. Factors to consider: (1) Transport cost for materials — R8-12/km for a 5-ton truck = R1,440-2,160 per delivery; (2) Multiple deliveries required (adhesive, grout, tiles separately) = R4,500-6,500 total transport; (3) Labour recruitment difficulty — skilled tilers may require accommodation allowances (R350-500/night); (4) Material wastage buffer must increase — 7-10% instead of 5% because replacement materials cannot be quickly sourced; (5) Plant and tool transport. For a R800,000 tiling contract, 25% (R200,000) may be high — 15-20% is more reasonable. Negotiate based on actual additional costs, not a flat percentage.

**Q:** 164. A project on a wine estate in the Western Cape falls within a heritage overlay zone. The tiling specification says "stone tiles to match existing." Who determines what "matches," and what if the heritage officer disagrees with the architect's choice?
**A:** In heritage overlay zones, the Heritage Western Cape (HWC) or municipal heritage officer has final authority on material compatibility — the architect's choice is subject to HWC approval. The process: (1) Architect proposes tile samples; (2) HWC reviews and approves/rejects; (3) If rejected, the architect must re-specify. Cost risk: if the architect's first choice is rejected after ordering, the client bears the cost of replacement tiles and any programme delay. As QS, include a heritage approval contingency of 5% and a 4-week programme allowance for the approval process. Obtain provisional approval of tile samples before placing orders — this is standard heritage practice.

**Q:** 165. A community centre in a township requires tiling but the client (an NGO) has no budget for a QS. The builder has given a verbal quote of R180,000. What are the risks of proceeding without QS involvement?
**A:** Significant risks: (1) No measured quantities — the builder may over-order materials and charge for wastage; (2) No specification — the builder may use substandard adhesive or tiles; (3) No contract — no security for either party, no defects liability period, no retention; (4) No payment certification — the NGO may overpay or face cash flow disputes; (5) No final account — disputes about what was included. Minimum QS involvement for R180,000: prepare a simple schedule of rates (R8,000-12,000), conduct a monthly site visit (R3,000-5,000), and prepare a final account (R5,000-8,000). Total: R16,000-25,000 — a small investment to protect R180,000.

## TECHNICAL EDGE CASES & BIZARRE BUT REAL SCENARIOS

**Q:** 166. A client wants tiles installed on the ceiling of an outdoor pergola — tiles facing downward, exposed to rain. Is this technically possible, and what does it cost compared to wall tiling?
**A:** Ceiling tiling (tiles fixed to overhead substrate) is technically possible but challenging: (1) Adhesive must have zero slip — specialist overhead-grade adhesive required (R35-50/kg); (2) Each tile must be supported from below until adhesive cures (temporary props or bracing); (3) Cutting waste increases to 15-20% due to precision required; (4) Safety — overhead work requires scaffolding and fall protection; (5) Gravitational pull on adhesive means thicker beds (8-10mm vs 3-5mm for walls). Labour constant: 1.2-1.5 hrs/m2 (3x wall tiling). Cost: R450-650/m2 compared to R220-320/m2 for wall tiling. Water drainage and freeze-thaw must also be considered for outdoor application.

**Q:** 167. A luxury home has a glass-bottomed walkway over a wine cellar, and the owner wants porcelain tiles on the glass. What is the weight per m2, and is the glass designed to support it?
**A:** Tiled floor system weight: 10mm porcelain tile (20 kg/m2) + adhesive (10 kg/m2 at 8mm bed) + grout (3 kg/m2) = 33 kg/m2 dead load. The glass must be engineered for this additional dead load plus live load (people walking). Standard structural glass walkways are designed for 3.5 kN/m2 live load — adding 0.33 kN/m2 for tiles is significant for glass design. A structural engineer must verify: (1) Glass capacity for additional dead load; (2) Deflection limits — tiling requires maximum deflection of L/360 to prevent tile cracking; (3) Adhesive compatibility with glass substrate (etching primer required — R45-65/m2). If the glass is inadequate, the entire walkway may need upgrading — a R150,000-300,000 structural modification, not just a tiling issue.

**Q:** 168. A pharmaceutical clean room requires coved tile skirting (100mm radius cove) at all floor-wall junctions for washability. How do I measure this, and what is the cost per linear metre?
**A:** Coved skirting uses purpose-made cove tiles (quarter-round profile) at the floor-wall junction plus capping tiles at the top. Measure: (1) Cove tiles in linear metres (room perimeter); (2) Capping tiles in linear metres; (3) Wall tiles above cove in m2. Cost per linear metre of coved skirting: cove tile R45-75/linear metre + capping tile R35-55/linear metre + labour at 0.3 hrs/linear metre (R45) + adhesive/grout R15 = R140-190/linear metre. This compares to R60-90/linear metre for standard straight skirting. The cove detail is essential for pharmaceutical compliance — no alternative. Include it as a separate item in the BOQ.

**Q:** 169. A zoo's reptile house requires heating cables embedded in the floor screed beneath tiles, with floor surface temperature maintained at 32 degrees C. How does this affect the tiling system cost?
**A:** Reptile house floor heating creates extreme thermal stress on the tiling system. Requirements: (1) Heating cables: R450-750/m2 including thermostat and installation; (2) Thick screed (minimum 50mm) to embed cables: R140-180/m2; (3) Class 3 flexible adhesive (mandatory for thermal cycling): R35-45/m2 (vs R18-25 for Class 1); (4) Flexible grout: R25-35/m2 (vs R15 for standard); (5) Movement joints every 3-4m (closer than standard 8-10m due to extreme temperature range). Total additional cost over standard tiling: R550-850/m2 for the heating system plus R30-40/m2 for upgraded tiling materials. The thermostat control system is measured separately under M&E.

**Q:** 170. A client wants "invisible" floor drains — the tile continues across the drain with only a 3mm slot visible. How much more expensive is this than a standard floor waste, and what drainage capacity is lost?
**A:** Slot drains (linear drains with a tile-invisible grate): R2,500-4,500/linear metre for the drain body + R350-550/linear metre for installation and connection. A standard 100mm floor waste: R450-850 each. For a 1.2m wide shower, a slot drain costs R3,000-5,500 vs R650 for a point waste. However, the slot drain has higher drainage capacity (30-50 litres/minute per metre) compared to a standard waste (20-30 l/min). The "invisible" slot drain actually has superior drainage — no capacity is lost. The premium is purely aesthetic. The tile must be precision-cut to fit the slot width — allow R120-200/linear metre for tile cutting and fitting.

**Q:** 171. A project requires tiling inside a walk-in freezer operating at -18 degrees C. The specification says "standard ceramic tiles." What is wrong with this specification?
**A:** Standard ceramic tiles and cement-based adhesive will fail in a -18 degrees C environment due to freeze-thaw cycling. When the door opens, warm moist air enters and moisture condenses on the cold tiles. This moisture freezes, expands, and causes spalling. The correct specification: (1) Tiles with water absorption below 0.5% (fully vitrified, no porous body); (2) Flexible adhesive rated for sub-zero application (Class 3 with epoxy modification); (3) Epoxy grout — cement grout fails in freeze-thaw; (4) Movement joints every 2-3m (closer than standard due to thermal expansion differential between cold floor and ambient walls). Cost premium: 40-60% over standard tiling. The tiling contractor must also work in cold conditions — insulated clothing, limited exposure time, reduced productivity (1.5x labour constant).

**Q:** 172. A yacht marina club house has an outdoor deck that the architect wants tiled. The deck is on piers over tidal water, with 2m clearance at high tide and 4m at low tide. What is the critical consideration for the tiling cost?
**A:** The critical consideration is the tidal moisture and salt spray affecting the underside of the deck and the substrate. Salt-laden air corrodes metal fixings and penetrates concrete, causing reinforcement corrosion that spalls the surface. Tiling requirements: (1) The concrete deck must have adequate cover to reinforcement (50mm minimum in marine environment); (2) Waterproof membrane essential — salt water will destroy adhesive bond; (3) Class 3 adhesive mandatory for marine environment; (4) Stainless steel or plastic edge trims — brass will corrode. Cost premium: 30-40% over standard outdoor tiling due to marine-grade materials. The substrate condition must be verified by a marine structural engineer before tiling.

**Q:** 173. A client wants to tile over an existing swimming pool's mosaic finish "because the mosaics look dated." The pool is 10 years old and the mosaics are sound. Can this be done, and what is the cost?
**A:** Tiling over existing pool mosaics is possible but not recommended. The correct method: (1) Acid-wash the existing mosaics to remove calcium build-up and open the surface profile; (2) Apply a bonding bridge/primer compatible with submerged conditions: R25-35/m2; (3) Install new tiles with epoxy adhesive (the only adhesive rated for permanent submersion): R45-65/m2; (4) Epoxy grout: R65-120/m2. Total preparation and material: R135-220/m2 + tile supply + labour. The pool must be drained for 3-4 weeks. The weight of the new tile layer (additional 15-20 kg/m2) must be verified against the pool shell capacity. Cost: R350-550/m2 — comparable to removing and replacing.

**Q:** 174. An airport terminal has a floor tiling specification requiring "resistance to de-icing chemicals and jet fuel spillage." What tile and adhesive specification meets this, and what does it cost?
**A:** Airport apron and terminal floor tiling in aircraft movement areas must resist: (1) De-icing chemicals (glycol-based, highly corrosive to cement); (2) Jet fuel (kerosene, degrades standard adhesive); (3) Heavy point loads (aircraft trolleys, 2+ tons per wheel). Specification: (1) Tiles: fully vitrified porcelain, minimum 20mm thick for airside, 10mm for terminal interior; (2) Adhesive: epoxy-based or polyurethane — cement-based adhesive fails with chemical exposure; (3) Chemical-resistant epoxy grout; (4) Expansion joints at 6m centres (aircraft movement creates slab movement). Cost: R550-850/m2 (airside) vs R280-450/m2 (standard commercial). This is a specialist application requiring Civil Aviation Authority compliance.

**Q:** 175. A mortuary requires floor tiles that can withstand daily washing with disinfectant chemicals and exposure to bodily fluids. The specification says "standard glazed ceramic." What should it say?
**A:** Mortuary floors require: (1) Chemical-resistant tiles — fully vitrified porcelain with confirmed resistance to formaldehyde, glutaraldehyde, and chlorine-based disinfectants (request test certificates); (2) Epoxy adhesive and grout — cement-based systems degrade with chemical exposure; (3) Falls to drainage points (1:50 minimum); (4) Coved skirting at all junctions (R140-190/linear metre); (5) Anti-slip rating of R11 minimum (wet disinfectant creates slip hazard); (6) Joint width maximum 3mm to prevent bacterial harborage. Cost: R420-580/m2 vs R220-320/m2 for standard commercial. Include a provisional sum for HEPA-filtered extraction during tile cutting — dust control is critical in mortuary environments.

## LOAD SHEDDING, SECURITY & FORCE MAJEURE

**Q:** 176. A tiling contractor has included a "generator hire" item of R18,000/month in his preliminaries because the site has no reliable grid power. Should I accept this as a legitimate preliminary cost?
**A:** Yes. If the site genuinely lacks grid power (common in rural areas or during infrastructure upgrades), the generator is an essential plant item, not a contingency. Verify: (1) Is the generator dedicated to tiling work or shared across trades? If shared, the cost should be apportioned; (2) Is the generator size adequate for the tiling equipment (wet saws, mixers, lighting)? A 25kVA generator rents for R12,000-18,000/month plus diesel (R80-120/hour runtime); (3) Has the contractor allowed for fuel in the R18,000 or is it additional? Accept the generator hire but verify fuel is separately costed or included. For a 6-month contract, generator costs can exceed R150,000 — significant.

**Q:** 177. A project in Johannesburg experienced 180 hours of Stage 4-6 load shedding during the tiling phase. The contractor claims 30% productivity loss and wants R85,000 compensation. How do I assess this claim?
**A:** Assess the claim methodically: (1) Verify the load shedding hours against EskomSePush or municipal records; (2) Determine which hours coincided with tiling work (night shift load shedding may not have impacted); (3) Calculate productive hours lost — not all load shedding hours are 100% lost (some tasks can continue with battery tools or generators); (4) Apply a productivity factor — typically 60-70% loss during load shedding hours for tiling (wet saws and cement mixers need power); (5) Calculate cost: productive hours lost x gang cost x productivity factor. If substantiated, the claim may be valid under contract provisions for "unforeseeable events" — but only if the load shedding severity exceeded what was foreseeable at tender. In 2024-2025, Stage 4 is arguably foreseeable — the contractor should have allowed for it.

**Q:** 178. A high-security site (government facility) requires all tiling contractors to be security cleared to "Secret" level. Only 3 tilers in the entire country have this clearance. What cost premium does this create?
**A:** Security-cleared specialist labour commands a significant premium: (1) Security-cleared tiler rate: R1,800-2,500/day (vs R850-1,200 for standard); (2) Limited pool means the contractor may need to import labour from other provinces — accommodation and travel costs R15,000-25,000/month per worker; (3) Security vetting for additional workers takes 3-6 months — not viable for urgent projects; (4) Productivity may be lower due to security restrictions (tool inspections, escorted movement). Total premium: 80-120% over standard tiling costs. If the project is urgent, the client must accept the premium or relax the security requirement for non-sensitive areas. This should be identified at feasibility stage — not discovered at procurement.

**Q:** 179. A project was halted for 3 weeks due to a strike by community members demanding 50% of tiling jobs. The contractor had already mobilised and incurred costs. Is this a force majeure event, and who carries the cost?
**A:** Community protests are not typically force majeure under JBCC (which covers war, natural disasters, epidemics). However, they may be a "contractor's risk event" or "neutral event" depending on contract drafting. The contractor generally carries the risk of labour availability and community relations. Costs incurred: (1) Standing time for mobilised gang: R15,000-25,000/week; (2) Site security during the stoppage; (3) Remobilisation costs; (4) Programme extension preliminaries. The contractor may claim under the "prevention principle" if the client failed to secure necessary permissions or community agreements. As QS, treat it as a delay event — assess cost and time impact, determine liability based on contract terms and the cause of the protest.

**Q:** 180. A contractor's tile delivery was hijacked en route from the supplier to site. The tiles were R340,000 value and uninsured. Who carries this loss?
**A:** Risk in building materials passes as specified in the contract. Under JBCC: (1) If the contractor arranged delivery (FOB or ex-works), risk passed to the contractor when the goods left the supplier — contractor's loss; (2) If the client arranged delivery (delivered to site), risk may not have passed to the contractor — client's loss; (3) If the contract is silent, common law applies — the party in possession bears the risk. In practice, the contractor usually carries risk from delivery to site. The lesson: the contractor should have goods-in-transit insurance (typically 0.3-0.5% of goods value). As QS, verify insurance provisions are adequate before certifying payment for materials not yet on site.

## CONTRACT ADMINISTRATION, DISPUTES & FINAL ACCOUNT

**Q:** 181. A contractor's final account includes R45,000 for "disposal of broken tiles" but his waste manifest shows only 12 tons were disposed of at a licensed facility. The BOQ allowed 5% wastage on 2,000m2 (100m2 = approximately 3 tons). How do I deal with the discrepancy?
**A:** The discrepancy suggests either: (1) The contractor disposed of waste at an unlicensed facility for part of the project; (2) The contractor is claiming for tiles that were broken due to his own negligence; (3) The waste manifest is incomplete. As QS: (1) Request all weighbridge tickets for the full project period; (2) Compare actual waste tonnage against the BOQ wastage allowance (3 tons allowed); (3) If actual waste exceeds the allowance significantly, investigate why — was the substrate defective? Were there design changes? Was there contractor negligence? (4) Only certify disposal costs for waste generated by the contract scope, not for contractor-caused breakage. If the contractor cannot substantiate licensed disposal for all waste, withhold payment pending documentation — illegal dumping is a criminal offence.

**Q:** 182. A subcontractor has issued a Section 77 notice (intention to suspend work for non-payment) after the main contractor failed to pay two progress certificates. The main contractor says the tiling is defective. What is the QS's role?
**A:** The QS's role is to independently quantify what is properly due. Under the Construction Industry Development Board Act (prompt payment provisions) and the subcontract, the subcontractor has a right to suspend if not paid within the contract period. As QS: (1) Re-measure the completed work — is the amount certified correct? (2) Identify any defects and quantify the cost of rectification; (3) Calculate the nett amount properly due after deducting retention and defects; (4) Recommend payment of the nett amount to avoid suspension. If the main contractor's defect claim is spurious (a common tactic to delay payment), say so in your report. Suspension will cost the project more than prompt payment.

**Q:** 183. The architect has issued a "direction" to change the tile colour from white to grey. The contractor says this is a variation; the architect says it's "just a colour change, same price." Who is right, and how do I cost it?
**A:** A colour change is a variation if the original colour was specified in the contract documents and the contractor priced for it. Even if the tile price is the same (white and grey may be the same cost from the manufacturer), the variation generates costs: (1) Tiles already ordered in white may need to be returned — restocking fee 15-25%; (2) New tiles must be ordered — potential lead time delay; (3) If white tiles were already delivered, storage or return costs; (4) Programme impact if the change occurs after ordering. As QS, value the variation at the contract rates for the tile supply (same rate if same price) but add the quantifiable costs of disruption. Do not accept "same price" without assessing these consequential costs.

**Q:** 184. A contractor has submitted a "loss and expense" claim of R180,000 due to "disruption caused by other trades working in tiling areas." What substantiation must I demand before assessing this claim?
**A:** For a disruption claim, demand: (1) A detailed diary showing dates, times, and descriptions of disruptive events — corroborated by site instructions or daily reports; (2) Evidence that the contractor gave written notice of the disruption at the time (not months later); (3) Records showing the actual gang deployment vs planned deployment — were tilers standing idle? (4) Comparison of actual productivity vs tender allowance for the disrupted period; (5) Financial records showing the additional cost incurred (overtime, additional supervision, extended preliminaries); (6) Mitigation efforts — did the contractor try to work elsewhere on site? Without contemporaneous records, the claim lacks credibility. Assess only the substantiated portion — typically 30-50% of initial disruption claims are unsubstantiated.

**Q:** 185. The final account has been agreed at R4.85 million. The contractor now discovers he underpriced the adhesive quantity by 15% — he used 20% more than his tender allowance but the BOQ rates are fixed. Can he claim the additional adhesive cost?
**A:** No. Under a remeasurable contract, the contractor is paid for the measured quantity at the contract rate — if he used more adhesive than his rate allows, that is his pricing error, not a basis for additional payment. The contract rate is deemed to include all materials, labour, and methodology necessary to complete the work. The only exception: if the specification changed (e.g., from standard to full-bed adhesion), the additional consumption is a variation. If the specification remained the same and the contractor simply underestimated his own consumption, he has no claim. This is a fundamental principle of lump sum and remeasurement contracts — the contractor carries his own rate build-up risk.

**Q:** 186. A client's representative on site has been signing daywork sheets without verifying the work. R280,000 of daywork has been certified. At final account, the client disputes the daywork as unsubstantiated. What is the QS's position?
**A:** This is a difficult position. If the client's representative signed the daywork sheets, the client is generally bound by those signatures — the representative had ostensible authority to sign. However, if the daywork was clearly fraudulent or excessive, the client may challenge it. As QS: (1) Verify the daywork sheets have the required signatures; (2) Compare the daywork against the contract's daywork provisions (were the correct rates applied? Was daywork only used where no contract rate exists?); (3) If procedurally correct, recommend payment — the client's recourse is against their representative, not the contractor; (4) If procedurally defective (unsigned, incorrect rates, applied where contract rates exist), adjust the certification. Going forward, advise the client to brief their representatives on signing authority and verification requirements.

**Q:** 187. The contract includes a "set-off" clause allowing the client to deduct costs of rectifying defects from amounts due to the contractor. The client has engaged another contractor to rectify tiling defects at R95,000 without giving the original contractor opportunity to rectify. Is the set-off valid?
**A:** Potentially not. Most standard contracts (JBCC included) require the client to give the contractor reasonable notice and opportunity to rectify defects before engaging others. If the client unilaterally engaged a replacement without notice, the set-off may be challengeable. As QS: (1) Verify whether written notice was given to the original contractor; (2) If no notice, the set-off is procedurally defective — the client should pay the certified amount and pursue the contractor separately for rectification costs; (3) If notice was given and the contractor failed to respond within the contract period (typically 7-14 days), the set-off is valid. Document the notice trail carefully — set-off disputes are common in final accounts.

**Q:** 188. A contractor's final account claim includes R45,000 for "professional fees for QS services to prepare the final account." Is this a legitimate contract cost?
**A:** No. A contractor's cost of preparing his own payment application or final account is part of his overhead — included in the preliminaries and profit percentage, not a separate claimable item. The contractor engaged his own QS (if he has one) at his own cost. The client's QS fees are also not claimable by the contractor — they are the client's professional fees. Reject this item. The only exception: if the contract specifically provides for reimbursement of the contractor's professional fees in dispute resolution or adjudication proceedings, and the final account preparation was part of a formal dispute process. Standard final account preparation is never a claimable extra.

**Q:** 189. A project has reached Practical Completion but 3% of tiling defects remain unresolved. The contractor says he will fix them during the defects liability period. The client wants to withhold the full retention (R420,000) until all defects are fixed. Who is correct?
**A:** Under JBCC, half retention is released at Practical Completion (subject to any deductions for defects). The remaining half is released at Final Completion. If defects exist at PC but the contractor has committed to rectify during DLP, the standard treatment is: (1) Certify PC; (2) Release 50% of retention (less any amounts for defects the contractor refuses to rectify); (3) Hold the remaining 50% until Final Completion; (4) List the defects on the defects list. The client cannot withhold the full retention solely because defects exist — the DLP exists precisely for this purpose. If the defects are major (structural, waterproofing failure), PC may not be achievable. But minor defects at PC are normal and expected.

**Q:** 190. A tiling contractor has submitted a "global claim" for R680,000 citing "numerous delays and disruptions" without apportioning the amount to specific events. How should a QS respond to a global claim?
**A:** Global claims (lump-sum claims without causal linkage) are generally not accepted in South African construction adjudication. The QS should respond: (1) Request the contractor to apportion the claim to specific delay/disruption events with causal linkage (event X caused delay Y which cost Z); (2) For each event, demand: date, description, notice given, substantiation of delay, financial quantification; (3) If the contractor cannot apportion because events were so intertwined that separation is impossible, accept a "rolled-up" claim only if: the contractor can prove the client caused the delay, the contractor's tender was realistic, and there is no better way to quantify the loss; (4) Apply a sceptical but fair assessment — global claims often contain inflated or duplicated amounts. Assess the substantiated portion only.

## THE TRULY UNUSUAL — EDGE CASES THAT ACTUALLY HAPPENED

**Q:** 191. Atiling contractor has included a "psychometric assessment fee" of R12,000 per tiler because the project is in a maximum-security forensic psychiatric hospital. Is this claimable?
**A:** Yes. High-security psychiatric facilities require all workers to pass psychological assessment and security vetting before site access. The assessment fee (R8,000-15,000 per person) is a real cost, as is the 3-6 week vetting delay. Additionally: (1) Tilers must be escorted at all times — productivity reduction of 20-30%; (2) No power tools with blades longer than 100mm (security restriction) — increased cutting time; (3) Tool inventory checks at every entry/exit; (4) Work only during designated hours when patients are in secure areas. Total premium over standard tiling: 40-60%. The psychometric fee is claimable as a preliminary item if it was a known contract requirement.

**Q:** 192. A mining company's head office wants floor tiles that incorporate their crushed ore waste (gold-bearing quartz) as a decorative aggregate. They want 5,000m2 of these custom tiles. How do I cost this?
**A:** This is a custom manufacturing project, not standard tiling procurement. Cost components: (1) Tile development and sampling: R150,000-250,000 (3-6 months); (2) Mould making for the custom size: R80,000-120,000; (3) Custom tile supply at R450-750/m2 (10x standard due to small-batch production); (4) Trial production batch: R50,000-80,000; (5) Quality testing and certification: R25,000-40,000; (6) Higher wastage allowance — 10-15% for custom tiles vs 5% for standard. Total: R2.5-4.0 million for 5,000m2. Treat as a design-and-build provisional sum with performance specification. The mining company may accept the high cost for the corporate branding value.

**Q:** 193. A contractor has claimed R35,000 for "tile acclimatisation" — storing imported tiles in an air-conditioned room for 14 days before installation because the site was in a humid coastal location. Is this legitimate?
**A:** Partially. Tile acclimatisation (allowing tiles to reach ambient temperature and moisture equilibrium before installation) is good practice, especially for large-format tiles in humid conditions where thermal/moisture shock can cause cracking. However, full air-conditioned storage is excessive — ambient storage under cover is sufficient for most tiles. A legitimate acclimatisation cost would be: covered storage space (R2,000-4,000 for 2 weeks), not air-conditioning. If the tiles are natural stone (particularly moisture-sensitive like green marble), controlled acclimatisation is justified. For standard porcelain, reject the air-conditioning cost but allow for covered on-site storage.

**Q:** 194. A zoo's primate enclosure requires floor tiles that are "gorilla-proof" — the primates have been known to tear up flooring. What specification and cost applies?
**A:** Gorilla and large primate enclosures require extreme-duty flooring: (1) Minimum 20mm thick full-body porcelain (not glazed — the glaze layer can be chipped); (2) Epoxy adhesive with steel reinforcement mesh embedded in the screed; (3) No perimeter gaps — tiles must be cut to fit tightly against walls with no edge to lift; (4) Screed must be minimum 75mm thick reinforced concrete, not standard 25mm screed; (5) Edge protection — steel angle strips at all exposed edges. Cost: R850-1,200/m2 — comparable to industrial flooring. The enclosure must be empty during installation (animal relocation costs are separate). This is a specialist zoo design consultant's domain — do not attempt to specify without specialist input.

**Q:** 195. A client wants to install LED lighting strips embedded in the grout joints between tiles on a feature wall. How does this affect the tiling cost, and who installs the LEDs?
**A:** LED grout lines are a specialist feature requiring coordination between tiling and electrical trades. Cost impacts: (1) Wider grout joints (8-10mm vs standard 3mm) to accommodate LED strip: minimal material cost difference; (2) LED channel extrusion recessed into the grout joint: R250-450/linear metre; (3) LED strip and driver: R180-350/linear metre; (4) Installation labour: tilers install the channel extrusion during grouting, electricians connect the LED strips after grouting — dual trade involvement adds 30-40% to labour; (5) Programming and commissioning: separate M&E cost. Total: R450-850/linear metre of LED grout line. Measure the linear metres of LED channels separately from tiling. The tiling contractor installs the channels; the electrical contractor installs the LED components.

**Q:** 196. A maritime museum wants tiles from a shipwreck (recovered ceramic cargo) installed as a feature floor. The tiles are irregular, 300 years old, and of varying thickness. How is this costed?
**A:** Shipwreck tile installation is specialist conservation work, not standard tiling. Cost approach: (1) Conservation cleaning and stabilisation of recovered tiles: R500-1,000 per tile; (2) Substrate preparation with adjustable bed system to accommodate varying tile thickness: R180-250/m2; (3) Hand-laying by conservator (not tiler) at 2-3 tiles per hour: labour constant of 2.0-3.0 hrs/m2; (4) Specialist adhesive compatible with archaeological ceramics: R65-95/m2; (5) No grout — tiles are butted with lime mortar infill; (6) Museum lighting and climate control must be operational during installation. Total: R2,500-4,000/m2. This is a provisional sum with a heritage conservator as the specialist contractor. Standard tiling rates are irrelevant.

**Q:** 197. A project requires tiling inside a Faraday cage room (EMI shielded). The specification says "standard floor tiles." What is the concern?
**A:** A Faraday cage room requires continuous electromagnetic shielding — all penetrations, including floor joints, must maintain electrical continuity. Standard tiles with cement grout create electrical discontinuity at every joint. The floor system must: (1) Use conductive epoxy adhesive and grout to maintain electrical continuity; (2) Incorporate a conductive underlayment bonded to the Faraday cage floor; (3) Have all tile joints bridged with conductive strips if epoxy grout is insufficient; (4) Be tested for shielding effectiveness after installation (R25,000-40,000 test). Cost premium: 50-70% over standard tiling. If the Faraday cage integrity is compromised by the tiling, the entire room fails certification. This is an M&E specialist specification, not a standard tiling item.

**Q:** 198. A client wants a "living floor" — tiles with embedded seeds that will germinate and grow moss between the tiles over time. Is this buildable, and what does it cost?
**A:** This is an experimental landscape architecture concept, not standard tiling. Technical approach: (1) Large-format porous tiles (terracotta or unglazed porcelain) with 20-30mm joints; (2) Substrate is a 150mm depth of growing medium, not standard screed; (3) Irrigation system embedded in the substrate; (4) Seeds or moss plugs planted in the joints after tile installation; (5) Drainage layer below the growing medium. Cost: R850-1,500/m2 for the complete system — comparable to intensive green roofing. The "tiling" component is minor; the growing system dominates. Maintenance is critical — moss requires shade and moisture. This should be priced as a landscape architecture provisional sum, not a tiling item. Most tiling contractors will not tender for this.

**Q:** 199. A contractor has included a "sacrificial anode" system to protect reinforcement in the concrete slab beneath the tiles. The project is 50km inland. Is this necessary, and what does it cost?
**A:** Sacrificial anode cathodic protection is used for reinforced concrete in chloride environments (coastal, industrial) or where carbonation has reached reinforcement depth. It is unnecessary 50km inland unless: (1) The concrete has severe carbonation (pH below 9); (2) There is a specific chloride contamination source (de-icing salts, industrial effluent). Cost: R450-850/m2 for impressed current cathodic protection, R250-450/m2 for sacrificial anodes. If the slab is sound and the environment is not corrosive, reject the item as unnecessary. If there is genuine reinforcement corrosion risk (confirmed by petrographic analysis), the issue is structural repair, not a tiling preliminary. The anode system is a structural maintenance item, not a tiling cost.

**Q:** 200. A client has asked me to prepare a "whole-life cost analysis" comparing 600x600mm porcelain tiles (R285/m2, 50-year life) with 500x500mm ceramic tiles (R195/m2, 25-year life). How do I present this comparison?
**A:** Whole-life cost analysis over 50 years: (1) Porcelain: initial cost R285/m2, no replacement, annual maintenance R5/m2 (cleaning, joint repair), 50-year cost = R285 + (R5 x 50) = R535/m2; (2) Ceramic: initial cost R195/m2, replacement at year 25 (R195/m2 in future value, discounted to present value at 8% = R28.50/m2 PV), annual maintenance R8/m2 (higher due to more frequent joint issues), 50-year cost = R195 + R28.50 + (R8 x 50) = R623.50/m2. The porcelain has lower whole-life cost despite higher initial cost. However, if the building has a 20-year design life, ceramic may be optimal. Present both scenarios with the discount rate and assumptions clearly stated. Whole-life cost is a powerful decision-making tool — it often justifies higher initial quality.
